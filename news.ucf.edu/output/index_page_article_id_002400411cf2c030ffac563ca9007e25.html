Woman Recalls Poor Treatment by White Students After Father's Lawsuit Integrated California Schools

By Mallery Laing Oct. 21, 2004

Related Contacts and Links:

  • Luis Martinez-Fernandez

    • 407-823-1174

  • Latin American, Caribbean and Latino Studies Program

  • Print this Page

Sylvia Méndez often begged her father to let her go back to the dreadful Mexican school after he won a legal fight in 1947 to enroll her and about 5,000 other Mexican-American children in white schools.

 

But Méndez knew she had to succeed, even though her white peers called her names and treated her poorly.

 

I would go home crying. Why am I going to that school where I am not wanted? she said during a presentation Wednesday at UCF. My father simply told me, I fought for you to go there.

 

Méndez, a 68-year-old retired nurse, devotes much of her life to telling others about Méndez v. Westminster, the California court case that opened up schools to Mexican-American students and laid some of the groundwork for the better-known Brown v. Board of Education decision, which led to the integration of African-American students in white schools.

 

I want this case recognized as a historical presence, Méndez said. I want it taught in history classes. We have come along way, but we still have a long way to go, she said.

 

In 1944, Méndez, 9, was denied entrance to the local white elementary school. A year later, her father and other parents filed a lawsuit against four Orange County, Calif., school districts on behalf of about 5,000 Mexican-American schoolchildren.

 

Méndez testified during the trial to show that Mexican-American students had the same capacity for learning as their white counterparts. The lawsuit succeeded, as a state and federal court ruled in favor of the Mexican families.

 

Sandra Robbie and KOCE public television produced an Emmy Award-winning documentary, Méndez Vs. Westminster: Para Todos los Ninos. Students and professors viewed the film on Wednesday at the Nicholson School of Communications and then discussed the case with Méndez.

 

During the celebration of National Hispanic Heritage Month from Sept. 15 to Oct. 15, Méndez met with President George W. Bush at the White House and also shared her story with key Democrats, including U.S. Sen. Hillary Rodham Clinton of New York.

 

Méndez said her visit to the White House was an honor. The president brought her in a room with a dozen different diplomats and other famous people and told them her fathers story. When he asked her to stand, she was stunned.

 

I couldnt even get up, she said. I was so surprised he knew the case, and I had no idea he would do that.

 

The Méndez case featured two participants who played major roles in the 1954 Brown v. Board of Education case. Thurgood Marshall advised the Méndez familys lawyers, and then-California Gov. Earl Warren signed the desegregation order following the Méndez ruling. In 1954, Marshall was the lead attorney for the Brown v. Board case, and Warren was the U.S. Supreme Courts chief justice.

 

Méndezs presentation was co-sponsored by the UCF Latin American, Caribbean and Latino Studies program; the Office of Undergraduate Studies; the Burnett Honors College; the Womens Studies Program; the Hispanic American Students Association; Lambda Theta Alpha and the Office of Diversity Initiatives.
