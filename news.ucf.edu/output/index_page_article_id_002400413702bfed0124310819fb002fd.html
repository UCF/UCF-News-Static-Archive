Congressman Frey's New Book, 'Political Rules of the Road,' to Benefit UCF Institute

By Chad Binette Dec. 2, 2009

Photo: Courtesy of Lou Frey Institute of Politics and Government

Lou Frey's UCF institute promotes civic education and engagement, particularly among young people.

If you have to explain yourself, you are in trouble. Never get so mad at someone that you can’t make a deal with him the next day. And never use a live animal in a TV commercial.

Those are three of the more than 500 tips found in former U.S. Congressman Lou Frey’s new book, “Political Rules of the Road.” The 170-page book mixes sage advice and humor to teach readers – particularly high school and college students – all about politics.

Frey founded the Lou Frey Institute of Politics and Government at UCF in 2002, and the book helps to fulfill the institute’s mission of promoting civic education and engagement, particularly among young people. Some of the book’s proceeds will benefit the institute.

“Political Rules of the Road” includes advice from more than 170 people, including presidents Gerald Ford, Jimmy Carter and George H.W. Bush, Vice President Dick Cheney and former House Speakers Newt Gingrich and Tip O’Neill.

The book’s co-editor is Aubrey Jewett, associate chair of UCF’s Political Science Department.

Frey, who represented Central Florida in Congress from 1969 to 1979, included two of his own rules in the book: 1.) If you have to explain, you are in trouble; and 2.) Don’t get in a fight with someone who buys his ink by the carload.

“The institute enables the next generation to learn how to be active participants in the democratic process,” Frey said. “‘Political Rules of the Road’ helps to achieve that goal by providing young people with practical and realistic guidance about the realities of serving their communities and country as political leaders.”

The book includes advice pertaining to campaigns and elections, congressional service, political parties, the media and more. Below are examples of the contributors’ advice:

-- “Let’s hear from the candidates, not their consultants. Too many of the latter are hired guns, for whom the lure of celebrity takes the place of conviction or even loyalty.” President Gerald W. Ford.

-- “We have a tendency to exalt ourselves and to dwell on the weaknesses and mistakes of others.  I have come to realize that in every person there is something fine and pure and noble, along with a desire for self-fulfillment. Political and religious leaders must attempt to provide a society within which these human attributes can be nurtured and enhanced.” President Jimmy Carter.

-- “If you’ve got him (an opponent) down, kick him. If you let him up, he’ll just be madder and meaner.” Jay Rhodes III, former Republican congressman from Arizona.

-- “It is easier to do what you believe is right and defend it than try to figure out what is politically popular and try to defend it.” Elizabeth Holtzman, former Democratic congressman from New York.

Frey will discuss the book on C-SPAN’s “Washington Journal” at 8:30 a.m. Friday, Dec. 4. He also has three upcoming book signings scheduled in Central Florida:

-- Dec. 7, 6:30 p.m., Winter Park Public Library, 460 E. New England Ave., Winter Park

-- Dec. 15, 7 p.m., Borders, 600 N. Orlando Ave., Winter Park

-- Jan. 9, 10 a.m., a book talk at the Orlando Public Library, 101 E. Central Blvd., Orlando

The Lou Frey Institute of Politics and Government is a nonpartisan organization housed in the UCF Department of Political Science. Institute programs encourage informed discussion of issues from diverse viewpoints, stimulate participation in the political process and support research on politics and policy.

Frey was recently honored with the U.S. Association of Former Members of Congress’ 2009 Distinguished Service Award for his efforts to improve civics education and encourage young people to be more actively involved in their government.

All profits from the book will benefit the Lou Frey Institute and the Association of Former Members of Congress.
