Exhibits, Events Tie Frankenstein to Biotechnology, Robotics, Diversity Issues

By Chad Binette Nov. 2, 2004

Photo: Kelly Petrik

UCF Libraries will host "Frankenstein: Penetrating the Secrets of Nature" through Dec. 10.

A series of events hosted by the University of Central Florida Libraries uses Frankenstein, the monster featured in the 186-year-old Mary Shelley novel, to teach lessons in biotechnology, diversity and a variety of other topics.

The lectures, movie screenings and workshops coincide with a traveling exhibition on Frankenstein that the UCF Library is displaying through Dec. 10. The main exhibit, Frankenstein: Penetrating the Secrets of Nature, is on loan from the American Library Association. Separate exhibits feature cloning patents, Victorian mourning and funeral customs, and cartoons drawn by Dana Summers of the Orlando Sentinel.

The story of Frankenstein fits in with so many areas of our culture, said Meg Scharf, the librarys associate director for Public Services. So many themes and ideas are contained in that one novel, and were trying to explore as many of them as we can.

Science-fiction author Ben Bova, Hollywood screenwriter Mark Kruger and several UCF professors in disciplines from biomedical sciences to philosophy will give presentations during the next three weeks. The series also includes the screenings of five Frankenstein movies and entries in the UCF Student Frankenstein Film Competition.

Kruger gave a presentation entitled Scary Movies and the Screenwriters Who Create Them on Monday, Nov. 8. Bova will speak at 7 p.m. Nov. 18 in the Live Oak Room of the UCF Marketplace building.

Other upcoming events include a discussion on Frankenstein and diversity at noon Nov. 16 and Frankensteins Three Brains: Cognitive Neuro-Science-Fiction, a presentation by Shaun Gallagher, chairman of the UCF Philosophy Department and director of the Cognitive Sciences Program, at noon Nov. 22.  Those events will be in Room 223 of the Library.

Eighty libraries in the United States are hosting the Frankenstein exhibit between fall 2002 and spring 2006. The UCF Library is the first of three in Florida to receive the exhibit; Putnam County libraries are scheduled to host it Dec. 22 to Feb. 8, and Stetson Universitys library is set to display the exhibit March 2 to April 15.

Using a $1,000 grant from the National Endowment for the Humanities and support from several area businesses, UCFs library staff put together a more extensive series of events than other libraries are offering along with the national exhibit, Scharf said.

The exhibit opened Oct. 27 at UCF. Frankenstein events at the Library began with an Oct. 26 presentation on how to put on fright make-up. Other events last week included a discussion on organ donation and an opening reception with a lecture by Rick Schell, UCFs assistant vice president of Academic Affairs and the dean of Undergraduate Studies.

A full schedule of public events during the Frankenstein series is posted below. There is no charge to attend any of the events or view the exhibits. More information on the Frankenstein events and displays and the librarys hours can be viewed at www.library.ucf.edu/frankenstein .

Schedule of events:

It was a Dark and Stormy Night, presentation by Rick Schell, assistant vice president of Academic Affairs and dean of Undergraduate Studies, 1 p.m. Nov. 14, Orlando Public Library, 101 E. Central Blvd. Call 407-835-7323 for more information.

The Frankenstein Complex and Its Impact on Robotics, presentation by Joanne Pransky, who focuses on the emotional aspects and psychological implications of a society in which robots are part of everyday life, 3 p.m. Nov. 15. Go to www.robot.md for more information.
