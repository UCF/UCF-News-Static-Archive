Three Join UCF's Pegasus Professor Elite

By Kimberly Lewis April 7, 2010

Photo: Ed Heiland

The Pegasus Professor Award recognizes extraordinary contributions to the UCF community through teaching, research and service.

An engineer dedicated to helping students succeed in science and math, an educator who has trained and mentored future teachers for 25 years and an optics scientist whose invention allows millions to see cell phone and computer screens in sunlight have won UCF’s most prestigious faculty award.

Michael Georgiopoulos, Jennifer Platt, and S.T. Wu received their Pegasus Professor awards today during the annual Founders’ Day ceremony. The award recognizes extraordinary contributions to the UCF community through teaching, research and service.

Since 2000, only 21 UCF professors have received the award.

As Pegasus Professors, Georgiopoulos, Platt and Wu each received a statue of the UCF Pegasus, a gold Pegasus Professor medallion and a check for $5,000.

Michael Georgiopoulos, College of Engineering and Computer Science, has taught at UCF since 1986.  He is the principal investigator on a $1.8 million National Science Foundation grant that funds UCF’s EXCEL program. Since its inception in 1986, EXCEL has become a national model for retaining students in science, technology and math disciplines – it has increased retention of those students at UCF by nearly 20 percent. While NSF funding for EXCEL expires this year, UCF will continue to support the program because of the positive results.

Georgiopoulos has led 48 funded grants totaling more than $8.5 million, chaired six committees and published more than 250 papers. He serves as associate editor of Neural Networks Journal and the Computer Journal. He has also been the graduate coordinator to more than 300 students, and since 2003 he has mentored more than 60 undergraduates. He also has established two new undergraduate courses, Current Topics in Machine Learning I and II.

“The extensive services Dr. Georgiopoulos has provided to the [electrical engineering] and [computer engineering] graduate programs are of great value,” said Issa Batarseh, director of the School of Electrical Engineering and Computer Science. “For the particular service he has offered to the graduate programs of EECS, I am forever indebted to him.”

Jennifer Platt is a professor and executive associate dean for Academic Affairs in the College of Education.  She began her career at UCF 25 years ago as an assistant professor and coordinator of exceptional education at the Cocoa campus. 

Platt’s devotion to teaching is exemplified in an award bearing her name created by students at the UCF Cocoa campus – the Jennifer Platt Award for Outstanding Student Achievement.

Platt has generated more than $1.7 million in research funding for projects focused on recruitment, development and retention in teacher education.  Her current research program, Paraprofessionals Acquiring Certification in Exceptional Education, will produce 51 new exceptional education teachers for Florida.

Platt has served on UCF’s Faculty Senate, chaired the University Research Committee, served on the Strategic Planning Executive Committee, served on the advisory board for the UCF Faculty Center for Teaching and Learning and chaired the Success to Significance Committee for the College of Education.

“Throughout the course of her 25 years of service to UCF, Dr. Platt has had a distinct influence on the growth and shape of the university, the professional development of faculty and staff members, and the lives and education of tens of thousands of students whom she has mentored and guided throughout their studies and beyond,” said Sandra Robinson, dean of the College of Education.

Shin-Tson (S.T.) Wu has been a professor in CREOL, the College of Optics and Photonics, since 2001. His contributions to UCF and to the science of LCD technology have been prolific.

Wu’s scientific discoveries and technological innovations have impacted millions of lives. Most notably, he invented the technology allowing people to read a laptop or cell phone screen under sunlight.

Wu has published six books, six book chapters, more than 350 peer-reviewed journal papers and more than 140 conference papers. He and his team have earned 66 patents.

During the past five years, Wu’s group has received 39 patents, nearly 20 percent of all UCF patents issued in this period. Wu’s accomplishments are a major reason why UCF recently ranked alongside Harvard, Princeton and MIT as universities leading the nation for the power and impact of patents.

“Dr. Wu has made numerous important scientific and technological contributions to the science and engineering of [LCDs … which] have added tremendously to the reputation and visibility of CREOL and UCF,” said Bahaa Saleh, dean of CREOL, the College of Optics and Photonics.
