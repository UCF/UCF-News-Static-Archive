Professor: Orlando Area May Become Next 'Mecca For Puerto Rican Migration'

By Chad Binette Sept. 3, 2005

Photo: Chad Binette

The Latin Music Institute's string ensemble performed a variety of Puerto Rican music Friday evening during a community event coordinated by the UCF Latin American, Caribbean and Latino Studies program.

Orange and Osceola counties will become the next mecca of Puerto Rican migration, likely surpassing Chicagos and Philadelphias populations of Puerto Ricans if current trends continue, a University of Puerto Rico professor said Friday.

Speaking at a community event coordinated by the University of Central Floridas Latin American, Caribbean and Latino Studies Program, Professor Jorgé Duany said the Orlando area then would have the second-largest Puerto Rican population in the United States, trailing only New York City.

Puerto Ricans continue to migrate to the United States for many reasons, including the higher poverty and unemployment rates in Puerto Rico and the high murder rate in San Juan, Duany said. Many Puerto Ricans are moving to Fort Lauderdale, Miami, Tampa and other Florida cities in addition to the Orlando area.

Duany addressed a crowd of about 150 people at Asociación Borinqueña during the first of three fall events celebrating the dynamic history, culture and economic transformation of Puerto Rico and the accomplishments of Puerto Ricans living in the United States. Summaries of upcoming events on Sept. 30 and Oct. 20 are included at the bottom of this article.

Luis Martínez-Fernández, who directs the UCF Latin American, Caribbean and Latino Studies program, also is teaching a new course on the History of Puerto Rico.

The class explores Puerto Ricos status as a commonwealth that is neither an independent nation nor a U.S. state, its relationships with other nations and the islands rapid economic growth during the past several decades. The course, which has an enrollment of 45 students, also includes sessions about the Puerto Rican experience in the United States.

Although Puerto Rico, relatively speaking, is a small island, it has had a very rich history, Martínez-Fernández said. In the 1930s, Puerto Rico was one of the poorest countries in the Caribbean. In 40 to 50 years, it became one of Latin Americas countries with the highest standard of living.

The three public events in the A Celebration of Puerto Rican History & Culture series combine lectures about Puerto Rican history with artistic presentations of photographs and music. They are being presented in English.

At Asociación Borinqueña, the Winter Park-based Latin Music Institutes Rondalla de Orlando string ensemble performed a variety of Puerto Rican music. Director Ángel Martínez explained the origins of the different types of music and the instruments used by the ensemble.

Orange County Commissioner Mildred Fernández was the guest of honor on Friday, and she briefly discussed how she values her Puerto Rican heritage.

Duany spoke about the stages of Puerto Rican migration, which began with small numbers of Puerto Ricans heading to New York City in the late 1800s. Many of them were political exiles, he said.

Despite the small numbers, the Puerto Rican movement to the U.S. had a strong and enduring impact culturally and politically, he said.

At least 500,000 Puerto Ricans traveled to the U.S. mainland between 1945 and 1965, a period known as The Great Migration, he said. By the early 1970s, when the Puerto Rican economy was booming while the economies of many U.S. cities were struggling, more Puerto Ricans were leaving the United States than migrating here.

In the 1980s, many Puerto Ricans were following a pattern known as circle migration, Duany said.

We were beginning to see large numbers of people who come for a while to visit or explore or study, and then go back to the island, Duany said, noting that at least 130,000 Puerto Ricans traveled back and forth to the island at least twice in the 1980s.

Puerto Ricans now comprise 11 percent of Orange Countys population, according to U.S. Census estimates, and they are taking on more prominent roles in business, government, education and many other aspects of society.

Through the new course and public events, UCFs Latin American, Caribbean and Latino Studies Program wants to help students and residents better understand Puerto Ricos culture and history.

Diversity is an important and valuable asset to our community, said Kathryn Seidel, dean of the UCF College of Arts and Sciences. Our Puerto Rican friends have made great contributions to Central Florida, and we look forward to recognizing those efforts. We will continue to educate our students and the greater community about the islands history and significance.

Major sponsors for the series are Darden Restaurants and the law firm Payas, Payas and Payas. Media sponsors are Al Día Today, El Nuevo Día, La Prensa, WKMG-Channel 6 and Telemundo-Orlando. Organizations that helped UCF coordinate events include El Nuevo Día, the Latin Music Institute, the Puerto Rico Federal Affairs Administration and the Orange County Hispanic Heritage Celebration Committee.

For more information, go to www.cas.ucf.edu/lacls .
