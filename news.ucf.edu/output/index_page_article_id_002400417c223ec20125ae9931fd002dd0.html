'News Media in Transition'

By Andrea Perez Jan. 27, 2010

Photo: Mark Freeman

Jamie McIntyre, a former CNN Senior Pentagon Correspondent who now is a blogger and consultant.

The tone was realistic yet encouraging during a UCF forum titled “News Media in Transition: Trends and Future Prospects” on Thursday, Jan. 21. The program, organized by the UCF Global Perspectives Office, featured several national and regional experts who offered their predictions for the future of news media, as well as insights into its current state.

An audience of about 250, including many UCF communication students, listened as the panelists spoke of the decline of print media and the advent of new and innovative ways to deliver news.

Suzanne Seggerman, president and co-founder of Games for Change and a former documentary film producer for PBS, gave the opening keynote presentation on video and computer games as a successful method of engaging people on social issues from around the world. Seggerman explained that people have always responded dramatically to new forms of media, and that the interaction inherent to video games -- something missing from newspapers and television -- translates well for learning to interpret global issues from various perspectives.

Seggerman described games such as Food Force, a U.N. World Food Program initiative where players take on the challenges of feeding the hungry, and Peacemaker, a game that allows players to adopt the roles of either the Israeli prime minister or the Palestinian president to bring peace to the Middle East. With more than 1 million downloads, and wide use in schools around the world, Peacemaker is an example of the power gaming can have in creating an enticing medium for understanding current global issues.

Charlotte Hall, editor of the Orlando Sentinel, began the Florida News Media panel by discussing the current transition of print news media. She explained that journalism is in the process of searching for its new business model. She noted that it is advertising that is changing and dying, not newspapers.

Sara Brady, of Sara Brady Public Relations and formerly of Bright House Networks, shared insights from her experiences as a journalist, as well as her present occupation. She underscored the need for online news sources to focus on obtaining accurate information as much as they emphasize getting that information first.

Ruben Funes, editor-in-chief of La Prensa, a weekly Spanish language newspaper, finished the Florida News Media panel by observing that, although he is confident newspapers will survive in print, he cannot predict how many pages will be left or the content that will prevail.

The National News Media panel began with James Hill, managing editor of The Washington Post Writers Group, who asserted that news media have always been in transition. Today, the Internet presents a challenge, but it also allows the news to travel further and faster, he continued. Hill said the Internet is part of the news media’s natural evolution. He expressed concern over shrinking newsrooms and job losses, though, adding, “surgery can only go so far; eventually the patient will bleed to death.”

Jamie McIntyre, a former CNN Senior Pentagon Correspondent who now is a blogger and consultant, called the transition a “hyper-revolutionary change.” He was candid in his assessment that print news is dead, with a few exceptions, and that the future will see people using alternative devices and methods for obtaining their news. For all of the journalism students on hand, McIntyre said: “Good news: There will be lots of jobs for journalists. Bad news: Most of them will pay slave wages,” referring to the unpredictability of blogging and other alternative careers in journalism.

Amitabh Pal, co-editor of the Progressive Media Project, closed the forum with a focus on diversity related to both the lag in representation of minorities in newsrooms and the variety of ways news is delivered. According to Pal, “diverse journalism means an increase in different perspectives.” He went on to suggest that this is something for current news media to not only accept but embrace.

In addition to the Global Perspectives Office, sponsors included the UCF Nicholson School of Communication, the UCF Political Science Department, UCF LIFE and the Global Connections Foundation.
