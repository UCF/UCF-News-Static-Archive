UCF Latin American Festival Honors Puerto Rico's Guitar Maker

By Christine Dellert Sept. 4, 2008

Photo: Courtesy of Velázquez Guitars

Don Manuel Velázquez, a world-famous classic guitar maker, will be honored at this year's UCF Latin American Cultural Festival. He still runs a workshop in Winter Springs.

Ever since he was 12, Don Manuel Velázquez has been making guitars. His first was crafted from a wooden cheese crate, abandoned outside a store in Puerto Rico.

Velázquez’s later works are in the hands of world-renowned musicians José Rey de la Torre, Paul Simon, Harry Belafonte and Keith Richards. He’s considered a national treasure in his home country.

Now 92, Velázquez still carves out guitars at his studio in Winter Springs alongside his son, Alfredo.

Regarded as one of the greatest classic guitar makers in the world, Don Manuel Velázquez is this year’s guest of honor at the University of Central Florida Latin American Cultural Festival of Orlando. The entire six-week festival, which begins Sunday, Sept. 7, and a special guitar tribute concert later this month are dedicated to him.

In its fourth year, the festival during Hispanic Heritage Month is a celebration of Latin American music, film, studies, tradition and culture, with events held on UCF’s campus and in the community.

“A wealth of cultural heritage originating in Latin America continues to migrate to Central Florida as our Hispanic population grows and becomes more diverse,” said José Maunez-Cuadra, the festival coordinator and director of UCF’s Latin American, Caribbean & Latino Studies Program. “We want to celebrate and encourage the rich contributions of Hispanics in Central Florida and the United States.”

The festival’s kick-off concert, “Rumba Latina,” will bring together musicians from the states and Puerto Rico to perform a Rumbón, a musical party with a unique Latin flavor. The concert starts at 4 p.m. Sunday, in University High School’s auditorium, 11501 Eastwood Drive in Orlando.

“It’s great for the whole Latin community,” said Nora Lee Garcia, a UCF flute professor who will perform at the concert. “My goal is to bring music that the new generation might not know, and this festival will ensure that a variety of Latino cultures are involved in activities, celebrations and events.”

Garcia also knows Velázquez’s work well, both as a professional musician and his daughter-in-law.

“Don Manuel is one of the most inspiring persons I know,” she said. “Every time I see him working, he is so focused and so full of energy.”

For the first time ever, Don Manuel and Alfredo Velázquez are making a guitar together, and it will be played Sunday, Sept. 28, during a tribute concert in the UCF Music Rehearsal Hall. Performing at the concert will be Virginia Luque, an award-winning Spanish guitarist and student of Andrés Segovia, the father of the modern classical guitar movement.

Tennessean film and classic guitar maker Paul Polycarpou will also screen a short documentary about Don Manuel Velázquez’s life and craft.

In his early 20s, Velázquez moved from Puerto Rico to New York City and found employment as a woodworker in the shipyards. He made his first guitars in the United States from salvaged mess tables off a sunken troop ship brought to the docks. From there, he opened a workshop and was touted by some of the top players in 1950s New York as “the best master in the United States.”

Velázquez has always had an admiration for the guitar and wants people to see the beauty of it through very elegant and profound sound, his son says.

“It’s certainly a great honor for him to be recognized,” Alfredo Velázquez said. “He’s very humble about it. But this is special, to be recognized here in Central Florida.”

Other events during the festival include concerts by the UCF Symphony Orchestra, Orlando Concert Band and St. Luke's Methodist Church of Windermere Chorus; art exhibits on campus and at the Maitland Art Center; a one-man comedy show about growing up in Puerto Rico and New York City; academic lectures about cultural identity and Mexican-American communities; and an Argentinean film presentation at the Enzian Theater.

Many of the events are free to the public. Sponsors include UCF, La Prensa, WKMG-TV-Channel 6, the Orlando Magic, Banco Popular, WUCF-89.9 FM, the Hispanic Chamber of Commerce of Metro Orlando and the Enzian Theater.

For more information on this year’s festival, call 407-823-1173 or 407-823-1174, or e-mail latinamerica@mail.ucf.edu. To see the events’ listing, go to http://www.cah.ucf.edu/lacfo/.

To learn more about Don Manuel Velázquez, visit his site at http://www.velazquezguitars.com.
