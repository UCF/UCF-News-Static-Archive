UCF Study: Term Limits Have Not Increased Competition in Legislative Races

By Chad Binette Feb. 27, 2006

Related Contacts and Links:

  • Scot Schraufnagel

    • 386-506-4007

  • Data: Legislative demographics before and after term limits

  • UCF Department of Political Science

  • Print this Page

Contrary to the hopes of supporters, term limits have failed to make Florida legislative races more competitive or increase the numbers of women and non-lawyers in the Legislature, a University of Central Florida political scientist has concluded.

Term limits began forcing some legislators to leave office in 2000, and elections since then have been decided by larger margins than elections from 1988 to 1998. The average margin of contested Senate races has increased from 19.5 to 23.6 percentage points, while the average margin of House races has risen from 20.8 to 22.4 percentage points, Assistant Professor Scot Schraufnagel found.

Seats open because of term limits often are won by candidates who have held other political offices and who raise a few hundred thousand dollars or more to keep highly qualified challengers out of races, Schraufnagel said. And the winning candidates often arent much different from the legislators they replace.

The 160-member Legislature included 123 men after the 2004 elections, up from 122 after the 1998 elections. Lawyers held 40 seats in the House and Senate after the 2004 elections compared with 38 seats after the 1998 elections.

While the names of legislators have by and large changed, much else has stayed the same, Schraufnagel said.

Florida was one of 21 states that adopted term limits for legislators beginning in the early 1990s. Since then, six states have voted down term limits or had them ruled unconstitutional by courts.

By forcing out long-tenured legislators thought to be out of touch with their constituents, supporters of term limits hoped to create more healthy competition for public offices and to make legislatures more diverse demographically and professionally.

Term limits have produced significant turnover in the House and Senate, as 55 of 120 House members and 11 and 40 senators were forced to give up their seats in 2000. Since then, an additional 22 House members and 12 senators have been forced out of office by term limits.

However, increased competition and demographic changes have not materialized in Florida, a state that provides good opportunities to study the effects of term limits because it has about the same number of Republicans and Democrats and held closely contested presidential races in 2000 and 2004.

Like before term limits took effect, career politicians are moving from one office to another within state government or between state and local governments. Former state representatives have filled 17 of the 23 Senate vacancies caused by term limits since 2000. Eighteen former city council members, mayors, county commissioners and state senators were elected to the 55 House vacancies created by term limits in 2000.

Minorities have made minor gains in the Legislature since 1999, but the study determined that those results are not statistically significant. The number of African-American state legislators has increased from 20 to 24, while the number of Hispanics has risen from 13 to 16. Those gains can be attributed at least partly to the way legislators redrew the boundaries of House and Senate districts in 2002, Schraufnagel said.

Schraufnagels findings will be published in the spring issue of the State Politics and Policy Quarterly journal.
