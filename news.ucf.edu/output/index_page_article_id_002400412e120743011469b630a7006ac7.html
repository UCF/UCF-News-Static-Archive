Battling Bigotry: Ex-Neo Nazi, Jena Student Protestors to Speak at Diversity Week

By Christine Dellert Oct. 12, 2007

Photo: Steve Williams

UCF student Angela King, a former neo-Nazi skinhead, will share her experiences and her road out of the racist movement during this year’s Diversity Week.

University of Central Florida student Angela King spent three years in prison and nearly a decade violently persecuting others. Determined to make up for her past, the former neo-Nazi skinhead will share those experiences and her road out of the racist movement during this years Diversity Week at UCF.

UCF is hosting a variety of sessions this week on diversity and tolerance issues related to the university and the community. Events include a video presentation and discussion from students who recently traveled to Jena, La.; a forum on immigrant and refugee children in local schools; and stories about a 1960s civil-rights activist.

A kick-off breakfast Monday featured keynote speaker Carmen Jones, founder and president of a Virginia-based marketing firm that works with businesses and the disabled community. While in college, Jones was injured in an auto accident that left her a paraplegic.

This years Diversity Week theme, Diverse Abilities: Expanding the Vision, comes from Jones experience, say coordinators at the Office of Diversity Initiatives, which organizes the weeks activities.

Diversity Week is the time when were the most visible to the university, said Melissa David, program assistant for the Office of Diversity Initiatives. This is our chance to showcase what we do and the types of events we host all year long.

Diversity Week runs Oct. 15 through Oct. 19. Most events are free and open to the public. For more information about the events, visit http://www.diversity.ucf.edu.

Heres a look at several of the weeks speakers and activities:

-- Angela King will speak at noon and 4:30 p.m. Oct. 17 in the Student Unions Cape Florida Room. She was arrested in 1998 and convicted for acting as a lookout during the armed robbery of a South Florida adult-video store. King has since graduated with her bachelors degree and enrolled in UCFs Masters in Interdisciplinary Studies program. She routinely speaks to college and community groups and has been interviewed by the Southern Poverty Law Center and National Public Radio.

-- UCFs African American Student Union (AASU) will present video clips from its trip last month to Jena, La. More than 50 UCF students joined the thousands of others in protest there. What Really Happened in Jena, Louisiana starts at 6 p.m. Oct. 15 in the Student Unions Cape Florida Room. Its perfect timing for us, said AASU Council Representative Alexis Chapin, a 20-year-old junior. Its mostly to bring awareness to UCF and the community because a lot of people didnt know what was going on, even though it was on the front page of the newspaper.

-- The Multicultural Student Center is hosting a forum on Fannie Lou Hamer, the famous voting-rights activist and civil-rights leader whom police beat up when she tried registering to vote in Mississippi in 1962. A member of the Student Non-Violent Coordination Committee, Hamer also helped organize the Mississippi Freedom Democratic Party to challenge white domination of the Democratic Party. Voicing the Vision: The Fannie Lou Hamer Story starts at 6 p.m. Oct. 16 in the Student Unions Key West Room.

-- Students and faculty will learn what it feels like to be a voting minority at an interactive session, Tunnel of Voting Oppression. Student groups hosting the Oct. 18 event will simulate mock voting situations where participants will be targeted based on the color card theyre handed. Each cards color and symbol will represent a different diversity group. Theyll face a lot of bigotry and oppression but wont know why, David said, adding that the activity heightens awareness about voter inequalities. The event starts at 3 p.m. in the Student Unions Cape Florida Room.

-- The College of Education is sponsoring several sessions, including a discussion Oct. 16 on the challenges of migrant, immigrant and refugee children in local schools. The 10:30 a.m. event will be led by Gillian Eriksson in the Teaching Academy.

-- Introductory language classes in Spanish, Turkish, American Sign Language, Arabic and Chinese will be offered throughout the week at the UCF Library.
