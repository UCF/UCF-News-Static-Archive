More than 100 Turn Out for First World Cup Game on Giant Screen at UCF

By Zenaida Gonzalez Kotala June 9, 2006

Photo: Zenaida Gonzalez Kotala

UCF students, faculty and staff and members of the community pack the Barbara Ying Center on Friday to watch the first game of the World Cup.

University of Central Florida student Makram Elkhachen walked around with a jersey from the Brazilian soccer team draped around his neck, howling with joy when Costa Rica scored its first goal against Germany Friday.

The mechanical engineering student was one of more than 100 students, faculty, staff and community members who jammed into the Barbara Ying Center at UCF to watch the first World Cup game on a 150-inch LDC projection screen.

The International Services Center and the International Students Association teamed up to sponsor a month-long World Cup Community Festival during which the games will be shown at the center. It gives everyone a chance to watch live soccer and munch on free snacks.

It was a great turnout, said ISC Director Nataly Chandia of the first game. I think it is important to integrate the students into the community and these kinds of events help do that. Its also good for the community because there is a good exchange of culture and world views.

Many in the community agree.

I think its a great idea, said Diane Machado who lives a few blocks from the university. She, her husband and 5-year-old daughter watched the first half of the game; Machado wore her USA jersey and her husband wore one for Brazil. I think UCF is such a big part of Orlando that it should do more of these kinds of things. It benefits the university and us in the community.

The UCF students who watched the match-up showed their allegiance by wearing their favorite teams jerseys and pumping their hands in the air when someone scored. Some even painted their faces in team colors.

This is great, said Elkhachen, a native of Venezuela. He said hes happy to see America get interested in soccer.

You know what its going to take to make soccer be like everywhere else: for the U.S. team to win or at least make it into the finals, Elkhachen said. Thats when it will happen, when every American will get the energy surrounding soccer.

The World cup is like a month-long Super Bowl for soccer fans around the world.

Soccers popularity has boomed in the United States over the past few years. There are three professional leagues, and more than 3.2 million boys and girls ages 5 to 19 are playing in U.S. Youth Soccer.

Its a cultural thing, said Cornelius Mamboleo, president of the ISA and a die-hard soccer fan. Every male grew up watching it and playing it, so the games, they are special.

Mamboleo, a native of Kenya and a senior majoring in organizational communication at UCF, said his father will likely be cooking up barbecue with friends for the games back home. For his part, he plans to watch as many games as possible at the Ying Center.

The festival and free public viewing will continue weekdays from 9 a.m. to 5 p.m. through July 5 -- just before finals. Get there early if you want a seat. Light food and refreshments are provided by ISA, which is funded by the Student Government Association. Prizes are being awarded each day.
