Building Hover Craft and Virtual Reality Part of Girls' Science Day at UCF

By Zenaida Gonzalez Kotala March 2, 2007

Related Contacts and Links:

  • Zenaida Gonzalez Kotala

    • 407-823-6120

  • Expanding Your Horizons

  • Print this Page

About 250 Central Florida middle school students will have a chance this month to create miniature hover crafts, wear virtual reality helmets that will let them roam around a community and see how rehabilitative robotics make the lives of people with physical disabilities a little easier.

Sixth- to eighth-grade students will have a chance to enjoy those activities and many more at an Expanding Your Horizons event at the University of Central Florida on March 31. The College of Engineering and Computer Science, with the help of several private donors, is hosting the day aimed at getting girls interested in science and math careers.

Associate Dean for Academics in the College of Engineering Jamal Nayfeh said hes eager to get the girls to UCF.

They'll see engineers working on new wheelchair designs, engineers developing better artificial materials for bone grafts, and the latest research applications for virtual reality just to name a few, Nayfeh said of the day.

But theres a more important reason to get them here, he said. If they get interested, perhaps theyll enroll and help change the statistics facing U.S. engineering.

Hopefully after the conference, they'll look at their Ipods and cell phones and realize that engineers made those technologies possible. Currently, less than 20% of students in undergraduate engineering programs in the United State are female. We want them to see the many women are engineers and contribute to daily living, too."

Jackie Sullivan, one of the organizers couldnt agree more. Shes earned her bachelors in 1987 and a masters in environmental engineer in 1991.

If we dont get more people involved in math, science and technology  and that includes women  were going to lose our edge and turn into a service oriented country instead of one known for its science and innovation.

Research shows that in elementary school girls and boys are equally interested in science and math, but girls seem to get lost in the middle school years. Girls who attend college tend to perform as well as or better than their male counterparts.

To help encourage girls to give the sciences, math and technology a try, UCF has organized hands-on science experiments in the morning, followed by tours of 14 labs at UCF and the adjacent Central Florida Research Park that are involved in cutting-edge science and technology.

For example, students can visit the Institute for Simulation and Training, where many UCF students learn to blend computer science, digital media and storytelling to create interactive experiences. Or, they can visit the Center for Advanced Transportation Systems Simulation, where scientists are working on ways to make traffic planning and operation smoother and safer in a fun and high-tech fashion.

Sullivan, and Joanne Kiriazes, a civil engineer who is a co-organizer of the event, have also scheduled 34 working female engineers to speak to students throughout the day. The engineers include Paula Stenzler, engineering manager for the Technical Services Division at the Universal Orlando resort.

We want girls to come and find out what kind of career options they have if they take the hard math and science courses now, Kiriazes said. That way theyll have a choice when theyre in college to pursue anything they want. If they dont take those courses, they limit their choices and, as well show during Expanding Your Horizons, there is so much you can do with science and technology, not just the traditional building of bridges.

Parents must register their children by March 22 online at http://women.cecs.ucf.edu/EYH/about.htm. No walk-ins will be admitted because of the planning necessary to ensure a personal experience for each participant. Questions can be emailed to: eyh@mail.ucf.edu
