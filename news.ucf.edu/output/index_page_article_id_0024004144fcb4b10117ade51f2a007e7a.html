UCF Researchers Testing Whether Road Markings Can Limit Red-Light Running

By Chad Binette Jan. 28, 2008

University of Central Florida researchers are testing whether “Signal Ahead” markings painted at an Alafaya Trail intersection will help reduce dangerous red-light running.

In earlier simulations in a UCF lab, drivers were three times less likely to run red lights on roads with “Signal Ahead” painted on the surface to guide them.

The markings are painted on Alafaya Trail, just south of its intersection with Corporate Boulevard near the UCF main campus. Drivers who have passed the markings when the light turns yellow have enough time to go through the intersection before the light turns red. Drivers who have not yet reached the markings when the light turns yellow should prepare to stop at the traffic light.

Because the markings address unintentional red light running caused by drivers’ bad judgment, they would complement cameras and hefty fines that local governments are using to change the behavior of drivers who purposefully drive through red lights.

“We hope the markings on Alafaya Trail will enable drivers to make faster and better decisions about what to do at a yellow light, making the intersection safer for all drivers,” said Essam Radwan, an engineering professor who also is executive director of the Center for Advanced Transportation Systems Simulation.

Since adding the “Signal Ahead” labels, UCF researchers have been studying drivers’ behavior without any public education on the meaning of those markings.  Now they plan to continue collecting data after informing the public of the markings’ purpose through the media and flyers at UCF and Siemens, two of the main sources of traffic in the area.

The data for the entire study period, scheduled to end in April, will be compared with data from Alafaya Trail and Central Florida Boulevard, an intersection without any pavement markings that researchers are monitoring.

The Florida Department of Transportation, which is funding the study, is interested in using the results to help reduce the red-light running and make intersections with traffic lights safer.

Before adding the markings on the road in September, researchers at UCF’s Center for Advanced Transportation Systems Simulation tested them in a driving simulator housed at the College of Engineering and Computer Science. The goal of the center is to use computer simulation, virtual reality and other advanced technologies to improve traffic planning, design and operations.
