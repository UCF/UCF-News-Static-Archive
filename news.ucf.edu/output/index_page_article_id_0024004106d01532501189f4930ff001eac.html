UCF Professor Designs Exercise Mats Used to Prep Local Students for Math FCAT

By Zeniada Gonzalez Kotala March 11, 2008

Photo: Jerry Klien

Students at Bonneville Elementary School in East Orlando practiced their multiplication tables by dancing and grooving to music on special exercise mats designed to teach them math facts.

Full Photo

  • Debby Mitchell, College of Education

    • 407-823-6598

  • Print this Page

Bonneville Elementary School third graders jumped around on colorful exercise mats to learn how to multiply and prepare for the Florida Comprehensive Assessment Test underway this week.

Debby Mitchell, an associate professor in the University of Central Florida’s College of Education, designed the Meaningful Movement Mats to encourage kids to exercise and to learn math and other academic skills such as colors and the alphabet.

Multiplication is one of the skills tested on the FCAT. Students practiced on the mats, which look like calculators. To multiply 2 times 2, students jump on the 2, the x symbol and then the 2 again before moving to the answer.

During the past year, the youngsters in Cindy Johnson’s Bonneville class have been pulling out the colorful mats for 15 minutes of play before they take weekly math quizzes. Another group of third-graders has stuck to traditional study methods and taken the quizzes without using the mats.

Students who used the mats have scored 30 percent better on the quizzes than those who skipped the exercise session.

“We’ve suspected for a long time that there is a link between physical activity, level of fitness and academic success,” Mitchell said. “We are now starting to see research produce findings that support our hypothesis.”

Those findings are supported by the conclusions of best-selling author and Harvard psychiatrist John Ratey, who explains the new science of exercise and the brain in his most recent book, “Spark.” Ratey shows that exercise is food for the brain and that regular movement appears to stimulate brain cells. He shares case studies featuring students around the country who have become more fit and improved their standardized test scores.

Mitchell, who specializes in physical education, launched GeoFitness Inc. in 2002 out of concern for the growing number of obese children in the country. Obesity is linked to diabetes and heart disease, which kill thousands each year. Her idea was to make exercising fun so it would become a lifelong habit. She first developed the exercise mats to look like giant calculators. She also created dance routines set to popular music that children could follow.

Knowing that research indicates that exercise can also improve learning, she developed three new exercise mats last year. One version helps school-age children learn math and reading skills. Another helps 4 to 8 year olds learn their colors, shapes and sequences. The third mat teachers about nutrition, including food awareness, serving sizes and calories. The idea remains the same: Set the problem that needs to be solved to music, keep it fun and get the kids sweating to the beat.

Bonneville Elementary Principal Kimberly Bias said she likes what she has seen in Johnson’s classroom. Bonneville is an A school, and a lot of that has to do with its consistently good FCAT scores.

“I believe all students can learn,” Bias said. “It is a matter of finding a way

in which they best learn -- movement can indeed be one way to increase learning.”

She expects that the good performance on math quizzes will translate into good math scores among third-graders on the FCAT.

Third-grade teacher Johnson said the response has been phenomenal, and not just because the children are learning math.

“Parents are excited to see movement in the classroom,” Johnson said. “With all the

testing and emphases on problem solving, we often forget that we are here to develop a well-rounded student and human being.”
