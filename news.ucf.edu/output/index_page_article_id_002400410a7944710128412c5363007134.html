UCF Experts Available to Discuss Oil Spill

By UCF Staff May 5, 2010

Photo: U.S. Coast Guard

Fire boat response crews battle the blazing remnants of the offshore oil rig Deepwater Horizon in late April.

Several University of Central Florida professors are available to discuss the many impacts of the Gulf of Mexico oil spill, including its effects on tourism and the environment, Florida's economy and the political future of oil drilling.

For example, a UCF scientist recently received a patent for an innovative way to clean up spills by cleaning up oil spills on water surface using carbon materials with a sponge-like structure.

Reporters are welcome to contact professors directly. For assistance in reaching any of the professors, reporters can call the UCF News & Information office at 407-823-5300.

Environmental and Ocean Politics: Peter Jacques, associate professor of Political Science, 407-823-6773 office, pjacques@mail.ucf.edu. Jacques can discuss how the spill is a "game changer" in national and state debates about drilling and how governments traditionally react strongly in the short term after spills and then return to largely ignoring the risks of drilling. Jacques is the author of three books, including "Ocean Politics and Policy" (2003) and “Environmental Skepticism: Ecology Power and Public Life" (2009).

Tourism: Abraham Pizam, dean of the Rosen College of Hospitality Management, 407-903-8010 office, apizam@mail.ucf.edu. Pizam can discuss the impact on tourism already experienced and anticipated in Florida and other Gulf states.

Marine Biology and Conservation: Linda Walters, professor of Biology, 407-823-2148 office, pesacks@earthlink.net. Walters can talk about the oil spill's impact on marine biology and discuss potential measures to preserve the ecosystems and marine species, especially along the Gulf coast.

Research on Cleaning Up Oil Spills: Nazim Muradov, principal research scientist, Florida Solar Energy Center (Cocoa campus), 321-638-1448 office, muradov@fsec.ucf.edu. He was granted a patent in April for a new approach to cleaning up oil spills on water surfaces using carbon materials with a sponge-like structure. Because this carbon material attracts oil but repels water, it can break up oil films and soak the oil up like a sponge. The oil-laden carbon particles can then be scrubbed off and recovered on shore. Muradov has conducted small-scale tests in a laboratory; the next phase would be obtaining funding and collaborating with an industrial partner to conduct larger-scale tests to determine the commercial potential of the technology.

Coastal Waterways and Storm Surges: Scott Hagen, associate professor of Civil, Environmental and Construction Engineering, 407-823-3903, shagen@mail.ucf.edu. Hagen can discuss how oil near Florida's shorelines could impact the Intracoastal Waterway, other waterways and marshes. He also can discuss how storm surges could affect the flow of oil near the state's shores during hurricane season. Hagen is the director of the UCF Coastal Hydroscience Analysis, Modeling and Predictive Simulations Laboratory (CHAMPS Lab), which is online at http://champs.cecs.ucf.edu.

Economy: Sean Snaith, director, Institute for Economic Competitiveness, 407-823-1451 office, ssnaith@bus.ucf.edu. Snaith can discuss the potential impact to the Gulf States' economies from the loss of tourism, a permanent ban on offshore oil drilling, damage to fishing areas and more.
