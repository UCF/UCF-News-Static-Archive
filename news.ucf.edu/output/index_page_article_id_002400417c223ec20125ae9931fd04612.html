Engineering Students Prepare for Global Economy

By Julie Primrose March 18, 2010

Photo: Jacque Brund

In an increasingly global economy, universities around the nation are developing more academic programs with an international focus. This allows students to learn how their industries of choice operate on a global scale.

The University of Central Florida College of Engineering and Computer Science has responded to this demand by offering a new minor in International Engineering. The objective of the minor is to provide students with opportunities to learn about and experience first-hand the global aspects of engineering, science and technology.

The minor pairs mostly third-year students with a partner university in a foreign country. Students are given the opportunity to study engineering practices abroad and learn about the social, professional and technical cultures of their host country.

“Students who have this international experience will certainly have a leg up on the competition in today’s job market, but that’s just the immediate benefit,” said Professor Avelino Gonzalez, coordinator of the International Engineering minor. “The real benefit will be down the road when they’re working professionals. They’ll have experience dealing with other countries and how they do engineering.”

Five students went abroad in spring 2009, the first year that the minor was offered.  Another 10 students have already departed or will soon go this spring. So far, students have traveled to partner universities in Spain, Sweden, Brazil, Poland and the Czech Republic.

Seven UCF engineering students have attended the Universidad Federal de Rio De Janeiro in Brazil as part of a U.S. Department of Education-Brazilian Ministry of Education partnership  that financially supports students to study abroad. Students are currently being sought for that program, as well as for the Atlantis DeSIRE program, which supports students who study abroad in in France, Poland and the Czech Republic.

Jonathan Torres, a senior majoring in Mechanical Engineering, studied at Universidad de Cantabria in Spain for five months last spring. Torres says his international experience has already started opening doors professionally, and he expects that it will continue to do so throughout his career.

“Most major engineering companies have a large international component. Having this minor will reflect that I have experience with international dealings,” Torres said. “I hope this will lead to higher starting positions and increased travel opportunities throughout my career.”

While abroad, students may be able to enroll in an internship or independent study in addition to traditional courses taught at the partner university. These options offer students a chance to receive hands-on experience and to see the engineering practices of their host country first-hand. 

“Overall, this has been a great experience,” Torres said. “It was a lot of hard work, but it was also a lot of fun. I’ve made lifelong friendships with people throughout Europe. It has opened my eyes to different cultures and has made me realize that people really aren’t so different.”

Students who want to learn more about the International Engineering minor can attend information sessions at 9:30, 10:30 or 11:30 a.m. on Friday, March 26. All of the sessions will be in room 101 of the College of Sciences Building.
