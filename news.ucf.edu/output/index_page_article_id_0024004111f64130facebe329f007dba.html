Too Many Gifts?

By Chad Binette December 15, 2003

Photo: Jerry Klein

"Just how big a sleigh do you have?"

Little Johnnys parents may want to think twice about giving him every toy and game on his long Christmas list if they want him to care deeply about others when he grows up, says a researcher studying greed and altruism.

"I dont think its helpful for a child to get the sense that if they just ask for it, itll be there  that youll give them everything they want," said Professor Edward "Mike" Robinson of UCFs College of Education. "If you get everything you ask for, you tend to ask for more next time."

Robinson began working in the summer as UCFs Robert Heintzelman endowed chair for "Understanding the Prevention of Greed and the Development of Altruism." In the spring, he will begin studying how greed and altruism develops in children ages 3 to 14 and ways parents and schools can stave off greed. He plans to monitor the same children in Seminole and Orange county schools from year to year.

Allowing children to experience giving and not just receiving at Christmas can help them become more altruistic, Robinson said. Parents can convince an older child, probably 12 and older, that they no longer need all of their toys and can give one or more of them to a child who would appreciate them more.

Younger children can split their allowance over the holidays, keeping some while donating a part to a charity. Its helpful in those situations for parents to talk with their children about what the recipients of the money can do with it, Robinson said. Then children can better understand the value of their donations to others.

Robinson also encouraged parents to take time during the holidays to volunteer with their children. They could go to a homeless shelter or a church or help clean an elderly neighbors yard. He said its important for parents to be enthusiastic about volunteering, because "if the adults are excited about it, then the children will be excited about it."

Ideally, Robinson said, parents should strive to find a balance between giving their children some of the presents they want and also making sure theyre teaching them the value of giving of their time and money.

Heintzelman, the founder of Heintzelmans Ford dealership in Orlando, left UCF $600,000 in his will for the endowed chair. He was known for quietly helping the less fortunate in a variety of ways, including piloting indigent patients stranded in the Orlando area back to their home communities and donating food and medical supplies to the needy in Honduras and El Salvador. Heintzelman was 82 when he died in 1996.
