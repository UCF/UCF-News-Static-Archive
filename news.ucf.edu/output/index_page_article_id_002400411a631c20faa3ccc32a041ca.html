Communicative Disorders Clinic Expands

September 22, 2003

Photo: COHPA

The Communicative Disorders Clinic served over 300 people last year.

A UCF teaching clinic offering low-cost or free services to people with speech, language and hearing disorders is expanding its services following a $200,000 renovation. The community can see the changes at the UCF Communicative Disorders Clinic first hand from 6 to 7:30 p.m. Sept. 22, during an open house. The event will include remarks by state Rep. David Mealor and tours of the clinic at the Research Pavilion, 12424 Research Parkway, Suite 155, in the Central Florida Research Park adjacent to UCF. Last year, the Communicative Disorders Clinic served more than 300 people from five counties in Central Florida. They included many adults with impaired speech and language associated with stroke, traumatic brain injury or degenerative diseases such as Lou Gehrigs and Alzheimers, and a large number of school-age children with special needs in communication, language and literacy. In many cases, the patients were uninsured or underinsured or had exhausted insurance benefits. No client is ever turned away because of an inability to pay, clinic Director Charlotte Harvey said. The renovation of the clinics facilities, which was completed in late August, is allowing the clinic to expand its services to the public. For example, rewiring of all of the clinics observation and treatment rooms now enables family members to watch and hear sessions in privacy and clinicians to record sessions for further evaluation. Other improvements and the addition of new equipment are allowing the clinic to develop new programs in auditory processing, voice care and language and literacy: * Auditory processing disorder program. With the addition of a full-time audiologist and state-of-the-art equipment, the clinic now offers comprehensive services for people with impaired hearing. The services include standard hearing evaluations for adults and children, and specialized evaluations of central auditory processing. The new program also offers hearing aid evaluations and fittings as well as instructions on hearing aid use. The clinic is developing other services such as infant screening and a hearing conservation program. * Voice care program. The clinic has added a videostroboscope, a sophisticated tool used in the evaluation of speech disorders. The slender wand equipped with a small camera at the tip can be slipped into a persons throat, enabling a clinician to view the larynx and vocal folds. The program uses the tool to evaluate speech disorders associated with heart surgery, Parkinsons disease and other neurological disorders, cancer, vocal nodules and voice problems related to occupations such as singing and public speaking. The program also includes a new portable swallowing station used to evaluate swallowing disorders that may accompany stroke and degenerative diseases. * Language and literacy programs. The clinic is currently developing an intervention program for preschool and school-age children with special needs in language, including developmental delays and autism. Another program will work with children who have severe reading and writing disabilities. In addition to these new programs, the clinic continues to provide a variety of other services including: * Evaluation and treatment of disorders of articulation (the ability to make speech sounds) and fluency (the ability to speak fluidly). * Screenings of children at private day-care centers and preschools for early detection of communication disorders. * Individual and group therapy sessions for adults with impaired speech, language or severe reading disabilities. * Support in the use of augmentative communication devices by people with Lou Gehrigs disease, also known as a myotrophic lateral sclerosis, or ALS. * Accent reduction programs and assessments of speech and language skills of non-native English speaking people. * Memory training for people with dementia, at the clinic and in skilled nursing facilities. The clinic employs eight full-time and six part-time licensed and certified speech-language pathologists. They work as clinical educators, supervising about 30 undergraduates and 60 graduate students in communicative disorders programs at UCF. The clinic offers the only opportunity at a state-supported university in Florida for students to gain clinical experience in speech, language and hearing disorders at the undergraduate level. The clinical experience obtained by graduate students is a critical component of their education and prepares them for externships in private practices, hospitals, rehabilitative and skilled nursing facilities, and public schools throughout the greater Orlando area.
