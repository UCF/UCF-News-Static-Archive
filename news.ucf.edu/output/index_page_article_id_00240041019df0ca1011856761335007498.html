UCF Biologists Use Fire to Improve Wetlands Within Florida Ranchland

By UCF Staff Feb. 26, 2008

Photo: Patrick Bohlen

A team of biologists set prescribed burns in February as part of a three-year research study looking at best land management strategies for wetlands within ranch lands.

Full Photo

  • John Fauth, Biology

    • 407-823-1661

 Three University of Central Florida biologists are participating in one of the largest studies ever done on wetlands in the nation.

Wetlands are important for the preservation of native animal and plant species. Wetland plants also are a vital resource for hungry cattle, especially during the dry season.

Professors John Fauth, David Jenkins and Pedro Quintana-Ascencio are collaborating with scientists from the MacArthur AgroEcology Research Center in Lake Placid, Fla., to understand how prescribed burns and cattle grazing influence the flora and fauna of small wetlands embedded within pastures.

The center is a 10,500 acre cattle ranch that supports long-term ecological research, education and stewardship. The center’s herd includes over 3,000 Brahman-cross cows that produce more than 2,300 calves each year, making it among the top 20 commercial cow-calf producers in Florida. Patrick Bohlen of MacArthur AgroEcology Research Center is leading the three-year project.

“All of us would agree that cattle ranchers are a very important part of Florida’s economy,” Fauth said. “About 15 percent of South Florida ranchland is made up of wetlands. They have a great value for conservation. What we’re trying to do with this experiment is to maximize the value of wetlands for conservation and cattle production.”

The study aims to identify a combination of fire and grazing strategies that enhance nutrient retention, prevent encroachment of shrubs, maximize conservation of native species and replace unpalatable plants with species preferred by hungry cattle. The center’s wetlands are interconnected via surface and subsurface flows to Lake Okeechobee and the Everglades, so information about nutrients will inform efforts to restore and manage this unique ecological system.

Ranchers traditionally burn pastures in the winter to stimulate new grass and eliminate shrubs.  Wetlands often are more productive than the surrounding pastures, and they naturally produce grasses and other plants that cattle love to eat.

Cattle often trample and degrade wetlands as they try and reach lush, tasty grasses. But wetlands are important for the local ecology. So it’s in the best interest of both ranchers and biologists to try and figure out a solution that benefits cattle and the wetlands.

Initial results of the project, now in its second year, show that fencing cattle out of wetlands during a dry year increases the dominance of dog fennel, a weed that is susceptible to fires. While ungrazed wetlands provide more fuel for prescribed burns, early results also indicated that burn crews had trouble maintining heavily grazed wetlands.

“If we can come up with good strategies, the wetlands could thrive and help maintain the biodiversity of the region while helping ranchers,” Fauth said.

The project is among the largest ever conducted on whole wetlands, and it is funded by the U.S. Department of Agriculture’s Managed Ecosystems Program. Many students are helping with field work and data analysis, and four UCF Biology graduate students are developing dissertations based on this project. 

Prescribed burns began in late January and should conclude in early March, weather permitting. Early data should be available to researchers by August.

UCF researchers have determined that wetlands on the MacArthur AgroEcology Research Center support more than 188 species of plants and diverse communities of insects, fishes, amphibians and wading birds.

UCF biologists have worked at the center since 2006. Revenues from the agricultural operation support research activities and allow scientists to monitor ecological effects of agricultural practices under real-world conditions. The center’s program is part of a global effort to manage, preserve and restore the multiple ecological services provided by working agricultural lands.
