President Hitt Presents Case for Medical School

By Tom Evelyn Jan. 27, 2005

Photo: Tom Evelyn

UCF President John Hitt speaks to the Board of Governors Thursday in Gainesville.

University of Central Florida President John Hitt on Thursday presented a case to the state Board of Governors for creating a medical school at UCF, saying the university was well-positioned to support a school that could help address Floridas growing shortage of doctors.

An early draft of a study commissioned by UCF shows there is a need for at least two new medical schools in Florida, Hitt told the board during a meeting at the University of Florida in Gainesville. The study also indicates that Orlando is an ideal location for one of those schools.

We think the study shows its a good idea, so were going to move forward with a proposal, Hitt said, adding that UCF hoped to present a formal proposal to the board within the next few months.

A study by the Council for Education Policy Research and Improvement and a study by MGT of America commissioned by UCF show there is a critical shortage of physicians in Florida that will continue to worsen. Experts say the shortage limits access, increases costs and hurts the quality of health care in Florida.

Orlando, the largest metropolitan area in the country without a medical school, is home to two of the largest medical systems in the country -- Florida Hospital and Orlando Regional Healthcare. Area health care administrators have endorsed building a medical school in Orlando.

UCF has put in place a strong foundation for a medical school, Hitt said. Among Floridas 11 state universities, UCF is the second-largest producer of bachelors degrees and the fourth-largest producer of medical school preparatory degrees. UCF also had the third-highest number of research contracts and grants between 1994 and 2003, and the university is the third-highest producer of patents.

A $10 million donation last year created the Burnett College of Biomedical Sciences, which could be the base for a medical college at UCF.

The university already has many medically related research centers and programs, including the Center for Research and Education in Optics and Lasers, the Institute for Simulation and Training, the Nanoscience and Technology Center, the Advanced Materials Processing and Analysis Center and micro-electrical mechanical engineering.

Related areas of research at UCF include simulated human patients and virtual endoscopic surgery devices for training medical professionals; nanoscience and drug delivery; mechanized microsurgery; cancer and quality of life for breast cancer survivors; and cardiovascular, infectious and neurodegenerative diseases.

Florida International University told the board Thursday that it also hoped to build a medical school at its campus in Miami. The University of Miami and Nova Southeastern University, both private schools, already have medical colleges at their South Florida campuses. UF, the University of South Florida in Tampa and Florida State University in Tallahassee also have medical schools.

In other action Thursday, the Board of Governors turned down FSUs request for a chiropractic school.

On Wednesday, the State University Presidents Association met at UF and agreed that the presidents needed to work together with the Board of Governors in lobbying for adequate funding.

Major priorities for SUPA include full funding for matching gift funds, faculty and staff salaries, and enrollment growth. The presidents and Board of Governors requested about $78 million this year to pay for expected enrollment growth. Gov. Jeb Bush has proposed $20.1 million.

All of us together, collectively, will work on these issues, board Chairwoman Carolyn Roberts said.

SUPA Chairman John Cavanaugh, who is president of the University of West Florida, said the presidents were successful two years ago in getting more money from the Legislature after uniting behind a campaign to show the benefits and needs of the public universities.
