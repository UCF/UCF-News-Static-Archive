TV Host Discusses Climate Change, Expanding Role for Youth

By Magdalena Lasota Sept. 10, 2008

Summer Rayne Oakes

Full Photo

  • Global Perspectives Office

  • Print this Page

Summer Rayne Oakes discussed environmental issues and pivotal role of the youth climate change movement, as well as innovative sustainable developments, during her appearance at University of Central Florida on Tuesday, Sept. 9.

Her speech, “We Can Kick Climate Change, One Step at a Time: An Expanding Role for Youth” constituted a co-curricular component of the “UCF General Education Program Unifying Theme: The Environment and Global Climate Change.”  About 425 students, faculty, staff and community members attended her presentation.

Oakes is an entomologist, environmental scientist, climate change activist and “eco-model.”  She also is a host, environmental expert and the youngest board advisor for the Discovery Channel’s Planet Green eco-lifestyle network.  Her first book, “Style, Naturally: The Global Guide to Sustainable Fashion and Beauty,” will be published in December.

Oakes stressed the core objective of her mission -- to increase the rate of engagement among young people in the fight against the climate change.  She spoke about her active involvement in the youth climate change movement through Energy Action, green-collar jobs, sustainable development and ecosystem conservation.  In addition, she talked about her first-time experience of organizing and lobbying on Capitol Hill with the youth-based movement Energy Action on the issue of global warming. “Powershift” brought together 6,000 young people who testified to government about the climate change.

Oakes recounted her own confusion about what to study in college in order to achieve her goals and address the issues that she considered important.  Her advisor suggested that she “do it all” when she was undecided about her major, and that significantly influenced the path of her career.  She reassured the audience members that they did not have to give up their passions and interests to become successful environmental activists.

“We do not have to be confined to our titles,” Oakes said. “We have to think outside the box and flirt with new ideas” to make the most of our mission and issues we feel strongly about. 

“We have to find that fire,” she added.

Oakes pointed to unique attributes the youth can offer in tackling climate change.  For example, this generation’s use of online tools such as YouTube and social networking sites has allowed it to connect, organize and act on environmental causes with astonishing success.  This generation, Oakes emphasized, is like “little volcanoes erupting around the world.” 

In conclusion, Oakes firmly articulated the need for more leadership to deal with environmental issues. She urged audience members not to be afraid to create something on their own in order to best pursue their passions. 

The event was sponsored by the UCF Global Perspectives Office, UCF Office of Undergraduate Studies, Lawrence J. Chastang and the Chastang Foundation, UCF Focus the Nation, UCF LIFE, UCF Political Science Department, UCF International Services Center and the Global Connections Foundation.
