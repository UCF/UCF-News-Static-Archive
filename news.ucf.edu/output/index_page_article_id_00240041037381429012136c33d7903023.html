UCF Awarded $570,000 Grant to Help Persons with Disabilities

By Zenaida Gonzalez Kotala Sept. 15, 2009

UCF earns half-a-million dollar grant to help persons with disabilities.

With a new $570,000 federal grant, aspiring teachers at the University of Central Florida will create 500 new social and educational service-learning projects that benefit area children and adults with disabilities.

The three-year grant – a partnership with the nonprofit UCP –will involve more than 3,000 UCF College of Education students. It is expected to directly impact 15,000 people with disabilities and their families in Central Florida.

The grant will provide much-needed services to people with a variety of disabilities in the region, and it will help to better prepare classroom teachers to work with all students.

“This grant gives our students an opportunity to not only feel more comfortable around children with special needs, but also to plan for and work with them,” said Rebecca Hines, associate professor in the College of Education and the lead researcher on the grant. “This will help our students better understand their needs and think about the best ways to help them in their classrooms.” 

The unique nature of the grant is that the money isn’t paying for lectures or heavy classroom instruction. Instead, the UCF students will be in the community working directly with people with disabilities and their families.

“These grants will help meet important social needs and put hundreds of thousands of students on a lifelong path of service,” said Nicola Goren, acting CEO of the Corporation for National and Community Service, which awarded the grant. “We know that students who become involved in their communities through organized service-learning activities experience a range of benefits, from improved academic achievement to the development of an ethic of civic involvement.”

The agency awarded 36 grants totaling nearly $20 million to engage students across the nation in service-learning projects that promote community service while enhancing students’ academic and civic skills. UCF is one of two Florida institutions to receive a grant.

Kelly Grillo, a doctoral student on the Exceptional Student Education Ph.D. track at UCF, can vouch for the powerful impact of service-learning activities. She was involved in one of Hines’ classes that required students to work on service-learning projects that benefitted children with special needs. 

“So many teachers plan for the middle group of children, thus making their lesson average,” Grillo said. “The project made me plan for the most athletic child and think of unique ways to include community members who may love art, but not have very good motor control. This grant will leave a large volume of UCF students with a life-changing experience.”

The Teachers in Action grant also will have a great impact in the community, where services are always needed.

“The grant will allow UCP to have extra hands and resources to pursue additional programs for individuals with special needs, as well as provide support to enhance existing programs,” said Ilene Wilkins, CEO of UCP and a lifelong advocate for people with disabilities.

The nonprofit organization has been assisting the community for years, but like all nonprofits, the agency’s resources are especially stretched during this challenging economy.

“However, the needs for our children and their families have actually increased,” Wilkins said. “We have many ideas for needed programs, and now with this new grant will actually have the resources to implement them.”

The group is already hard at work. A Lunch Buddies program at the new UCP Bailes campus has been launched, and the partners will host a community Halloween Carnival at the Loch Haven Park Neighborhood Center next month, said Trae Stewart, an assistant professor of Education at UCF who is a co-investigator on the grant. For more information, visit www.teachersinaction.org .

“Ultimately, we want our teachers to have authentic opportunities to interact with persons with disabilities in their own communities so they will better understand differences,” Hines said. “Every teacher today will have such a diverse mixture of students in their classroom, and we want them to feel comfortable and motivated to serve them all.”
