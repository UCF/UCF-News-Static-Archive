A New Generations Abstract Eye Candy

By Susan Loden May 24, 2004

Photo: FHE

"Awakes" a Flying Horse edition by John O'Connor.

Two new-school, system-based abstractionists, New Yorker John OConnor and Ryan Burkhart, director of UCFs Flying Horse Editions fine arts research press, will reopen the revamped Anita S. Wooten Gallery in Orlando on Friday, July 2, with their two-month exhibit, Parallel Inclines.

Parallel Inclines runs through Aug. 28, with an opening reception July 2, from 6:30 to 9 p.m. in the Wooten Gallery on the east campus of Valencia Community College. Admission is free; the gallery is open 8:30 a.m. to 4:30 p.m. on weekdays.

On July 3, Flying Horse Editions will honor OConnor with a soiree at 7 p.m. at its studio/gallery in the Central Florida Research Park.

In the Wooten exhibition, OConnor will present three intensely detailed drawings as large as 6-feet wide, and two smaller works. Burkhart will show eight of his lyrical paintings and drawings. Also featured are two collaborative water-based screenprints, OConnors Awakes and Sleeps, printed by Burkhart, a master printmaker and owner of H20 Graphics. These are OConnors first prints, crafted when he was a Flying Horse artist-in-residence at the University of Central Florida in January.

OConnor and Burkhart, in their early 30s, are emerging to the fore of American abstraction in the 21st century, from the first generation of artists profoundly influenced by computers, video games and other communication technologies that dominated their childhood.

Both produce lighthearted, skillfully-colored, spirited works. They are represented by Pierogi Gallery, Brooklyn, N.Y., and Steve Williams Contemporary Art, Jacksonville, Fla., and are among the Flying Horse Editions artists to be added to Rutgers Universitys graphic arts archive. Through the Rothschild Foundation, the Museum of Modern Art in New York recently added a large OConnor drawing to its collection. Burkharts work is in international venues, including museums in Antwerp, Belgium, Northern Ireland and Seoul, Korea.

The pair abandoned the emotionalism of the Abstract Expressionist of the 1950s and the Op-Artists manipulation of patterns and visual parlay by devising individual, elaborate systems to create non-contrived abstract images that exist only on paper or canvas. OConnor plots and adds color to loosely overlapping or intersecting patterns derived from odd statistical data, such as fluctuations of his own temperature, his itches or his recorded sleep-talking.

Burkhart interprets and colorfully enhances elements and forms that arise out of materials. He usually strives to create an illusion of motion, as in his Wooten-exhibited Within the Ebb, which playfully captures the feeling of slowly drifting clouds through his painted and drawn containers or organisms that are a cross between things iconic, organic and graphic. They are separate from one another, moving in a steady flow together, he says.

Because his art is not tied to a narrative, Burkhart says it is more akin to poetry than to a novel. By controlling the elements within a piece, focus is more on the poetic elements than on making a piece of art that is about something tied to a specific story. It is open to interpretation, which I really like and people enjoy. The process of how the art is made is hidden in the final piece. It is a process and it takes a very astute viewer to find it. I think of my work as a challenge that often starts with a general question, such as, What would happen if I trace a line 50 times? Or, What will it look like if I paint the same line again and again?  

Burkhart is not directly influenced by other artists. Yet, he is very active in looking at other artists, personally and professionally, for Fying Horse Editions. He has expertly selected contemporary heavy-hitters including Thomas Nozskowski, David Humphrey, Steven Sorman, Tony Fitzpatrick and Tom Burckhardt to work with him, and master book binder Larry Cooper to create Flying Horse prints and artists books. John OConnor, I definitely respect, Burkhart says. Our work will have a nice resonance together in Parallel Inclines. 

I try to find ways to change information and make it visual by putting it through a process, OConnor says. If I get bored, I change up the developing image. Often, he is surprised by the outcome, as with C.W.S.T., a massive work in the exhibition. C.W.S.T. translates OConnors grafts documenting his walking-steps, weight and temperature changes and other physical elements into an abstract image that unintentionally resembles an airplane.

Ryan and I get at abstraction in different ways. Mine is obviously processed. With Ryans, you dont see the process. I like the idea of making something I have never seen before. Im not trying to make it look like something.

It was easy to work with Ryan, says OConnor. He made a lot of great suggestions on Awakes and Sleeps. Collaborating with him on the prints wasnt work. It was a lot of fun and I will return to Flying Horse to make more prints and perhaps an art book drawn from my sleep-talking.

Both have roots in figurative art. OConnor, whose bachelors degree in graphic design is from Westfield (Mass.) State College, got his masters in fine art at Pratt Institute in Brooklyn and worked as a fine arts artist and teacher. There he was introduced to systemic abstracts and learned about John Cage. He was profoundly influenced by Cages technique of drawing art from random acts, such as the rolling of dice.

Burkharts choice to pursue his bachelors degree in art from Ohio State University and to earn a masters in fine arts under print-master Lynwood Kreneck (another Flying Horse resident artist) at Texas Tech University, grew out of an early study of poetry and creative writing, which continues to feed his art. In 2001, he came to UCF as director of Flying Horse Editions and to teach art.

Parallel Inclines was curated by Anita S. Wooten Gallery Director Jackie Otto-Miller. For more information, call 407-582-2298 or go to http://valenciacc.edu/gallery. Information on Flying Horse and the OConnor prints is available at www.flying-horse-editions.org. For more information on the soiree, call 407-823-4178.
