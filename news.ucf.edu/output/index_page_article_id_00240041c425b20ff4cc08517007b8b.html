NASA Working to Ensure Safe Shuttle Launch, UCF Alumnus Tells Symposium

By Chad Binette Oct. 5, 2004

Photo: Kelly Petrik

Bill Parsons, manager of NASA's Space Shuttle Program, speaks at a space symposium hosted by the Lou Frey Institute of Politics and Government as Roger Handberg, chairman of the UCF Political Science Department, looks on.

NASA is making significant progress in ensuring that the launch of the next space shuttle will be safe, although the anticipated timing of the launch has been pushed back two months due to the recent hurricanes, Space Shuttle Program Manager Bill Parsons said Tuesday.

Speaking at a space symposium hosted by UCF's Lou Frey Institute of Politics and Government, Parsons outlined several steps NASA is taking to make future flights safer. Those include adding cameras to track how debris affects the shuttle, strengthening parts of the shuttle and coming up with a last-resort rescue plan for the crew if damage would prevent the shuttle from landing safely.

Weve made a lot of progress, said Parsons, a UCF alumnus who has managed the shuttle program since May 2003, three months after the Space Shuttle Columbia accident.

Other top NASA officials, four congressmen and astronaut Frank Caldeiro, also a UCF alumnus, joined Parsons on Monday and Tuesday at the symposium entitled The Space Program: Past and Future, Public or Private?" Other speakers included author Andrew Chaikin and John Pike, an often-quoted critic of U.S. space policies. Symposium events were open to the public, and most of them were held at the Student Union.

U.S. Reps. Tom Feeney, Ric Keller, John Mica and Dave Weldon, all Florida Republicans, gave presentations on Monday. Reporters who cover the space program and engineers who provide the ground support needed for launches also participated.

William F. Bill Reedy, NASA's associate administrator for space operations on Monday compared the accomplishments of the private SpaceShipOne craft to the pioneering effort of Charles Lindbergh, the first pilot to fly solo and nonstop across the Atlantic Ocean.

Readdy recalled that while Lindbergh's feat was impressive, the flight took 33 hours and he and his plane had to return home on a ship. It took a long time for aviation to reach the point where jets could fly hundreds of passengers across the Atlantic and back safely.

Readdy and Chaikin, the author of A Man on the Moon: The Triumphant Story of the Apollo Space Program, cautioned that residents shouldn't expect private space travel to quickly become part of everyday life.

"From an economic standpoint, commercial space travel is going to have a tough time," Readdy said, noting the struggles of conventional airlines.

However, Readdy said private spacecraft eventually could help supply the International Space Station, leaving space shuttles to focus on more technical tasks. He also said the pioneering spirit generated by SpaceShipOne will lead to renewed interest in space in schoolchildren and in research.

During a panel discussion with Parsons and Pike, Kennedy stressed that while space ventures in the 1960s was a race with the Soviet Union, the current vision of the space program involves a long-term journey of exploration for humanity. He said universities will be a centerpiece in NASAs plan to achieve that goal.

Pike, the director of Globalsecurity.org, said hes concerned the space program could become a historical sideshow and not survive an era where there isnt a primary political purpose for space flight like there was during the Cold War. He said he doesnt think the United States can achieve Bushs vision of establishing a permanent presence on the moon by 2020 while staying within the current constraints of NASAs budget.

Former astronaut Sally Ride, the first American woman in space, was scheduled to speak Monday night but did not attend because of an illness.

The Lou Frey Institute of Politics and Government, a nonpartisan group that supports civics education and political research, has hosted four conferences since it was founded in 2001. Frey worked to increase NASAs funding and bring much of the shuttle program to Florida while he served in Congress from 1969 to 1979.

Frey said he's disappointed that there's less of an effort to care for the space program today. The space program represents about 0.6 percent of the federal budget, compared with about 5.4 percent during the 1960s.

However, Frey said, "I think the best is yet to come. Never, ever, ever quit (exploring)."

UCF works with NASA on a variety of research projects and is the host of the Florida Space Institute. UCF also is the lead university participating in the Spaceport Research and Technology Institute at the Kennedy Space Center.

The symposium was funded largely by a grant from the U.S. Department of Educations Fund for the Improvement of Post-Secondary Education.
