UCF Students Compete for a Shot at Starting Their Own Business

By Zenaida Gonzalez Kotala April 9, 2007

Related Contacts and Links:

  • Cameron Ford, The Joust

    • 407-823-3700

  • Center for Entrepreneurship & Innovation

  • Print this Page

UCF doctoral student Angel Alvarez envisioned a new company last year that develops compounds to help treat neurodegenerative diseases by boosting the supply of healthy new neurons from the patients own stem cells.

Alvarez developed the business plan for the company as part of a University of Central Florida competition known as The Joust. Alvarez and his team of fellow students won the competition. Since then, the company opened a lab in the Central Florida Research Park next to UCF. A compound developed in the lab has moved into the preclinical testing stage with its first drug candidate, NBI-18.

This week a new batch of UCF students will compete in the Joust. Semi-finalists will face a panel of judges drawn from the business community from 2 to 4:15 p.m. on Thursday, April 12, at the Fairwinds Alumni Center. Finalists will pitch their ideas to a new panel of judges on Friday, April 13, and the winners will be announced later that day.

The proposals are judged on several factors, including the quality of the service or product, financial feasibility and presentation skills. A total of $10,000 is up for grabs, and the grand-prize winner gets a spot in the Technology Incubator in Research Park.

Alvarez works in Professor Kiminobu Sugayas lab at UCFs Burnett College of Biomedical Sciences. He figured that developing new compounds to treat diseases such as Parkinson and Alzheimers, which are the focus of Sugayas research, would be a great business opportunity. He enrolled in the graduate business-planning course offered by the College of Business Administration and worked with a team of MBA and engineering students to create a plan the company -- NeoCytex Biopharma Inc.

Today, the company has a CEO -- Nilabh Chaudhary  and the drug candidate NBI-18 is showing great promise in animal trials. Other products in development appear to accelerate healing from wounds, fractures and muscle injuries, according to the companys Web site and Alvarez.

The Jousts organizer, Professor Cameron Ford, said that is exactly the path he hopes competition winners will follow.

NeoCytex is an example of what were trying to do, bridging the academic and business world to better the local economy and, in this case, increase health options for patients in the future, Ford said. By creating novel business proposals in the classroom, infusing them with wisdom from experienced entrepreneurs working in UCFs Venture Lab and Small Business Development Center and nurturing the best proposals in the UCF Technology Incubator, we can use research to benefit society and the economy.

Ford, the director of the Center for Entrepreneurship & Innovation at UCF, sees the Joust as a way to create a culture of entrepreneurship in the UCF community and partnerships with regional business and government leaders interested in improving the Central Florida economy.

Sugaya and Chaudhary, friends since Suagayas days at the University of Illinois at Chicago, co-founded the company. Alvarez works with Sugaya as he pursues his doctorate.

Setting up NeoCytex has been very difficult -- a formidable challenge, Alvarez said. I think the Joust has been a wonderful help because it brings to the table business leaders and potential investors.

Chaudhary, whos been involved in other start-up companies, agrees.

Development of innovative new products takes a lot of energy, commitment and marketing to investors, Chaudhary said. The Joust participants get a 360-degree view of the process.

And the follow-up help from the Technology Incubator is welcomed, he said.

Gordon Hogan and Carol Ann Dykes (at the incubator) have been quite helpful in helping us navigate through the startup phase, including technology licensing and approaches for generating state and federal resources, he said.
