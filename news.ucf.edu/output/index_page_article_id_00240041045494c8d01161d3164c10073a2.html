UCF Economist Predicts Depreciating Dollar Makes U.S. 'Big Lots! for Brits'

By Christine Dellert Nov. 29, 2007

Photo: UCF News file

Sean Snaith

Full Photo

Related Contacts and Links:

  • Sean Snaith, UCF Institute for Economic Competitiveness

    • 407-823-1451

  • UCF Institute for Economic Competitiveness

  • Print this Page

The falling U.S. dollar makes America look more like a 'Big Lots! for Brits' this holiday season than the currency powerhouse of yesteryears, a University of Central Florida economist says.

While everyday American Joes will continue pinching pennies next month, the dollar's slide means European tourists will buy -- and spend -- more as they're visiting in larger droves this year than last, said Sean Snaith, director of the UCF Institute for Economic Competitiveness.

That's the top finding in Snaith's fourth-quarter national forecast released today. The forecast covers economic projections through 2010. His release coincides with this morning's preliminary Gross Domestic Product figures from the Bureau of Economic Analysis in the U.S. Department of Commerce.

Snaith's forecast is good news for destination hot-spots, such as Orlando, Las Vegas and New York, just entering their peak seasons.

"Tourism should benefit as the U.S. dollar continues to depreciate throughout the remainder of the year and into the first half of 2008," Snaith said. "Foreign capital flows helped keep interest rates low during the housing boom, and now foreign purchases of American goods and services are helping to boost our economy, offsetting the downward pull of the housing correction."

Although the weakened dollar also has strengthened exports, it still won't produce the number of overseas travelers seen before Sept. 11, 2001, another UCF expert predicts.

"Today we have fewer tourists coming from overseas than we had before 9/11, despite the fact that the U.S. dollar is lower compared to most currencies (there), which shows you that it’s not the economics that are working here," says Abe Pizam, dean of UCF’s Rosen College of Hospitality Management. Pizam cites stricter security methods and unpopular foreign policy decisions as the main reasons for that trend.

Additionally, Pizam says, the dollar's free fall coincides with fewer Americans traveling overseas -- and paying "exorbitant" prices if they get there.

The dollar's seven-year slide should end in 2009, when the "greenback will eke out a modest appreciation," Snaith says in his report.

To view Snaith’s entire economic forecast, visit http://www.iec.ucf.edu.

Other highlights from his report include:

-- American consumers' spending this holiday season should be on par with last year. Retailers' aggressive pricing and other offers will be moderately successful. But no records -- neither high nor low -- will be set this holiday season.

-- More housing pains remain. "If we can get through the first six months of the year (2008), the fever will break, and the economy will be on its way to recovering from its bout of housing flu," Snaith says. But he adds that the "real danger" could occur in the first half of the year, when a large wave of sub-prime loans will be re-priced at high rates.

-- Snaith predicts that existing home sales will begin to recover in mid-2008, barring any additional shocks to the economy.

-- Unemployment will peak at 5 percent in 2008 before falling back to 4.5 percent in 2010.

-- "Say it ain't so Ben. Say it ain't so." Snaith asks if the Bernanke Fed has abandoned inflation targeting as a strategy for monetary policy.

Snaith is the director of the UCF Institute for Economic Competitiveness. He is a national expert in economics, forecasting, market sizing and economic analysis. He authors quarterly reports about the economy. He is a member of several national forecasting panels, including the Western Blue Chip Economic Forecast panel, the National Association of Business Economics Quarterly Outlook Survey Panel, the Federal Reserve Bank of Philadelphia’s Survey of Professional Forecasters, Bloomberg U.S. Economic Indicator Survey and USA Today Economic Survey Panel. Blue Chip recently named him the most accurate forecaster for California in 2006.

The UCF Institute for Economic Competitiveness’ mission is to expand public understanding of the economy by convening business leaders, scholars, policy makers, civic groups and media to discuss critical issues.
