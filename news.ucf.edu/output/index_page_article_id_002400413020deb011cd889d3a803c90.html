High Schoolers Learn About Florida's Government at Civic Leadership Academy

By Courtney Gilmartin Aug. 20, 2008

The two-week program organized by the Lou Frey Institute of Politics and Government included guest speakers, role-playing simulations, writing exercises and field trips.

A group of high school students learned how local and state governments work during the University of Central Florida’s annual Civic Leadership Academy this summer.

Hosted by the Lou Frey Institute of Politics and Government at UCF, the program’s courses provided an introduction to the workings of state and local governments, with a special emphasis on Florida. This year’s two-week program included presentations by guest speakers, role-playing simulations, writing exercises and field trips.

“The Civic Leadership Academy really grew out of the institute’s core mission, to strengthen civic education for young people across Florida,” said Doug Dobson, director of the institute.  “Basic political attitudes are formed at an early age, so it’s critically important for us to reach out to students to help them understand the world of politics and civics that is going on around them.”

Incoming junior and senior high school students were carefully selected from a pool of applicants based on their leadership, community service and intellectual ability.  They participated in activities from 9 a.m. to 9 p.m. and lived in residence halls on campus during their two weeks here.

A favorite activity for several students was called “Civic Leadership Through a Religious Lens.” Representatives from Christian, Jewish and Islamic faith centers educated the students in what Nate Schultz, a senior from Clearwater, called “the best thing to happen here.”

Schultz, who hopes to become a doctor, said that the leadership academy “taught me how the American government works and how to be an active citizen.”

Orlando Congressman Ric Keller visited the students to talk about the ups and downs of his job.  During a question-and-answer session with the students, topics such as the war on terror, taxes and drilling for oil were discussed.

“I’m so excited to see young people involved in the governmental process and politics,” Keller said.

Other highlights included a two-day trip to Tallahassee, a tour of the Orange County Supervisor of Elections office and a trip to a homeless shelter.

Each activity was aimed at helping students develop a final project that was presented to current and former elected officials, university administrators and educators.

The goals of the academy, which was first held last summer, are to prepare future leaders for ethical and responsible citizenship and public service, connect youths from different backgrounds with each other and public leaders, and energize our democratic system by promoting civic engagement in youth leaders.  To learn more about the Lou Frey Institute and the Civic Leadership Academy, visit http://www.loufrey.org.
