President Hitt Joins Colleagues in Leading Climate Commitment Effort

By Chad Binette Feb. 19, 2007

Photo: Jacque Brund

Pete Newman and James Davis of Facilities Planning and David Norvell of the Physical Plant's Center for Energy and Sustainability look over early sketches of the planned Physical Sciences building, which will be built according to the U.S. Green Building Council’s Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design (LEED) silver standard.

University of Central Florida President John Hitt has accepted an invitation to join a leadership circle of university presidents committed to reducing campus emissions of greenhouse gases.

The invitation from the 12 founding members of the American College & University Presidents Climate Commitment leadership circle follows an emphasis by Hitt and UCF during the past year on several energy conservation and sustainability initiatives. UCF has adopted the U.S. Green Building Councils Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design (LEED) standards for buildings that are new or undergo major renovations and started using biodiesel fuel for all diesel-powered university vehicles.

I share my colleagues concerns about the many harmful effects of global warming and look forward to working with them to find innovative energy solutions for universities nationwide, Hitt said. Preserving the natural beauty of our nation and conserving our precious energy resources must be priorities for us all.

The founding members of the climate commitment effort, a group that includes University of Florida President Bernie Machen, are striving to have signatures on letters of support from more than 1,000 university presidents by the end of 2009.

The presidents are working with the Association for the Advancement of Sustainability in Higher Education, ecoAmerica and Second Nature. Forty-six presidents, including Hitt, have joined the leadership circle, and another 27 are charter signatories for the climate commitment effort, according to the associations Web site.

The climate commitment pledge asks universities to develop an institutional plan within two years outlining how they can neutralize their effects on the climate. In the meantime, universities are asked to initiate at least two of five actions that will help to reduce their emissions of greenhouse gases.

UCF already practices three of those initiatives and has begun working toward a fourth.

The first standard calls for universities to establish a policy requiring all new campus construction to be built to at least the silver standard of the nationally recognized LEED standards. UCF administrators adopted the silver standard last year. Developed by the U.S. Green Building Council, the standards are designed to conserve water and energy and improve indoor environmental quality. UCF will first apply the standards to a new Physical Sciences building on the East Orlando campus. Construction is scheduled to begin in the fall. Future expansions of the Student Union and Recreation and Wellness Center also will meet the LEED silver standard.

In addition, UCF is recommissioning heating, ventilation and air-conditioning systems in four to five buildings a year to ensure that they use energy as efficiently as possible.

The second standard calls for an energy-efficient purchasing policy that requires the purchase of Energy Star-certified products whenever they are available. UCF already purchases such appliances, and a provision requiring that is part of a proposed comprehensive energy and sustainability policy for the university.

A third standard involves encouraging the use of and providing access to public transportation for faculty, staff, students and visitors. More than 6,000 students a day ride UCF shuttles that run to and from apartment complexes within a mile of campus, and UCF is exploring the use of shuttles to help fans get to football games when a new stadium opens on campus this fall.

UCF is starting to make progress toward a fourth standard, which calls for universities to purchase or produce at least 15 percent of their electricity consumption from renewable sources. UCF plans to install solar panels at the new residence halls near the Convocation Center to provide hot water for students. Physical Plant and the Florida Solar Energy Center are also seeking other partnerships and grants for solar photovoltaic cells.

The Center for Energy and Sustainability within the Physical Plant is leading the energy conservation efforts on campus. The unit also is developing a carbon footprint report for the entire campus that will serve as an inventory of campus emissions and baseline for the future. The inventory will help guide future efforts to areas in which reductions would have the most impact.

UCF received its first shipment of biodiesel fuel in January and began using it in 12 university vehicles. As the university replaces other vehicles, it plans to purchase new diesel models that can use biodiesel fuel. The university also is talking with the operators of its shuttle system about the possibility of using biodiesel fuel in the student shuttles.

To encourage students to conserve energy, UCF is holding a competition between residence halls this month. The residence hall that uses the least energy compared to February 2006 usage will win prizes. UCF also has installed about 200 thermostats that automatically reduce air conditioning or heat levels when a room is unoccupied.

For more information on the American College & University Presidents Climate Commitment initiative, go to www.presidentsclimatecommitment.org.
