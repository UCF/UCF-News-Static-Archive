Former Secretary of Defense Cohen Discusses 9/11 Attacks, War on Iraq

By Chad Binette March 24, 2004

Photo: Jerry Klein

Cohen drew media coverage.

One day after testifying before a federal commission examining the Sept. 11 terrorist attacks, former U.S. Secretary of Defense William Cohen told a UCF audience Wednesday that its critical that the United States persuade other nations that terrorism is a serious problem beyond America.

There is no place thats safe, he told a crowd of about 500 at the UCF Student Union. Theres no hiding place anymore. No country can say  I dont have a problem and therefore my citizens are safe. 

During a forum coordinated by the UCF Office of the Special Assistant to the President for Global Perspectives, Cohen spoke about the war on terror, the difficulties with balancing privacy and security and his belief that the United States needs to be more involved in the Middle East peace process.

Cohen, who represented Maine in the U.S. Senate for 18 years before he became President Clintons secretary of defense in 1997, addressed some of the same concerns as he did Tuesday while testifying before the National Commission on Terrorist Attacks.

He said he believes Americans remain too complacent about terrorism and that complacency was one of the major reasons why Sept. 11 happened in the first place.

The simple answer is that we were too complacent, Cohen said Wednesday. We became overconfident and thought no one would dare to attack us on our own soil.

The United States also needs to persuade other nations that terrorism is not simply our problem if it wants to transform the Middle East into a more democratic region, Cohen said.

Whether or not Americans agree or disagree with the objective of the war, they should not support pulling out troops now, because that would lead to chaos and massive bloodshed, Cohen said.

Spain, where terrorists killed more than 200 by bombing trains this month, has said it will pull its troops out of Iraq by June 30. Reactions like that show terrorists that they can make a nation fold just by inflicting casualties there, he said.

Cohen said the only argument that he supported for going to war with Iraq was to prevent Saddam Hussein from using weapons of mass destruction. He said theres no doubt that Hussein had such weapons at one point. The question, he said, is whether it was reasonable to assume that he still had them.

Cohen acknowledged how difficult it can be for inspectors to find weapons in such a large country. He compared their task to the likelihood of the audience of about 500 finding caches of weapons in California if they were hidden there.

In addition to the Global Perspectives Office, other sponsors of Cohens speech were the UCF Student Government Association, the Orlando Sentinel and Global Connections for Central Florida.

Cohen met with about 20 students participating in the Junior Achievement International Job-Shadowing Event before he spoke to the larger audience. The students, juniors and seniors in International Baccalaureate programs at local high schools, spent the day at UCF to learn more about careers related to international affairs.

The students also met with Carmenza Gonzalez, vice president of international business development with the Metro Orlando Economic Development Commission, and Ulric Haynes, a former U.S. ambassador to Algeria and a former consultant and executive dean for international relations at Hofstra University.
