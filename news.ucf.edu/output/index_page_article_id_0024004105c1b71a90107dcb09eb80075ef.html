NASA Hydrogen Research Grant Pushes Funding Past $30 Million

By UCF Staff Dec. 16, 2005

Photo: UCF File

UCF’s Florida Solar Energy Center is leading a hydrogen research project in which Florida universities are developing new ways to use the valuable fuel source.

NASA has approved a $7.4 million grant to fund another year of a hydrogen research project in which Florida universities are developing new ways to use the valuable fuel source, pushing the total funding for the four-year project to more than $30.3 million.

Researchers at the seven state universities have conducted more than 70 research projects on hydrogen production, safety, storage, cryogenics, fuel cells and other related areas that have positioned Florida as a major player in future NASA missions to space and in helping achieve energy independence for the nation.

NASAs decision to continue funding for this project is exciting news for all of us who drive vehicles here on earth as well as the people flying into space, said M.J. Soileau, vice president for research and commercialization at the University of Central Florida.

The multi-year NASA hydrogen research program has created a powerful partnership that brings together Florida university researchers with NASA Glenn, the nations premier space research facility and the sponsor of the program, and with NASA Kennedy, the nations premier space launch facility.

The program supports ongoing work at UCFs Florida Solar Energy Center, which leads the programs effort, and at UCF and the University of Florida, which co-manage the program. The other participating institutions are Florida International University, Florida State University, Florida A&M University, the University of South Florida and the University of West Florida.

FSEC research so far has developed a smart paint that reveals hydrogen leaks in tanks and other locations by changing color; provided laboratory success in extracting hydrogen from landfill gas as well as producing hydrogen from used lube oil; employed cryogenics to densify hydrogen so that smaller amounts of fuel can be used on the space shuttle, which will allow larger payloads; and implemented the use of metal hydrides for storage and purification, a research success that will have direct implications in future vehicles on earth.

Appropriations Chairman Bill Young has led efforts in Congress to support the program since its inception in 2001. Congressmen Dave Weldon and Tom Feeney have also helped to maintain funding for the project.

Floridas leadership in hydrogen technology stems from over 25 years of practical experience at Kennedy Space Center combined with this multi-year university research effort, Feeney said.

It is important that Florida continues to lead in hydrogen research, Weldon said. With todays rising energy costs, its time for us to develop viable alternative forms of energy to meet our nations needs and Florida definitely needs to be a leader in this endeavor.

A new energy economy, with hydrogen produced from renewable resources, promises ultimate relief from increased energy demands, air pollution and rising energy costs. Hydrogen, one of the most abundant elements in nature, can be produced from many feedstocks anywhere in the world. Our energy needs, from transportation to electric power generation, can be satisfied while being less polluting.

The hydrogen research also supports the space industry in Florida, home to the Space Shuttles at the NASA Kennedy Space Center and commercial and military spacecraft at Cape Canaveral Air Force Station.

These programs will lead the U.S. into the future of next-generation space vehicles, FSEC Director Jim Fenton said. The fuel for these space vehicles is hydrogen, and for Floridas energy independence, the future fuel for our state must be hydrogen made from renewable energy for both transportation and electric generation. Its very gratifying to see that the hydrogen economy weve been working toward is a lot closer today.

Visit www.fsec.ucf.edu for more information on UCFs Florida Solar Energy Center.
