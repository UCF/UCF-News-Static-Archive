Ex-Mafia Crime Boss to Speak to UCF Athletes, Public about Gambling Addiction

By Chad Binette March 21, 2006

Michael Franzese, a former New York Mafia crime boss who speaks to professional and college athletes around the country, will discuss problem and compulsive gambling Wednesday, March 22, at the University of Central Florida.

Franzese will meet with UCF student-athletes at 7 p.m. at the Fairwinds Alumni Center on the East Orlando campus. Drawing on his own experiences in organized crime, he will discuss how gambling can quickly become addictive and the consequences that it can bring. The session is mandatory for UCF student-athletes.

Franzese will discuss similar topics during a free public presentation at 9 p.m. Wednesday in the first-floor ballroom of the Fairwinds Alumni Center, which is across from the UCF Arena.

Franzeses visit to UCF comes in the midst of a Florida trip that includes visits with professional baseball players at spring training. He also has spoken at several major universities, including Michigan, Texas and Penn State.

Franzese was one of the youngest Mob bosses, and he coordinated operations in gambling, loan sharking and corporate crime before he was indicted in the 1980s and sentenced to 10 years in prison.  He then vowed to leave the Mob and, at the request of the Federal Bureau of Investigation, began educating athletes about the dangers of gambling.

The UCF Task Force on the Prevention of Gambling is sponsoring the Michael Franzese event with support from the Florida Council on Compulsive Gambling and UCF Athletics.

The task force was created last summer by Student Development and Enrollment Services to educate the campus community about student gambling and the potential for some students to experience negative psychological, financial and academic consequences as a result of compulsive gambling. The task force also will explore gamblings links to other addictive behaviors, such as the abuse of alcohol and other drugs and the excessive use of the Internet. The panel will recommend appropriate gambling-related messages, policies and student activities to the UCF administration.

Task Force members include representatives from UCF Athletics, the Counseling Center, Student Government Association and other campus offices. Other members are from the Florida Council on Compulsive Gambling and the Orange County Coalition for a Drug Free Community.  

The task force sponsored a variety of educational efforts during National Problem Gambling Awareness Week, which was March 6 to 10. Pat Fowler, executive director of the Florida Council on Compulsive Gambling, will train the UCF Counseling Center staff on April 19. The training will help the counselors and other staff recognize and intervene with students with problems or concerns related to compulsive gambling.

The task force is a great vehicle to increase the visibility of issues connected to gambling and to provide our students with the information they need to make responsible choices and to recognize the signs of a problem. said Laura Riddle, chairwoman of the task force and associate director of Alcohol and Other Drug Counseling Services at UCF. The availability and accessibility of legalized gambling opportunities has increased tremendously in recent years.

According to the Florida Council on Compulsive Gambling, most people can gamble responsibly, but others encounter financial, emotional, physical or criminal consequences once they get involved. Signs of problem gambling include losing money they cannot afford to lose, borrowing money to pay off gambling debts and developing conflicts with friends, family or school related to their gambling.

Betting on sports can lead to other severe consequences for student-athletes, who can be removed from their teams or expelled from school for violating NCAA rules on gambling.
