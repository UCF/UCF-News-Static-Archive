Former Israeli Prime Minister Ehud Barak to Speak Thursday at UCF

By Chad Binette Oct. 25, 2006

Related Contacts and Links:

  • John Bersia, Global Perspectives

    • 407-823-0687

  • Chad Binette, News & Information

    • 407-823-6312

  • Global Perspectives Office

  • Print this Page

Former Israeli Prime Minister Ehud Barak will address the challenges of terrorism, international diplomacy and globalization during a speech at 3 p.m. Thursday, Oct. 26, at the University of Central Floridas main campus.

Baraks presentation in the Pegasus Ballroom of the Student Union is free and open to the public. The speaker has stipulated that no photographs or video footage may be taken at the event.

Barak served as Israels prime minister from 1999 to 2001 after a decorated, 36-year career in the Israeli Defense Forces. He lists his administrations main accomplishments as striving for peace in the Middle East, helping to lead his country out of a prolonged recession, reducing inflation and achieving record foreign investments in Israel.

Barak also ordered the withdrawal of the Israeli Defense Forces from South Lebanon in May 2000, ending nearly two decades of Israeli occupation of that area. In the late 1990s, he unsuccessfully tried to negotiate peace agreements with Syria and Palestinian authorities during sessions that U.S. President Bill Clinton hosted at Camp David.

As chief of the general staff of the Israeli Defense Forces, Barak was involved in the negotiation and implementation of a 1994 peace treaty with Jordan. During his military career, Barak helped to coordinate a 1976 rescue effort for passengers on an Air France aircraft that was hijacked by terrorists and forced to land in Uganda.

Barak also is a former minister of the interior, foreign affairs and defense for Israel.

Sponsors of his presentation are the UCF Global Perspectives Office, UCF Student Government Association, Jewish Federation of Greater Orlando, UCF Middle Eastern Studies Program, UCF Political Science Department, UCF History Department, Global Connections Foundation and the Orlando Sentinel.
