UCF Students' Self-Navigating Car to Compete in DARPA Urban Challenge

By Suhtling Wong Oct. 10, 2007

Photo: Jacque Brund

UCF engineering alumnus Ben Patz, CEO of Coleman Technologies Inc. and UCF students Remo Pillat and Gary Stein examine the Team UCF car that will be competing in the DARPA Urban Challenge.

A self-navigating Subaru Outback that UCF engineers designed to adapt to traffic patterns, obstacles and driving conditions will compete in the national semifinals of the Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency's (DARPA) Urban Challenge.

Thirty-six teams from across the country will compete Oct. 26 to 31 at the former George Air Force Base in Victorville, Calif. The robotic vehicles must operate autonomously, without human intervention, and obey California traffic laws while performing maneuvers such as merging into moving traffic, navigating traffic circles and avoiding moving obstacles. The vehicles will have six hours to traverse a complex road system that will simulate a military supply mission.

UCF's car, named Knight Rider VIP, will compete alongside vehicles from schools such as the Massachusetts Institute of Technology, Princeton and Cornell. DARPA selected UCF as a semifinalist after it passed a site visit in June, when the vehicle was tested for navigation, tight turns and obstacle avoidance. The top 20 teams will advance to the finals on Nov. 3 with top prizes of $2 million, $1 million and $500,000.

The DARPA Urban Challenge is the third in a series of annual competitions held to foster the development of autonomous robotic ground vehicle technology for use in military operations. The competition was created by the Department of Defense's central research and development organization to help develop unmanned road vehicles that could potentially save lives in hostile war zones.

In the evenings and on weekends, the car is tested on campus roads and the deserted rooftop of a parking garage. Using their own cars, team members simulate traffic conditions the vehicle may encounter. UCF engineering alumnus Ben Patz, CEO of Coleman Technologies Inc., and lead engineer Don Harper, of the College of Engineering and Computer Science, combined their expertise to help equip Knight Rider VIP with sensors, a GPS system and computers. Harper donated the vehicle in 2004 for the project.

"My job is to make sure the hardware components are tested and working reliably," he said. "It's great to see three years of work come together. It's been a great collaborative effort between students, university staff, faculty and our industry partner."

Patz said he envisions several applications of autonomous vehicles in the future and believes the technology has great possibilities beyond the competition.

"The ability of the military to send supplies or provide reconnaissance missions in hostile regions will help save lives," he said. "Businesses could also benefit from lower costs in the delivery of equipment and supplies."

For more information on the DARPA Urban Challenge, go to www.darpa.mil/grandchallenge/index.asp.
