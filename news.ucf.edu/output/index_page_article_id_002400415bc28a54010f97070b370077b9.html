United Kingdoms First Lady Calls for Improving Human Rights, Especially for Children

By Mark Freeman Jan. 25, 2007

Related Contacts and Links:

  • Mark Freeman

    • 407-823-0935

  • UCF Global Perspectives Office

  • Print this Page

The global community faces a tremendous challenge in the 21st century of dealing with problems surrounding the rights of children, and the United States should be a leader in that effort, said Cherie Booth, a founding member of the Matrix Chambers Law Group of London and the wife of U.K. Prime Minister Tony Blair.

Addressing a crowd of about 500 Tuesday in the UCF Student Union, Booth emphasized that the Declaration of Independence serves as an example of how early visionaries in American history paved the way for those who followed to answer the challenges we have faced and will continue to face in the future. She said the United States has often been a leader and champion of the human-rights cause. She also pointed to the important role that the United States  and specifically former President Harry Truman  had in formulating the monumental United Nations Declaration on Human Rights. 

Booth described how about 400 million children have no access to safe drinking water. Consequently, many children acquire illnesses from unsafe water and die each year. On that subject, she invoked the words of former President John F. Kennedy, who said, Anyone who can solve the problem of water will be worthy of two Nobel prizes  one for peace and the other for science.

Education is also key to helping to solve the problems children face around the world, Booth said. She stressed growing evidence that suggests those with an education are more likely to become immunized against infectious diseases.

Although the United States has often been a leader in global efforts, America  and particularly U.S. courts  has been reluctant to adequately address some of the worlds challenges related to human rights, Booth said. In recent years, however, she said she has seen more of a willingness by the U.S. Supreme Court to look abroad for guidance with regard to human rights. Nevertheless, challenges continue to mount, and facing them will be difficult, she said.

Booth said she was extremely encouraged by the success of the University of Central Florida in becoming a leader in education, as well as in producing bright young individuals who can tackle the problems of this era. She noted multiple times during her presentation that she was thrilled to see so many students who are obviously concerned with global issues that are of great importance. She said she is confident that those same students will be successful in their efforts to make the world a better place.

After answering some pre-submitted questions that were of general interest, Booth took several questions from the audience. Topics included the plight of children in war-ravaged places such as Iraq and Afghanistan and rape as a crime against humanity.

Sponsors of Booths program were UCFs Global Perspectives Office and Student Government Association; Akerman Senterfitt; UCFs Office of International Studies; Brooksville Development Corp.; C.T. Hsu and Associates; Larson Allen; UCFs Metropolitan Center for Regional Studies, Political Science Department, Middle Eastern Studies Program and Diplomacy Program; AFLSE Florida North; AFLSE Florida Central; AFLSE Florida South; and the Global Connections Foundation.
