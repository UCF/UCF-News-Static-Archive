'Connect With Music' Course Exposes Infants, Toddlers to Music and Movement

By Shobana Daniell March 18, 2006

Photo: Jacque Brund

Flutist Aaron Goldman of the Orlando Philharmonic Orchestra performed for the infants and toddlers who attended a fall session of the "Connect With Music" course.

A handful of babies and toddlers looked up with smiles as soon as they heard flutist Aaron Goldman play The Nutcracker Suite. They bounced on their mothers and grandparents laps and rolled on the floor, and one little boy tried to grab Goldmans flute.

With great enthusiasm and energy, College of Education professor Mary Palmer sang Shake My Sillies Out while wiggling her hands and head. The kids bobbed their heads and wiggled their hands at their own pace, and some even wandered off to look for toys or make friends with the fellow toddlers.

The students in professors Palmer and Kay Allens Connect With Music courses may be the youngest ever at UCF. The six-week course is designed to expose 0- to 2-year-old children to music and movement, helping them develop an enhanced vocabulary and improved motor skills later in life. Musicians such as Goldman from the Orlando Philharmonic Orchestra perform during the final week of each session.

Christine Hanlon, the mother of 18-month-old Mika, said she has noticed a big difference in Mikas reaction to music since she enrolled in the class.

He gets very excited and pays close attention to music now, said Hanlon, who takes turns with her husband bringing Mika to the class. Mika becomes engaged in music unlike any other activity.

The course capitalizes on findings from experiments that prove interactive musical games with children and parents help the intellectual growth of kids, Palmer said.

Merely listening to music passively is not enough, she said.

Undergraduate and graduate music education students help the professors and musicians with nursery rhymes such as Twinkle, Twinkle Little Star while using various facial and hand gestures. The adults help the babies imitate the singers movements. The theory behind this approach is that correlating music to movement encourages the babies more than 100 billion brain cells to form connections  synapses  sort of like building a neural network.

Many small musical instruments enhance the musical experience and get the toddlers attention. Parents receive copies of the songs and are encouraged to practice at home with the kids. In addition, a bag filled with musical instruments, music-related books, props and CDs is sent with each family for at-home music-making. The adults in the class agreed that an added benefit of this course is the parents and children developed a new and deeper connection with each other.

The next session is scheduled from April 3 to May 8; the class times are: 10 to 10:40 a.m. for infants 12 months and younger and 9 to 9:40 a.m. for toddlers 12 to 24 months. The cost is $65 per session, and early registration is recommended as class sizes are limited. For more information, call 407-823-1297 or go to www.academy.ucf.edu.
