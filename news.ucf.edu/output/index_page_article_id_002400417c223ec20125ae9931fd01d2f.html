Negro Leagues Legends to Visit UCF

By Chad Binette Updated March 17, 2010

Photo: Courtesy of Bob "Peach-head" Mitchell

Bob "Peach-head" Mitchell pitched in the Negro Leagues from 1951 to 1957.

Four Negro Leagues legends will discuss their experiences on ballfields throughout Florida and the country – including the racism they often encountered – at noon Friday, March 19, at the UCF Library.

The legends also will participate in festivities and throw out the first pitch at a UCF home baseball game against Presbyterian College at 6:30 p.m. Friday. 

UCF is hosting the events in conjunction with “Pride & Passion: The African American Baseball Experience,” an exhibit that tells the story of African-American baseball players in the United States from the Civil War through the present. The exhibit relates their experiences to the struggle for civil rights.

The exhibit on the Library's first floor opened March 5 and runs through Thursday, April 15. Admission to the exhibit, the panel with the Negro Leagues legends and other lectures and discussions is free.

Players scheduled to participate are Bob “Peach-head” Mitchell, who pitched for the Kansas City Monarchs and two Florida teams from 1951 to 1957; Arthur Hamilton, a catcher from 1953 to 1959; Leon L. Harris, who pitched for the Kansas City Monarchs in 1954; and James “Son” Copeland, who played for the Bradenton Nine Devils.

“We had to have a certain amount of self-discipline to survive while traveling around to different cities under the ill winds of racism,” Mitchell said. “It’s very important for kids today to hear those stories.”

The forum will be moderated by UCF communications professor Anthony Major, who pitched in the Negro Leagues in Florida in the late 1950s. Major serves as the program director for UCF's Zora Neale Hurston Institute for Documentary Studies.

The UCF Libraries were selected as one of 50 nationwide sites for the “Pride & Passion” exhibit, which was organized by the National Baseball Hall of Fame and the American Library Association and funded through a grant from the National Endowment for the Humanities. The traveling exhibit is based on a permanent exhibit at the National Baseball Hall of Fame in Cooperstown, N.Y. A portion of the exhibit is funded through a grant from the UCF Office of Diversity Initiatives.

To tell the story of Central Florida’s rich but often forgotten baseball history, UCF also is displaying collections from partners such as the Sanford Museum, the Wells’Built Museum of African–American History and area residents Carol Mundy and Gary Taylor.

For more information on the exhibit and a full schedule of events, visit http://www.library.ucf.edu/baseball.

Other Upcoming Events:

All events will be held in room 223 of the UCF Library on the main campus, 4000 Central Florida Blvd., unless otherwise noted.

March 18, 10 a.m.: Discussion and book signing by Matt Tavares, author of the children’s book, “Henry Aaron’s Dream.”

March 22, 7 p.m. Lee Lowenfish, author of “Branch Rickey: Baseball's Ferocious Gentleman,” will present “Conservative Revolutionary: How Branch Rickey's Signing of Jackie Robinson Jump-Started the Civil Rights Movement After World War Two.”

March 30, 7 p.m.: Richard Lapchick, UCF endowed chair and director of the DeVos Sport Business Management Program and Institute for Diversity and Ethics in Sport: “It Wasn't Just Baseball: Great African-American Basketball Players Back in the Day.”

April 5, Noon: William Sutton, professor, UCF’s DeVos Sport Business Management Program: “Jackie Robinson: Intended and Unintended Consequences of Breaking the Color Barrier.”

April 12, 7 p.m.: Richard Crepeau, UCF professor of History: “The Negro Leagues: What Have We Learned and Where Do We Go from Here?”
