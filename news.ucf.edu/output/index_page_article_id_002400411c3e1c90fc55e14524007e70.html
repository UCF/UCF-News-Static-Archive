Brown vs. Board Events Continue as UCF Celebrates 50th Anniversary of Decision

By Chad Binette May 12, 2004

One of the nations most thorough commemorations of the 50th anniversary of the landmark U.S. Supreme Court decision that led to the integration of public schools will conclude in the next two weeks with public events at the University of Central Florida and four other venues.

 

UCF is coordinating the Brown, Black & White series, which will continue Thursday, May 13, with a presentation by physicist and Jones High School graduate Sylvester James Gates Jr. Gates will speak at a forum at 7 p.m. at the Orlando Science Center, 777 E. Princeton St. A free planetarium show will follow Gates' presentation about how his high school classes inspired him to pursue a career in physics and his achievements as a physicist. The event is presented by the City of Orlando.

 

Gates, who is a professor at the University of Maryland, will be the featured speaker Friday, May 14, at a physics colloquium at UCF. The colloquium will begin at 4 p.m. in Room 360 of the Math & Physics Building. Refreshments will be served at 3:30 p.m. in Room 318.

 

Local school and church choirs will perform during a Lift Every Voice and Sing celebration of African-American choral music at 7 p.m. Sunday, May 16, at First United Methodist Church of Orlando, 142 E. Jackson St. The UCF Gospel Choir, Jones High School Choir and choirs from the First United Methodist and New Covenant Baptist churches are among the scheduled participants. UCF professors Belinda Boyd, Spencer Downing and JoAnne Stephenson also are scheduled to participate.

 

On Monday, May 17, the day of the 50th anniversary of the Brown vs. Board of Education decision, a video presentation and panel discussion entitled Brown v. Board Reflections will be held at 7 p.m. at the Fine Arts Concert Hall (Building G) at Seminole Community Colleges Sanford/Lake Mary Campus.

 

Panelists include U.S. District Judge John Antoon II, 18th Judicial Circuit Chief Judge James E.C. Perry and Linda Chapin, director of the UCF Metropolitan Center for Regional Studies. Other panelists include Ingrid Nathan, who was the first African-American student to attend an integrated junior high school in Seminole County; Percy Luney Jr., dean of the Florida A&M University College of Law; and Rufus Brooks, who will represent the National Association for the Advancement of Colored People. Ned Julian, attorney for the Seminole County School Board, also is scheduled to speak. The event was organized by the 18th Judicial Circuit, Seminole Community College and the Seminole County Bar Association.

 

The WellsBuilt Museum of African-American History will present a panel discussion on March 19 at Valencia Community College. The forum will be from 6 to 8 p.m. in Room 202 of Building 6 on Valencias West Campus, 1800 S. Kirkman Road, Orlando. The panel will feature Royce B. Walden, the first African-American administrator for the Orange County school district, and attorneys W. George Allen, Joel Sharp and Harley Herman.

 

Allen is the first African-American who graduated from the University of Florida College of Law. Sharp served as legal counsel for Orange County school officials when schools were integrated. Herman worked on behalf of Virgil Hawkins, who was denied admission to the UF College of Law in 1949 because of his race.

 

For more information on the Brown, Black & White series, go to www.brownblackandwhite.org.
