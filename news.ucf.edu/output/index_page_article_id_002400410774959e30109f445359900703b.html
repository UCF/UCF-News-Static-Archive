Study: Graduation Rates for African-American Student-Athletes Improving

By UCF Staff April 7, 2006

Photo: Courtesy of UCF Athletics

The gap in graduation rates between white and African-American student-athletes has narrowed during the past 20 years, according to a study by Richard Lapchick of UCF's Institute for Diversity and Ethics in Sport.

Graduation rates of African-American college student-athletes have improved dramatically since they were first calculated for students who entered college in 1984, a new University of Central Florida study shows.

The study entitled Significant Progress for African-American Students was released Thursday on National Student-Athlete Day, a day when the positive achievements of student-athletes are celebrated. The studys author is Richard Lapchick, director of UCFs Institute for Diversity and Ethics in Sport.

Lapchick said low graduation rates for African-American student-athletes have been his top concern during the nearly 20 years that he has been studying graduation rates.

The improvement shown in this study is the best academic news I have seen, he said. The increases for all categories of African-American student-athletes during that period were between 15 and 18 percentage points. That is very good news for college sport.

The study examined graduation rates for students who entered school between 1984 and 1998. The Institute for Diversity and Ethics in Sport looked at Federal Graduation Rates and the NCAAs new Graduation Success Rates and compared them to the overall student body graduation rates of students in the Federal Graduation Rates. 

Graduation rates for all African-American student-athletes increased from 35 percent among those who entered school in 1984 to 52 percent for those who started school in 1998.

The graduation rate for male African-American student-athletes jumped 15 percentage points, from 33 percent to 48 percent, during that time. Female African-American student-athletes who entered school in 1998 graduated at a 63 percent rate, up from 45 percent for those who started school in 1984.

While graduation rates for white student-athletes also improved during that time, the gains were not as substantial, meaning that the gap between white and African-American student athletes has narrowed.

It should be noted that African-American student-athletes, including revenue sport student-athletes, graduate at a higher rate than African-Americans who are not student-athletes, Lapchick said, noting that the respective rates are 52 percent for African-American student-athletes and 43 percent for all African-American students.

The Institute for Diversity and Ethics in Sport, which is part of the College of Business Administration, serves as a comprehensive resource for issues related to gender and race in amateur, collegiate and professional sports. The institute researches and publishes a variety of studies, including annual reviews of student-athlete graduation rates and racial attitudes in sports, as well as the Racial and Gender Report Card, an assessment of hiring practices in coaching and sport management in professional and college sport.

The full report can be downloaded at http://www.ncasports.org/press_releases.htm.
