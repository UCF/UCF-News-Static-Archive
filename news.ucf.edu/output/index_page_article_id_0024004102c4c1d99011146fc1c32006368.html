NBA First Mens League to Receive A-Minus Grade for Diverse Hiring Practices

By UCF Staff May 10, 2007

Photo: Fernando Medina (NBAE/Getty Images)

Otis Smith (left), general manager of the Orlando Magic, is shown with player Keyon Dooling (center) and Coach Brian Hill (right). The NBA received a combined ‘A-minus’ grade for race and gender in hiring practices in the latest study by UCF's Institute for Diversity and Ethics in Sport.

The National Basketball Association became the first mens sports league to earn an A-minus for race and gender hiring practices in an annual report card issued by the University of Central Floridas Institute for Diversity and Ethics in Sport.

The NBA, which consistently earns the top grade among mens leagues in the report cards, received an A-plus for racial diversity in hiring and a B for gender. The combined A-minus grade is the highest ever for a mens professional sports league.

The NBA showed dramatic improvement in almost all areas for race in both the league office and at the team levels, wrote report author Richard Lapchick, who compiled the report with help from UCF graduate students Marina Bustamante and Horacio Ruiz.

The Institute for Diversity and Ethics in Sport analyzed data for the 2006-07 season for players, coaches, league office management, team administrators, head trainers and others.

Minorities held fifteen percent of the NBAs team vice president positions, the highest amount ever reported in any of the report cards on professional sports. Women occupied 39 percent of the professional positions in the league office. Thats a larger percentage than any other mens professional league has recorded in a report card.

The NBA also has the largest number of African-American head coaches, 12, in the history of any major professional sports league. Forty-one percent of assistant coaches are minorities, and thats the largest percentage ever for the NBA.

Robert Johnson, owner of the Charlotte Bobcats, remains the only African-American who is the majority owner of a major sports franchise. Seventy-five percent of NBA players are African-American.

UCFs Institute for Diversity and Ethics in Sport publishes Racial and Gender Report Cards to indicate areas of improvement, stagnation and regression in the racial and gender composition of professional and college sports personnel. To view the full NBA report, go to http://www.ncasports.org/press_releases.htm.
