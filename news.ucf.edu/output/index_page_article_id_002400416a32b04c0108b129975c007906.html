UCF to Test Emergency Communications Systems for Schools Statewide

By Chad Binette Jan. 27, 2006

Aiming to improve emergency communications on Florida campuses, university and law enforcement officials will test a siren, reverse 911 system and other notification methods Monday, Feb. 6, at the University of Central FloridaÂ’s main campus.

Funded by the U.S. Department of Homeland Security through the state Department of Education, the tests will help determine the best ways to communicate quickly and clearly with students, faculty and staff in situations such as terrorist acts or major hurricanes.

Beginning at 2 p.m. Monday, Feb. 6, researchers will test the effectiveness of communicating through a siren, an FM radio broadcast, a reverse 911 system and a Web-based system. A few hundred student observers stationed throughout the campus will participate in the tests, and WUCF-FM 89.9 is assisting with the radio broadcasts.

Two sirens, which will consist of alert tones followed by voice instructions, will be audible on campus and possibly in some nearby neighborhoods. Those tests will be conducted for about 90 seconds each from 2:45 to 3 p.m. The sirens will be on trucks provided by the vendors and will not remain on campus after the tests.

The sirens will be the loudest in the area bounded by the Fairwinds Alumni Center, the Classroom I and Health and Public Affairs buildings and the North Parking Garage. The anticipated noise level of 90 decibels there will be similar to the noise level heard in a mechanical factory.

Some residents who live near UCF likely will hear the sirens, but the anticipated noise levels off campus of 60 decibels or less should not be disruptive. Sixty decibels is the sound level of a typical conversation.

Test results will be used to determine what types of communication systems might be installed at the stateÂ’s colleges and universities and issues that need to be addressed when training people to use the systems. The stateÂ’s Higher Education Subcommittee of the Working Group on Domestic Security will provide about $790,000 this year toward the purchases of equipment, and additional funds may be awarded later.

The needs of each campus also might be different, as some schools have high-rise buildings close together while others have one- to two-story buildings scattered over larger areas.

Florida A&M University helped UCF obtain the equipment needed for the demonstrations through a state grant.
