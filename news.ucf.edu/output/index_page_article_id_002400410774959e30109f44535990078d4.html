Ex-Mafia Crime Boss Warns Student-Athletes of Dangers of Gambling

By Chad Binette March 23, 2006

Former New York mob crime boss Michael Franzese warned University of Central Florida student-athletes Wednesday that gambling, even seemingly innocent poker games, can become an addiction that harms family and friends and lead to overwhelming financial problems.

Franzese, who once ran a bookmaking operation for the mob, spoke candidly about his days in the Columbo crime family and dispelled some of the mystique that society seems to give betting on sports and poker. He addressed student-athletes and the public Wednesday evening during separate sessions at the Fairwinds Alumni Center.

Ive seen many lives destroyed, Franzese said. Ive seen more people have problems with gambling than I have with drugs and alcohol. And there are some serious consequences if you get in over your head.

Franzeses presentations at UCF came in the midst of a Florida trip that includes visits with professional baseball players at spring training.

The UCF Task Force on the Prevention of Gambling sponsored the Franzese event with support from the Florida Council on Compulsive Gambling and UCF Athletics. The task force was created last summer by Student Development and Enrollment Services to educate the campus community about student gambling and the potential for some students to experience negative psychological, financial and academic consequences as a result of compulsive gambling.

The task force also will explore gamblings links to other addictive behaviors, such as the abuse of alcohol and other drugs and the excessive use of the Internet. The panel will recommend appropriate gambling-related messages, policies and student activities to the UCF administration.

Franzese, who said several athletes worked for his mob bookmaking operation, reminded student-athletes that they are not allowed to bet on any college or professional sporting events and that they cannot give out inside information about their teams.

He said all of the gambling Web sites are illegal and based outside the United States, so anyone who bets on them is taking a chance with identity theft by providing personal information and credit card numbers. Another problem is that the Web site operators sometimes refuse to give winnings to bettors, who have little recourse because theyre dealing with illegal entities, Franzese said.

The Internet is not a safe place for you to gamble, he said.

While poker games may seem innocent, players who lose more money than they can afford to lose can get in deeper trouble trying to recover their losses, Franzese said. Also, its almost impossible to win at poker regularly without becoming a professional and practicing seven to 10 hours a day, he said.

Franzese was one of the youngest Mob bosses, and he coordinated operations in gambling, loan sharking and corporate crime before he was indicted in the 1980s and sentenced to 10 years in prison.  He then vowed to leave the Mob and, at the request of the Federal Bureau of Investigation, began educating athletes about the dangers of gambling.

Members of the UCF task force dealing with gambling issues include representatives from UCF Athletics, the Counseling Center, Student Government Association and other campus offices. Other members are from the Florida Council on Compulsive Gambling and the Orange County Coalition for a Drug Free Community.  

The task force sponsored a variety of educational efforts during National Problem Gambling Awareness Week, which was March 6 to 10. Pat Fowler, executive director of the Florida Council on Compulsive Gambling, will train the UCF Counseling Center staff on April 19. The training will help the counselors and other staff recognize and intervene with students with problems or concerns related to compulsive gambling.

The task force is a great vehicle to increase the visibility of issues connected to gambling and to provide our students with the information they need to make responsible choices and to recognize the signs of a problem. said Laura Riddle, chairwoman of the task force and associate director of Alcohol and Other Drug Counseling Services at UCF. The availability and accessibility of legalized gambling opportunities has increased tremendously in recent years.
