Task Force Recommends Spending $1.7 Million to Reduce Underage Drinking

By Chad Binette Oct. 8, 2008

The Orange County Underage Drinking Task Force today recommended spending about $1.7 million in a comprehensive effort to reduce underage drinking and improve the health and well being of youth countywide.

The 25-member panel called for a “red tag” ordinance that would allow the county to restrict the hours of businesses that repeatedly sell alcohol to minors. The task force also supported increased alcohol education campaigns from middle school through college; a  campaign to discourage parents from serving alcohol to underage students at parties; and the creation of databases to track alcohol-related arrests, injuries and deaths.

Orange County Mayor Richard Crotty and University of Central Florida President John Hitt established the task force in February. Since then, law enforcement officers, educators and health care professionals, along with students, retailers and other community leaders, have worked closely together to solve what the mayor has described as the most pervasive drug problem facing youth.

“This task force gives our community a solid plan to effect change,” Crotty said. “We owe it to our youth and our young adults to stay the course and solve this problem.”

When he announced the task force’s creation, Crotty cited national statistics showing that about 5,000 people under age 21 die annually as a result of underage drinking. In Orange County, a 2006 survey indicated that 38.4 percent of high school students reported using alcohol in the past 30 days.

Hitt sought to bring community partners together to discuss solutions that can help to save lives and improve students’ chances of succeeding before and during college. He said he would like to see the task force become a model for university-community partnerships around the country.

“The recommendations you discussed today are thorough and impressive and will help us address the formidable challenge of reducing underage drinking,” Hitt said. “I pledge the full support of UCF’s resources as we reach out to parents, students, retailers and others in the community to implement your recommendations.”    

While the task force held its final meeting today, a “One Voice” committee will be appointed to create an action plan and aggressively seek grants to fund the recommendations.  Orange County Public Schools, the Orange County Drug Free Coalition and UCF already have received nearly $1.5 million in grants that could fund many of the proposals focused on prevention, education and helping youth who have substance abuse problems. 

Orange County Director of Public Safety Mike McCoy and Rich Morrison, a regional vice president with Florida Hospital, served as co-chairs of the task force.

“Our dedicated members combined their diverse areas of expertise with an enthusiasm for not only studying the problem but figuring out how to solve it,” McCoy and Morrison said. “We hope our efforts will lead to significant, long-term changes that reduce underage drinking and improve the health and well being of our youth.”

Some of the final recommendations include:

-- Drafting a “red tag” ordinance that would allow the county to restrict the hours of operation of businesses that repeatedly sell alcohol to minors.

-- Increasing alcohol education efforts at middle and high schools and colleges. One goal is to correct students’ exaggerated perceptions about how much their peers drink.

-- Hiring three alcohol, tobacco and other drug specialists to implement and coordinate programs and activities aimed at preventing alcohol use by middle school and 9th-grade students. They also would develop programs for the parents of those students.

-- Calling on the law enforcement and health care communities to coordinate new databases that can provide important data about alcohol-related arrests, injuries and deaths. Faculty in UCF’s College of Health and Public Affairs would assist. The goal is for the newly collected data to guide future strategies to prevent underage drinking.

-- Training health care providers, particularly those in emergency rooms, to identify and assist patients with substance abuse problems. UCF would provide the training services.

-- Creating a countywide Campus-Community Committee organized by the Orange County Drug Free Coalition and a pilot program in which student leaders would serve as liaisons to their neighbors and neighborhood associations.
