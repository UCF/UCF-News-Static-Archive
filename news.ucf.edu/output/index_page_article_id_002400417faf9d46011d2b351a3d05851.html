UCF ChinaVine Hosts Chinese University Visitors and Photo Exhibition

By Christine Dellert Oct. 23, 2008

Photo: Jacque Brund

Since its inception nearly two years ago, the University of Central Florida’s ChinaVine project has led U.S. students halfway around the world preserving ancient folk culture for a new generation.

This week, the project’s Chinese partners got their first taste of Orlando life with a visit to UCF’s main campus. President Pan Lusheng, from Shandong University of Art and Design, along with others from the university’s China Folk Art Research Institute, made the 7,500-mile trek to sign a new agreement between the two universities that continues their partnership in filming and photographing traditional Chinese folk art and village life.

Their work appears on the ChinaVine Web site -- ChinaVine.org -- which is dedicated to teaching English-speaking audiences about long-standing Chinese customs and folk culture through modern technology.

The project is a collaboration between not only UCF and Shandong University of Art and Design, but also the University of Oregon and Beijing Normal University.

The UCF team, made up of mainly film and art students, has traveled to China four times during the past two years, leading up to the summer Beijing Olympics. The group documented painters, toy makers, dough artists and other often-forgotten art forms in the Shandong countryside, south of Beijing. Its videos and photo slideshows appear on the Web site along with descriptions of the villages and art forms.

“ChinaVine is a good example for us on how to protect the cultural heritage of China,” Pan said Wednesday through a translator to about a dozen of UCF’s ChinaVine members. “With the development of the economy and the modernization in China, the traditional culture is disappearing.”

He added that he hoped the project would expand and document more remote areas of China and spoke about the value of incorporating traditional art into modern life.

ChinaVine leaders also say they’re exploring the possibility of setting up a formal exchange program between UCF and Shandong University of Art and Design. While UCF students would travel abroad to study and document folk culture, Shandong’s students would come here for film or other classes in the College of Arts and Humanities.

“We want to further the friendship,” said Kristin Congdon, a UCF film and philosophy professor leading the project. “This project is about visually communicating folk art and traditions for a broader audience, but it’s also providing invaluable cultural experiences for students here and abroad.”

The new memorandum of understanding signed Wednesday between the two universities will continue this unique research project, as its creators plan to publish more videos and student blogs on the site.

UCF ChinaVine is organizing its first photo exhibition, which runs from Nov. 17 through Jan. 5 in the Burnett Honors College and coincides with the university’s International Education Week. The exhibit -- all photos by UCF students -- will also travel to the University of Oregon and later be placed in an Orange County library.

“The Chinese art not only belongs to China, but to all over the world,” said Zhao Yi, vice-director of the China Folk Research Institute at Shandong University of Art and Design.
