Tips for Speaking With Media

July 17. 2008

Photo: Jacque Brund

Television and print reporters often speak to our faculty for their expert opinion.

-- Write down the reporter’s name, organization and contact information.

-- Listen to what they are looking for and if you are not the right person let them know. Suggest someone else if you can.

-- Don’t feel rushed, especially if the question is a little complicated. If you need more time to gather your thoughts, ask for it. Keep in mind reporters usually are working on tight deadlines. So get back to them quickly or have them call you again within a reasonable amount of time.

-- Think about what you want to say. Generally the first thing to pop in your mind is not the best. Pick a two to three key points you want to make and make them often.

-- Be brief in your responses. Reporters like short quotes, and you can minimize errors by keeping it simple. Avoid jargon and making jokes or additional comments that could be taken out of context.

-- Bring up ideas or facts that you consider important to understanding the topic.

-- Nothing is off the record. If you don’t want it in print or on video, don’t say it.

-- If you are unclear about the angle of the story or specific questions being asked, let the reporter know. Ask him or her questions.

-- If a reporter makes a comment that is incorrect, don’t hesitate to set them straight.

-- Mention UCF during your discussion. For example, “On our research at UCF we found  . . .”

-- Don’t exaggerate. If you don’t know, say so.

Tips for a scheduled in-person interview

-- Be on time and be prepared.

-- Research your topic and prepare two to three talking points you’ll want to focus on. Repeat them often.

-- Have pertinent background information or facts that add to your explanation for reference.

-- Anecdotes and analogies are helpful in explaining difficult topics or making them more personal. If you can explain something in easy terms with a little humor, you will likely be asked for your expertise again.

-- If you have additional background information you want the reporter to have, provide it in writing as short bullet points. This will provide reference points for them after the interview.

Special considerations for television interviews

-- Be on time.

-- Offer a visually pleasing location for the interview. Offices are dull. If you are doing research, a lab or field location may be better.

-- Have props. If you are explaining DNA, have a model or some other prop that can help you visually explain a concept to a reporter.

-- Dress in neutral colors and avoid stripes or busy patterns.

-- Speak in short and clear sentences.

-- Repeat your main points at least three times.

-- Avoid jargon.

-- Maintain eye contact.

-- Look at the person interviewing you, not the camera.

-- Avoid saying no comment. The phrase has a connotation of guilt and can lead viewers/listeners to think you are hiding something.

-- Be aware of your tone and keep it conversational.

After the Interview

-- Don’t expect to see a printed story before it goes to press. Many reporters will be happy to read back a quote to you, but they will not provide you a written copy before it goes to press. The same can be said for television news. If you want a copy of the segment, have a friend record it.

-- If the news story contains an error, ask the reporter to correct it. Otherwise, at any time in the future, the error could be repeated.

-- Please notify the News & Information office about any media contacts you have had, any questions or information generated by them. You are not required to contact us, but it helps us keep track of media coverage and can help us generate more coverage for you and the university.
