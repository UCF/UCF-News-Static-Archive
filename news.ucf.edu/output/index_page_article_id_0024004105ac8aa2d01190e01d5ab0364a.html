Eduardo Salas, Mary Lou Sole Named 2008 UCF Pegasus Professors

By Brandon Bielich April 2, 2008

Photo: Jacque Brund

UCF President John Hitt presents Eduardo Salas, of Psychology and the Institute for Simulation and Training, with his Pegasus Professor award.

Professors Eduardo Salas and Mary Lou Sole have earned the top faculty honor of being named Pegasus Professors for their devoted service to the university and its students and their notable accomplishments in their respective fields.

Since 2000, 13 professors have received the Pegasus Professor Award, which recognizes sustained excellence in teaching, research and service.

As Pegasus Professors, Salas and Sole each will receive a statue of the UCF Pegasus, a gold Pegasus Professor Medallion and a check for $5,000.

Eduardo Salas, of the UCF Psychology Department and the Institute for Simulation and Training, helps organizations improve teamwork skills and develop procedures for effective decision making in stressful environments.

Salas is working to design tools and strategies to minimize human errors in aviation, law enforcement and medical environments, including the UCF College of Medicine.

Some say Salas, a Trustee chair in Psychology, is a “household name” in the field of Industrial and Organizational Psychology. He recently accepted an associate editorship of the top journal in the field.

Salas is the program director for Human System Integration Research at the Institute for Simulation and Training, and he is known as a leader within national and international research communities, earning about $20 million in external grants and contracts since 1999. In 2002, he was named UCF Researcher of the Year.

Many UCF post-doctoral and graduate students consider Salas, who graduated from UCF and earned a master’s of science degree in Industrial Psychology in 1980, to be their mentor. Salas often allows his students to co-author his research papers and presentations.

“Dr. Salas transformed my understanding of what it takes to be a successful student, scientist and professional,” wrote Kevin Stagl, a former student of Salas’, in a nomination letter. “It has been more than 16 months since I received my Ph.D. at UCF, and Eduardo and I still speak at least biweekly about my career objectives.

“It is difficult to imagine someone being more dedicated or passionate about ensuring the long-term success of his current or former students,” Stagl wrote.

Mary Lou Sole, a professor in the UCF College of Nursing since 1991, specializes in critical care. Her research contributions include studies of ventilator-associated pneumonia, airway management and mechanical ventilation.

At UCF, Sole was influential in the establishment of the master’s and doctoral programs in the College of Nursing. Today, she coordinates the Clinical Nurse Specialist graduate program, and she recently helped found the Clinical Nurse Leader graduate program.

In collaboration with UCF Health Services, Sole has tested and implemented a Web-based triage system. The National Institute on Alcohol Abuse and Alcoholism awarded Sole a grant of more than $500,000 for her study of alcohol screening and intervention at college clinics.

Throughout the past 15 years, Sole also has worked in intensive care units at Orlando Regional Medical Center, and she is the clinical research scientist there. In 2006, then-Florida Gov. Jeb Bush appointed her to the Florida Biomedical Research Advisory Council.

Sole has received numerous university honors, including the Excellence in Undergraduate Teaching Award, the Excellence in Professional Service Award; and UCF’s Excellence in Research Award, which she has won twice.

Jean D’Meza Leuner, dean of UCF’s College of Nursing, praised Sole’s passion and commitment to the university and college and described her as an excellent ambassador for both.

“Dr. Sole’s commitment to excellence is evident in all her work -- both at UCF and in her professional activities,” Leuner wrote in her letter nominating Sole for the Pegasus Professor award. “She continuously strives to enhance the quality of life of students, staff and fellow faculty through her many research, teaching and service activities.”
