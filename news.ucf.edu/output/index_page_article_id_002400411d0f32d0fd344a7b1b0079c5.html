About 50 Residents Attend Master Plan Public Hearing

By Chad Binette July 15, 2004

Photo: Jacque Brund

Bill Merck, vice president for Administration and Finance, explained the contents of UCF's draft 2005 master plan to a crowd of about 50 in the Visual Arts Building auditorium.

About 50 residents learned about UCFs plans for new academic buildings, land conservation and new parking garages Wednesday during the first of two public hearings on the proposed 2005 campus master plan.

 

Bill Merck, UCFs vice president for Administration and Finance, and Pete Newman, director of Facilities Planning, explained the draft plans contents before listening to comments from about a dozen residents.

 

We hope you will support us and continue to give us suggestions as we move forward, Merck told the crowd in the Visual Arts Building auditorium on UCFs main campus.

 

The master plan, a blueprint for campus development, includes academic buildings, student housing, roads, environmental protection and other projects. It supports UCFs strategic plan, which outlines the universitys academic mission and needs.

 

Were continuing to try to do our part to make sure we are a university that is dynamic and is meeting all of the needs of our students who come here and the students who want to come here, Merck said.

 

  

Highlights of the draft plan include new academic buildings in the campus core and a convocation center adjacent to the arena. The convocation center, which would host graduation ceremonies, concerts and athletic events, would seat about 10,000 and would be surrounded by student housing, restaurants and shops.

 

The plan calls for the addition of 2,000 on-campus beds for students and parking garages that would add at least 1,400 spaces. An environmental center to support public and institutional needs of the Arboretum and Biology Department could be built. And UCF would expand its areas for storm-water retention ponds.

 

Merck and Newman also showed residents a possible path for a bicycle and jogging trail that would be part of Orange and Seminole counties plans to connect a series of trails. The new trails would make it more convenient for students to ride their bicycles to the campus, Merck said.

 

UCF also hopes to encourage more students to ride free shuttles next year, Merck said. A shuttle service that UCF started in 2002 took about 4,500 student cars off of the campus and nearby roads last fall.

 

I think youll see even more reductions in traffic, Merck said.

 

 

Additional athletic practice, training and competition fields also are part of the draft plan, which will be reviewed by a host of public agencies before a second public hearing is held in October. The UCF Board of Trustees likely will vote on the plan in November.

 

The master plan is posted at www.fp.ucf.edu. Copies are available for review at the UCF Library on the main campus. Copies also can be purchased at the Waterford Lakes Kinkos, 899 N. Alafaya Trail, Orlando.
