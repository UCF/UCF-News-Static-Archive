UCF Center Helps Professors Engage Students With Technology and More

By Brandon Bielich Feb. 25, 2008

Photo: Courtesy of Faculty Center for Teaching and Learning

Debbie Hahs-Vaughn (foreground) speaks with colleagues at a Faculty Development Conference, where faculty gather to work on projects, interact with colleagues from other disciplines and explore fresh approaches to teaching and learning.

Students in Thomas Brueckner’s Physics class grasp clickers, devices that resemble remote controls, as questions show up on Power Point slides.

They are silent and fully engaged in Brueckner’s lecture, thinking about whether to click true or false to the questions on the screen.

Brueckner learned how to incorporate the clickers into his classes during a workshop presented by the UCF Faculty Center for Teaching and Learning. The center helps professors discover how to implement new technologies, such as clickers, in the classroom, and to learn other effective teaching methods.

“Our mission is faculty success: We find out what the faculty need and then we meet their needs,” said Alison Morrison-Shetlar, the center’s director and dean of the Office of Undergraduate Studies. She added that faculty success also leads to student success.

Interaction between students and teachers is a vital part of a valuable education and experience, whether in a group of 30 or 300 students. Incorporating clickers into discussions allows students to actively take part in a lecture. Teachers poll students and receive instant feedback about topics covered in class.

Brueckner began using clickers to quiz students a few years ago after working with the faculty center.

“My objective is always to get students to think, and when I see the entire room become quiet, that’s when I know the students are really thinking,” Brueckner said. “And that’s when it’s fun.”

The faculty center, which celebrated its 10th anniversary in January, teaches UCF staff effective ways for adopting technology – such as clickers – in hopes that all students will receive the value of face-to-face communication, regardless of class size.

Over the decade, innovations such as blogs, podcasts and Facebook have led to new faculty center workshops that detail effective methods for implementing the new tools.

“As things change, we will change,” Morrison-Shetlar said. “That’s why it’s so important that we teach because we have to keep up” with the most current issues in society and teaching.

UCF Psychology professor Jay Brophy, who has worked with the center, uses Facebook in his classes to learn more about his students.

In the past, he would ask his students what they like to do, watch, read and listen to in their spare time. Now, he views their Facebook profiles.

“It let’s me get to know who my students are, what they’re interested in and weave those things into my instruction,” Brophy said.

Although students may be unfamiliar with the faculty center, or even unaware of its existence, its faculty programs ultimately impact thousands of UCF students.

For example, one professor who teaches four classes of 30 each semester attends a workshop at the center and then applies the lesson in the classroom. At that rate, about 120 students each semester benefit from just one professor participating in the workshop.

“There is a huge ripple effect,” Morrison-Shetlar said. “Is every student touched? Probably over time – yes.”

In addition to students, the center impacts many UCF professors.

“Telling me about how to use this system or that system is nice, but the real strength at the faculty center is the people that are there,” Brueckner said. “Every single person there is helpful and friendly.”

For Brophy, programs such as teaching circles, where professors meet to discuss issues in their respective fields, provide a friendly atmosphere to meet other instructors.

“It’s very possible to spend a long, long time at UCF without getting to know people outside of your discipline,” Brophy said. “The faculty center broadens the circle of people that faculty and staff get to know at the university.”

“I go there to talk about ideas all the time,” Brueckner said. “It’s a great place to do work.”
