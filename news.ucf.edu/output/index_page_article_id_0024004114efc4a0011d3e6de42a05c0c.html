UCF Professor Tells Lincoln's Story Through Music

By Christine Dellert Oct. 27, 2008

Photo: Courtesy of Stella Sung

Stella Sung is a renowned local composer and director of UCF's Center for Research and Education in Arts, Technology and Entertainment.

Before composing her latest piece on Abraham Lincoln, Stella Sung hit the history books.

What she found was not only an iconic image known around the world for freedom, equality and opportunity, but a man conflicted and often wrestling with melancholy.

“I learned that Lincoln was a fascinating personality; kind and gentle, yet ambitious with tenacious perseverance,” said Sung, a University of Central Florida Music and Digital Media professor. “He was a man with humor, but also with a dark and deep melancholy that affected him from about the age of 24 onward.”

Her latest work, “Lincoln’s Battle,” explores the range of emotions faced by the 16th president during a tumultuous time in U.S. history. The Orlando Philharmonic Orchestra will perform the piece during a concert Saturday, Nov. 1, dedicated to the 200th anniversary of Lincoln’s birth.

“Lincoln’s Battle” combines familiar melodies from the Civil War period, including “Dixie and “The Battle Hymn of the Republic,” to help “express some of the darkness and some of that struggle,” Sung said.

It’s also the first piece that Sung has written for The Orlando Philharmonic Orchestra since being named its inaugural Composer-in-Residence in August. In her new role, Sung will continue creating and identifying works for the Philharmonic and will act as “another spokesperson for contemporary music,” she says.

“I’m very happy to be named the Composer-in-Residence and for the opportunity to give back to the community and the orchestra,” she added. “I remember when the orchestra first started. Its growth and development over the past 15 years has been remarkable and has also inspired me as an artist and composer.”

Several works during Saturday’s concert will be accompanied by photochoreography -- projected visual images timed to music. The combination of pictures and powerful sound creates a “truly immersive experience,” Sung says, which she hopes will draw new and younger audiences to the concerts.

Sung’s other commissioned works for the Philharmonic included “Constellations” in 2003 to celebrate the orchestra’s 10th anniversary and last season’s “Rockwell Reflections,” a musical and visual tribute to the life of artist Norman Rockwell.

“What we know now, history will prove correct -- Stella is one of the great American composers of our generation,” said the Philharmonic’s executive director, David Schillhammer. “To have her work and live here in Central Florida is a blessing to the community, and now to the Philharmonic. Not only is she a composer of significant stature, she is also a great human being who understands the power that music, and especially new music, plays in our psyche and our lives.”

In addition to her work as a composer, Sung is the director of UCF’s Center for Research and Education in Arts, Technology and Entertainment (CREATE). She began piano lessons at 8 and studied extensively throughout high school. A native Floridian, she was a piano major at the University of Michigan. Sung later earned her master’s degree in Composition from the University of Florida and her Ph.D. in piano performance from the University of Texas at Austin.

Sung has received recognition and awards from the National Endowment for the Arts, Meet the Composer, the MacDowell Colony and other leading music organizations. She has also won two Florida Individual Artist’s Fellowships, sponsored by the state Division of Cultural Affairs; a 2005 Florida Artist’s Enhancement Award; and the national 2007-10 Phi Kappa Phi Artist Award. She is a Distinguished Alumna of the University of Florida. Her work is published by Theodore Presser Company, Southern Music Company and French company Henry Lemoine, and is available on Koch International Recordings, Cambria Recordings and Sinfonica (Italy) recordings.

The Orlando Philharmonic Orchestra’s “Lincoln’s Year” concert begins at 8 p.m. Saturday, Nov. 1, in the Bob Carr Performing Arts Centre. It will be conducted by Christopher Wilkins. The final work, “Lincoln Portrait” by Aaron Copland, will be narrated by Mark McEwen, former weatherman for CBS’ “The Early Show” and Local 6 news anchor. Artist James Westwater created the symphonic photochoreography.

For more information, visit http://www.orlandophil.org. Tickets are available to students with valid ID for half price in the $26 or $36 seating levels. Call 407-770-0071 to order.

In order to purchase a Student Rush ticket, you must come to the Bob Carr Performing Arts Centre, at 401 W. Livingston St., and purchase at the Orlando Philharmonic window. Rush tickets cannot be purchased or reserved over the phone. These tickets are available on a first-come, first-serve basis.
