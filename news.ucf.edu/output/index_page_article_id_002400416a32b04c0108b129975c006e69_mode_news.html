Study: UCF Med School Could Have $6.4 Billion Economic Impact in Florida

By Tom Evelyn Feb. 23, 2006

Photo: File rendering

A University of Central Florida medical college could create nearly 26,000 jobs and have an economic impact of $6.4 billion over 10 years if related life-sciences businesses form and create jobs at the same rate as experienced in other leading clusters, according to a study announced today.

UCF estimates the state cost to support the medical college over the same period would be $194.3 million, meaning Florida could earn more than $32 for every dollar it invests if the life-sciences cluster develops as expected.

Â“In addition to helping to address the need for more physicians and improving health care, it is clear that the college would have the benefit of adding thousands of jobs and billions of dollars of economic activity.Â”

The state Board of Governors, which oversees Florida public universities, is expected to vote March 23 on a proposal to create a UCF college of medicine in Orlando. Florida International University is also requesting a medical college.

The UCF economic impact study, which was conducted by the Milken Institute and Arduin, Laffer & Moore Econometrics, found that a medical college in Orlando could:

-- Generate $1.4 billion in economic activity from the college alone; $6.4 billion with a life-sciences cluster.

-- Create 6,470 new jobs; 25,730 with the cluster.

-- Increase wages by $517 million; $2.3 billion with the cluster.

-- Add $81.4 million in tax revenue; $365.2 million with the cluster.

Â“Based upon our extensive analysis of the role of medical schools in supporting the formation of life-sciences clusters around the country, OrlandoÂ’s potential to become a leading center will be greatly enhanced by the presence of a medical school,Â” said Ross DeVol, director of regional economics at the Milken Institute. Â“A medical school can be utilized to spin out research and attract firms that want access to top-notch medical research.Â”

A UCF medical college and its medical degree program would capitalize on the universityÂ’s strengths in biomedical sciences, modeling and simulation, and optics and photonics while fulfilling the stateÂ’s need for more physicians.

The college would be established in fall 2006, if approved by the Board of Governors and funded by the Legislature. The degree calls for a four-year curriculum that begins clinical education in fall 2008 and would produce about 120 medical graduates each year. Students also would have opportunities to study at the University of Puerto Rico School of Medicine and to participate in shared research programs.

Two of the largest medical systems in the country, Florida Hospital and Orlando Regional Healthcare, would partner with UCF to establish 95 new medical residencies. Most medical students who study and complete their residencies in Florida stay in the state.

The hospitals also would pay 40 percent of the salaries for 50 clinical faculty members and provide support staff and clinical and office space, all of which would reduce the cost to the state for operating the medical college.

UCF would cover the initial capital costs for the medical college building and library with private donations. The university has raised more than $52 million in donations and land for the college.

Tavistock Group has pledged $12.5 million in cash and 50 acres of land valued at more than $8 million to establish the UCF Healthcare Campus at Lake Nona, where the medical college would be located if it is approved.

Al and Nancy Burnett donated $10 million that in part will be used with state matching and construction funds to pay for the Burnett College of Biomedical Sciences building, which would be located with the medical college if it is approved. The biomedical sciences college has been established, and its new facility will be built regardless of the decision on the medical college.
