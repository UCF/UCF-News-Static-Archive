LEAD Scholars Sharing Advice on Leadership, Career Skills

By Chad Binette March 28, 2005

Photo: Chad Binette

Three Points Elementary fifth-graders formed a "human knot" during an exercise on leadership with UCF's LEAD Scholars.

The University of Central Florida LEAD Scholars will be busy this week sharing the lessons theyve learned.

 

One group of the LEAD Scholars will share advice with fifth-graders on how to become leaders and deal with bullies; another group will join Seminole County Regional Chamber of Commerce members to teach career skills to high-schoolers.

 

LEAD Scholars are UCF freshmen and sophomores who take leadership classes and volunteer in the community to learn how to become leaders on campus and after they graduate. Many LEAD Scholars are active in student government and other campus organizations and volunteer many hours in the community during their final two years at UCF.

 

Being part of LEAD Scholars has helped me stay motivated and involved within the community, says sophomore Chelsea Stone, of Santa Rosa County, who says getting close to a small group of students and faculty helped her adjust to life on a large campus. Im surrounded by people who are going out and doing things, and the program has taught me a lot about leadership.

 

As sophomores, LEAD Scholars work on semester-long projects such as presenting leadership skills and anti-bullying tactics to elementary school students and teaching career skills to high school students. The LEAD Scholars develop lessons and business plans with help from faculty advisors and then lead the class presentations. In other projects, students are coordinating community-service efforts for local agencies and learning and applying leadership principles through a class modeled after The Apprentice television show.

 

The Three Points Elementary School sessions on Tuesday, March 29, and Thursday, March 31, will include skits that show how good leaders can deal with bullying, and the fifth-graders will draw pictures of themselves in leadership roles and write something nice about all of their classmates.

 

Stone and her classmates taught the first of two lessons to the fifth-grade classes three weeks ago. The LEAD Scholars then explained how leaders dont have to hold official positions such as president; they can be servant leaders who lead by helping others. They concluded by urging students to perform a Going Against the Grain activity in which they lead by example and resist peer pressure.

 

In prior years, LEAD Scholars have worked with Three Points Elementary School students on designing a flower garden as a tribute to veterans, writing letters to military personnel overseas and creating books-on-tape for primary school teachers.

 

Its such a positive experience for our kids, said Three Points Elementary teacher Gordon Miranda. And they get a glimpse of college students and college life.

 

Another group of LEAD Scholars will teach classes at Lake Mary High School for five consecutive weeks beginning Tuesday, March 29. With help from Seminole County Regional Chamber of Commerce volunteers, the LEAD Scholars will show high school sophomores how to keep personal budgets, apply for jobs, interview for jobs and develop personal, educational and professional goals.

 

The chamber launched the Success at Work II program in Seminole County eight years ago. About 70 businesspeople now help to teach career and life skills every year at Lake Mary, Lyman and Seminole high schools and Crooms Academy. The LEAD Scholars are participating this year for the first time. They will work with the businesspeople to develop lesson plans for the courses five weeks based on a general outline of course objectives and suggested activities in a booklet provided by the Chamber of Commerce.

 

It really prepares these young people for life skills that theyre going to need relatively quickly, because many of them have part-time jobs by the time theyre 16, said Owen Wentworth, chairman of the chambers Education and Workforce Development Committee. One difficulty for business leaders can be connecting with the students in a social environment, because theyre several years older. I think one thing the LEAD Scholars are going to bring to this is a closer connection to the students in terms of age and probably credibility about what theyre going to have to deal with during the next few years.

 

Jill Ehling, who teaches the career skills practicum for UCF students, also emphasized the value of college students identifying more closely with high school students because of their age. In addition to learning about job skills, the high school students will be able to ask questions about how to fill out college applications and what college life is like, she said.

 

UCFs LEAD Scholars program, which includes nearly 550 students this semester, will celebrate its 10th anniversary in the fall. The program is based on three main principles: leadership, learning and service. Students must maintain a 3.0 grade-point average, and they also are required to complete 10 hours of community service each semester, a threshold that is intentionally set low, program director Gary Morgan-Muskin said.

 

Were trying to instill in them the personal spirit of giving back to the community, he said. The hope is that in getting them started, they realize the commitment as a leader that they should be making and do more.

 

While several universities offer leadership programs for students, few combine the classroom, community and social aspects of LEAD Scholars while keeping the same group of students together for two years.

 

Weve taken pieces that other schools use and put them together here, Morgan-Muskin said. Theres nothing else like it in Florida.

 

Laura Creegan, a former LEAD Scholar who graduated with a bachelors degree in psychology in December, said the program showed her the many ways in which she could get involved on campus and in the community and helped her build strong friendships. Partly because of her LEAD Scholars experiences, she also decided she wants to pursue a masters degree in higher education student affairs.

 

It was one of my most rewarding experiences at UCF, said Creegan, of West Palm Beach. Administrators in the program have made a huge impact on my life and have been great mentors. And, coming to a school with as many students as we have, it was nice to really bond with some of the students in LEAD Scholars.
