Commander Confident NATO Will Succeed in Transforming Afghanistan

By Chad Binette Sept. 21, 2007

Photo: Jacque Brund

Supreme Allied Commander Gen. James Jones speaks at UCF on Thursday.

Supreme Allied Commander Gen. James Jones expressed confidence Thursday that NATO will transform Afghanistan into a contributing member of the family of nations but cautioned that such a dramatic change will take time.

Jones told a University of Central Florida audience that he believes the North Atlantic Treaty Organizations member nations can handle the military challenges in Afghanistan and that some of the more critical efforts involve building a functioning police department and helping to strengthen the economy.

NATOs 26 member nations plus 11 partner nations that are not full members are working together in Afghanistan. NATO now is responsible for overseeing peace and security efforts in about 85 percent of the country, Jones said.

Dont forget that the reason we are in Afghanistan is that it was the home of global terrorism, Jones said. We will not let that happen again.

Jones addressed a crowd of about 400 at the Fairwinds Alumni Center in a forum sponsored by the Global Perspectives Office. He addressed how NATOs role has changed since its founding after World War II and explored current challenges such as countering terrorism and securing enough funding from member nations.

Jones leads Allied Command Europe, which includes the NATOs military forces in Europe and strives to maintain the peace, security and territorial integrity of European nations that are NATO members.

Jones also serves as commander of the United States European Command. He commands the European components of the U.S. Army, Navy, Air Force, Marines and Special Operations Command. Together, those forces aim to support and achieve U.S. interests and objectives in 93 countries in Central and Eastern Europe, Africa and part of the Middle East.

The accord that created NATO was signed in April 1949 in the aftermath of World War II. Member nations shared the philosophy that an attack on one of them was an attack against all of them.

Jones said the role of NATO changed after the collapses of the Soviet Union and Berlin Wall because there no longer was one clear enemy. NATO has had to transform itself into a more proactive and adaptable organization since then, Jones said.

About 38,000 NATO troops are serving on three continents today. That includes about 16,000 troops who are keeping the peace in Kosovo and others in the Mediterranean area and Africa. A small number of NATO troops are in Iraq, helping to provide the Iraqi army with equipment and providing training for government and military officials, Jones said.

As the political will for expanding NATO operations continues to grow, it is important that member nations begin contributing more money, Jones said.

Other challenges for NATO involve securing access to energy sources such as oil pipelines and protecting against the spread of weapons of mass destruction, Jones said. NATO also must fight narcoterrorism, the use of drug profits, such as from opium in Afghanistan, to fund terrorist efforts, he said.

Jones cited NATOs support of the Kurds in 1991 as an example of the impact the organization can have in the world. Threatened by Saddam Hussein, the Kurds fled their homeland into Turkey and didnt return until they felt that NATO troops could keep them safe. For many Kurds, it was the first time in their lives that they had ever seen a uniform that they werent afraid of, Jones said.

In addition to the Global Perspectives Office, sponsors of the event Thursday were the Florida Eastern Europe Linkage Institute, the UCF Office of International Studies, the UCF Student Government Association, the UCF European Studies Program, the UCF Political Science Department, the UCF Middle Eastern Studies Program, the Global Connections Foundation and the Orlando Sentinel.
