Dean of British Medical School Emphasizes Universities Global Role

By Mark Freeman Feb. 6, 2007

Related Contacts and Links:

  • Mark Freeman, Global Perspectives

    • 407-823-0935

  • Global Perspectives Office

  • Newcastle University

  • Print this Page

Todays world is a small place, and humankind faces a series of threats  including poverty, resource shortages, environmental changes, war and terrorism. Universities in the 21st century can help address these challenges by sharing individual experiences and looking for global connections, Reg Jordan, dean of International Medical Education at the University of Newcastle upon Tyne, England, said during a weeklong stay in Central Florida. 

Jordan was invited to visit UCF last year, when he helped host a university-led mission to the United Kingdom. He was appointed dean of International Medical Education in 2006 after serving in various other university positions, including deputy dean of Undergraduate Medicine. He has focused much of his career on research, primarily in the area of immunology.

Jordans first stop was a visit to the M.D. Anderson Cancer Center in downtown Orlando. The tour showcased state-of-the-art medical technology, including new scanners capable of pinpoint precision in diagnosing cancers. Jordan provided information about his universitys leading-edge research into stem cells.

A key reason for Jordans visit was to share ideas about UCFs new medical school. He met one-on-one with Dean Deborah German of the UCF College of Medicine, Provost Terry Hickey and President John Hitt. Jordan also spent time with other university officials and deans, and he attended the State of the University presentation on Friday.

Jordan was a featured speaker at the Tiger Bay Clubs monthly meeting, a Burnett Honors College luncheon, a Global Connections Foundation regional meeting and in various classes at UCF and Rollins College. In addition, he taped a segment of the Global Perspectives show, which will air later on the UCF Channel.

Jordan was given a behind-the-scenes tour of the veterinary facilities at Walt Disney Worlds Animal Kingdom. He directs a nonprofit organization in England that promotes opportunities in higher education and curricula, including veterinary sciences. It was especially useful for him to see first-class care and state-of-the-art technology that treats the exotic wildlife at Animal Kingdom, as well as native animals.

Jordan frequently talked about the similarities between his home region of Northeast England and Central Florida, and he said the similarities outweighed differences.

Of course, theres the medical school. But beyond that, both of us have regional populations of between 2 million and 3 million, Jordan said. Both are seeking to take forward an agenda of economic and cultural development.

In addition, the English northeast is a post-industrial area, with its economic origins in the industrial revolution and sectors such as shipbuilding, steel and coal. Because of the demise of these heavy industries, the region finds itself in an era of economic regeneration, making a transition to a modern, knowledge-based, high-technology society.

In Central Florida, Jordan said, the economic origins were mainly in agriculture and tourism.

But there is still a great desire to diversity the economy, a regeneration of possibilities, if you will, along the lines of high technology, international trade and, of course, health sciences, he said. Also, both regions are a step removed from the center nationally, and this is a plus. 

Universities are a critical part of the regeneration process, both in Northeast England and in Central Florida, Jordan said.

Universities, if they correctly follow their purpose, are an integrative part of civilization, not a bolt-on extra, he said. They should stand for excellence, but also for a purpose, one that is wider than the narrow interests of those universities per se. Its better for them to deploy their resources synergistically, making them one limb of a tripod for regional development.

Further, universities have a vital role in helping to extend a regions reach into the outside world, Jordan said. His own university is planning to open a branch of its medical school in Malaysia. Jordan stressed that a critical mission for universities is to expose students to informed discourse on the wider issues of today, not just on the subjects in which they are majoring.

Medical students, for example, also need to understand poverty, terrorism and other major issues, Jordan said.

Jordans visit, which was sponsored by the UCF Global Perspectives Office and the Global Connections Foundation, tied into the Global Perspectives Offices university-wide theme for 2006-2007, The Future of Europe. For more information, please visit www.ucfglobalperspectives.org .
