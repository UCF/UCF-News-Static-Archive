UCF Economic Forecast Calls for Better Housing Market, Rise in Unemployment

By Zenaida Gonzalez Kotala June 1, 2007

Photo: UCF News file

Sean Snaith's latest economic forecast contains good news for home sellers but warns that unemployment may start to rise.

Full Photo

  • Sean Snaith, Institute for Economic Competitiveness

    • 407-823-1451

  • Economic Forecast

  • Print this Page

For those with a house on the market, theres good news: the worst is over. But a University of Central Florida economic forecast that released May 31, also delivered bad news: unemployment rates should rise.

The biggest impact of the housing slowdown on the economy is over, said Sean Snaith, director of UCFs Institute for Economic Competitiveness, which will issue the quarterly U.S. Economic Forecast. But unemployment rates are going to start going up through the rest of 2007 before stabilizing in 2008 at about 5 percent.

While the media have been focusing on the housing market, other alarms are starting to go off, including the paltry Gross Domestic Product performance expected through the end of the year. And if the hurricane season, which starts June 1, results in major disruptions to gas supplies, it could spell trouble.

The potential for a supply surge in prices coupled with existing global demand could lead to a much larger surge in energy prices with economic repercussions much more far-reaching than what we have experienced to date, Snaith said.

Other variables such as rising gas prices, the war in Iraq, terrorism abroad and a presidential election in 2008 add to the uncertainty. Even so, Snaith said the economy is not likely to fall into a recession.

Here are highlights from UCFs forecast. To access the entire report, visit: http://www.bus.ucf.edu/hitec/Forecasts.htm.

*The housing soufflé continues to cool:  Housing starts decline through the third quarter in 2007 and then begin a slow upward climb through 2009.

*Mortgage rates slowly creep to 6.9 percent in 2009. Excess supply of new and existing homes in many markets will put downward pressure on prices through 2007 and into the first quarter of 2008.

*The Federal Reserve is in fine-tuning mode when it comes to interest rate adjustments, and there will be no major movements in interest rates over the forecast horizon. Snaith anticipates a single 0.25 percent reduction in the federal funds rate to soften the impact of the housing sector on the economy by the end of the year.

*Paltry GDP growth in the first quarter of 2007 will accelerate through the rest of 2007; growth for the full year is expected to come in at 2 percent.

*Unemployment rates end their three-year decline and begin to rise slightly in 2007 but will remain below 5 percent before falling back to 4.7 percent in 2009.  Payroll job growth slows to 1 percent in 2008 before recovering to 1.5 percent in 2009.

*Energy prices continue to plague the economy and threaten to reignite inflation.

*The U.S. economy resumes above-trend growth in 2008 and 2009 at 2.8 percent and 3.3 percent, respectively.

*Core inflation remains above the Feds comfort zone at 2.2 percent, but the rate of overall CPI inflation falls to 2 percent in 2008 and 2009.

For those with a sense of humor, Snaith provides a short list of movie remakes that Hollywood could use if it wants to capitalize on the hysteria over the housing market.

Snaith is an expert in economics, forecasting, market sizing and economic analyses.
He is a member of several national forecasting panels, including the Western Blue Chip Economic Forecast panel, the National Association of Business Economics Quarterly Outlook Survey Panel, the Federal Reserve Bank of Philadelphias Survey of Professional Forecasters, Bloomberg U.S. Economic Indicator Survey and USA Today Economic Survey Panel.

The UCF Institute for Economic Competitiveness mission is to expand public understanding of the economy by convening business leaders, scholars, policy makers, civic groups and media to discuss critical issues.

Snaith issues quarterly forecasts on the national and Florida economies. The Florida forecast includes following metropolitan areas: Daytona, Fort Myers, Gainesville, Ocala, Lakeland, Palm Bay-Melbourne, Pensacola, Miami, Jacksonville, Tallahassee, Tampa and Orlando. Quarterly reports will follow.
