Secretary of State, Former Ambassador Emphasize Benefits of Free Trade

By Gabby Othon Jan. 27, 2005

Related Contacts and Links:

  • John Bersia

    • 407-823-0688

  • UCF Global Perspectives

  • Print this Page

Florida Secretary of State Glenda Hood and former U.S. Ambassador to Colombia Myles Frechette stressed the importance of free trade with Latin America during a forum Thursday at the University of Central Florida.

 

Hood presented the most robust defense of free trade, telling the audience of about 200 how the Free Trade Area of the Americas agreement helps the state. An agreement hatched by the leaders of 34 nations in 1994 sets out the vision for a free trade area spanning the entire hemisphere. However, many details of a more thorough agreement about trade between the nations still need to be worked out.

 

Hood said that although challenges in negotiation would likely result in a weaker version of the original agreement, it would still maintain the integrity of its original principles: strengthened democracy, prosperity, better protection of human rights and sustainable development.

 

A former Orlando mayor, Hood also focused on the importance of choosing Miami as the host for the Free Trade Area of the Americas Secretariat.  She listed twelve major selling points, the most notable being that in many ways, Miami is already viewed as a gateway to the Americas.

 

Frechette, whose experience in Latin America and the Caribbean spans several decades, provided the most sweeping view of free trade, saying that globalization is a train coming down the tracks, and you cant stop it.

 

He said thats a signal to countries in the region to embrace the idea of hemispheric free trade, make compromises and move on. Otherwise, he warned, other fast-developing countries and regions -- notably China and Asia -- could gain an advantage.

 

If we really believe that the only way is to be interdependent, we must be willing to cooperate, Frechette said.

 

Frechette, a native of Chile, is a former president of the Council of the Americas, and he served as U.S. ambassador to Colombia in the 1990s. Based on his experience living and working throughout Latin America and the Caribbean, he said he believes that free trade can help all nations progress.

 

Andrew LaVigne, executive director of Florida Citrus Mutual, discussed the free-trade issue from the perspective of the citrus industry. He maintained that his group supports free trade, but he said hes worried that the states citrus industry could be destroyed by foreign competition, especially from Brazil.

 

The panel was moderated by Terry McCoy, director of the Latin American Business Environment Program at the University of Florida. Some students participated in a Free Trade Area of the Americas protest outside the Student Union before and during the presentation.

 

The forum was sponsored by the UCF Global Perspectives Office, College of Business Administration and Political Science Department, along with Orange County government, the Orlando Regional Chamber of Commerce, the Orlando Sentinel, the Metro Orlando International Affairs Commission and Global Connections for Central Florida.
