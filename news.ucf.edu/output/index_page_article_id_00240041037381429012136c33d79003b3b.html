UCF Filmmakers Succeed With Low-Budget Techniques in Bad Economy (With Video)

By Christine Dellert July 27, 2009

Photo: Courtesy of Dino Gallina

Outside a half-renovated house in Winter Park, aspiring filmmakers from three area schools are hard at work, battling cameras, lights and computers in 90-plus-degree heat.

Director Dino Gallina shouts from a makeshift tent in the front yard. His project, “Red Tide,” is the fifth feature-length film from the University of Central Florida’s MFA graduate program in Entrepreneurial Digital Cinema.

With a focus on low-budget, independent filmmaking, the Master of Fine Arts program at UCF became the first accredited film program in the country to require its students to create full-length films and raise at least $50,000 in production costs to complete their degrees.

Raising the money can be a daunting task, especially in today’s economy, but UCF’s graduate filmmakers are earning international accolades with acceptance to some the world’s premier film festivals.

“We’re unique because we ask students to climb this enormous hill and make a feature film,” said Stephen Schlow, who heads UCF’s Film Department.

Recent graduate and Ecuadorian filmmaker Laura Lopez’s UCF project, “Acts of Mercy,” was screened last month at the 12th-Annual Shanghai Film Festival. Last year, another UCF graduate film student, Danny Daneau, was a presenter at the Sundance Film Festival.

This summer, two UCF graduate students are shooting feature-length films in Central Florida. Each film is budgeted at about $50,000. Back from filming in Tarpon Springs, Gallina will direct and produce the remainder of his film in Winter Park and Orlando.

Another UCF graduate film student, Andrew Kenneth Gay, recently began production on his film, “A Beautiful Belly,” about one man’s prospects of fatherhood.

“The nature of UCF’s program makes low-budget, entrepreneurial filmmaking more accessible,” says Gallina, whose film, “Red Tide” tells the story of a Greek sponge fisherman’s two sons in Tarpon Springs, a town where Gallina and his dad boated together years ago.

When one brother falls in with a dangerous crowd in “Red Tide,” it’s up to the other to save him and their father’s old fishing boat. “I wanted to tell a story that’s honest,” the 28-year-old says. “It’s a story about how the evils of the world can penetrate our lives without us knowing it.”

Gallina’s film is also unique because it’s the first time that students from UCF, Valencia Community College and Full Sail University have worked together on a feature-length project. Gallina praises the collaboration among his crew and says he hopes for more partnerships in the future.

Requiring its students to make a feature film is “a really ambitious thing for a school to get behind,” says Gay, who attended UCF’s Film program as an undergraduate. “It’s a lot of hard work, and it’s very much dependent on the student’s own tenacity. But I probably wouldn’t be making a feature right now if I weren’t in this program.”

Gay has student volunteers and professionals helping on the set of his film, which follows several weeks in the life of a young couple expecting their first child. The project, he said, was inspired by his own experiences as a “young married guy” and thoughts about how to balance a successful career and future family.

Twenty-two students have enrolled in UCF’s graduate film program since it began in mid-2005. In addition to making a feature-length film, students are required to take classes and conduct independent research in the theory and practices of storytelling, managing and selling their work and achieving artistic goals.

Students also take classes in entrepreneurship and business planning through the UCF College of Business Administration, where they’re taught how and where to raise funds for production.

For more information on UCF Film, go to www.film.ucf.edu.

Learn more about Gallina’s film at www.dinojgallina.com/RedTide.html and Gay’s film at www.abeautifulbelly.com.
