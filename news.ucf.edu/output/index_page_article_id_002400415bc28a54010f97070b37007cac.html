Green Roof Conserves Energy While Reducing Runoff, Beautifying Student Union

By Sam Gardner Jan. 5, 2007

Photo: Sam Gardner

The green roof on the UCF Student Union expansion is covered with plants ranging from dune sunflowers to bougainvillea, and three-fourths of the vegetation is native to Florida.

High atop the Student Union rests a garden that soaks in rainwater, beautifies UCFs campus and saves energy costs. As part of a research study by the UCF Stormwater Management Academy and the state Department of Environmental Protection, the green roof that crowns the building provides a visual and environmental appeal.

Installed in spring 2005, the green roof coats half of the 3,200-square-foot expansion added to the Student Union in 2004. The roof is covered with plants ranging from dune sunflowers to bougainvillea, and three-fourths of the vegetation is native to Florida.

Funded through a $340,000 grant from the state Department of Environmental Protection, the project should also extend the life of the roof and reduce storm-water runoff and pollution.

The green roof is also projected to cut energy costs in the Student Unions expansion area. Through plants and a layer of soil, green roofs reduce energy costs by keeping the roof warmer during winter and cooler during the summer. 

Youre protecting the building itself from the wavering temperature changes outside, said Martin Quigley, director of the UCF Arboretum.

Quigley and Martin Wanielista, director of the Stormwater Management Academy, report that, at almost two years old, the green roof has responded well to all weather conditions and is exceeding expectations.

Green roofs are common in cities such as Toronto, Seattle and Chicago, but there are only a few in Florida. The study on the Student Union is only the second research project of the Department of Environmental Protection, and UCF is the first university in the state to install a successful green roof.

UCF hopes to set a trend for other universities and businesses to take on environmentally responsible practices such as green roofs, which create both economic and social benefits while protecting valued natural resources.

Another green roof installed by the Stormwater Management Academy will be seen by thousands of delegates to the International Builders Show in February at the Orange County Convention Center. One of the shows features will be the New American Home in the Lake Eola historic district, and that home includes small green roof areas on the roof and on overhangs by windows.

The Student Union green roof requires little maintenance and typically is irrigated with water collected from cisterns attached to the Student Union.

On the Student Union green roof, the maximum temperature during an average day in summer 2005 was 91 degrees Fahrenheit, compared with 130 degrees on the conventional roof surface, according to an analysis by Jeff Sonne, a senior research engineer with UCFs Florida Solar Energy Center.

During January and February 2006, when the outside air temperature dropped below 55 degrees, the average surface temperature of the green roof was 60 degrees. That was 11 degrees warmer than the average temperature of the adjacent conventional roof.

While the green roof clearly reduces heating and cooling expenses, pinpointing the amount of savings is difficult because heating, ventilation and air conditioning expenses cannot easily be separated for sections of the addition covered by the two types of roofs. Also, the conventional Student Union roof on the addition is well insulated and light-colored; savings would be greater for an older roof with a darker color, Sonne said.

The green roof on top of the Student Union can be seen from the third-floor student lounge and also hanging over the edge of the building, but it is not open to visitors.

The students who have seen it think its cool, Quigley said.

If the experiment continues to go well and the community embraces the idea, the university will consider installing green roofs on other buildings and creating one that allows public access.
