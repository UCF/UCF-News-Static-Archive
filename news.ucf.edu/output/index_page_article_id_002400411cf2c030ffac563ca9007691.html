UCF Students Can Access Health Information, Advice on 24/7 WebMed Site

By Chad Binette Nov. 21, 2004

Photo: Jerry Klein

Students can input symptoms into the 24/7 WebMed system, or they can search for information about illnesses or prescription drugs.

Instead of waiting in line to see a doctor, University of Central Florida students who, say, injure an ankle playing basketball, get bitten by ants or have sore throats can go online for immediate, free medical help.

When they log on to the 24/7 WebMed site, students can look up information on specific illnesses or allow the site to guide them through a series of questions about symptoms to find out what is ailing them. The site will recommend options such as going to the hospital immediately, scheduling an appointment with a doctor or, for minor injuries, taking actions to treat themselves.

Students have visited 24/7 WebMed more than 7,000 times since the site became available in the spring. That includes 579 visits in September, the first full month of the fall semester, and 650 visits in October.

We know this generation of students is accustomed to using the Web to find information of many kinds, said Dr. Michael Deichen, associate director of Clinical Services at the UCF Student Health Center. Its only natural for them to use the Web as a health resource.

Its also unusual for a university to offer students such a thorough, online health care resource. UCF, the only university using 24/7 WebMed, has a three-year contract with DSHI Systems of Rockledge, Fla., to provide students with free access to a system thats also used by the Department of Defense and some managed care providers.

In the pilot project, DSHI Systems and the UCF School of Nursing will gather research data on the most common health problems that students experience and students satisfaction with online health-care systems.

Deichen said parents and students appreciate 24/7 WebMed because it provides a safe, reliable and interactive assessment of health concerns. Students benefit from help in determining whether they should seek care on an emergency or non-emergency basis.

Deichen said another major advantage of 24/7 WebMed is that students may be more likely to seek information about embarrassing diseases or problems on the Web site than at a doctors office. The system also can alert medical authorities to an outbreak of a disease or bioterrorism if they notice a flurry of requests with certain symptoms.

Students access 24/7 Web Med through www.my.ucf.edu, a university Web portal that includes information on class schedules, university events, financial aid and other topics. Many students find the advice or information they need online and do not need to follow up with a doctor. In October, only 20 of the 650 visits to 24/7 WebMed led to follow-up visits to the Student Health Center.

When students log on to 24/7 WebMed, they first see a disclaimer informing them that the site is not a substitute for physical evaluation and treatment and that they should seek immediate medical attention for emergencies. Students then indicate whether they are male or female. An image of a body then shows up on the screen, and the students click on a body part where they are experiencing pain. The computer then asks a series of yes-or-no questions to determine the problem and how it should be treated.

Students also can search through 24/7 WebMeds large database of information about diseases, drugs, poisons and other health-related issues.

Senior Janae Corrado, who has used 24/7 WebMed four times when she had either a sore throat or an insect bite, said she recommends the system to other students.

Its so convenient to just type in your symptoms and get an idea of what you need to do and how to treat it, she said. I dont have to take time out between classes and (rowing) practice and everything else going on. I can find out on my own.
