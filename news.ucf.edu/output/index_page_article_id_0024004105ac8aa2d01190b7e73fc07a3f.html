MTV's Pak: 'Real Test' for Young Voters Comes After Election

By Chad Binette April 1, 2008

Photo: Armando Solares/Solares Photography Inc.

A seven-year veteran at MTV, Pak discussed her job and the 2008 presidential election Monday at a UCF political symposium.

With the “YouTube election” giving them unprecedented access to political candidates, young voters are getting a lot of attention this year.

But for MTV news reporter SuChin Pak, the “real test” of the power of young voters will come a year from now, after the next president has taken office.

“Will we continue to be as engaged and excited about politics?” she asked a University of Central Florida audience. “Will we hold the next president accountable?

Pak addressed a crowd of nearly 500 -- mostly young people -- Monday evening at the Lou Frey Institute of Politics and Government symposium on “The Road to the White House: Tough Choices.”

In prior campaigns, “young people did not suffer from a lack of interest. They suffered from a lack of access,” Pak said.

This year, however, candidates are reaching out to youth through Facebook, MySpace and YouTube. A debate that eventually aired on MTV first was shown on MySpace.

A seven-year veteran at MTV, Pak reported for the Choose or Lose campaign in 2004. The series featured live election-night reports from Washington, D.C., analysis of youth voting patterns in swing states, coverage of protests and reports from the Republican and Democratic national conventions. This year, instead of sending Pak on the road, MTV found a high school or college student in each state to report on campaign news there.

Pak said that as an MTV reporter, she has covered the rise and fall of boy bands, “Diddy’s many name changes,” Grammy Awards and the Video Music Awards. But she said she enjoys covering the more serious news for the network, even though it can be a challenge to try to present political coverage in a way that interests young people.

Noting that she tries to “put a face that looks like you on every issue that we talk about,” Pak showed a short clip from her documentary, “My Life Translated.” The episode covered how Americans define patriotism and loyalty to their country today and what it means to be a good citizen. It featured a Lebanese student who signed up to join the U.S. Army, which concerned his parents because their main loyalty remained with Lebanon.

Prior to Pak’s speech, several UCF professors and other experts discussed a variety of issues related to the presidential election. Speakers included Lew Oliver, executive director of the Orange County Republican Party; Ulric Haynes Jr. a former ambassador to Algeria; and Stephen Sloan, a UCF professor and terrorism expert. Other participants included Paolo Spadoni, an expert on Cuban politics with the Rollins College Department of Political Science and Carlos M. Colombo, a UCF alumnus and immigration attorney.

The Lou Frey Institute of Politics and Government is a nonpartisan organization housed in the Department of Political Science at the University of Central Florida. The institute promotes civic education for college and high school students, members of the Central Florida community and the general public. Institute programs encourage informed discussion of issues from diverse viewpoints, stimulate participation in the political process and support research on politics and policy.

Co-sponsors of the symposium included the UCF Department of Political Science and Global Perspectives Office and the law firm of Lowndes, Drosdick, Doster, Kantor & Reed.

For more information, go to www.loufrey.org.
