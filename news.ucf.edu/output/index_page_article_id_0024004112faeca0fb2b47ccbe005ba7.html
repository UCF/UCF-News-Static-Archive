Media Coverage of Civil Rights Forums

By Chad Binette March 11, 2004

Related Contacts and Links:

  • Keisha Biggs, program coordinator for UCF

    • 407-823-093

  • Deanna Brown, public defenders office

    • 407-836-4788

  • Spencer Downing, UCF history professor

    • 407-823-018

A Pulitzer Prize-winning newspaper columnist and a former Florida state attorney who refused to press charges against the Rev. Martin Luther King Jr. for demonstrating in St. Augustine in 1964 are among the speakers at upcoming events commemorating the U.S. Supreme Courts Brown v. Board of Education decision. A panel featuring Chicago Tribune columnist Clarence Page, who has covered civil rights and urban affairs since the 1960s, will examine media coverage of civil rights Monday, March 15, at the University of Central Florida. "The Press and Civil Rights: Covering the Movement, Then and Now," which is free and open to the public, will be held from 10 a.m. to noon in the Pegasus Ballroom of the UCF Student Union. Page will be joined on the panel by Moses Newson, a journalist who covered desegregation throughout the South and later served as executive editor of the Afro-American newspapers in Baltimore, and Eugene Patterson, a former editor of the Atlanta Constitution and St. Petersburg Times whose editorials supporting civil rights won a Pulitzer Prize in 1967. Keith Woods, a Poynter Institute for Media Studies faculty member who is an expert on news coverage of race relations, will serve as the moderator. The Orlando Sentinel is sponsoring the panel and is presenting several professional development workshops for journalists and journalism students on improving news coverage of race relations and diversity. The workshops will be from 1 to 4:30 p.m. in the Key West Ballroom of the Student Union. Advance registration is required. For more information, call David Damron at the Orlando Sentinel at 407-420-5311. Mondays events are part of a series commemorating the 50th anniversary of the U.S. Supreme Courts Brown v. Board of Education decision, which eventually led to the integration of schools. UCF, the Orange County Regional History Center and the New Covenant Baptist Church of Orlando are among the venues hosting Brown v. Board events. Former State Attorney Dan Warren, who was the special counsel assigned by the governor to handle the 1964 racial crisis in St. Augustine, will speak Thursday, March 18, at a "Civil Rights Heroes" forum at the Orange County Regional History Center, 65 E. Central Blvd. Organized by 9th Judicial Circuit Public Defender Robert Wesleys office, the event begins at 5:30 p.m. March 18 and continues from 10 a.m. to 5 p.m. on March 19. Other speakers in the public defenders Gideon Celebration 2004 include Rufus Brooks, who was principal of Eccleston Elementary School when Orange County schools were first integrated; Bishop Holifield, former general counsel of Florida A&M University; and Richard Lapchick, director of UCFs Institute for Diversity and Ethics in Sport. The Gideon Celebration is an annual event held in honor of the anniversary of the U.S. Supreme Courts 1963 ruling in Gideon v. Wainwright that states must provide free counsel to poor people charged with felonies. All Gideon Celebration activities are open to the public, and most are free. There is a $10 fee to attend the luncheon featuring Lapchick at noon Friday, March 19. Go to www.pd. circuit9.org for a full schedule of events. Reservations for the luncheon and other presentations should be made by calling 407-836-2199 or sending an e-mail to pubdef@circuit9.org. In addition to "The Press and Civil Rights" and "Civil Rights Heroes," the following Brown v. Board events are scheduled through the end of March. Check www.brownblackandwhite.org for updates and a full schedule of events. -- Teachers who worked in Central Florida schools when they were first integrated and members of three families that joined in a 1962 lawsuit filed by parent John P. Ellis against Orange County schools to get his daughter admitted to a then-segregated school will discuss their experiences from 6 to 8 p.m. March 17 at the WellsBuilt Museum of African American History, 511 W. South St., Orlando. Free and open to the public. Call 407-245-7535 for reservations. William E. Leuchtenberg, professor emeritus of history at the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill, will discuss the influence of President Harry Truman and his administration on the Brown v. Board decision. 7 to 9 p.m. March 24, Pegasus Ballroom, UCF Student Union. Free and open to the public.
