Woodward, Lawmakers Reflect on Watergate at UCF Symposium

By Chad Binette March 29, 2004

Photo: Jerry Klein

Photos from the era set the tone at the Watergate panel.

President Gerald Fords pardon of Richard Nixon in the wake of the Watergate scandal was an act of statesmanship, compassion and political self-sacrifice, investigative reporter Bob Woodward told a UCF audience Monday night.

Woodward, speaking at a Watergate symposium organized by the Lou Frey Institute of Politics & Government, said that when he first learned of the pardon in 1974, he thought the motives werent so pure and that the pardon was just another deal struck as part of Watergate politics.

Woodward, now an assistant managing editor at The Washington Post, said he changed his mind six or seven years ago after he interviewed the former president a few times. He said he believed Fords assertions that the country could not stand to have the scandal linger for another few years while Nixon went on trial and possibly to jail and that Nixon was so despondent that he might have taken his own life had he not been pardoned.

I think you can argue that the pardon cost him the 1976 election, Woodward said.

Woodwards speech to a standing-room-only crowd at the UCF Student Union concluded the first of two days of a symposium co-hosted by the UCF Political Science Department.

Woodward spoke mostly about Watergate. He said it didnt even take much curiosity to pursue the story because one shocker after another kept coming out of their investigations.

Asked if Americans will ever know the identity of Deep Throat, one of Woodward and Carl Bernsteins main sources for the Watergate stories, Woodward said its a fascinating human story that will be told but added that the man doesnt want his name revealed as long as he is alive.

Discussions on Monday morning featured Frey; the Rev. John Taylor, director of the Richard Nixon Library & Birthplace; and two members of the House Judiciary Committee that voted to impeach Nixon. Students from UCF and Freedom and University high schools discussed why youths should care about Watergate even though the scandal unfolded 30 years ago.

Frey, a Republican who represented Central Florida in Congress from 1969 to 1979, told a standing-room-only crowd that Richard Nixon broke my heart and that Watergate is one of the dumbest acts in our nations history.

Frey, who was one of Nixons five youth chairmen for the 1972 campaign, said he admired the presidents efforts to benefit cancer research, clean air and the United States relationship with China and felt like a piece of me broke when he heard the presidential tapes and realized Nixon had tried to cover up Watergate.

Frey ended up voting for impeachment. He said he still cannot figure out the motivations for the Watergate break-in. Nixon won 49 of 50 states, and it was clear that there was no way George McGovern was going to defeat him, Frey said, adding that political parties dont keep valuable information at their headquarters anyway.

Watergate, above all, proved that our Constitution works, Frey said. We have a rule of law and no person, even our president, is above the law.

Frey also recalled the day in June 1973 when Nixon came to UCF to speak at a commencement ceremony. Typically, congressmen flock to Air Force One when a president is in town, but Frey said there wasnt anybody on that plane but me. No one wanted to be close to the president.

Taylor, the director of the Nixon library, was close to the president several years after Watergate. Taylor joined Nixons staff in 1980 and became his chief aide in 1984.

Taylor linked the Vietnam War with Watergate, saying that he wonders what would have happened had the scandal not weakened the president so much that he couldnt keep the promises he made to continue to support South Vietnam.

 

Nixon inherited more than 500,000 troops in Vietnam and a U.S. population that was deeply divided on the wars merits when he took office in January 1969, Taylor said.

Noting that the first word students who visit the Nixon library associate with Nixon is almost always Watergate, Taylor said youths should at least be taught to think about Nixon as a wartime president who felt many of the same pressures that President Bush experiences today with regard to the soldiers in Iraq.

In comments videotaped in Japan, where he now is the U.S. ambassador, former U.S. Sen. Howard Baker Jr. (R-Tenn., 1967-1985), said Watergate is arguably the most significant political event in the last 30 years.

To me, the real lessons of Watergate are to face up to the facts, assume everything will be made public and, most of all, assume cover-ups never work, he added.

Elizabeth Holtzman, a New York Democrat, and Tom Railsback, an Illinois Republican, served together on the House Judiciary Committee that voted to impeach Nixon.

Holtzman stressed that she believes the impeachment process was fair and praised the courage of the Republicans and southern Democrats who took a political risk in supporting the impeachment.

Railsback said discussions about hush money on the presidential tapes brought him to reluctantly support impeachment after he initially did not believe the president was involved. He said he was the first Republican panel member to cast a yes vote on an impeachment count.

I was one sad guy, he said.
