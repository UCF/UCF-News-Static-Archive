WUCF-89.9 FM Begins Streaming Secondary Digital Channel Online

By Kayonne Riley June 2, 2007

Related Contacts and Links:

  • Kayonne Riley

    • 407-823-5162

  • WUCF-FM

  • Print this Page

WUCF-FM, a listener-supported radio station licensed to the University of Central Florida, began streaming the broadcasts of a new secondary digital channel, 89.9 -- HD2, this week at www.wucf.org.

WUCF launched the new channel, which broadcasts public radio news and talk programs, on May 5. Listeners with high-definition radio receivers can tune in to 89.9 -- HD2 for broadcasts of National Public Radio, Public Radio International and BBC news and talk programs. They can tune in to 89.9 -- HD1 for jazz and other programming that airs on the main WUCF channel.

WUCF has been broadcasting to Central Florida listeners since 1979. The station continues a public radio tradition of community cultural outreach with a program schedule of straight-ahead jazz, music that is often referred to as AmericaÂ’s classical music. National Public Radio news and cultural programs such as "Jazz Profiles with Nancy Wilson," "Jazz from Lincoln Center with Wynton Marsalis" and the seasonal "Metropolitan Opera" broadcasts highlight WUCF's weekly schedule.

In March 2005, 89.9 FM became one of the first local radio stations to begin broadcasting in HD digital. The transition to digital broadcasting was made possible by a Corporation for Public Broadcasting digital conversion grant that funded a transmitter and equipment to broadcast via the newly licensed iBiquity HD digital technology.

By broadcasting programs digitally, HD Radio technology enables AM and FM radio stations to take a technological leap from the analog broadcasts of the past. Listeners tuning in on an HD radio will experience CD-quality sound and crystal-clear reception with no audio distortion. Additionally, second and third channels can be broadcast over the same frequency, allowing stations to run more than one program format. The secondary channels are required to be commercial-free, meaning that listeners will receive uninterrupted music, talk, news or sports programs.

Currently, stations broadcasting in HD Radio are operating in a hybrid mode of both analog and digital in order to reach both types of receivers. Eventually, as analog receivers are replaced by digital tuners, broadcasters may be able to turn off their analog signals and use that spectrum for more digital program channels or data applications.

HD Radio is a digital radio standard developed by iBiquity Digital and recognized by the U.S. Federal Communications Commission. It works the same way that traditional analog transmits, except that the audio is digitally formatted and transmitted as a continuous digital data stream in addition to the analog waveform signal. Radio stations send out the analog and digital signals on the same broadcast frequency, along with the signals for text data that allow listeners with HD radio receivers to get messages such as the artistsÂ’ names, song titles, traffic information and other items of interest.

For more information and programming schedules, go to www.wucf.org.
