Sanford Exhibit Pays Tribute to Navy's First Black Pilot

By Christine Dellert April 26, 2010

Photo: Official U.S. Navy Photograph

Ensign Jesse L. Brown, the first African-American naval aviator, in the cockpit of a Corsair fighter.

Jesse Leroy Brown was the U.S. Navy’s first African-American aviator, operating from the flight deck of an aircraft carrier during the Korean War.

After his plane was shot down behind enemy lines on Dec. 4, 1950, his squadron mate and wingman Lt. Thomas Hudner heroically tried but ultimately failed to save him. Brown’s body was never recovered.

Brown’s life, death and legacy are the focus of a new touring exhibit at the Orlando Sanford International Airport that runs through June 30.

The public is invited to the airport Saturday, May 1, when retired Navy Capt. Hudner will be visiting the exhibit for the first time. A special reception for Hudner will be held at 2 p.m. Saturday in the airport’s Vigilante Room, 1200 Red Cleveland Blvd. in Sanford.

Hudner also will unveil Brown’s portrait by African-American Sanford artist Carl “Raphael” Purdy.

Purdy’s other work and his model of a Corsair plane are centerpieces of the exhibit, entitled “A Pilot Lights the Way: A Tribute to Jesse Leroy Brown and Blacks in Aviation.”

Later this summer, the exhibit will travel to military bases, museums and other venues in celebration of 100 years of naval aviation.

President Harry Truman awarded Hudner the first Korean War Medal of Honor for his celebrated military accomplishments and courageous attempt to save Brown’s life.

When asked why he did it, Hudner replied, “I was trained that you never leave a man down, besides that, Jesse was my friend and squadron mate. We all felt that way.”

In 1972, the Navy commissioned the USS Jesse L. Brown, the first naval warship named for an African-American naval hero.  

This exhibit debuted in September at the University of Central Florida Library. It is accompanied by a documentary film, “Jesse Leroy Brown: First African American Naval Aviator.” The film was produced with the help of Defense International Service, the UCF College of Arts and Humanities and UCF Radio/TV Professor Anthony Major, director of the Zora Neale Hurston Institute for Documentary Studies. Alzo Reddick, director of UCF’s Soldiers to Scholars program; and Vice President for University Relations Dan Holsenbeck helped with the production.

The film is available online at http://zora.cah.ucf.edu.

The exhibit’s curator is local African-American poet, author and historian Valada Parker Flewellyn.

The exhibit also has been displayed at Oakmonte Village in Lake Mary, the Warbird Adventures museum in Kissimmee and the Florida Institute of Technology’s College of Aeronautics.

The exhibit’s next stop is the Naval Aviation Museum in Pensacola in July. It will ultimately become a permanent display at the museum in 2012.

Contributions from EYESEEIMAGES, UCF’s Zora Neale Hurston Institute of Documentary Studies, the Naval Aviation Museum, members of Jesse L. Brown’s squadron and other generous supporters made this exhibit possible.
