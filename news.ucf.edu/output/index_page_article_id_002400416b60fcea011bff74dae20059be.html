UCF Among Most Sought-After, Selective Medical Schools in Country

By Zenaida Gonzalez Kotala Aug. 26, 2008

3-D rendering of the College of Medicine at Lake Nona.

Before its doors open, the University of Central Florida’s College of Medicine is already among the most sought-after and selective medical schools in the country.

So far this year, more than 10 percent of those applying to medical schools nationwide have applied to UCF, based on data reported through the Association of American Medical Colleges on Aug. 22.

UCF has received more than 2,703 applications from 379 schools for its 40-student inaugural class in 2009. Among them are candidates from the top 25 universities nationwide as ranked by U.S. News and World Report’s 2009 “America’s Best Colleges” guide.

Admissions Director Robert Larkin said the quality of the applications has been as impressive as the quantity.

“I’ve been in this business for eight years, and I’m seeing a lot of things I’ve never seen before,” Larkin said. “For example, the MCAT scores and GPAs are among the highest I’ve ever seen.”

As of Aug. 22, UCF will accept one of every 67.5 applicants for the charter class. That’s more selective than Harvard (1 in 40.2), Yale (1 in 50.2) or Duke (1 in 53) were last year, according to data in the 2009-2010 Association of American Medical Colleges Medical School Admission Requirements guide.

UCF’s selectivity will increase as more applications are received. Applications will be accepted until Dec. 1. 

“We’re looking for good MCAT scores and GPAs, and we’re also looking for leaders who have demonstrated compassion for others,” said Dr. Deborah German, dean of the College of Medicine. “We want students who will make a difference in the lives of their patients and who will make the discoveries of this century, students with a good mind and a good heart for medicine." 

UCF is offering a one-of-a-kind scholarship program to its charter class. Each student accepted will receive $20,000 for tuition and $20,000 for living expenses and fees for all four years of the medical degree program.

The scholarships were funded completely by nearly $7 million in community donations from across the Orlando area. Although UCF is a new medical school, it’s already made history – the Association of American Medical Colleges says UCF will be the first medical school ever to provide full scholarships for four years to an entire class.

The College of Medicine will open its doors on Aug. 3, 2009, with an initial class of 40 students and eventually will produce about 120 medical graduates a year.

The four-year medical program will capitalize on UCF’s existing strengths in biomedical sciences, modeling and simulation, and optics and photonics. Other programs in materials science, psychology, hospitality, chemistry, film and digital media, and nursing will support the program.

The college will be located at the UCF Health Sciences Campus at Lake Nona, on 50 acres that will also include a medical library and other health sciences programs. The California-based Burnham Institute for Medical Research, a Veteran’s Administration hospital, Nemours Children’s Hospital and M.D. Anderson Cancer Research Institute will also call Lake Nona home. Together these entities are creating a medical city.
