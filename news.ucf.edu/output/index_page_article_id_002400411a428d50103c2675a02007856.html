College Sports Hiring Earns B-Minus Grade for Race, B-Plus for Gender

By UCF Staff June 2, 2005

Photo: UCF File Photo

The latest study issued by the UCF Institute for Diversity and Ethics in Sport graded college sports programs for racial and gender hiring practices.

Universities athletic programs improved their record of hiring women during the past year but lost ground on hiring practices by race, according to a report card issued Wednesday by Richard Lapchick, director of the University of Central Floridas Institute for Diversity and Ethics in Sport.

 

The 2004 Racial and Gender Report Card for College Sport, which reviewed head and assistant coaches, athletic directors and other athletic administrators, gave universities a B-minus for race and a B-plus for gender. College sports programs earned B grades in both categories in 2003.

 

Because there are more professional positions in college sports than in all of the professional sports combined, the prospects of expanding opportunities for women and people of color are greatest at the college level, the report said. An enormous number of professional positions exist at the NCAAs 1,028 active member institutions, including 117 schools in Division I-A, 118 in Division I-AA and 91 in Division I-AAA.  Division II has 281 active member institutions, and Division III has 421.

 

The report card for college sports was the final one that will be issued this year. Earlier report cards covered the National Basketball Association, Womens National Basketball Association, Major League Baseball, the National Football League and Major League Soccer.

 

The full report card on college sports can be viewed at www.ncasports.org/articles.htm.

 

When compared to grades for the professional sports leagues, college sports B-minus for race was next-to-last, ahead of only Major League Soccers C-plus. However, college sports B-plus for gender was better than all of the mens leagues and only trailed the WNBAs A for gender.

 

The college sports programs combined grade of B was third, behind the WNBAs A and NBAs B-plus.

 

College sports overall grades were boosted by As in the following categories: race for mens Division I basketball coaches, mens assistant coaches and student-athlete opportunities; and gender for NCAA headquarters, head coaches of all womens teams and womens Division I basketball teams, assistant coaches on womens teams, senior womens administrators and female student-athlete opportunities.

 

College sports overall grades were hurt by Fs in the following categories: race for Division I head football coaches; both race and gender for athletics directors, conference commissioners and presidents; and gender for faculty athletics representatives.

 

According to the report card, 58.2 percent of mens Division I basketball players in 2003-04 were African-American. Thats the highest percentage in more than a decade. Forty-six percent of Division I womens college basketball players were African-American, which is an all-time high percentage in that category.

 

Nearly one-fourth of all Division I mens basketball coaches were African-American, which also is an all-time high percentage. However, opportunities for African-Americans in other men's sports "remained poor," the report indicated.

 

Participation by Hispanic men in basketball, football and all Division I sports combined increased, while their participation in baseball decreased. The percentage of Hispanic women playing basketball, track and field, cross country and all Division I sports combined increased.

 

All of the Division I-A conference commissioners are white men. However, the NCAA made a major commitment to the issue of diversity by creating a new position for a vice president for diversity and inclusion, the report noted.

 

UCFs Institute for Diversity and Ethics in Sport publishes the Racial and Gender Report Cards to indicate areas of improvement, stagnation and regression in the racial and gender composition of professional and college sports personnel and to contribute to the improvement of integration in front office and college athletics department positions.   The Racial and Gender Report Card asks, Are we playing fair when it comes to sports?  Does everyone, regardless of race or gender, have a chance to make and run the team?
