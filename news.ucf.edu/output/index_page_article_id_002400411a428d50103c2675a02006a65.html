Magazine Names UCFs Peter Delfyett Outstanding Black Professional in Science

By Chad Binette July 18, 2005

Peter Delfyett

Full Photo

Related Contacts and Links:

  • UCF College of Optics and Photonics

  • Science Spectrum Magazine (Career Communications Group)

  • Print this Page

Peter Delfyett, a professor of optics, physics and electrical and computer engineering at the University of Central Florida, has been named an outstanding black professional in science by Science Spectrum magazine.

 

The magazine honored 68 top black, Asian American, Hispanic and American Indian scientists who have all made a significant, quantifiable, personal impact on the industry, while making contributions that have uplifted their communities and maintaining a powerful position of influence regarding public policy for minorities in science.

 

In addition to Delfyett, who specializes in laser and fiberoptics research, the Science Spectrum Trailblazers included the director of the National Institutes of Health, the president of Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute, a Cornell University dean and the New York state health commissioner.

 

Delfyett earned a similar honor last year from Science Spectrum. He and the other winners will be honored at a luncheon Sept. 16 at the Minorities in Research Science Conference in Baltimore.

 

It's a wonderful tribute to Peter to once again recognize him for his strong dedication to science, to his students and to his peers, said Eric Van Stryland, dean of the College of Optics and Photonics and director of the Center for Research and Education in Optics and Lasers and the Florida Photonics Center for Excellence. He inspires those around him to excel, and he infects them with the incredible enthusiasm he displays. It is wonderful to have him on our faculty.

 

Delfyett has taught at UCF for 11 years. In 2001, he won the Pegasus Professor Award, the highest honor the university gives to a faculty member who demonstrates excellence in teaching, research and service.

 

Delfyett and his team of doctoral students and scientists are trying to increase the speed at which data can be transmitted through semiconductor chips like the ones found in computers. Improving that speed would help computers download information from the Internet much faster and make computer networks work faster.

 

Delfyett visits UCFs Burnett Honors College and area middle and high schools occasionally to encourage students to pursue careers in science and engineering. He is a member of the Orlando Science Centers advisory board.
