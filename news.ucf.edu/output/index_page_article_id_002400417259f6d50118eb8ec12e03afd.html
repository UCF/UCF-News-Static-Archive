UCF on YouTube: Teaching Students About Meningitis

By Christine Dellert March 27, 2008

Photo: Courtesy of UCF Health Services

Anyone with questions about meningitis or the vaccination can visit or call the UCF Health Center.

Full Photo

  • Christine Dellert, UCF News & Information

With the Florida Board of Governors approving new rules regarding meningitis vaccinations today, the University of Central Florida is educating students in new ways about the dangers of meningitis.

This week, UCF released a video about the symptoms and methods of preventing the disease. The video appears on UCF’s YouTube site, http://youtube.com/ucf, and the UCF News site, http://news.ucf.edu. Various UCF health and student services Web sites will add links to the video in the coming weeks.

Since January, two University of Central Florida students have been recovering from unrelated cases of meningitis or the bacteria that can cause it. The UCF community extends its strongest wishes for complete recoveries to those students.

Meningococcal meningitis affects nearly 3,000 Americans annually, with a greater chance of occurring on college campuses and housing where young adults live in close quarters. The potentially life-threatening disease can spread rapidly, infecting the membrane that surrounds the brain and spinal cord.

UCF currently requires all students living in on-campus residence halls to be vaccinated for meningitis or present a waiver acknowledging they understand the risk but prefer not to receive the vaccine.

Today, the Florida Board of Governors tightened vaccination policies, requiring all new incoming students at each state university to be vaccinated or produce a waiver opting out. This new policy will go into effect July 1.

UCF doctors and health administrators encourage all students to be vaccinated for meningitis. Vaccinations are available by appointment at the UCF Health Center at a charge of $103, a reduced rate from the usual charge.

Classic symptoms of meningitis include high fever, confusion and neck stiffness. Those symptoms can develop over several hours or over one to two days. Other symptoms may include headache, nausea, vomiting, sensitivity to light and seizures.

Anyone who has questions or concerns regarding meningococcal meningitis can call the UCF Health Center at 407-823-2701 or visit www.hs.ucf.edu.

To learn more about meningitis and available vaccinations, visit the National Meningitis Association at www.nmaus.org, the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention at www.cdc.gov or the American College Health Association at www.acha.org.
