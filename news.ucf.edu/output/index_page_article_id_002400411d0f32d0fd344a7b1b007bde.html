Video Game Created by Four UCF Students Wins National Competition

By Chad Binette July 2, 2004

Related Contacts and Links:

  • Jason Schwartz

    • 407-617-3224

  • Hidden Agenda contest

  • Print this Page

While theyre creating body armor and battling each other in a new video game developed by four University of Central Florida students, middle schoolers might not even realize that theyre learning about chemical elements.

 

This stealth approach to educating young video gamers earned Jason Schwartz, David Nikdel, Alex Hamrick and Joshua Sachs the top prize of $25,000 this week in the national Hidden Agenda video-game development contest. The UCF team beat out four other finalists with "MeChem," a game built from scratch in six months.

 

We didnt want any of the materials to directly preach to the students that this is an element and what it does, because that is what really turns students off, Schwartz said. Young kids tend to remember information about video games as long as it helps them win. Why cant that information be useful?

 

Students who play MeChem learn about the properties of elements, such as when they melt and how they react to each other, during battles with the armor and weapons theyve created.

 

The contest, hosted by the University of Texas at Austin, was developed by the Liemandt Foundation, a nonprofit group that promotes education through technology. Judges, who included middle school students, rated games developed by five finalists for their entertainment and educational values. More information is available at www.hiddenagenda.com.

 

The students victory came as UCF prepares for the fall 2005 opening of the new Florida Interactive Entertainment Academy, which will teach students how to design and program video games. The academy, funded largely with a $4.2 million state grant, is a partnership between the UCF School of Film and Digital Media and Electronic Arts.

 

The four members of UCFs Team Whitestar are brothers in the Phi Gamma Delta fraternity. Schwartz, of Oviedo, is a graduate student studying modeling and simulation. Hamrick, of Tampa, graduated in the spring with bachelors degrees in math, statistics and economics. Nikdel, of Winter Haven, and Sachs, of Orlando, are computer science majors.
