UCFs First International Brain Conference Open to Scientists, Caregivers, Others

By Barb Abney Updated Dec. 15, 2006

Related Contacts and Links:

  • Barb Abney, Office of Research

    • 407-823-5139

  • Kiminobu Sugaya, Biomedical Sciences

    • 407-823-1524

  • International Brain Conference

  • Conference brochure

  • Print this Page

Scientists, caregivers and other community members who have experienced the devastating toll of Alzheimers and other neurological disorders are invited to attend the University of Central Floridas first International Brain Conference in January.

The conference from Jan. 19 to 21 at UCFs Rosen College of Hospitality Management will explore the latest strategies and research in the treatment of diseases of the aging brain, including Alzheimers disease.

Kiminobu Sugaya, a UCF professor of biomolecular sciences and a pioneer in the use of adult stem cells to ultimately treat debilitating brain disorders, is hosting the three-day event. Florida Hospital, Orlando Regional Healthcare System, Orange County government, the Florida High Tech Corridor Council, the Alzheimers Association and the Alzheimers Resource Center are joining UCF as sponsors.

Conference speakers will address the pathology of the aging brain, the evaluation of neurological disorders, treatment technologies,  research topics and the future direction of research.

Keynote speaker Ezio Giacobini, a professor of geriatrics at the University of Geneva Medical School in Switzerland and a professor emeritus at the Southern Illinois University School of Medicine, has written and published extensively on the role of the cholinesterase inhibitors, which have shown promise in recent medications developed to maintain cognitive function in Alzheimers patients.

Deborah German, the dean of UCFs new College of Medicine, will speak during the conferences opening night activities.

We are excited to bring the worlds leading brain researchers to Orlando and to share state-of-the-art research with the UCF and Central Florida communities, Sugaya said.  Since this will be the first international medical conference at UCF, we also thought it was appropriate to ask Dr. German to join UCF Provost Terry Hickey in making opening night remarks.

Special rates for the conference are available for UCF faculty and students, hospital residents and allied health professionals. Early registration, which includes a $50 discount, concludes on Jan. 10.

The conference has been certified for up to nine continuing medical education credits.
For more information, go to www.brainconference.org or view the conference brochure at www.research.ucf.edu/pub/IBCatUCF.pdf.
 
Conference fees include meals prepared at the Rosen College of Hospitality Management.
