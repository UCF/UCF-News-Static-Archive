UCF Economic Forecast Indicates 2007 Will Not Be a Year of Doom and Gloom

By Zenaida Gonzalez Kotala Jan. 31, 2007

Photo: UCF News File Photo

Sean Snaith

Call 2007 the year of the slow-moving roller coaster with few thrills or steep drops.

Rising interest rates, slowing profit growth and increased economic uncertainty will result in a slowing down of investment spending, said Sean Snaith, director of the Thomas D. Garafolo Institute for Economic Competitiveness and an economics professor at the University of Central Florida.

But all of those ingredients should not have a profoundly negative impact on the economy, Snaith said in his 2007 National Economic Forecast, which was released today.

In 2007 we will see a slowdown but not a downturn," Snaith said. "The current 'kevlar expansion' has proven itself to be virtually immune to energy shocks, hurricanes, the cooling housing market and two years worth of interest rate hikes, and by 2008 we anticipate robust growth to resume. The housing bubble is destined for an appearance on the TV show MythBusters.

Among the highlights from the report:

*The Federal Reserve is unlikely to cut short-term interest rates.

*Economic growth in the first half of the year will be tepid, but look for the economy to accelerate in the second half of the year.

*The U.S. economy should slow through the second quarter of 2007 before accelerating back to 3 percent growth in 2008.

*2007 will bring with it a double-digit decline in housing starts?, as developers cut back on projects in the wake of high inventories and moderated demand. In 2009, the housing sector should be out of the doldrums, and residential fixed investment should have a sustainable growth rate of 3.9 percent.

*Mortgage rates will slowly creep to 7.14 percent in 2009, while the excess supply of homes in many markets will push prices downward.

*Less tax revenue is expected, which will result in a deficit that worsens in 2007 before improving in 2008-2009.

*Unemployment rates will end their three-year-slide and rise slightly in 2007, but will remain below 5 percent through 2009. Payroll job growth will slow to 1 percent in 2007 before recovering to 1.7 percent in 2009.

*The dollar will continue to depreciate against major trading partners through 2008. The trade deficit will experience a modest improvement over the same period.

Snaith said none of the presidential candidates are talking about the most serious threats to the economy -- the spiraling costs of Social Security, Medicare and Medicaid.

If Baby Boomers do not accept higher retirement ages and cuts in benefits, with elimination of benefits for some, they are screwing over their children and grandchildren, Snaith said.

To access the entire report, visit: http://www.bus.ucf.edu/hitec/Forecasts.htm.

Snaith is an expert in economics, forecasting, market sizing and economic analyses.

He is a member of several national forecasting panels, including the Western Blue Chip Economic Forecast panel, the National Association of Business Economics Quarterly Outlook Survey Panel, the Federal Reserve Bank of Philadelphias Survey of Professional Forecasters, the Bloomberg U.S. Economic Indicator Survey and the USA Today Economic Survey Panel.

He also compiles quarterly forecasts for the state and 12 metropolitan areas several times a year. Those areas are: Daytona, Fort Myers, Gainesville, Ocala, Lakeland, Palm Bay-Melbourne, Pensacola, Miami, Jacksonville, Tallahassee, Tampa and Orlando.

The UCF Institute for Economic Competitiveness mission is to expand public understanding of the economy by convening business leaders, scholars, policy makers, civic groups and media to discuss critical issues.
