Woman Whose Fathers Lawsuit Led to the Integration of Mexican-American Students to Visit UCF

By Matthew Dunn Oct. 18, 2004

Related Contacts and Links:

  • Luis Martinez-Fernandez

    • 407-823-1174

  • UCF Latin American, Caribbean and Latino Studies Program

  • Mendez v. Westminster

  • Print this Page

Sylvia Méndez, a Mexican-American whose father filed a lawsuit 59 years ago challenging the segregation of California schools, will speak and show a short documentary film about her experiences Wednesday, Oct. 20, at the University of Central Florida.

 

Méndez v. Westminster: For All The Children/Para Todos Los Niños, will be shown at 3 p.m. in the Nicholson School of Communications auditorium, Room 101. After the screening, Méndez will discuss her experiences and the legacy of her fathers lawsuit. The event is free and open to the public.

 

Before the U.S. Supreme Court ruling in Brown v. Board of Education, which led to the integration of African-Americans in public schools, the little-known case of Méndez v. Westminster won access for Mexican-Americans to white schools. In 1944, 9-year-old Sylvia Méndez was denied entrance to the local white elementary school. A year later, her father and other parents filed a lawsuit against four Orange County, Calif., school districts on behalf of about 5,000 Mexican-American schoolchildren. 

 

Méndez testified during the trial to show that Mexican-American students had the same capacity for learning as their white counterparts. The lawsuit succeeded, as a state and federal court ruled in favor of the Mexican families.

 

The Méndez case, which concluded in 1947, featured two participants who played major roles in the 1954 Brown v. Board of Education case. Thurgood Marshall advised the Méndez familys lawyers, and then-California Gov. Earl Warren signed the desegregation order following the Méndez ruling. In 1954, Marshall was the lead attorney for the Brown v. Board case, and Warren was the U.S. Supreme Courts chief justice.

 

Méndezs presentation is co-sponsored by the UCF Latin American, Caribbean and Latino Studies program; the Office of Undergraduate Studies; the Burnett Honors College; the Womens Studies Program; the Hispanic American Students Association; Lambda Theta Alpha and the Office of Diversity Initiatives.

 

For more information on the Méndez case, go to www.mendezvwestminster.com. For more information on the UCF Latin American, Caribbean and Latino Studies program, go to www.cas.ucf.edu/LACLS.
