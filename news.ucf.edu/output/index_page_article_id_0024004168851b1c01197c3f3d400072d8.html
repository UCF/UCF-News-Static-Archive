UCF Physics Professor Brings World Expertise to Orlando to Campus

By Zenaida Gonzalez Kotala April 23, 2008

Photo: Jacque Brund

Richard Klemm is a research professor in physics.

Full Photo

  • Zenaida Gonzalez Kotala, UCF News and Information

    • 407-823-6120

  • Print this Page

UCF Professor Richard Klemm is one of the pioneer scientists in the field of layered superconductivity. During his 30-plus-year career, he has written several articles that are among the most cited in his field. He also has worked with two Nobel Prize winners and knows two others personally. 

Klemm’s latest scholarly work will appear in the April 1 edition of the journal Physical Review B. The findings of his research have potential applications in creating quantum computers and in developing highly sensitive magnetic or electrical field sensors, which could be used in communications and other industries.

Klemm will travel to Japan later this month to write a book on the history and physical properties of layered superconductors. He was invited to Tsukuba Science City so he could have access to some of the latest technology. The city is home to more than 60 research institutes, including the University of Tsukuba, the KEK high-energy research center, and the main research center of the Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency.

Klemm grew up in Eugene, Ore., and attended Stanford University during the Vietnam years. After a short stint at a company in Stanford’s Industrial Park, he attended graduate school at Harvard. He has conducted research around the globe, and the American Physical Society recently named him an outstanding referee because of his excellent work reviewing and editing scholarly physics papers from around the world.

Physics Chair Talat Rahman said UCF is lucky to have Klemm, who also teaches several advanced graduate courses. 

“His experience and breath of knowledge are surely benefiting our graduate students,” Rahman said. “He’s worked with some of the best in the field, and his ongoing research holds much promise for the future.”

From 1983 to 1985, Klemm worked with Robert Schrieffer, who shared the 1972 Nobel Prize in Physics for the Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer theory of superconductivity. A few years ago, Klemm worked with Alexei Abrikosov, who shared the prize in 2003 for his work on vortices in superconductors. Klemm’s work also has been cited several times by K. Alex Mueller, who shared the Nobel Prize in 1987 for discovering superconductivity in ceramic materials.

Klemm got into physics almost by accident. During his youth, he spent most of his summers working in his father’s organic chemistry lab at the University of Oregon. He always thought he would follow in his father’s footsteps.

But once he graduated from Stanford and went to work, his academic career took a turn. He was in the company’s lab “fooling around” with an idea he had read about in a German journal. He ran an experiment based on his hunch and, along with Stanford Professor T. H. Geballe and graduate student Frank DiSalvo, wrote about the results. The success of the experiment sparked the field of layered superconductors and launched the career of DiSalvo, who is now a professor at Cornell University.

“What I recognized was that one experiment might work in another area that no one had thought about before,” Klemm said. “Not bad for a kid who hadn’t yet been to grad school, eh?” 

Shortly after the experiment in 1970, he went back to school for a doctorate at Harvard University. Since then, he has been teaching, researching and traveling across the globe in an effort to expand the knowledge of this and other areas of physics. 

He has worked with scientists in Russia, Germany, Canada and Japan. His latest work is a joint article with physicist Dmitri V. Efremov in Germany. And he hopes to work with several Japanese scientists while at Tsukuba this summer.

“There’s so much more out there,” Klemm said. “There’s so much more exploring and discovering to do.”
