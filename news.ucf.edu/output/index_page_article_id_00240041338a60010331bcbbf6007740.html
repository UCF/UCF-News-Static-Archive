Orange County Review Confirms Findings of Stadium Traffic Study

By Tom Evelyn April 26, 2005

Photo: Jacque Brund

The Orange County Board of County Commissioners hears a presentation Tuesday of UCF's Campus Master Plan and proposed stadium.

A proposed on-campus football stadium at the University of Central Florida would have less than a 10 percent impact on area roads and public services, Orange County has concluded.

Senior Assistant County Attorney Joel Prinsell on Tuesday told the Board of County Commissioners during a presentation on UCFs Campus Master Plan that a county review confirmed the results of a traffic study commissioned by UCF. Seminole County has also concurred with the study, which shows that traffic to and from campus for a sold-out Saturday game would be about five-eights of that of a normal weekday.

An impact of less than 10 percent requires only a minor amendment to add the stadium, if it is approved, to the master plan, a state-mandated blueprint for the universitys growth and development.

UCFs Board of Trustees on April 14 endorsed the concept of the stadium and authorized spending up to $1 million of an anonymous donation to draft and negotiate finance and construction agreements. A formal vote by the board on whether to build the stadium will likely be scheduled in the next few weeks.

On Tuesday, UCF President John Hitt and Bill Merck, vice president for Administration and Finance, presented information on the master plan and proposed stadium to the County Commission during a discussion at its regular meeting.

Prinsell discussed the countys role in the master plan, saying it was limited to review of the plan, and residents Linda Dorian and Barry Savchuk shared their concerns about the stadiums proposed northern location on the campus.

Hitt told the commissioners that UCF has grown tremendously in size and quality and is pursuing projects such as a medical school and expanded regional campuses that will support and benefit from that success. In addition, the university and nearby Central Florida Research Park boost the area economy with a combined annual impact of about $3.3 billion, about $400 million of which comes from student spending.

One of UCFs greatest accomplishments, however, may be its more than 140,000 graduates. When universities do their jobs well, they transform lives, Hitt said.

Merck explained that while UCF is one of the largest universities in the country, its also one of the most ecologically sound. More than 600 of the campus 1,415 acres are in a conservation designation, and the campus now has a lower storm-water runoff rate than before it was developed.

Were making a huge effort  to manage our property properly, Merck said.

The master plan, which looks out 10 years and is updated every five years, supports the universitys academic and research missions and includes academic buildings, student housing, athletics facilities, environmental protection and other campus projects.

Construction of the proposed 45,000-seat stadium could begin this summer and be completed by fall 2006 at an estimated cost of $43 million. The stadium, which would host an average of six games a year, would be built and supported by ticket and concessions sales, donations, corporate sponsorships and advertising. No taxpayer money or tuition would be used.

The proposed stadium site in the athletics complex in the north end of campus contains the Arena and new student housing that will eventually be home to 2,000 students. A new alumni center is under construction nearby, and a convocation center is planned.

The Board of Trustees received feasibility and traffic studies and heard public comments on the proposed stadium at its past two meetings. University officials also have held a community forum and two open houses and have been meeting with homeowners groups and area chambers of commerce to discuss the proposed stadium.

UCF currently plays its home football games in the Citrus Bowl in downtown Orlando.

For more information on the proposed on-campus stadium, visit www.stadium.ucf.edu.
