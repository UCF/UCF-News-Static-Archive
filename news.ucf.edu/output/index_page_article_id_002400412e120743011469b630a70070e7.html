UCF Report Card Praises NFL for Progress in Racial Hiring Practices

By UCF Staff Sept. 26, 2007

Photo: Courtesy of Indianapolis Colts

Tony Dungy, coach of the Indianapolis Colts, became the first African-American coach to win a Super Bowl.

The National Football League is improving its racial hiring practices, according to the latest report card issued Wednesday by the University of Central Floridas Institute for Diversity and Ethics in Sport.

While UCF sports researchers kept the NFLs rating at a B-plus, they lauded the league for its high-profile and high-achieving African-American coaches during last years Super Bowl.

The report cited the Super Bowl match-up between African-American coaches Tony Dungy and Lovie Smith as the biggest breakthrough for the NFL this past year. Dungy, coach of the Indianapolis Colts, became the first African-American coach to win a Super Bowl.

NFL Commissioner Roger Goodell has now completed his first year, and the results augur well for the future of more opportunities for people of color in the NFL, the reports authors wrote.

Richard Lapchick, director of the Institute for Diversity and Ethics in Sport, wrote the report with student co-authors Boma Ekiyor and Horacio Ruiz, both of whom are enrolled in UCFs DeVos Sport Business Management Program.

Though the report describes African-American advances, it calls NFL teams hiring of women as poor, especially compared to the significant progress on race.

The percentage of women serving as team vice presidents and in other senior administration positions, however, did increase slightly in 2006 compared to 2005.

Amy Trask of the Oakland Raiders remained the only female president/chief executive officer of an NFL team in 2006.

Most of the report covers data for the 2006 NFL season. The percentage of African-American players in the NFL increased from 65.5 to 67 from 2005 to 2006. The percentage of assistant coaches who are African-American increased from 34 to 38 during that time.

The league has six African-American head coaches and five African-American general managers this season.

UCFs Institute for Diversity and Ethics in Sport publishes racial and gender report cards to indicate areas of improvement, stagnation and regression in professional and college sports personnel. The institute also strives to contribute to the improvement of integration in front office and college athletics department positions.

To view the full study on the NFL, go to http://www.ncasports.org/press_releases.htm.
