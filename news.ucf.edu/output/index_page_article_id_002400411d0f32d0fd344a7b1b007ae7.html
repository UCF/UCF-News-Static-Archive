Competitive State Legislative Elections Rare Due to Redistricting, UCF Study Finds

By Chad Binette July 9, 2004

Related Contacts and Links:

  • Scot Schraufnagel

    • 386-506-4007

  • Print this Page

Floridians can be sure that their votes will be among the most important in the presidential election. When it comes to state legislative seats, however, most Florida residents shouldnt expect to cast any meaningful votes.

 

About two-thirds of the legislators elected in 2002 ran with no opposition from the other major party, meaning they were virtually guaranteed victories a few months before the general election. Most of the races that pitted Democrats and Republicans against each other were decided by margins of more than 10 percentage points.

 

UCF political science professor Scot Schraufnagel concluded that a main reason why so few races are competitive is the fact that legislators draw their own districts. Legislators create many safe seats for candidates from their own party and pack voters from the other party into a smaller number of districts, leaving few districts where races are competitive.

 

There is a cause for concern when one considers the quality of democracy in Florida state politics, Schraufnagel said. Everything points to Florida being a state with highly competitive state legislative elections, but the reality is that less than 10 percent of Floridians are voting in a competitive state legislative race. More than 90 percent of their votes are meaningless.

 

While the 2004 general election is still four months away, Schraufnagel expects many legislative candidates will virtually lock up victories next week when no one from a major party qualifies to run against them. The deadline for candidates to qualify for legislative seats is Friday, July 16.

 

Based on voter history, Florida is a state where there should be plenty of competitive races, Schraufnagel said. Out of the 9.5 million Floridians registered to vote in May, Democrats outnumbered Republicans by only about 350,000. President Bush won the state by only 537 votes four years ago. And Floridians have elected a Republican governor and Cabinet members while sending two Democrats to the U.S. Senate.

 

When legislators redraw the boundaries of their districts every 10 years, however, they can group voters in ways that produce few competitive races. African-Americans, who mainly vote Democratic, are grouped in large numbers in fewer than 20 House districts.

 

The 2002 elections, the first after the latest redistricting by legislators, saw many more uncontested races than the average number of uncontested races from 1988 to 2002.

 

In 67 percent of the House races and 65 percent of the Senate races in the 2002 general election, candidates ran with no opposition from a Democrat or a Republican. Of the 40 House races in which candidates from the two major parties competed, only 12 were decided by less than 10 percentage points. Only three of the 14 Senate candidates who were opposed by a candidate from the other major party won by less than 10 percentage points.

 

Term limits, which began to force legislators to abandon their seats in 2000, have not helped to produce more competition in elections. Schraufnagel and UCF graduate student Nemanja Pintar found that races in 2000 and 2002 for seats that legislators were forced to vacate because of term limits have been decided by larger margins than other legislative races and are more likely to go uncontested.

 

These districts are monopolies, Schraufnagel said. A typical term-limited seat is one where party operatives go out and recruit a quality candidate and then pump extra money into the race. They create a barrier to entry for the other party and ward off any challengers.
