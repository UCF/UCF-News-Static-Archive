UCF, Scripps Sign Pact to Foster Research Cooperation

By Chad Binette and Tom Evelyn Updated 8 a.m. March 23, 2006

Photo: UCF File Rendering

If approved, the medical college would be based at the UCF Healthcare Campus at Lake Nona.

The Florida Board of Governors is scheduled to vote today whether to create a college of medicine at the University of Central Florida just days after UCF officials signed an agreement to foster collaborative photonics and biomedical research with the Scripps Research Institute.

The joint cooperation agreement signed Monday outlines many of the general terms applicable to future research involving UCF and Scripps Florida scientists, allowing researchers to approve short contracts outlining the particulars of their projects before they can collaborate. Partnerships could involve several UCF researchers at the College of Optics & Photonics and the Burnett College of Biomedical Sciences.

The Board of Governors, which oversees the state's 11 public universities, is scheduled to vote this afternoon on proposals for medical colleges at UCF and Florida International University. The board will meet at 1 p.m. at Florida State Universitys Turnbull Center for Professional Development, 555 W. Pensacola St., Tallahassee.

The college could be started in the fall, if approved by the Board of Governors and funded by the Legislature. The degree calls for a four-year curriculum that could begin clinical education in fall 2008 and produce about 120 medical graduates each year.

A UCF medical college and its medical degree program would capitalize on the universitys strengths in biomedical sciences, modeling and simulation, and optics and photonics while fulfilling the states need for more physicians.

The partnership with Scripps builds on many of those same strengths. UCF and Scripps share many common research interests, including working toward better therapies for cardiovascular disease, cancer and neurodegenerative diseases such as Alzheimers.

Scripps and UCF share in the state of Floridas mission to develop life-science research and business that will diversify and boost the states economy, said UCF Vice President for Research M.J. Soileau. This agreement will allow Scripps and UCF to more effectively compete as partners for the grants that support this mission.

UCF researchers also can provide technology in areas such as body imaging that can help Scripps drug discovery efforts.

Aristide Dogariu, an associate professor of optics who visited Scripps on Friday, has developed advanced body imaging technology that can help researchers determine the effects of drugs in mice. While conventional imaging relies on standard microscopes and detection schemes to monitor light indicators or optical tags that help researchers track the progress of medication, Dogarius technology allows researchers to do complex analyses of light interaction with tissue to quantify the effects of experimental drugs on cellular structures.

Scripps Florida, which is temporarily located in a building on Florida Atlantic Universitys campus in Jupiter, already has approved agreements with the University of Florida and FAU similar to the contract signed with UCF.

Just as Gov. Jeb Bush envisions Scripps as a linchpin of the states efforts to attract more biotechnology companies to Florida, UCFs proposed medical college would help Central Floridas economy and improve the chances of attracting life-science businesses to the region.

An economic impact study conducted by the Milken Institute and Arduin, Laffer & Moore Econometrics shows that an Orlando medical college alone could generate $1.4 billion in economic activity and more than 6,400 jobs. The impact could be as much as $6.4 billion and nearly 26,000 jobs if a life-science cluster of related research and business develops around the med school, similar to the clusters that have formed in other cities.

Two of the largest medical systems in the country, Florida Hospital and Orlando Regional Healthcare, would partner with UCF to establish 95 new medical residencies. The hospitals also would pay 40 percent of the salaries for 50 clinical faculty members and provide support staff and clinical and office space, all of which would reduce the cost to the state for operating the medical college.

Including state matching funds and the value of the Tavistock Groups pledge of 50 acres at Lake Nona, UCF has received $100.3 million of pledges for the medical college. The UCF Foundation announced a $1.5 million pledge Wednesday from the Dr. Phillips Institute in Orlando, and a $1 million donation from Walt Disney World was announced last week.

The donations would help pay for the medical college building, a second biomedical sciences college building and a library at the UCF Healthcare Campus at Lake Nona, where the medical school will be located if approved.
