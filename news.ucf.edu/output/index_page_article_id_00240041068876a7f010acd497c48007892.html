Magazine Ranks UCFs Efforts to Retain, Educate Hispanic Students Among Best

By Zenaida Gonzalez Kotala May 9, 2006

Photo: Jerry Klein

Hispanic Outlook in Higher Education has again named UCF one of the best 100 colleges for graduating Hispanic students, ranking the university in the top 20 in several categories.

As the Hispanic population booms in Central Florida, the University of Central Florida continues to make strides to recruit, retain and graduate Hispanic students.

Thats why Hispanic Outlook in Higher Education Magazine has named UCF one of the best 100 colleges in the United States for Hispanic students.

The magazine for college educators ranked UCF 16th in its list of universities that help Hispanic students earn bachelors degrees. The university moved up three spots from last year.

Using data from the U.S. Department of Education, Hispanic Outlook rated public and private universities throughout the country for its rankings, which appear in the magazines May 8 issue.

UCF ranked 42nd, down one spot from last years rating, for working with Hispanic students to obtain masters degrees. And, for the first time, UCF was named among the best for helping Hispanic students earn doctorates, ranking 83rd.

Nine Florida universities, including Florida State, the University of Florida and Nova Southeastern, earned a spot on the 13th annual list in all three categories.

"These universities are doing a commendable job of recruiting, retaining, educating and graduating Hispanics," said Hispanic Outlook publisher Jose Lopez-Isa.

The magazine will be sent to high schools across the country with high Hispanic student populations "to provide students and their parents information about what schools may be best when they pursue a college education," Lopez-Isa said.

UCF has increased its number of Hispanic graduates over the past three years. In 2004-05, 848 Hispanic students earned their bachelors degrees from UCF. Thats an increase from 758 in 2003-04 and 706 in 2002-03.

"We think Hispanic students, like other groups on our campus, are very important," said Maribeth Ehasz, vice president for Student Development and Enrollment Services at UCF. "Our approach is different here. We target our services so students of all backgrounds can be successful."

Most traditional, large universities expect students to adapt to the college setting -- a one-size-fits-all approach. But at UCF, Ehasz and her staff, as well as the Hispanic Retention Committee, analyze what students need each year and adapt or create programs to reach them.

For example, this summer UCF will offer an orientation session in Spanish for incoming students and their families for the first time.

"For Hispanic students, like African-Americans, family is very important," Ehasz said. "For each group there are special issues or concerns, and at UCF we try to address each of them."

Some issues of special concern for Hispanic students include staying connected to their families, finding role models to whom they can relate among faculty and, in many cases, financial responsibilities to family members.

"If students select UCF -- a large metropolitan research university -- I can assure them they will get the academic and social support they need to create an engaging environment that means something to them," Ehasz said. "That will lead to success."

For more information on the rankings, visit www.hispanicoutlook.com.

Summary of Hispanic Outlook rankings for Hispanic students at UCF:

-- 16th for bachelors degrees

-- 42nd for masters degrees

-- 83rd for doctorates

-- 8th for bachelors degrees in health sciences

-- 10th for bachelors degrees in psychology

-- 13th for bachelor's degrees in public administration.
