Photos in UCF Art Gallery Exhibit to Feature Modern Images, Vintage Processes

By UCF Staff Oct. 20, 2006

Photo: Courtesy of Bonni Benrubi Gallery

While digital photography technology improves at an exponential rate, several well-known contemporary artists are looking to the techniques of photographys dawn for inspiration.

Out of Darkness: The Contemporary Revival of Early Photography, which will be exhibited from Oct. 26 to Dec. 7 at the University of Central Florida Art Gallery, brings together the work of 15 cutting-edge photographers working with archaic media. Those artists include Chuck Close, Sally Mann, Jerry Spagnoli and Abelardo Morell.

Contemporary photographers now have access to nearly instantaneous image capture and reproduction. The tools and speed of digital photography reflect a culture that demands immediate gratification and instant access.

This exhibition, in contrast, examines contemporary photographers who choose to have direct contact with the photographic process in its most basic forms. Instead of working with current digital technology, these photographers have revived forgotten techniques and processes.

By returning to the roots of photography, which date back hundreds of years, the artists in this show are revitalizing a medium that is again in transition -- from chemical to digital processes. The exhibit includes ancient to merely antique processes, such as camera obscura and daguerreotypes.

The principles of the camera obscura -- a natural phenomenon in which the light that passes through a small hole in a darkened room projects an image on the opposite wall -- have been known since the 15th century. 

The daguerreotype, named for inventor Louis Daguerre, is one of the earliest types of photography. Popularized in the 1840s, daguerreotypes are created by exposing an image directly onto a mirror-polished surface of silver bearing a coating of light-sensitive silver halide particles. Unlike later photographic processes that supplanted it, the daguerreotype is a direct positive image-making process with no negative original.

Some of the photographers included in the exhibition work within the familiar genres of portraiture, landscapes and figures, while others create uniquely personal worlds. This exhibition explores the motivations behind reviving an antiquated process in a technologically advanced time. And, more importantly, it raises the question, What do these processes tell us about our current culture?

Curators for the exhibition are Theo Lotz, director of the UCF Art Gallery, and E. Brady Robinson, an assistant professor of art. The exhibition was developed with help from a grant from the Winifred Johnson Clive Foundation, which is based in Pittsburgh.

The UCF Art Gallery is open 9 a.m. to 4 p.m. Monday through Friday and from 1 to 5 p.m. Saturdays. A six-part film series, Images: A Hundred Years of Photography will be shown on Saturdays at 2 p.m.  For more information, go to www.art.ucf.edu.
