StoryBox Technology to Help Students Learn About Ethics, Critical Thinking

By Chad Binette Feb. 15, 2007

Photo: Courtesy of Ken Ingraham

This cutaway view of a 3-dimensional model shows the structure of the StoryBox, which is one of the components of a grant UCF received to help teach ethics and critical thinking on campus.

Role-playing sessions in an interactive simulator known as the StoryBox will allow UCF students to experience ethical and critical thinking dilemmas as part of a national initiative to instill personal and social responsibility.

The University of Central Florida will help lead the first phase of the Association of American Colleges and Universities national Core Commitments initiative. The association awarded UCF a $25,000 grant that will be matched by the university and UCFs College of Arts & Humanities, increasing the total award to $75,000 over the next two years.

UCF was one of 18 universities nationwide selected to participate in the AAC&U program. Rollins College, the U.S. Air Force Academy and Michigan State University are among the other participants.

The digital media component of UCFs grant will involve researchers at the Interactive Performance Lab of UCFs School of Film and Digital Media. Led by lab director Jeff Wirth, the researchers will develop scenarios that will place students in the middle of ethical dilemmas.

The StoryBox is a 16-by-16-foot box with black fabric walls, surround-sound audio and a video camera in each corner. Participants enter the box, where they become the central character in improvised stories created by trained inter-actors who also are inside the box.  The stories change based on how the subject responds, and the cameras allow others outside the box to watch how the scenario unfolds.

The stories, which typically last 15 to 20 minutes each, often are not resolved at the end, leaving participants and the audience watching the scenario on video to think afterward about how they should respond.

The other four components of UCFs initiative will involve:

-- Offering more seminars in academic integrity for students. The seminars are now given to students who are required to attend because of conduct violations. UCF will begin to offer them to other students. The seminars cover topics such as plagiarism and how to deal with students in group projects who do little or no work and let others pick up their slack.

-- Encouraging more student and faculty participation in ethics task forces. UCF has a Faculty Senate Ethics Task Force that includes student and faculty representatives. Similar bodies could be developed at the department or college level to encourage more input and dialogue on ethics.

-- Creating more ethics bowl teams and competitions among UCF students and departments. In the competitions, students must analyze and give presentations on scenarios involving ethics and critical thinking. A case could involve determining how to deal with family differences over the care of a critically ill relative.

-- Increasing professional development opportunities for faculty through the Faculty Center for Teaching and Learning and including professors proposed ways to teach ethics and critical thinking in classes.

AAC&U selected the 18 participating universities based on the work already accomplished in the spirit of Core Commitments and on a plan to deepen and extend that work on campus. Each school is working to implement AAC&Us recommendations that every college and university foster civic, intercultural and ethical learning and emphasize personal and social responsibility for all students.

Nancy Stanlick, a philosophy professor, and Alison Morrison-Shetlar, interim dean of Undergraduate Studies and director of the Faculty Center for Teaching and Learning, will lead the UCF effort. Other faculty and administrators who are involved are Rick Schell, vice provost for Academic Affairs; Alvin Wang, dean of the Burnett Honors College; Patricia MacKown, director of Student Legal Services; Stephen M. Fiore, a research scientist at the Institute of Simulation and Training and an assistant professor of Cognitive Sciences in the Department of Philosophy; and Martha Marinara, an associate professor of English.

We are pleased that the AAC&U recognized the emphasis that UCF has placed on ethics and critical thinking, Stanlick said. We hope that the new and more interactive approaches associated with this grant will help our students develop a deeper appreciation of those skills and values that are important to our campus community.

For more information on AAC&U, go to www.aacu.org. More information on the Interactive Performance Lab is available at www.iplonline.org.
