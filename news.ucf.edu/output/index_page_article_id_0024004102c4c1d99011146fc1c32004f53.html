UCF Office Aims for Students to Make Responsible Choices About Alcohol

By Jessica Saggio Aug. 10, 2007

Photo: UCF News file

In a program organized by the Alcohol and Other Drug Prevention Programming office, faculty and staff "ambassadors" mingle with tailgaters at UCF football games. Students also can receive free water and food.

While alcohol remains a nearly unavoidable pressure for many college students, the University of Central Floridas Alcohol and Other Drug Prevention Programming office takes pride in helping to reduce students high-risk drinking.

The program, known for its non-judgmental approach, is a resource that provides students with the information they need to make responsible choices. By avoiding scare tactics and supporting autonomy, the program seeks to reward responsible students and communicate the harms of alcohol abuse while offering guidance to those who seek help.

The ultimate goal is to augment the academic success of our students, said Tom Hall, director of the Alcohol and Other Drug Prevention Programming office. Alcohol abuse is often cited as a factor for poor academic performance. The program supports students who are struggling with substance use to recognize the need to change and, in turn, achieve their academic goals.
 
A national survey administered annually to a randomly selected group of undergraduate students has shown a 9 percent decrease in high-risk drinking at UCF since 2000. Hall attributed much of the change in drinking habits at UCF to the success of the alcohol prevention program.

UCFs Alcohol and Other Drug Prevention Programming office, established in 2002. consists of three full-time staff members, a part-time clinical psychologist and 20 students who help to educate their peers.

The office attracted national attention earlier this year when it received a Prevention and Excellence Award from Outside the Classroom, a Boston-based company that develops online alcohol prevention programs. UCF was chosen for this award along with seven other schools in the nation.

The program includes several free services for students. The REAL Project (Research, Education, Assessment, and Leadership) uses graduate and undergraduate peers to advocate healthy lifestyles and disseminate health promotion information. The REAL Project provides many online references, including a blood-alcohol level calculator and party-smart tips for students who plan on attending or hosting a party.

Alongside REAL, the BASICS and CHOICES programs offer professional assistance in overcoming alcohol issues. BASICS provides students with face-to-face sessions with a counselor, while CHOICES offers a group setting in which student beliefs and attitudes toward risky behaviors are examined. The university requires students who break a university policy related to substance use to complete a REAL educational program.

Students also can access E-Chug and E-Toke, two electronic experiences that help to assess habits with alcohol and marijuana. The system provides useful information to students, such as how many calories they are consuming and how much exercise it will take to burn those calories.

Another Web-based program is the Alcohol.Edu class, which freshmen are encouraged to take before they arrve in the fall. The online course informs students of facts and figures about alcohol consumption and how to handle themselves in environments where risks are present.

With the new Bright House Networks Stadium almost complete, the Alcohol and Other Drug Prevention Programming Office is working on efforts to persuade students and other fans to behave responsibly on football game days.

The office will continue its Good Sport program, which rewards students spotted promoting positive school spirit and ideals. In partnership with the Student Government Association and several other student organizations, tailgating students also will receive free food and drinks again this year. Last year about $15,000 worth of pizza and $15,000 worth of Coca-Cola products were given away to students during pre-game tailgating. Another 5,000 bottles of Dasani water were given away to tailgating students who signed commitments to be designated drivers.

For more information on the Alcohol and Other Drug Prevention Programming office, go to http://www.aod.sdes.ucf.edu/.
