UCF Student Chosen for Teach for America to Return to Her Community

By Zenaida Gonzalez Kotala Dec. 11, 2006

Related Contacts and Links:

  • Zenaida Gonzalez Kotala, UCF News and Information

    • 407-823-6120

  • Print this Page

Elizabeth Rodriguez, of Orlando, has been selected as part of the 2007 Teach for America core. She is the eighth University of Central Florida student to be accepted into the national program since its inception in 1989.

Rodriguez is one of 4,400 students nationwide who will teach at-risk students in 25 urban and rural communities across the country this summer.

By then, Rodriguez will have her degree in social science education. Shell return to the Miami-Dade area where she grew up to give something back.

Im so honored, Rodriguez said. The interview and selection process was really tough. But Im so excited about being able to teach students in my community who dont always get all the opportunities.

The goal of the organization is to ensure educational excellence for all students, no matter their economic reality. The program started when Wendy Kopp pitched her senior thesis in college. She suggested that college graduates could contribute to society by giving up two years of their early career to benefit children in low-income areas. It had modest beginnings.

But 17 years later the national organization is so popular that it can accept only one out of every five applicants. More than 19,000 applied for this summers class. Accepted students had an average GPA of 3.5, and 96 percent of them held leadership positions at their universities.

We know that with the opportunity and support they deserve, kids in our communities can and will excel, Kopp said. Seeing that happen year after year, in classrooms across the country, has fueled not just our sense of urgency, but our sense of responsibility to do more.

Rodriguez, whose father is from Cuba, said she is eager to return to the place she grew up.

I remember some teachers who were great, passionate and inspiring, like my eighth-grade science teacher. It wasnt my subject, but he made me want to learn it, she said. Then I remember others, who you could tell just didnt really want to be there. It was just a job for them. I wanted to be one of those inspiring teachers and have an impact on student lives. And I couldnt be happier that Ill start that journey in my own community.
