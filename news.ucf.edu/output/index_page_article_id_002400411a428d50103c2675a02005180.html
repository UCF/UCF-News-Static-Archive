Board of Trustees OKs Med School Proposal; UCF to Seek State Approval

By Tom Evelyn Sept. 22, 2005

Photo: Jacque Brund

Provost Terry Hickey outlines UCF's proposal for a medical college at the Board of Trustees meeting on Thursday.

The University of Central Florida Board of Trustees on Thursday unanimously approved a proposal for a medical college that could begin holding classes in 2008 if approved by the state Board of Governors.

The Board of Governors, which oversees Floridas 11 public universities, is expected to consider UCFs proposal and another from Florida International University at its Nov. 17 meeting in Jacksonville.

The UCF medical degree and college would capitalize on the universitys strengths in biomedical sciences, modeling and simulation, and optics and photonics while fulfilling the communitys need for more physicians, according to the proposal released Thursday at the Board of Trustees' regular meeting.

If approved by the state, the college would be established in fall 2006. The degree calls for a four-year curriculum that begins clinical education in fall 2008 and would produce about 120 graduates each year. Students also would have opportunities to study at the University of Puerto Rico School of Medicine and to participate in research programs.

Through private fund-raising, UCF would be able to cover most of the initial facility costs. The university has already raised $10 million that, with a state match, will help pay for the Burnett Biomedical Sciences building. UCF plans to raise another $20 million that, with a match, would pay for a second college building.

The university would request about $4.7 million in state appropriations in the first year and about $18.2 million a year when the college is in full operation.

We think we can provide a very cost-effective medical education, Provost Terry Hickey said.

Florida needs at least two more medical schools to address the growing demand for doctors in the state, according to a study by MGT of America. The annual need for new physicians in Florida is expected to grow from 2,800 in 2003 to 4,200 in 2021.

A medical college in Orlando, the largest metropolitan area in the country without such a school, would improve healthcare in Central Florida and inject millions of dollars into the area economy, Hickey said. Other medical schools in the state average more than $2.7 billion each in annual economic impact.

In other action at the Board of Trustees meeting:

-- Vice President for Development Bob Holmes announced a $2.5 million donation from Dr. P. Phillips Inc. to create a new real estate school and institute in the College of Business Administration. The trustees approved naming the Dr. P. Phillips School of Real Estate and the Dr. P. Phillips Institute for Research and Education in Real Estate. Read the full story.

-- Trustees approved naming the new Engineering III building the Harris Corporation Engineering Center following a $3 million gift from the Melbourne company that was announced last month. Read the full story.

-- The board approved an outstanding performance review and one-time incentive bonus of $50,000 for President John Hitt. The trustees also directed staff to prepare a new, five-year contract for Hitt that would start next year.

-- Trustees approved a joint resolution with Brevard, Lake-Sumter, Seminole and Valencia community colleges to help expand access, meet growth demands and provide a competitive, skilled workforce in Central Florida through increased cooperation. UCF accepts more community college transfers than any other university in Florida. We have a critical mass of success that we think we can build on, said David Harrison, vice provost for Regional Campuses.

-- The board approved a master of arts degree in applied experimental and human factors psychology. The degree will be implemented immediately.

The next Board of Trustees meeting is scheduled for Nov. 9. Visit www.bot.ucf.edu for more information.
