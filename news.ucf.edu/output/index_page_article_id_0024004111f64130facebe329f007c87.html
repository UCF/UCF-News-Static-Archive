Honored for Reducing Air Pollution

By Chad Benette December 3, 2003

Photo: UCF News File Photo.

The new look of the bus fleet.

The Central Florida Clean Air Team will honor the University of Central Florida next week for reducing air pollution by running student shuttles that keep more than 4,000 vehicles a day off the campus and nearby streets.

The team, a coalition of businesses and government, health, transportation and environmental organizations, will present the 2003 Clean Air Award to UCF President John Hitt at 11 a.m. Dec. 8, outside the Student Union. Hitt and Orange County Commissioner Ted Edwards will speak during a brief ceremony that is open to the public. Edwards is board chairman of METROPLAN ORLANDO, one of the Clean Air TeamÂ’s 21 members.

The Clean Air Award is given annually to a business or organization that "promotes clean air through innovative programs or practices." The three prior winners are the community of Celebration, Rosen Hotels & Resorts and Orlando Regional Healthcare.

The free shuttles, which are operated by Coach USA, run five days a week from 7 a.m. to 10 p.m. when school is in session. Shuttles stop at 14 off-campus housing complexes within a mile of campus. They make round trips about once every 15 minutes. Other buses run circular routes on campus, allowing students to get from their residence halls to classes or other buildings across the campus without driving cars.

About 5,000 to 6,000 students a day ride the shuttles. A survey showed that about 80 percent of the riders, 4,000 to 4,800 students a day, would drive their own cars to and from school if the shuttles had not been running.

UCF expects to spend about $2 million this school year on the shuttles, said John Clark, manager of parking and transportation services. The university started the shuttle service in January 2002 to reduce traffic and parking congestion on campus. Reducing pollution "turned out to be a very welcome effect," Clark said.

As part of the nomination for the clean air award, UCF engineering professor C. David Cooper conducted a study to assess the impact of the shuttles on air pollution. His research showed that the shuttles reduce the amounts of the three major air pollutants that vehicles heading to and from campus emit: carbon monoxide, nitrogen oxides and volatile organic compounds, which are unburned fragments of gasoline and diesel.

The Central Florida Clean Air Team was established in 1999 to address concerns about air pollution in the Orlando area. The team aims to raise public awareness about air pollution and focuses on small steps individuals and organizations can take to make the air cleaner. The team includes UCF, the City of Orlando, Orange County, the Florida Department of Environmental Protection and the Florida Department of Transportation.
