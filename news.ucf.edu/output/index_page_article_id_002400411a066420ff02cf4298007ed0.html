UCF to Seek Aid to Better Protect Residence Halls, Research from Hurricanes

By Chad Binette Sept. 17, 2004

Related Contacts and Links:

  • Linda Gray

  • Tom Evelyn

    • 407-823-5988

  • Chad Binette

    • 407-823-6312

Determined to further ensure the safety of students, faculty and staff and to protect valuable research materials during hurricanes, the University of Central Florida will seek federal and state aid to strengthen windows and doors in residence halls and research labs.

 

UCF had an estimated $8.4 million of damage during hurricanes Charley and Frances, which hit Central Florida in mid-August and early September respectively.  Hurricane Charley inflicted severe roof damage to the student Recreation and Wellness Center and knocked down hundreds of trees, while Hurricane Frances caused water damage to many buildings, including the Library and classroom buildings.

 

In addition to seeking damage costs, UCF will ask the state and federal governments for about $13.7 million to improve buildings on the main campus and at the Central Florida Research Park, the Rosen School of Hospitality Management, the Florida Solar Energy Center in Cocoa and Kennedy Space Center. While the universitys brick buildings are well built and can survive many storms, window treatments, shutters and other improvements can make them even safer, said Jim Uhlir, director of Environmental Health and Safety.

 

State and federal mitigation grants could help UCF pay for the proposed improvements aimed at making buildings stronger. Reimbursements for damage-related costs could begin arriving within a few months, Uhlir said.

 

UCFs planned precautions come as forecasters are predicting more hurricane activity in the Atlantic Ocean in the coming years. The prospects of multiple storms affecting Central Florida annually, and of more severe hurricanes hitting the region, heighten the importance of strengthening buildings and utility services as much as possible.

 

While were upset at the damage to our buildings, particularly the student Recreation and Wellness Center, and the loss of trees during hurricanes Charley and Frances, were thankful that everyone in our campus community remained safe, President John Hitt said. Now were focusing on ways that we can improve our residence halls, Student Health Center and other campus buildings so that we can better withstand future storms and further ensure the safety of our students, faculty and staff.

 

The proposed upgrades include improving windows and doors in all of UCFs on-campus residence halls, a move that could allow more students to stay in their own rooms when the campus is closed for hurricanes. During Hurricane Frances, some students were allowed to remain in their rooms, but others stayed in shelters at the communications and classroom buildings. Improvements to the residence halls could include hurricane shutters or laminated glass on windows.

 

UCF also wants to upgrade generators to help Student Health Services and the Marketplace cafeteria operate during emergencies. A proposed new Central Emergency Operations Center on the main campus would house a police dispatch center and allow critical departments to be housed under one roof and collaborate more easily during disasters. A new center also would give emergency officials better technological capabilities to monitor storms.

 

Isolated power grids that now serve the Lake Claire Apartments, Greek Park and the Burnett House, the home of President John Hitt, would be connected to the main power network on campus. There were no power outages in the main network during both hurricanes.

 

Estimated costs of the proposed improvements include $1 million to strengthen window and door areas at five student residence-hall complexes  Apollo, Libra, Nike, Hercules and Lake Claire. The anticipated cost of repairs at the Recreation and Wellness Center is $2.7 million; a new roof and other repairs related to water damage in the College of Optics and Photonics building will cost about $720,000.

 

Replacing the universitys damaged Hitachi scanning electron microscope at the Advancd Materials Processing and Analysis Center at Research Park is expected to cost about $300,000. Repairs to prevent future window and roof leaks at the Florida Solar Energy Center in Cocoa are expected to cost about $750,000.

 

UCF also wants to spend about $300,000 on a mobile disaster response unit that would include generators, dehumidifiers, pumps and lights. The trailer could be used at any UCF campus and also could be used to help other Central Florida residents recover from disasters.

 

To cover the costs of damage, the university first will seek aid from the states Risk Management Trust Fund, through which the 11 state universities are insured. UCF then will submit whats not covered by the trust fund to the Federal Emergency Management Agency.
