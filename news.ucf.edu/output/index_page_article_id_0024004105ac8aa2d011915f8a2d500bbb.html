Tree Planting, Energy Presentations Highlight Research Week Festivities

By Barb Abney and Brandon Bielich April 3, 2008

Photo: Jacque Brund

A tree-planting ceremony Monday at Ferrell Commons was a symbolic gesture to offset any carbon emissions participants in Research Week may have expended to get to and from campus.

UCF Research Week 2008 will leave a lasting imprint on the campus in the form of five Nuttall Oak trees planted in the Ferrell Commons area on Monday. While the trees will provide a tangible reminder of the week’s activities, organizers hope that some of the messages delivered will make an impression with students and the community.

The week’s presentations were focused on the theme, “The Environment: Conservation, Energy and Quality of Life.” The theme was chosen because of its relevance to the campus, the community and the world at large. 

Jim Fenton, director of UCF’s Florida Solar Energy Center, opened the week with a call for greater energy efficiency. While researchers at FSEC are studying carbon neutral biofuels, more efficient solar panels and energy-saving home design, Fenton acknowledged that many new technologies are still prohibitively expensive. He expressed support for governmental incentives to encourage large-scale changes such as generating more energy from the sun instead of from nuclear or coal-powered plants. 

Members of the UCF Sustainability Alliance gave an overview of the many efforts on campus to achieve greater energy efficiency, from erecting more than 1,000 cardboard boxes every football game day to recycle aluminum cans and plastic bottles to installing retrofitted lighting fixtures in campus buildings to reduce energy consumption. The Sustainability Alliance includes initiatives on recycling, energy, stormwater use, environmentally friendly cleaning supplies and more.

To symbolically offset any carbon emissions participants in the week's events may have expended to get to campus, Research Week sponsored the planting of five Nuttall Oak trees in the Ferrell Commons area. Martin Quigley, director of Landscape and Natural Resources at UCF, emphasized the importance of using native plants and shade trees in campus landscaping instead of “chemically dependent” plants that introduce chemicals into groundwater and can require more of that water to maintain.

Also on Monday, UCF researchers discussed “Water’s Journey through the Everglades,” a sustainability simulation that allows viewers to experience the formation, destruction and restoration of the Florida Everglades, a main source of the state’s drinking water.

Faculty from UCF’s Institute for Simulation and Training (IST) and the Computer Science, Biology, Statistics and English departments are developing the exhibit, which is a four-year project funded by a $2.7 million grant from the National Science Foundation.

“The goal is to make people more aware of the impact they have on the environment,” said Eileen Smith from IST. “Although humans have had a great impact on the Everglades, we can’t throw in the towel. The Everglades is still beautiful; this asks the question, ‘How do we make sure it is still here in 100 years?’”

Upon completion, the exhibit will become part of the new EcoDiscovery wing at the Museum of Discovery and Science in Fort Lauderdale.

One of the highlights of Wednesday’s events was a presentation on conservation issues affecting Central Florida and planning for the future. Orange County Commissioner William Segal gave an overview of some of the county’s efforts to conserve limited resources. Reed Noss, a UCF biologist; Linda Chapin, director of the Metropolitan Center for Regional Studies at UCF; and Duane DeFreese, former vice president of research for Hubbs Sea World Research Institute and now a UCF faculty member, discussed conservation, growth management and the importance of creating an economic sector for marine science. 

Research Week, which also included a graduate research forum, will continue Friday, April 4, with the Showcase of Undergraduate Research. The showcase will be held from 2 to 5 p.m. in the Pegasus Ballroom of the Student Union.

Go to www.researchweek.ucf.edu for more information.
