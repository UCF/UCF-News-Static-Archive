NBA Remains Top League for Race, Gender Hiring Practices

By UCF Staff June 7, 2006

Photo: Fernando Medina (NBAE/Getty Images)

Otis Smith (left), general manager of the Orlando Magic, was one of seven African-Americans in charge of day-to-day basketball operations as general managers or vice presidents in the NBA in 2005-06, according to the study by UCF's Institute for Diversity and Ethics in Sport. Smith is shown here with player Keyon Dooling (center) and Coach Brian Hill (right).

The longtime leader among sports leagues for race and diversity hiring practices, the National Basketball Association received its highest marks ever this year in a University of Central Florida report card released Wednesday.

The NBA received an A for race, a B-minus for gender and a B-plus overall in the annual report card issued by UCFs Institute for Diversity and Ethics in Sport. Institute director Richard Lapchick is the reports lead author.

While the NBA has received the top grade for race among all mens sports leagues in the 12 previous reports, Lapchick praised the league for making dramatic improvements in hiring practices in the league office and for individual teams.

The report analyzes the racial breakdowns for players, coaches, team and league administrators, team physicians, head trainers and broadcasters. The report is based on rosters for the 2005-06 season and on owners, head coaches, team presidents and general managers who were in those positions on May 26.

The NBA received an A-plus for race for players, league office professionals and head and assistant coaches and an A for race for team senior and professional administrators. For gender hiring practices, the league earned an A for the league office and for professional administrators and an F for team vice presidents and senior team administrators.

The percentages of Latino and international players in the NBA continue to climb. Seventy-three percent of NBA players were African-American. Thats the smallest percentage since 1990-91.

However, the NBA tied its record for minority team vice presidents, and a record 33 percent of professional positions in the league office were occupied by minorities. Robert Johnson, the owner of the Charlotte Bobcats, is the only African-American majority owner of a team in major mens professional sports.

Women held 41 percent of professional positions in the NBA league office. While thats a decrease from 43 percent in the last report, the new number remains higher than what any other mens professional league has attained in prior report cards.

UCFs Institute for Diversity and Ethics in Sport publishes Racial and Gender Report Cards to indicate areas of improvement, stagnation and regression in the racial and gender composition of professional and college sports personnel.

To view the full report, go to http://www.ncasports.org/press_releases.htm.
