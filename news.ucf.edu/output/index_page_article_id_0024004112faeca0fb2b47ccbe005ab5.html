Governor, KSC Director Visit Robotics Competition

By Chad Binette March 12, 2004

Photo: Jerry Klein

Robots battle each other and try to complete tasks, such as chinning themselves on this bar.

Arefin Bari thought of his Fort Lauderdale high schoolÂ’s robotics team as a fun activity two years ago. Now he views the team as a springboard to studying mechanical engineering in college and then a successful career.

Bari, a junior at Dillard High School, is the mechanical leader for one of 41 teams at the FIRST Robotics Florida regional competition this weekend at the UCF Arena. He and more than 50 teammates spent six weeks designing, building and programming a robot that can pick up balls, pass them to human players and hang from a 10-foot-high bar.

"Robotics has turned my life around," said Bari, who wants to attend Cornell or Carnegie Mellon universities or the Massachusetts Institute of Technology.

On Friday, the second of three days in the competition, students were welcomed by Gov. Jeb Bush and Jim Kennedy, director of Kennedy Space Center. Bush also visited the pit area to shake hands and pose for photos with students.

Before Bush and Kennedy spoke with students, they addressed a crowd of about 100 business and education leaders at a luncheon in the UCF Arena skybox.

The governor praised FIRST (For Inspiration and Recognition of Science and Technology) Robotics competitors as the "doers, dreamers and innovators of tomorrow."

"We should hold them up high as examples of what we want our state to be," Bush said during the luncheon. "We brag Â’til the sun sets about how great our football teams are. I hope and pray that our state also brags about the young kids who are going to be the engineers, the scientists and the folks that are really going to transform our state."

Kennedy told the students that they are the right ages to be the first men and women to step foot on Mars in about 30 years. During the luncheon, he said heÂ’s confident that shuttle flights will resume in about a year.

He also talked about the shared services center that NASA intends to build to consolidate services such as human resources and financial management. The Central Florida Research Park is one of the sites being considered for the new center, which Kennedy said will employ about 500 at first and eventually could have 1,000 to 1,500 employees. Kennedy said he believes the proposal put together by Central Florida governments, agencies and the Research Park, which is adjacent to the UCF campus, "has a tremendous chance of winning" the new center.

Several companies represented at the FIRST Robotics luncheon served as mentors to students in the competition. Students at UCF and other universities also helped the students.

Chris Watz and Jared Huffman, both seniors at Rockledge High School, said they enjoyed working with and learned a lot from NASA and Kennedy Space Center employees on their robot. The Rockledge team, which also included students from Cocoa Beach High School, was one of many that wore funky costumes for the competition. The squad wore pink shirts and pink wigs, and Watz wore a kilt. Team members also gave Bush a pink wig that he wore while posing for a photo with them.

The colorful costumes are one of many creative aspects of a competition that requires students to have skills in computer engineering, programming, budgeting and even shooting balls into goals. The teams also scout each other, looking for vulnerabilities they can exploit in their opponentsÂ’ robots.

The UCF College of Engineering and Computer Science co-hosted the event with NASA/ Kennedy Space Center. Go to www.firstfloridaregional.org for more information.
