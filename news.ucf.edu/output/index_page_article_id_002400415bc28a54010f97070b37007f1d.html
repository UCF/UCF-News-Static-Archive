Successful Prescribed Burn Tuesday Helps to Reduce Wildfire Threats

By Chad Binette Updated Dec. 19, 2006

Photo: Jacque Brund

The prescribed burn went as planned Tuesday.

The University of Central Florida conducted a successful prescribed burn Tuesday on Arboretum land south of Orion Boulevard and east of Gemini Boulevard.

The burn helped to lessen the threats of future fires and restore animal and plant habitats in a 20-acre area that was struck by a wildfire two years ago. Since that blaze, the underbrush has grown dense again, increasing the chances of another wildfire, said Alaina Bernard, program coordinator for the Arboretum.

Residual smoke from the burn may be visible from the UCF campus for the next couple of days, Bernard said.

The St. Johns River Water Management District, Florida Division of Forestry and Orange County Fire Rescue assisted with the burn led by Bill Keenan, a UCF graduate student in biology. UCF Physical Plant employees also helped with the effort.

Bernard said UCF conducted the burn this week because classes are not in session and there is less traffic on campus.

The burn was UCFs second this year. A successful burn was conducted in March on about 16 acres of Arboretum land that also had become overgrown with dense vegetation.

UCF also conducted a successful prescribed burn on about 25 acres in May 2005. The plan for that burn helped UCF win a Campus Ecology Recognition award from the National Wildlife Foundation. The award is given to universities committed to restoring wildlife habitat and slowing global warming.

From an ecological perspective, prescribed burns improve wildlife habitat and restore vegetation diversity and health. The burns also limit the possibility of wildfires and make them easier to control when they do occur.

The university also will use other methods, such as machinery, to remove debris that can spark wildfires.
