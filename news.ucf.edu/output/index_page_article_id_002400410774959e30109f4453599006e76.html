Daughter of GM Official Who Helped to End Apartheid Promotes Corporate Ethics

By Mark Freeman April 13, 2006

Hope Masters

Full Photo

Hope Masters, daughter of the late Rev. Leon H. Sullivan, discussed her fathers struggle to end apartheid in South Africa and create a set of guiding principles for corporate social responsibility last week at the University of Central Florida.

Masters, also the founder of the Washington, D.C.-based Leon H. Sullivan Foundation, gave a heartfelt account of her fathers life growing up in Philadelphia and participating in the civil rights movement. Sullivan, a Baptist minister guided by his faith, became increasingly aware that thousands of African-Americans in Philadelphia were unemployed. 

Sullivan helped to organize large selective patronage campaigns to boycott Philadelphia companies that refused to hire African-Americans.  Masters discussed how the boycotts were successful, opening up thousands of jobs for African-Americans.  However, many African-Americans lacked training, so her father created the first Opportunities Industrialization Center. Masters also said her fathers boycotting campaigns helped to inspire the Rev. Martin Luther King Jr. and others in the civil rights movement. 

Masters discussed the role her father played at General Motors after he joined the Board of Directors in 1971. At the time, GM was the largest employer in South Africa, and Masters noted how troubled her father became upon learning about the system of segregation in South Africa. He used his position at GM to initiate an international movement to dismantle apartheid in South Africa.

The result was the development of the Sullivan Principles, an ethical code of conduct for human rights and equal opportunity for companies operating in South Africa. The Sullivan Principles later became the Global Sullivan Principles in November 1999, when Sullivan and UN Secretary-General Kofi Annan officially introduced them to the world.

Masters said her father overcame many obstacles, including the fact that many in the United States were uninformed about what was occurring in South Africa. Many suggested that he would fail, Masters said.

She recounted a time when he was forced off his plane by the police prior to leaving South Africa, taken to the police station and forced to remove his clothing while being questioned by police officers. He was then subsequently banned from returning to South Africa until the late 1980s. 

A large portion of Masters discussion focused on the Sullivan Foundation, which she leads as president and chief executive officer. The foundation encourages companies to publicly endorse the Global Sullivan Principles. She encouraged the audience to visit www.thesullivanfoundation.org and see which companies have publicly endorsed the principles.

Masters described enthusiastically the biennial Leon H. Sullivan Summits that take place in Africa. Beginning in 1991 in Cote d'Ivoire, the summits have offered the chance for the worlds political and business leaders, delegates representing national and international civil and multinational organizations and members of academic institutions to discuss the problems facing Africa and to highlight the positive aspects of the continent. 

Masters concluded by encouraging students to become involved in the global campaign for stronger corporate social responsibility that her father started more than three decades ago.  She also discussed scholarship and internship opportunities that the Sullivan Foundation offers students. For more information, go to www.thesullivanfoundation.org.
