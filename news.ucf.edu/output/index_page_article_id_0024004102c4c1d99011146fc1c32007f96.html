NCAA Womens Basketball Tourney Teams Well Ahead of Men in Classroom

By Chad Binette March 14, 2007

Photo: Courtesy of Holy Cross Athletics

A UCF study on recent graduation rates of teams playing in the 2007 NCAA basketball tournaments credited Holy Cross for being the only school that had men's and women's teams with 100 percent graduation rates. Seven other schools graduated all of their women's players, and two others graduated all of their men's players.

Most of the media attention and office chatter about the upcoming NCAA basketball tournaments will focus on the mens teams. But when it comes to success in the classroom, the student-athletes in the womens field remain far ahead of their male counterparts.

Fifty-two of 64 teams in the womens tourney have graduated at least 70 percent of their players, compared with only 24 of 65 mens teams, according to the study by Richard Lapchick, director of the Institute for Diversity and Ethics in Sport at UCF. The data reflects six-year graduation rates for freshmen classes that enrolled between 1996 and 1999.

The academic success of the women in the tournament is a great story, Lapchick said. They are the epitome of what it means to be a student-athlete.

Lapchick released his annual report, Keeping Score When It Counts: Graduation Rates for 2007 NCAA Mens and Womens Division I Basketball Tournament Teams, on Wednesday. The report was co-authored by graduate student Marina Bustamante.

Lapchick is concerned about the gap between graduation rates for white and African-American basketball players among both the mens and womens teams. While 53 schools in the womens tourney graduated 70 percent or more of their white players, only 38 reached that standard for African-American players. Among schools competing in the mens tournament, 41 graduated 70 percent or more of their white players and only 19 graduated 70 percent or more of their African-American players.

While rates for both groups have improved over the last few years, a significant disparity remains between graduation rates for white and African-American basketball student-athletes. Lapchick said.

Eight schools posted 100 percent graduation rates for female basketball players using the Graduation Success Rate standards developed by the NCAA, while only three schools achieved 100 percent with their male players.

Holy Cross is the only school that posted 100 percent graduation rates for mens and womens teams in the tournament. Florida and Weber State graduated 100 percent of their male players, while Belmont, Marquette, Nebraska, Notre Dame, Tennessee, Vanderbilt and Wisconsin-Green Bay graduated all of their female players.

UCFs Institute for Diversity and Ethics in Sport serves as a comprehensive resource for issues related to gender and race in amateur, collegiate and professional sports. The institute researches and publishes a variety of studies that deal with graduation rates and hiring practices in professional and college sports. The institute also monitors critical ethical issues such as gambling, performance-enhancing drugs, violence in sports and the potential for exploiting student-athletes. The institute is part of the DeVos Sport Business Management Graduate Program at UCFs College of Business Administration.

To view the institutes full analyses of mens and womens tournament teams, go to http://www.bus.ucf.edu/sport/cgi-bin/site/sitew.cgi?page=/ides/index.htx.
