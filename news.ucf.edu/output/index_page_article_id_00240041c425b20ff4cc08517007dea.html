NASA Administrator: SpaceShipOne Flights Exciting, 'Necessary First Step' to Private Space Flight

By Chad Binette Oct. 5, 2004

Photo: Jerry Klein

NASA's Bill Parsons will speak at the space forum this morning.

NASA's associate administrator for space operations on Monday compared the accomplishments of the private SpaceShipOne craft to the pioneering effort of Charles Lindbergh, the first pilot to fly solo and nonstop across the Atlantic Ocean.

Speaking at a space symposium hosted by UCF's Lou Frey Institute of Politics and Government, William F. "Bill" Readdy recalled that while Lindbergh's feat was impressive, the flight took 33 hours and he and his plane had to return home on a ship. It took a long time for aviation to reach the point where jets could fly hundreds of passengers across the Atlantic and back safely.

Readdy and author Andrew Chaikin, who also spoke to the crowd at the Student Union, cautioned that residents shouldn't expect private space travel to quickly become part of everyday life.

"From an economic standpoint, commercial space travel is going to have a tough time," Readdy said, noting the struggles of conventional airlines.

However, Readdy said private spacecraft eventually could help supply the International Space Station, leaving space shuttles to focus on more technical tasks. He also said the pioneering spirit generated by SpaceShipOne will lead to renewed interest in space in schoolchildren and in research.

Readdy and Chaikin, author of A Man on the Moon: The Triumphant Story of the Apollo Space Program, spoke at the evening session of The Space Program: Past and Future, Public or Private?"

The symposium continues today, Oct. 5, with presentations by Jim Kennedy, director of Kennedy Space Center, and Bill Parsons, a UCF alumnus who manages the Space Shuttle program. They will discuss the shuttles return to flight and will  be joined by John Pike, an expert on space exploration and the director of GlobalSecurity.org, a nonprofit organization studying security challenges facing the United States.

Chaikin, whose book spawned a Tom Hanks-produced miniseries on HBO, will speak at 9 a.m. today. Chaikin also will speak at the concluding awards night, which will be at 5:30 p.m. today at the University Club of Orlando, 150 E. Central Blvd.

All of the symposium presentations are free and open to the public. Except for a concluding awards night, all of the events will be in the Pegasus Ballroom of the Student Union.

Astronaut Frank Caldeiro, who is a UCF alumnus, and U.S. Reps. Tom Feeney, Ric Keller, John Mica and Dave Weldon spoke during Monday's session. Other speakers included reporters who cover the space program and engineers who provide the ground support needed for launches.

Former astronaut Sally Ride, the first American woman in space, was scheduled to speak Monday night but did not attend because of an illness.

The Lou Frey Institute of Politics and Government, a nonpartisan group that supports civics education and political research, has hosted four conferences since it was founded in 2001. Frey worked to increase NASAs funding and bring much of the shuttle program to Florida while he served in Congress from 1969 to 1979.

Frey said he's disappointed that there's less of a vision and not as much of an effort to care for the space program today. The space program represents about .6 percent of the federal budget, compared with about 5.4 percent during the 1960s.

However, Frey said, "I think the best is yet to come. Never, ever, ever quit (exploring)."

UCF works with NASA on a variety of research projects and is the host of the Florida Space Institute. UCF also is the lead university participating in the Spaceport Research and Technology Institute at the Kennedy Space Center.

The symposium is funded largely by a grant from the U.S. Department of Educations Fund for the Improvement of Post-Secondary Education. For a full symposium schedule or more information on the institute, go to www.loufrey.org .
