Mother-Daughter Civil Rights Activists Encourage Students to Get Involved

By Mallery Laing April 1, 2005

Photo: Courtesy of FAMU College of Law

Members of the Due family signed books after their presentation Wednesday at Orlando City Hall. Seated from the right are: Tananarive Due, Patricia Stephens Due, John Due, Johnita P. Due and Lydia Due Greisz.

Author and civil rights activist Tananarive Due and her mother, Patricia Stephens Due, emphasized the importance of supporting causes and becoming involved in the community Thursday during a presentation at the University of Central Florida.

 

Speaking to womens studies groups and creative writing students, the Dues told inspirational stories about their lives during a time of civil unrest in the 1960s and discussed the book they co-wrote, Freedom in the Family: A Mother- Daughter Memoir of the Fight for Civil Rights.

 

Tananarive Due was born into a household of activists. Her mother and father John Due, a civil rights attorney who graduated from the Florida A&M University College of Law in 1963, attended many rallies and were members of the NAACP for as long as Tananarive Due can remember.

 

Patricia Stephens Due told a story of a Tallahassee lunch counter sit-in in 1960, when she was arrested and imprisoned for 49 days. She kindly spoke of the people who sat beside her and were there throughout all of the grassroots movements.

 

Stories like that inspired Tananarive Due to get involved in the civil rights movement.

 

It was my destiny to be a writer, she said. I felt as a journalist, I could go on the road and interview those people, these aging activists, before they were all gone.

 

Their memoir, published in 2003, was originally to be entitled Ordinary People, Extraordinary Things. Patricia Stephens Due said she believes that is what the civil rights movement was all about.

 

We all are Martin Luther Kings and Rosa Parks, she said. We just have to do something for people to see that.

 

Freedom in the Family was named the Best Civil Rights Memoir in 2003 by Black Issues Book Review. The Black Caucus of the American Library Association also recognized it as an Honor Book in the annual Literary Awards.

 

While answering questions after their presentations, the Dues spoke of the ways students can get involved in todays movement, including education and health care.

 

Dont try to mold yourself after someone else, but find out what you are passionate about, Tananarive Due said. Patricia Stephens Due added, Then take small steps and see your successes.

 

The Dues, including father John and daughters Johnita and Lydia, also spoke Wednesday at Orlando City Hall about their activism. At the City Hall event, Patricia Stephens Due also emphasized the role of ordinary people in the civil rights movement.

 

The persons who impressed me the most are the persons whose names you wouldnt even recognize, she said.

 

The Dues signed copies of Freedom in the Family after the City Hall presentation, and John and Johnita Due and Lydia Due Griesz spoke to FAMU law students earlier Wednesday.

 

Tananarive Due also spoke to a Film and Digital Media group late Thursday afternoon about one of her novels, My Soul to Keep, being turned into a feature film.

 

Sponsors for the weeks events included the FAMU College of Law and the UCF School of Film and Digital Media, Office of Undergraduate Studies, College of Arts and Sciences, Womens Studies Program, Department of English and Womens Research Center. Other sponsors were City of Orlando Commissioner Daisy W. Lynum and The Academy Foundation.

 

Students and others can learn a lot from the Dues courage and dedication to civil rights, the family said.

 

Be sure to document what has happened in your family so that you know who you are and so that those stories will live on forever, Patricia Stephens Due said.

 

Bernadette Davis of the FAMU College of Law contributed to this report.
