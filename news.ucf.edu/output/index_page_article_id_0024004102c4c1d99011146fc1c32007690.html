Baseball Earns Higher Marks in UCF Study Despite Decline in Black Players

By Chad Binette March 30, 2007

Photo: Michael Binette/Sports Pics

Richard Lapchick's analysis of Major League Baseball praises the near-record totals for the percentage of players who are minorities and for improving diversity inside the commissioner’s office and among team coaches, vice presidents and senior administrators. However, the continued decline in the percentage of African-American players remains a concern.

Major League Baseball has received its highest grades ever for racial and gender diversity at a time when the percentage of African-American players in the game continues to fall.

Richard Lapchick, director of the University of Central Floridas Institute for Diversity and Ethics in Sport, gave baseball a solid B+ for racial diversity and a C+ for gender in his annual report card, which was released Thursday.

Lapchick lauded baseball for making improvements in diversity inside the commissioners office and among team coaches, vice presidents and senior administrators. He also praised baseball for holding its inaugural Civil Rights Game on Saturday, when the St. Louis Cardinals and Cleveland Indians will play an exhibition game in Memphis.

But Lapchick also acknowledged that the declining numbers of African-American players in the game remains a major concern. The study showed that 8.4 percent of players on 2006 rosters were African-American, and thats the smallest percentage since the report cards first were compiled in the mid-1980s.

That has been a concern of Major League Baseball and leaders in the African-American community as the numbers have consistently gone down, Lapchick said. On the other hand, with 40.5 percent players of color, MLB is close to its all-time high of 42 percent set in 1997.

In 1997, when baseball celebrated the 50th anniversary of Jackie Robinson becoming the first African-American to play in the major leagues, 17 percent of players were African-American. Since then, the percentages of Hispanic and Asian players have increased as the percentage of African-American players continues to fall.

In 2006, 29.4 percent of players were Hispanic, and 2.4 percent were Asian.
 
Lapchicks record card also noted that six of 30 major league managers starting this season are minorities and that Ken Williams of the Chicago White Sox and Omar Minaya of the New York Mets are the only general managers who are minorities.

The Institute for Diversity and Ethics in Sport publishes Racial and Gender Report Cards for all major sports. The institute strives to call attention to areas where improvements have been made and to help bring more diversity to areas where it is needed.

The baseball report was co-authored by Boma Ekiyor and Horacio Ruíz, students in UCFs DeVos Sport Business Management program.

To read the full study on baseball, go to http://www.ncasports.org/press_releases.htm.
