UCF Economic Competitiveness Center Releases Florida and Regional Economic Forecast

By Zenaida Gonzalez Kotala Nov. 17, 2006

Photo: Jacque Brund

Sean Snaith directs UCF's Institute for Economic Competitiveness.

Full Photo

Related Contacts and Links:

  • Sean M. Snaith, director, Institute for Economic Competitiveness

    • 407-823-1451

  • Florida and Metro area Economic Forecast

  • Print this Page

The Florida economy will continue to see economic growth from 2006 to 2009, with the Orlando-Kissimmee metropolitan statistical area expected to witness employment growth and population growth above state averages. The forecast comes from the quarterly state and regional forecast released today by the University of Central Floridas Institute for Economic Competitiveness.

"Floridas current expansion is a marathon and not a sprint; and you cant run 4 minute miles for an entire marathon," said Sean M. Snaith, director of UCFs Institute for Economic Competitiveness. "The all-out dash fueled by the housing sector was never a sustainable path for the economy and will leave the economy catching its breath in 2007. Some may see this deceleration as the beginning of the end of this economic expansion; I see it as a sign that it has become sustainable."

The report provides a comprehensive look at Florida and 12 metropolitan areas, including Daytona, Fort Myers, Gainesville, Ocala, Lakeland, Palm Bay-Melbourne, Pensacola, Miami, Jacksonville, Tallahassee, Tampa and Orlando. The complete forecasts may be downloaded at http://www.iec.ucf.edu .

Highlights from the report are:

  Payroll employment forecast to grow at 3.1 % in 2006 with growth slowing to 1.8% in 2007 and accelerating to 2.7% by 2009.

 High-growth sectors forecast to be: Professional and Business Services and Transportation, Warehousing and Utility, Leisure, and Education and Health Services.

 The housing soufflé has finished baking and is out of the oven and cooling quickly. Housing starts peaked in 2005 and begin to fall off from 2006 to 2009 as mortgage rates rise to 7.26% by 2009. The forecasted level of housing starts for 2009 will still be 30,000 more than in 1999.

 Construction employment growth slows from 7.8% in 2006 to less than 1% in 2007 and 2008 but rebounds again in 2009.

 Gross State Product to grow at nearly 9% in 2006, slowing to an average growth rate of 6.2% from 2007 to 2009.

 Unemployment falls to 3.1% in 2006 before rising slightly to 3.2%, where it will remain in 2007 and 2008.

 Personal income growth accelerating from 6.1% in 2006 to 7.4% in 2008.
