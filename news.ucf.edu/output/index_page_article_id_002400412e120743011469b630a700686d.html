Battling Bigotry: Ex-Neo Nazi Shares Her Story During Diversity Week

By Christine Dellert Oct. 18, 2007

UCF student Angela King, a former neo-Nazi skinhead, shared her experiences and her road out of the racist movement during this year’s Diversity Week.

University of Central Florida student Angela King spent three years in prison and nearly a decade violently persecuting others. Determined to make up for her past, the former neo-Nazi skinhead shared her experiences and her road out of the racist movement during this year's Diversity Week at UCF.

"My life has not been easy," said King, now a student enrolled in UCFs Masters in Interdisciplinary Studies program. "I created so many obstacles for myself, but I don't let it hold me back."

King spoke to about 50 students, faculty and staff Wednesday afternoon in the Student Union's Cape Florida Room.

Growing up in South Florida, she befriended a local skinhead group during her difficult teen years and acted out in hopes she'd attract attention from her parents and teachers. But while those antics snowballed, her loneliness swelled.

"I was becoming angry and started acting out, stealing. I was hurt," she said. "There were things wrong, [but] I didn't have anywhere to turn. I tried to numb myself."

So she gulped up literature from her neo-Nazi friends and traveled up and down the country before she was arrested in 1998 and convicted for acting as a lookout during the armed robbery of a South Florida adult-video store. King was ordered to serve time in a federal prison.

Her transformation that started in prison continues, she said. King has since graduated with her bachelor's degree from UCF. And she is working on her graduate studies. She routinely speaks to college and community groups and has been interviewed by the Southern Poverty Law Center and National Public Radio.

Other discussions about diversity and tolerance and educational sessions on hate groups were held during the week.

Students from UCF's African American Student Union (AASU) presented video clips from their trip last month to Jena, La. More than 50 students joined the thousands of others in protest there.

"It's perfect timing for us," said AASU Council Representative Alexis Chapin, a 20-year-old junior. "It's mostly to bring awareness to UCF and the community because a lot of people didn't know what was going on, even though it was on the front page of the newspaper."

A kick-off breakfast Monday featured keynote speaker Carmen Jones, founder and president of a Virginia-based marketing firm that works with businesses and the disabled community. While in college, Jones was injured in an auto accident that left her a paraplegic.

This year's Diversity Week theme, "Diverse Abilities: Expanding the Vision, comes from Jones experience, say coordinators at the Office of Diversity Initiatives, which organizes the weeks activities.

"Diversity Week is the time when we're the most visible to the university," said Melissa David, program assistant for the Office of Diversity Initiatives. "This is our chance to showcase what we do and the types of events we host all year long."

Diversity Week runs Oct. 15 through Oct. 19. Most events are free and open to the public. For more information about the events, visit http://www.diversity.ucf.edu.
