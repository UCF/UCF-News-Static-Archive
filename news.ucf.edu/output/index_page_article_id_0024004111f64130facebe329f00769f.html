Van Stryland to Lead Optics Society

By Chad Binette November 5, 2003

Photo: Jerry Klein

Van Stryland to head OSA.

Full Photo

Related Contacts and Links:

  • Eric Van Stryland

    • 407-823-6835

  • Print this Page

A prominent international optics society has named Eric Van Stryland, director of the School of Optics/CREOL and FPCE, as its next vice president, a move that will bring more attention to the University of Central Florida's research on optics and lasers.

Optical Society of America members in October selected Van Stryland as their vice president for 2004. Van Stryland then will serve one year as president-elect before becoming the society's president in 2006.

"Fifteen thousand scientists and engineers will know about the School of Optics/CREOL and FPCE," Van Stryland said. "That's a big deal. It helps us in recruiting students, and it helps us recruit faculty."

M.J. Soileau, UCF's vice president for research, said Van Stryland's leadership positions will bring "tremendous exposure" to the school. As president, Van Stryland will write a column for Optics and Photonics News, a monthly magazine issued by the society. He also will speak throughout the nation and world about optics and photonics.

The 87-year-old Optical Society of America has more than 15,000 members, including engineers, educators, scientists and 22 Nobel Prize winners. Van Stryland wants to further the society's commitment to education and international outreach.

The OSA's recognition of Van Stryland is one of several honors he and the School of Optics and Center for Research and Education in Optics and Lasers have received this year. The society's 2004 fellows include UCF professors Jannick Rolland and Aristide Dogariu. Criteria for the selection of fellows, a group that already includes Van Stryland, include having a "record of significant publications related to optics" and "remarkable achievement in optics."

Also this year, Van Stryland was selected as a Pegasus Professor, a UCF award that honors faculty who have made an extraordinary contribution to the university through excellence in teaching, research and service.

The Florida Photonics Center of Excellence opened in the spring at the School of Optics. The state is spending $10 million to set up the center, which is striving to produce technologies that will benefit the economy. Photonics is the technology behind fiber-optic communications, optical data storage, music and movies on CDs and DVDs, and lasers in manufacturing, medicine and defense.

The School of Optics/CREOL and FPCE has 25 professors and about 130 students.
