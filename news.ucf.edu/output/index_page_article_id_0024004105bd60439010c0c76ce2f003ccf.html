Judaic Studies, Central Florida Hillel Thriving at UCF as Jewish Population Grows

By Sam Gardner and Zenaida Gonzalez Kotala Dec. 14, 2006

Related Contacts and Links:

  • Chad Binette, UCF News & Information

    • 407-823-6312

  • Sam Kauffman, Central Florida Hillel

    • 407-362-3568

  • UCF Judaic Studies Program

  • Central Florida Hillel

  • Print this Page

University of Central Florida sophomore Heather Blackman is getting ready to go home just in time for Hanukkah, which begins Friday, Dec. 15.

I love Hanukkah, she said. I think it tends to get over-sensationalized because of its proximity to Christmas, but thats OK. Were still celebrating a miracle.

Blackman is one of about 4,500 UCF students -- about 10 percent of the universitys total enrollment -- who are Jewish. Shes minoring in Judaic Studies, a program that, along with the local Hillel organization, is thriving.

Whats happening at UCF is a reflection of the growing Jewish population in Florida. According to FloridaJewish.com, more than 750,000 Jews call the Sunshine State home. There are more than 17 synagogues and 21 Jewish organizations in Lake, Orange, Osceola and Seminole counties.

The growth in population and the strength of UCFs programs caught the attention of Union for Reform Judaism. It recently listed UCF as one of the nations top universities for Jewish students and opportunities for them to become involved.

UCF is one of four Florida public schools that Union for Reform Judaism ranked in the top 20 in the nation. The rankings also include the University of Florida and Florida International and Florida State universities.

Enrollment in Judaic Studies courses at UCF increased more than 30 percent over the past year, from 888 student credit hours in fall 2005 to 1,213 in fall 2006.

I think having the program is great, said Blackman, a journalism major who hopes to write about Israel when she graduates in a few years. I have a lot of classmates who are not Jewish, and its great to see them taking the time to learn and help end the ignorance out there about Judaism.

The Judaic Studies program, which celebrated its 20th anniversary in 2004, offers courses in Jewish history, the Hebrew Bible, literature and culture. Courses include Elementary Hebrew Language, History and Literature of the Holocaust and a course that focuses on Kabbalah, a form of Jewish mysticism.

Students enrolled in Judaic Studies can earn a minor or a certificate. The Judaic Studies Program may pursue the creation of an undergraduate major.

Central Florida Hillel, which serves students from UCF, Rollins, Stetson and surrounding community colleges, also is experiencing significant growth. Hillel is the largest Jewish campus organization in the world, and Central Florida Hillel works to increase the number of Jewish students connecting with other students who share their religion and culture.

We bring many cultural events such as comedians and musicians for students to enjoy, in addition to celebrating the holidays and festivals, said Program Director Samuel Kauffman. Now that the Jewish community understands how large the group is, they have made a conscious decision to support the organization, allowing it to grow and meet the needs of the students.

Central Florida Hillel estimates that more than 1,400 students signed in during different activities, with more participating in holiday celebrations.

As the university has grown and become a first-choice institution, the Jewish population has increased rapidly, Kauffman said.

Central Florida Hillel endorses MAJOR, Making a Jewish Occupational Relationship, a program designed to advance careers with the help of local Jewish professionals who act as mentors and speakers. The program creates job networking, shadowing and internship opportunities.

UCF also offers a Jewish fraternity and sorority. The Alpha Epsilon Pi fraternity was founded at UCF in 1997, and the universitys chapter has 50 members. Alpha Epsilon Phi, the Jewish sorority on campus, formed in 2005.

The Judaic Studies Program has offered a distinguished lecturers series for the past 22 years. This fall, Judaic Studies and Central Florida Hillel joined together, along with the Burnett Honors College, to host two seminars this fall with Uzi Avner, an archaeologist for the Arava Institute for Environmental Studies and the Eilat campus of Ben-Gurion University in Israel. Avner led two well-attended presentations in November. They were lectures on The Desert Roots of Ancient Israel and Standing Stones in the Desert.

Blackman said she hopes Judaic Studies continues to thrive and expand so more people will learn about Judaism. While Hanukkah isnt the most important day in the Jewish calendar, it sure can be fun, she added.

Blackman said she spends most of the eight days of the celebration with family, lighting the menorah during private celebrations.

One of those eight days we have a huge celebration, where everyone and their grandmother come to one family members house, she said. We eat, enjoy each others company, do a gift grab bag and celebrate the miracle. Its great. I love that. I cant wait for it this year.
