UCF Hosts First Spanish Orientation Session for Parents of Incoming Students

By Zenaida Gonzalez Kotala July 3, 2006

Photo: Jacque Brund

Orientation for parents includes walking tours of campus.

For the first time at the University of Central Florida, parents of students accepted into the university will have the option of attending orientation sessions in Spanish, beginning July 10.

There is a big push at UCF to ensure that students from all segments of the community have accurate and complete information about the university. Officials say they recognize how important the growing Hispanic community is and want to make sure they know they are wanted.  

We want parents to feel welcome, to know what a great university we have and the services their children have access to, said Joe Ritchie, director of Orientation Services.

Gloria Garcia, a Boca Raton parent, speaks English but she signed up for the Spanish orientation session on July 10-11.

I understand English, but I dont understand all the nuances of the language, she said in Spanish. And this is a very important time to understand every subtlety. Its the first time my daughter will be away from home and Im concerned about that. I want to know everything I can to help her and us with the transition.

Her daughter Emilia Coll, graduated from Spanish River High School and earned a scholarship. Garcia and her husband also put away money for her tuition through the Florida Prepaid College Plan.

They visited UCF and another Florida university. Garcia said they liked UCF best because they felt welcomed. And although it was not the deciding factor, she noted that the other university did not offer the Spanish option for parents. Students at UCF get the English version.

Ritchie got the idea for the bilingual sessions last year when he attended a conference. Representatives from Oregon State University were presenting how they operated their Spanish orientation and Ritchie thought, We should be doing that.  

As part of the session, parents will listen to presentations about everything from college expectations to campus safety. They will also learn about the services available to students while they are here, such as the resource and the health centers. A campus tour and time for questions and answers is also part of the itinerary.

UCF also translated its orientation handbook, which parents will get during the session. It includes information about parking fees, dining services, academics, deadlines and support services.

When parents leave theyll have lots of information they can share with their students and the names of people who speak Spanish and whom they can call, Ritchie said.

About 25 family members have signed up for the session. Ritchie expects that they are parents of students who have never left home or who perhaps are the first to attend college in their families.

Thats how we geared the program, Ritchie said. The English program is for a more general audience.

About 13 percent of the students at UCF are Hispanic. Officials in the department of admissions said that first-generation students tend to find it more difficult to understand how to get into a university and often parents do not know how it works either.

Families have to be proactive and students have to start early, said Gordon Chavis, assistant vice president for Undergraduate Admissions. Students cant be afraid to do that.

Chavis, the first in his family to earn a college education, said it is important that students and their families seek out information and prepare for college.

I think thats a real shocker for a lot of students -- that they have to take responsibility for their academic careers, Chavis said. And staying on top of that will also help them stay in school and succeed.

That being said, having parents clued into how the university works, helps students thrive, he said.
