UCF Sponsors 39th Annual Division of Planetary Sciences Conference in Orlando

By Zenaida Gonzalez Kotala Updated Oct. 8, 2007

Photo: Jerry Klein

Organizations and companies related to space exploration set up booths at the Division of Planetary Sciences Conference Monday. Many groups, like NASA, had multiple exhibits that showed some of the latest images available from probes and other spacecraft.

Dozens of students from Howard Middle School and Oak Hill Elementary School toured the exhibit hall at the Rosen Centre Hotel in Orlando on Monday, Oct. 8, as part of the UCF-sponsored Division of Planetary Sciences Conference.

Some students watched as representatives from NASA showed them images from Pluto and Saturn. Orlando Science Center representatives showed other students the magic that oxygen and a few combustible gases can do, much like the atmospheres of different planets.

"It's really cool," said Jessica Miranda, an eight-grade honor student at Howard Middle. "It's fun, and I've met some really, really cool people. I just met a scientist from Japan."

Miranda is among the 1,500 students expected to visit with scientists during the conference. It's part of the outreach program the Division of Planetary Sciences does each year.

"Sceince is important because of the implications for the future, but it also important because it can be used to motivate students to learn," said Humberto Campins, director of the Planetary Sciences Group at UCF and director of this year's DPS conference. "One university is getting help from the National Science Foundation, and it is using astronomy to get non-native English speakers motivated about learning English faster so they can get involved in all the exciting discoveries happening in science."

The weeklong conference draws about 1,000 scientists from around the globe. The astronomers present papers about their research and speak about common issues facing them, including technology and how to interest more women and younger people in science careers.

This years conference -- the first in Florida -- will run through Oct. 12 at the Rosen Centre Hotel on International Drive.

"This is an exciting moment for planetary sciences," Campins said. "And for UCF to have the opportunity to host the event is historic. It is another sign that we are growing and gaining a reputation of excellence in the field."

UCF has no astronomy degree program, yet it boasts eight faculty members who regularly publish research that has implications for interplanetary travel in the future. The faculty has received millions of dollars worth of grants from NASA and the National Science Foundation.

The university also has the largest public telescope in Florida and offers public viewings on the first and third Wednesday of each month. Many of the students who graduate with physics degrees with an emphasis in astronomy immediately pick up jobs with NASA and other companies such as Lockheed Martin.

"This is a wonderful opportunity for the students," said Susan A. Leeds, a science teacher at Howard Middle School in Orlando. She teaches sixth, seventh and eighth-graders science  and she brought her earth space honors classes to the conference.

"It's a great way to show them how much they can do and how much more there is to explore. And the presenters are wonderful. They are asking questions and getting the students to ask questions. It's not just a lecture and that's very engaging for the students."

For more information about the conference, visit http://physics.ucf.edu/DPS07/.

Here are some interesting facts about the conference:

-- Seven UCF planetary sciences professors and four students will present their research findings. Each professor has published research in a variety of highly respected scientific journals. The undergraduate students, Sara Navarro (junior, Carlsbad, N.M.), William Bowman (senior, Jacksonville), Kelsey Hargrove (senior, Satellite Beach) and Devon Clautice (senior) will present on a variety of topics from asteroids to extra-solar planets.

-- More than 1,000 schoolchildren from Orange County will visit the conference. They will see a room full of poster presentations that highlight the research findings from many of the conference attendees and participate in more than 10 interactive science stations. This is something DPS does every year to encourage children to seek careers in mathematics and science.

-- A large contingent of scientists from South America and Spain are expected at this years conference. One of UCFs professors works part-time in Spain using the largest telescope in the world. Hispanic astronomers are making names for themselves in the planetary sciences world. Early this summer Spaniards discovered the first planet outside the solar system that appears to be "Earth-like."

-- UCF is contributing to the local economy by hosting the DPS conference. According to the Orlando/Orange County Convention & Visitors Bureau, meeting visitors spent an "average of $858 per person per trip for an estimated economic impact of $3.40 billion in 2006." (http://www.orlandoinfo.com/research/meetings/groups.cfm )

-- NASA will contribute heavily to the conference, offering a variety of talks and workshops about topics such as writing winning grant proposals and a discussion about the latest technology NASA is considering for solar system exploration missions of the future. Visit http://physics.ucf.edu/DPS07/event-details.php?abnight#abnight for a complete schedule

-- Female scientists will discuss "Women in Leadership" during a lunch program from noon to 1:30 p.m. on Oct. 10.
