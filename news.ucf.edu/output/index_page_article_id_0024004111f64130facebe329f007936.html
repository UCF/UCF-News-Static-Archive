Dark, Funny Art at Flying Horse

By Susan Loden October 16, 2003

Photo: FHE Photos

An abstract by artist John O'Connor.

Bill Fick's images of expressive, dark, lumpy, oozing human heads and anthropomorphic animal figures follow the tradition of the medieval exaggerations of Hieronymus Bosch. His relief prints are scary, even odious, yet riveting, ironic and funny  a yucky kissing cousin of the political cartoon brazenly elevated to fine art, while playfully critiquing the enigma that is modern America. Fick will be artist-in-residence at Flying Horse Editions fine arts press at the University of Central Florida from Oct. 27 to Nov. 10. The North Carolina printmaker will add to his portfolio, which holds works with titles like, "Little Teenie Thumbknuckles; She Just Wants to Be Famous, Beautiful and Rich," and "The Happy Hog: Don't be Frightened, it's Only a Pig." Fick, director of Cockeyed Press, has been in about a dozen solo exhibitions, including shows at Show World, New York City; Duke University Institute of Arts; and Slocumb Galleries at East Tennessee State University. Among his many group exhibitions is Colorprint U.S.A. Fick's art is in the collections of Fogg Art Museum, Harvard University; Baltimore Museum of Art; Museum of Fine Arts Boston and more. He is a recipient of a National Endowment for the Arts Visual Arts Fellowship. He has taught or been a visiting artist at many institutions, including Pratt Institute, the University of Texas, Frogman's Print and Paper Workshop, Mason Gross School of Arts at Rutgers University, School of the Museum of Fine Arts Boston, and the University of Connecticut. Fick's bachelor's degree is from Duke University and his master's is from the University of North Carolina-Greensboro. For more than 13 years, the nonprofit Flying Horse has offered revered artists and writers the opportunity to work with Flying Horse Edition master printers, typesetters and bookbinders to create unique, prized graphics, while experimenting in the field and inspiring and interacting with UCF art and English students. "Our focus is on publishing works by leading contemporary artists and authors," says Flying Horse Editions Director Ryan Burkhart. "We bring them together with our faculty, Flying Horse partners  H2O Graphics, Hoopsnake Press, Gardyloo Handmade Books and Stone Wing Press  to create significant works of art at the university, while experimenting with ancient and modern methods. Our students and faculty are enriched by this research and it's a nice opportunity to add substantive pieces of art to our Art Department collection and to the Special Collections Department of the UCF Library." Private collectors and institutions through donations fund Flying Horse Editions projects. In return, patrons are given museum-quality prints and artists' books created by visiting artists. Donors are also invited to meet visiting artists at soirees in the Flying Horse Editions studio, gallery and reading room at 12479 Research Parkway, Central Florida Research Park, Orlando. Works by visiting artists and partner-artists is always on exhibit at no charge at Flying Horse Editions. The soiree with Bill Fick is scheduled from 7 to 10 p.m., Nov. 1. Abstract artist John O'Connor will follow Fick at Flying Horse, from Nov. 17 to Nov. 25, to play with math, statistics and analytic strategies in a Rube Goldberg manner. He unites artistry and intuition with complex systems to explore down-to-earth topics that challenge contemporary society  from earthquakes to hair loss, itching, memory loss and calorie burning. His pseudo-scientific, vivid  sometimes psychedelic  soft swirls and intriguing angles passionately embrace technology while winning the heart of high art. OConnor usually works in graphite and colored pencil on paper, with numbers and written text a part of or accompanying many works, connecting internal thought to the external environment. A resident of New York State, his work was chosen for the 2000 Peddie Schools Mariboe Gallery national, juried, group exhibit with five other artists. His "Untitled Z," a series of ovals surrounded by numbers and diagrams was said to dominate that exhibit and present diagrammatic systems "in an earnest, but suspiciously manic way." Flying Horse's latest artist's book, "Lonely Tylenol," is by Guggenheim Museum of Art prize-winning artist David Humphrey, a two-time National Endowment for the Arts recipient, and Sharon Mesmer, who has a New York Foundation for the Arts grant in poetry. By year's end or early in 2004, Humphrey and Mesmer will meet Flying Horse investor/collectors at a soiree for the release of their art book. "Lonely Tylenol" weds Humphrey's transparent drawings, water-based screen prints based on digital photos of his abstract paintings, and other digital photos to Mesmer's racy words to create, he says, "an urban romance, with somewhat bawdy, highly associative, comic imagery." "While contemporary (graphic arts) techniques may be faster, the quality of impression may not be as rich in texture and tone as with early methods," says Art Department Chair and Hoopsnake Press owner Ke Francis. "Handmade paper, real leather and a hand-stitched spine produce a feeling of quality that hasn't been surpassed by modern technology. We're choosing the best of classic or contemporary expression to expand into a new sphere where tactile qualities of embossment and rich application of ink can be applied to computer-generated imagery." Canadian "Pop Surrealist" Medrie MacPhee, winner of the American Academy in Rome Artist's Prize, and American arts writer Ted Mooney will craft a Flying Horse book in the spring. MacPhee, against a background of intense color, presents figures that are a mix of human and mechanical elements. Her humorous, wry, street-smart art breaks boundaries between nature and technology.
