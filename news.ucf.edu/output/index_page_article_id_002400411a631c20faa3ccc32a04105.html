Freshman Assignment: Brown vs. Board

By John McLain September 6, 2003

Related Contacts and Links:

  • John Schell, Undergraduate Studies

    • 407-823-4197

  • Print this Page

No other university campus in the United States is mounting a freshman assignment quite like it: An in-depth exploration of the landmark Supreme Court decision that ended segregation in American public schools and ushered in "equal protection of our laws."

UCF is commemorating the 50th anniversary of Brown vs. Board of Education by giving all 6,000 freshmen a living history lesson. Each incoming student got a two-page summary of Chief Justice Earl Warren's decision and 20 pages of related newspaper stories and editorials, pro and con. The meaning and impact of the historic law will was mentioned in 18 orientation sessions and during the university convocation Aug. 19.

John Schell, dean of undergraduate studies, says faculty will disperse into freshman housing for discussions in an across-the-curriculum project including professors in psychology, education, composition, speech, social work, history and political science.

UCF wants to include the entire community in this project, with speakers, debates, a documentary and film series, teach-ins, personal testimonials and other events. Schell, whose Office of Undergraduate Studies proposed the concept, says it will include class discussions in every discipline, guest speakers and films.

The kick-off event for this year's Brown v. Board undergraduate conversation will be the appearance of the top expert on this topic, Harvard Distinguished Professor of Education, Gary Orfield, Sept. 11, 7 p.m. in the Student Union.

Schell says assigning a traditional text wasnt practical because "our large and diverse student population, with widely varying interests, makes it nearly impossible to find one book that everyone will read." The page-long summary of the decision and backup material was distributed as:

* An e-mail attachment going to freshmen when theyre invited to one of the 18 orientation sessions held over the summer. 

* Through an online site where students can review the materials.

* 3,000 copies distributed in classrooms and dorms for discussions.

This two-year project on the decisions anniversary will weave its history across the curriculum, including many events outside class. The Program is being put together in cooperation with the UCF Metropolitan Center for Regional Studies, directed by Linda Chapin, which is planning community events to complement the university programming. Community groups involved in the area-wide initiative include the Wells Built Museum of African-American History, the Orlando Sentinel and the Orange County Regional History Center.
