Minority, First-Generation Scholarships Available

By Chad Binette January 13, 2004

Related Contacts and Links:

  • Print this Page

The University of Central Florida will participate this year for the first time in a federal program aimed at ensuring the success of minority and first-generation college students seeking doctoral degrees.

Twenty-five UCF students will be selected as Ronald E. McNair Scholars later this month. The students will be assigned to individual faculty mentors. Many of them will receive $2,800 stipends to help with summer research projects and will be able to take a research-related course one summer for free.

The main goal of the national effort named after astronaut Ronald E. McNair is to increase the number of minority students who get doctoral degrees, said John Schell, UCF's dean of undergraduate studies. Like other participating schools, UCF will keep track of McNair scholars after they get their bachelor's degrees to find out how many end up getting doctoral degrees.

Receiving a McNair scholarship can make it much easier for students to get into graduate school, Schell said. Recipients often stay in touch with each other after they enter graduate school, and those contacts also can help with career-related networking.

About 180 universities in the United States offer McNair scholarships. The group includes three other Florida schools: the University of Florida, Florida A&M University and Florida International University.

The program allows UCF to offer more help to minority students and allows the university to increase research opportunities for undergraduates, Schell said.

"We can serve our students better, and we hope this is going to help our undergraduate retention and graduation rates," Schell said.

Students who are African American, Hispanic, Native American or Alaskan natives can participate regardless of their families' incomes. White and Asian students are eligible if they are first-generation college students and can demonstrate a need for financial help.

UCF will hire a director and assistant director to help run the program on campus.

McNair was an astronaut aboard the Space Shuttle Challenger when it exploded in 1986. After the explosion, Congress funded the Ronald E. McNair Post-Baccalaureate Achievement Program to benefit minority students and low-income students whose parents didn't go to college.

McNair earned a doctoral degree in laser physics from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology in 1976. He was selected to join the space shuttle program in 1978.

To be eligible for a McNair scholarship, students must take courses full-time and either be U.S. citizens or permanent U.S. residents. They must have a cumulative GPA of 2.75, have completed 60 credit hours and have a serious interest in pursuing a doctoral degree. The deadline for applications is Jan. 20.

For more information, call 407-823-4197 or go to www.undergraduatestudies.ucf.edu and select McNair Scholars Program from the menu. Interested students also can send an e-mail to schell@mail.ucf.edu to receive an application.
