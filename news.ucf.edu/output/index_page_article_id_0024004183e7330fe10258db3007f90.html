Miss America Tells Graduates: 'Have Passion, Remember Roots, Vote'

By Tom Evelyn July 31, 2004

Photo: Tom Evelyn

Miss America 2004 Ericka Dunlap speaks to a reporter before addressing UCF students at commencement on Saturday.

Having achieved one of her lifelong goals before graduating college, Miss America 2004 Ericka Dunlap returned to the University of Central Florida on Saturday to tell fellow students who were graduating to be optimistic and follow your heart.

Dunlap, an advertising and public relations major at UCF, spoke to about 760 graduates from the College of Arts and Sciences and the College of Education. More than 2,600 UCF students signed up to graduate during the three ceremonies Saturday at the UCF Arena.

I understand what its like to feel the anticipation and await your name being called for you to move on to your next level of success, said Dunlap, who plans to resume her studies at UCF in January. She finishes her year as Miss America in September.

In a light-hearted but inspirational speech, Dunlap punctuated parts of her talk by asking several times, Can you hear me now? to which the audience, at her direction, would respond, You go girl.

Dunlap used her own story of never giving up on her childhood goal of becoming Miss America to make the first of her three points: Have a passion for your purpose.

Basically, it all boils down to conviction and following your gut, she said. Be optimistic and follow your heart.

Making her second point, Dunlap called on the graduates to remember your roots.

I do attribute much of my personal growth and  mature perspective to my experiences here at UCF, she said.

On her final point, urging the graduates to vote this November, Dunlap told the audience that commencement should mark the continuation of a commitment towards being progressive. So dont regress.

Dunlap has spent the past year speaking to audiences across the country about her platform, United We Stand. Divided We Fall Behind: Celebrating Diversity and Inclusion.

The first African-American to win the Miss Florida pageant, Dunlap plans to go to law school and become a sports and entertainment attorney. At UCF, she has served on the President's Leadership Council, whose members are selected for their excellence in leadership, scholarship and service to the university and the Orlando community.

UCF President John Hitt told graduates Saturday he was honored to know Dunlap and that she joined a growing list of superstar students at UCF who had wildly different dreams, such as Rhodes Scholar Tyler Fisher and former quarterback Daunte Culpepper.

You can fulfill those life dreams here at UCF, Hitt said.

Carolyn Roberts, chairwoman of the state Board of Governors, and state Sen. Daniel Webster, R-Orlando, also addressed graduates Saturday.

Roberts, who spoke to graduates of the College of Business Administration and the Rosen College of Hospitality Management, called UCF a leader of this dynamic community and praised the work of Hitt and his wife, Martha.

This university is getting bigger, and so is its reputation for greatness, she said.

Roberts called on the graduates to stay in Florida because the state needs them to be leaders in teaching, business and other fields. If students must leave the state, she urged them to start making plans to come back.

Webster, who spoke to graduates from the College of Engineering and Computer Science, the College of Optics and Photonics and the College of Health and Public Affairs, told students that they can become better leaders by learning the values of getting up early in the morning, serving others and reading.

Although no professors will be making you read, please keep reading, he said.
