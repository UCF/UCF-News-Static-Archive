UCF Group Preserves China's Lost Art for a New Audience Online

By Christine Dellert Aug. 7, 2008

Photo: Courtesy of UCF ChinaVine

UCF students and professors have traveled to China during the past year filming folk artists for the online ChinaVine project.

In rural villages and bursting cities, Erika Rydell searches for China’s living treasures.

She’s found them riding the back of an old motor bike and in cramped country homes, where generations of painters, kite makers and gourd carvers are keeping heritage alive in a country that’s constantly pushing forward.

Rydell is part of a group of University of Central Florida students and professors dedicated to preserving China’s ancient folk culture for a new generation using the Web.

They created ChinaVine, a project that places vanishing art forms and village life online. Its videos offer a glimpse of traditional shoemakers, woodblock painters and more, with detailed stories, descriptions and photos. ChinaVine aims to preserve Chinese folk culture for a modern, English-speaking audience.

The project is more than a year in the making, coinciding with preparations for the 2008 Beijing Olympics. Several of the artists ChinaVine documented online are already performing for athletes at the Beijing Olympic Village. Others may be featured during Friday's Opening Ceremony.

“With art, you don’t have to speak the same language,” said Rydell, a senior film major who spent nearly three weeks in China in March. Alongside about a half-dozen other film students, she documented painters, toy makers and dough artists in the Shandong countryside and city of Shanghai.

Their work appears on the ChinaVine Web site.

So much emphasis has been placed on folk art because of the Olympics, group members say. Still, the country is modernizing so rapidly that many Chinese growing up in the cities don’t know about traditional crafts. And the artwork is almost always new to the mainly English-speaking audience ChinaVine is trying to reach.

“What we want to do is communicate ideas visually, as much as we possibly can,” said Kristin Congdon, a film and philosophy professor leading the project. “And things there are changing, fast. That’s why it’s so important to go to China and document.”

The UCF team has traveled to China four times in the past year, with help from its partners at the University of Oregon and in China at Shandong University of Art and Design, where an extensive folk art collection sparked interest in the project.

Collaboration is key in a research effort this large, says Congdon, who was part of a similar interactive Web site about Florida folk artists several years back. ChinaVine is modeled after that project, FolkVine.org, and is attracting a lot of attention.

Another partnership is in the works with Beijing Normal University, and the ChinaVine team has received calls from Confucius institutions and folk art societies around the world.

The project is unique because it is “student-driven,” the team says. In all, about 50 humanities, digital media and film students and faculty at the three universities are involved. The students are responsible for almost all of the fieldwork.

During a May trip to China, Blair Remington, a junior and honors art-graphic design major, photographed not only artists, but the people, marketplaces and meals. “I was trying to document everything we saw,” she said, smiling. “They probably thought I was just a tourist.”

Most memorable, Rydell says, are the people.

“I was just taken aback by how friendly everyone was over there,” she said. “Even without knowing the language, we all have things in common. I convinced one man to let me ride on the back of his motorcycle through the village, filming. They were all so welcoming.”

UCF plans to host the president of Shandong University of Art and Design (SUAD) and four members of the SUAD ChinaVine team in October. The UCF team wants to take additional trips to more of China’s remote areas next year to continue the project.

ChinaVine also has plans to expand its Web site, adding more videos, interactive features and points of view from student travelers, says the project manager, Tomas Valladares. “We need to incorporate our voices into it,” the senior said.

The effort is about more than preserving culture and art. It’s also altering the perspectives of those involved.

That’s because the journeys and experiences are “life-changing,” Congdon says. “They are for me, for anybody who goes over.”
