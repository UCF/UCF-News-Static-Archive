Experts Discuss Ethics

By Chad Binette November 7, 2003

Photo: Jacque Brund

Ethics forum at Leu Gardens Friday.

Full Photo

Story Images

Click thumbnails for full-size images.

Related Contacts and Links:

  • Print this Page

Businesses would likely behave more ethically if they were required to change auditors periodically and if executives were held more accountable for wrongdoings, University of Central Florida professor Robert Ford said at a forum Friday.

Ford, a business management professor, joined Lt. Gov. Toni Jennings, retired Time Warner Inc. chief executive officer J. Richard Munro and several other UCF professors at a forum on ethics at Harry P. Leu Gardens.

About 220 people attended the forum, which was sponsored by the UCF Metropolitan Center for Regional Studies and the Department of Philosophy. The crowd included students from UCF, Orlando area high schools and one middle school.

Ford, Munro and UCF professors Jim Gilkeson and Mary Uhl-Bien discussed factors that created the business climate that produced recent scandals and possible reforms that could discourage unethical behavior in the future. Ford said unethical behavior has always existed in businesses but added that the creation of 24-hour television news stations has drawn more attention to them.

In addition to rotating auditors and increasing accountability, he said other ideas to improve ethics in business include more oversight of how boards of directors are selected and encouraging business leaders who are ethical about wrongdoings to raise more of a public outcry about those who are not.

Munro, who retired about 10 years ago, said he believes greed began to "creep in" more into the corporate world in the mid-1980s. Since then, he said, the salaries of chief executives have skyrocketed, and the media has begun to treat them like celebrities.

"Greed is not new, but it seems to have taken on proportions beyond belief," he said.

During the discussion on politics, Jennings said she believes its appropriate for political leaders, athletes and others in the spotlight to be held to higher ethical standards.

"Anyone whos a focus of young people should govern themselves as if everyone is watching," she said. "Thats important because young people are impressionable."

Richard Lapchick, program director for sports business management at UCF, said theres often a perception among athletes that its OK to bend rules to seek an edge as long as they arent caught. He said parents, who want their children to play professional sports or win scholarships, can encourage that mentality, as can coaches behind closed doors.

UCF womens basketball coach Gail Striegler stressed the community service work, such as reading to children, that her players and other athletes at UCF perform every week.
