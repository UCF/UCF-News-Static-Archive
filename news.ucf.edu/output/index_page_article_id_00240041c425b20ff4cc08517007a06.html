Diversity Week Kicks Off With Breakfast Featuring Spelman College President

By Chad Binette and Tom Evelyn Oct. 11, 2004

Photo: Jerry Klein

Beverly Daniel Tatum, president of Spelman College in Atlanta, was the keynote speaker Monday at the Diversity Breakfast.

Universities face a challenge in encouraging students to create inclusive communities where they interact with peers from different races, Spelman College President Beverly Daniel Tatum said Monday at the UCF Diversity Breakfast.

Tatum said many students arrive at college after growing up in homogenous neighborhoods and attending high schools that were not too diverse. Some students also arrive with misperceptions and stereotypes about other races that they picked up from relatives or on television, she said.

Its a wonder it (creating inclusive communities) happens at all, Tatum said. Were asking people to do something that theyve never experienced, for the most part.

Tatum, a psychologist who has studied racism and its effects in the classroom, was the keynote speaker at the Diversity Breakfast at the Student Union. The breakfast kicked off UCF's Diversity Week.

The 12th annual Diversity Week celebration, with a theme of Living the Vision, also features daily workshops and panel discussions and will conclude with the Indigenous Heritage Festival. Featured speakers include poet Cornelius Eady and author Michelle Malkin.

This weeklong event was designed to provide opportunities to explore various histories, cultures and issues of our local, national and world communities, said Valarie Greene King, director of Diversity Initiatives at UCF. From its inception, a primary focus during Diversity Week has been the promotion of communication and social justice as strategies to build a better world for humankind.

Tatum, a clinical psychologist whose areas of research include black families in white communities and racial identity in teens, has taught a course on the psychology of racism for more than 20 years. She has served as the president of Spelman, a historically black college for women in Atlanta, since 2002.

Tatum is the author of Why Are All the Black Kids Sitting Together in the Cafeteria? And Other Conversations About Race and Assimilation Blues: Black Families in a White Community.

Tatum urged UCF students, faculty and staff to affirm their identities, build communities and cultivate leadership. Its important to build communities where everyone is included, she said, and its critical for students to learn how to work with people who are different from them.

After Tatum spoke, King presented awards to Dick Nunis, chairman of the UCF Board of Trustees, President John Hitts office and the USPS Staff Council. She also honored Peter Ensenat, former associate director of Human Resources, and Stephen Levensohn, a former professor of religion, philosophy and humanities, for their support of diversity initiatives. Ensenat and Levensohn died during the past year.

Other speakers this week include Eady, the author of six books of poetry and the co-founder of Cave Canem, a summer workshop retreat for African-American poets. He will speak at 7:30 p.m. Wednesday, Oct. 13, in the Pegasus Grand Ballroom.

Malkin, a syndicated columnist and Fox News commentator, will speak at 3 p.m. Thursday, Oct. 14, in the Pegasus Grand Ballroom. She is the author of Invasion: How America Still Welcomes Terrorists, Criminals, and Other Public Menaces to Our Shores and In Defense of Internment: The Case for Racial Profiling in World War II and the War on Terror.

Eady and Malkins talks are free and open to the public, as are the other workshops and panel discussions.

Diversity Week will conclude with the Indigenous Heritage Festival from 10 a.m. to 7 p.m. Saturday, Oct. 16, at the Student Union. This free, inaugural event will bring together indigenous peoples of the world to reach out to the Central Florida community, according to the organizers.

For more information on Diversity Week and a complete schedule, go to www.diversity.ucf.edu.
