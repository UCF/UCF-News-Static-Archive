UCF Economist: Floridas Economic Future is Teetering but Should Be All Right

By Zenaida Gonzalez Kotala Oct. 1, 2007

Photo: UCF News File

Sean Snaith

Full Photo

Related Contacts and Links:

  • Sean Snaith

    • 407-823-1451

  • UCF's Institute for Economic Competitiveness

  • Print this Page

A hurricane or other unforeseen event could send Floridas economy into a tailspin, but the outlook for 2009 and 2010 is better if the state can weather the storm this year, UCF economist Sean Snaith said in a forecast released today.

Another Katrina type of event would be enough to push a teetering economy over the brink of recession, said Snaith, author of the Florida/Metro Economic Forecast.

Snaith is the director of the University of Central Floridas Institute for Economic Competitiveness. He recently was named the most accurate forecaster in California for his work there.

The report includes highlights and a quarterly forecast for Florida and 12 metropolitan areas through 2010 and long-term annual forecasts through 2036. The metropolitan areas are: Ocala, Orlando, Palm Bay-Melbourne, Pensacola, Tallahassee, Tampa-St. Petersburg, Gainesville, Deltona-Daytona, Jacksonville, Lakeland, Miami/Fort Lauderdale and Naples. 

The full report can be seen at http://www.bus.ucf.edu/hitec/Forecasts.htm.

Snaith pondered whether or not the Federal Reserves decision on Sept. 18 to reduce the interest rate will do more harm than good in the long run because of the potential for higher inflation. But, barring other suprises, he expects that once past 2007, things will slowly get better beginning in 2008.

After enduring a substantial deceleration caused by the slowing housing sector and the financial repercussions of the sub-prime meltdown and credit crunch, the economy will begin to accelerate slowly in 2008, Snaith said. 

Some sectors, such as construction, will take longer to heal than others, he added.

The rest of 2007 and 2008 will be tough for home sellers, much like 2004-2005 was tough for buyers. Snaith calls it market karma, but he doesnt predict the housing market will crash.

If you are waiting until this occurs to buy, you will spend the rest of your life as a renter, he said.

Orlando Summary

The Orlando MSA is expected to experience one of the highest growth rates in personal income at a rate of 7.0 percent. Additionally, the area is expected to achieve high levels of employment and population growth at 3.2 percent and 2.7 percent, respectively.

Payroll employment growth is expected to be the highest in the professional and business services and education and health services sectors, at average annual growth rates of 6.4 percent and 3.1 percent, respectively.

Here are some Florida highlights from the forecast:

    Construction employment will shrink in 2007 and 2008 but will rebound in 2009-2010.

    Unemployment will rise slowly to 3.9 percent in 2008 before falling back to 3.7 percent by 2010.

    Surges in energy prices and the housing slowdown will pull down economic growth in 2007 to 3.8 percent, but real disposable income is expected to grow an average of 4.7 percent per year from 2008 to 2010.

    Retailers can expect the slowest growth in retail spending since 2001 with a rate of just 3.84 percent in 2007. Things will slowly pick up again beginning in 2008.

    High-growth sectors are forecast to be professional and business services and transportation, warehousing and utility, education and health services and leisure

    The Gross State Product will grow at an average annual rate of 5.9 percent from 2007 to 2010

Snaith is a national expert in economics, forecasting, market sizing and economic analyses. He authors quarterly reports about the economy. He is a member of several national forecasting panels, including the Western Blue Chip Economic Forecast panel, the National Association of Business Economics Quarterly Outlook Survey Panel, the Federal Reserve Bank of Philadelphias Survey of Professional Forecasters, Bloomberg U.S. Economic Indicator Survey and USA Today Economic Survey Panel.

The UCF Institute for Economic Competitiveness mission is to expand public understanding of the economy by convening business leaders, scholars, policy makers, civic groups and media to discuss critical issues.
