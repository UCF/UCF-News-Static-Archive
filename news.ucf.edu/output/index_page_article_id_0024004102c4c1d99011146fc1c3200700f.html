Joust Winners to Launch Magazine, Pilot Training Companies

By Zenaida Gonzalez Kotala April 14, 2007

Photo: Courtesy of Center for Entrepreneurship and Innovation

David Brim (left) and Scott Wall (right) won the Joust business plan competition this year. Cameron Ford (center) presented them with the winners' check.

Full Photo

Related Contacts and Links:

  • Cameron Ford, the Joust

  • Center for Entrepreneurship & Innovation

  • Print this Page

Central Floridians who want to rent-to-own a home will have a new real estate magazine at their disposal thanks to UCF students David Brim and Scott Wall.

The business and finance majors won this years Joust competition at the University of Central Florida on April 13. The Joust is a contest open to any UCF student. Students present a business proposal and make a pitch to a panel of judges who are business leaders in the community.

The winner gets $5,000 and a year at UCFs Technology Incubator in the Central Florida Research Park, which helps new businesses grow and mature. The goal of the competition is to create a culture of entrepreneurship in the UCF community and to create partnerships with regional business and government leaders interested in improving the Central Florida economy.

Brim and Wall pitched their real estate magazine as a way to fit a growing niche in the local area. As lenders become stricter about who qualifies for loans, more people who may not qualify under traditional criteria are looking for ways to buy their homes. The two students pitched their idea for Marketing Done Smart, the company that plans to launch the direct-mail magazine.

The second-place team said it would use the $2,500 it earned to fill a different kind of niche. Precision Aviation will train pilots who want to earn their sport-pilot license. The license, approved by the Federal Aviation Administration in September 2004, has fewer requirements and is less expensive to obtain than a traditional pilots license. It is granted only for lightweight flying vehicles such as gliders and small planes that have two seats and fixed landing gear. The health requirements are much less stringent as well. Anyone who is healthy enough to have a drivers license can qualify for a sport-pilot license.

The team of students that pitched Precision Aviation included undergraduates Andrew Chan, Wendy Harrington, Monika Escobar, Phil Brooks and Jason Hulse. Cameron Ford, the director of the Center for Entrepreneurship and Innovation who introduced the Joust to UCF a few years ago, said this team intends to launch its business with the cash prize.

They are very excited, Ford said. Thats one of the exciting things about this years competition. A lot of the teams dont require a lot of capital to launch.

Ford said the judges had a tough time deciding who among the 12 semi-finalists would make it to the final round and then picking the winner.

The proposals this year were really diverse, and the competition was fierce, Ford said.

The proposals were judged on several factors, including the quality of the service or product, financial feasibility and presentation skills. A total of $10,000 was up for grabs. Seasons 52 restaurant, part of the Darden Corporation, sponsored the $10,000 in cash prizes presented to the finalists.

The third-place team earned $1,500. Rob Wolfe and Akram Abdul-Caer, MBA students at UCF, will use the money to operate Apharee Publications. The company will translate and publish authentic, classical Islamic texts in English.

Fellow MBA students Mike Radu, Geoff Dombrowski and Brian Lutchman plan to use their $1,000 prize to launch Wok-On-In, a healthy, fast noodle restaurant. Dishes will include noodles with exotic and fragrant sauces and ingredients, according to the teams presentation.

This year was fantastic, Ford said. And for the first time, undergraduates took the top two places.
