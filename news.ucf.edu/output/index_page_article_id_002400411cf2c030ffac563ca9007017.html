Anthropology Professor Solves Mummy Mysteries on Discovery Show

By Chad Binette Dec. 10, 2004

Photo: Atlantic Productions

UCF anthropology professor John Schultz is one of five "mummy investigators" on the Discovery Channel's "Mummy Autopsy."

UCF anthropology professor John Schultz is accustomed to helping law enforcement agencies locate bodies that have been missing for a few years and determining the causes of their deaths.

As one of five mummy investigators on a new television show on the Discovery Channel, Schultz tries to solve the mysteries surrounding the deaths of people who have been dead for hundreds or even thousands of years.

Mummy Autopsy premiered Dec. 7, and will air for 12 more weeks at 9 p.m. Tuesdays. Schultz, a forensic anthropologist, is featured in the first five episodes and several of the final eight shows.

During the first episode, Schultz and James Murrell, a doctoral student from the University of New Mexico, examined the body of an Asian man who died on an island off of the coast of Peru in the 1830s. The island was a quarantine center for people whom Peruvian authorities believed had the plague.

With help from a historical clothing expert and two pathologists, Schultz and Murrell found that the man had a painful buildup of fluids in both lungs and a parasite native to China in his liver. They also found that he probably would have survived those ailments had he not been sent to the island, where he contracted another parasite that likely killed him.

The Discovery Channel will replay the first hourlong episode, which features Schultz in the second half hour, at 3 p.m. Saturday, Dec. 11. While most of Schultzs segment was filmed in Peru, the show includes a few scenes from the UCF campus.

In future episodes, Schultz will examine the partially mummified bodies of two soldiers from the 1879 War of the Pacific and a mummy discovered in the Andean foothills of Peru.

I appreciated the way that they wanted to integrate the science and focus on it, Schultz said. Its very important that they see it as science.

Schultz, who was recommended for the show by one of his former professors at the University of Florida, spent four weeks in Peru this summer to film five segments of Mummy Autopsy. He traveled to England, Ireland and Wyoming for other episodes.

The investigators can draw some conclusions at the filming sites, such as the estimated ages of victims and whether there appear to be signs of trauma. They sometimes have to await the results of laboratory tests to reach conclusions about how the victims probably died.

The show teaches historical lessons in addition to explaining how researchers can draw conclusions based on skeletons, organs, clothing and artifacts. The first episode, for example, discussed Chinese immigration to Peru during the early 19th century and the governments decision to send to the quarantine island anyone who arrived at the countrys border appearing badly ill.

Schultz began teaching at UCF in fall 2003. He teaches classes in forensic anthropology and biological diversity. He occasionally assists the Orange County Sheriffs Office and Orange County Medical Examiners Office in locating bodies and determining causes of death. The Sheriffs Office gave him a commendation in the spring for helping to find and excavate the bodies of two murder victims, one of whom was buried for 15 years.

Schultz and colleague Tosha Dupras are helping to train law enforcement officials in forensic archaeology methods. The Department of Sociology and Anthropology received a grant from the Sheriffs Office, which the UCF College of Arts and Sciences matched, to help pay for a ground-penetrating radar system. Schultz uses the system for forensic research and to locate the graves of murder victims.

Schultz wrote his doctoral dissertation on trying to improve techniques for using ground-penetrating radar, which he either harnesses to his body or places on a cart, to find hidden bodies. His research involves burying pig cadavers, changing the conditions of the grave, and then returning to those sites regularly to determine how computer images of the graves taken by the radar system change over time as the cadavers decompose and dirt in the grave compacts.
