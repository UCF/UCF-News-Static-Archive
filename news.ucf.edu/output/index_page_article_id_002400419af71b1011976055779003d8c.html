UCF Environmental Center Keeps Campus Green, Species Alive

By Brandon Bielich April 22, 2008

Photo: Brandon Bielich

The Arboretum, a 12-acre bio-geographical garden, showcases native plants from regions such as Southeast Asia and Japan.

For the UCF Environmental Center, keeping the campus “green” means more than planting trees. The center manages 800 acres of UCF land, improves storm-water drainage and preserves the habitats of more than 45 animal species, all while increasing sustainability.

The center maintains several sections of the UCF campus, including the Arboretum, a 12-acre bio-geographical garden that showcases native plants from regions such as Southeast Asia and Japan. The Arboretum, located between the Harris Corporation Engineering Center and CREOL, is open to the public.

“We want people to feel the essence of these regions as they walk through our gardens,” said Alaina Bernard, assistant director and environmental steward at the UCF Environmental Center. “We also want to educate the community and show people that they can have the same type of technique and style as a Japanese garden, while using native species” such as azaleas, Bernard said.

The Natural Areas, located across from Parking Garage C, feature multipurpose trails allowing opportunities for playing disc-golf, hiking, walking, and learning about native ecosystems.

The course offers a fun and educational experience for serious and recreational players. Each hole has a learning station, including a map of the hole and facts about nature and the environment. The black-and-gold disc-golf mats are made from recycled tires, a subtle example of UCF’s sustainability.

The newly constructed Pole Barn accents the south entrance to the Natural Areas’ 40 acres of conservation land and provides information about recreational opportunities in the green space on campus. The Pole Barn was made from recycled materials donated by COD Trees Inc. of Oviedo. Its surface edges are pervious concrete, which means storm-water drains through it, instead of resting on top of it. 

“If the rain is hitting concrete, it can’t permeate into the ground,” Bernard said. “That pervious material is important.”

Aiming to be good stewards of UCF’s watershed, the Environmental Center is working with Landscape and Natural Resources to improve retention ponds on campus. All of the storm-water on campus is filtered into those ponds.

About 75 percent of UCF’s storm-water drains into the CREOL Pond, adjacent to the nature trails. To help filter the water, the university installed a Baffle Box, a machine that collects trash to prevent it from entering the pond.

Bernard and volunteers from Focus the Nation recently planted 50 donated trees around the pond, and those trees will provide a more natural filtration approach. As they grow, the trees will stabilize the banks of the pond so the Environmental Center can add other plants to further enhance the filtration of storm-water and create new habitats for aquatic species.

There are 47 identified amphibian, bird and mammal species on the UCF campus; the state of Florida lists 19 of them as threatened, species of special concern or endangered.

Environmental Center staff and volunteers restore damaged habitats in addition to creating new ones.

Conducting prescribed burns helps fire-dependent trees and species. Long-leaf pines have cones that drop and open up with intense fire. Without fire, those trees cannot grow.

Fire also allows native grasses to grow, and it reduces the density of palmettos, a non-native species that is typical in an upland pine habitat. The grasses enable the endangered gopher tortoise to move through the habitat and build burrows, which they share with other species.

“Each species has a unique role in this world and the loss of that species would be catastrophic,” Bernard said. “There’s no replacing them; once they’re gone, they’re gone. Whatever system is messed up by the loss of the species will not be replaced.

“For me, it goes beyond the right thing to do. It’s the necessary thing to do.”

UCF’s sustainability efforts will serve as a role model for other national institutions.

The Association for the Advancement of Sustainability in Higher Education selected UCF to take part in its STARS – Sustainability Tracking, Assessment, and Rating System – program this year.

STARS is a self-assessment plan that will monitor UCF’s sustainability progress and compare it to other institutions, establishing a baseline for sustainability to implement throughout the country.

The program is similar to LEED – Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design – green certification, except LEED applies to a single building, and STARS will include the entire UCF campus.
