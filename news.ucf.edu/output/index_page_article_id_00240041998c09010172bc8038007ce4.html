UCF to Host Premiere of Film on Caribbean Architecture, Culture

By Sae Schatz Jan. 27, 2005

Photo: Gladstone Yearwood

Chattel houses in Barbados are an integral part of director Gladstone Yearwood's film, which will be shown Feb. 2 at the Downtown Media Arts Center.

Chattel House, a documentary about Barbados architecture and culture that University of Central Florida Professor Gladstone Yearwood directed, will be shown for the first time in the United States on Wednesday, Feb. 2, at the Downtown Media Arts Center in Orlando.

 

Sponsored by UCFs Latin American, Caribbean, and Latino Studies program, the screening will begin at 6:30 p.m. and will be followed by a question-and-answer session with Yearwood, who is a UCF film professor and the director of the universitys Zora Neale Hurston Film Institute. The screening at the Downtown Media Arts Center, 39 S. Magnolia St., is free and open to the public.

 

Chattel House begins with images of still-standing slave houses in Barbados and then follows the history of Caribbean-style architecture. The film chronicles development and change in Barbadian cultural history and draws on the voices of Barbadians who tell their own stories. The world premiere for the film was in October at the Third Festival of African and Caribbean Film at the University of the West Indies in Barbados.

 

An active filmmaker since the 1980s, Yearwood has taught at universities in the United States, Canada, Africa and the Caribbean. His work emphasizes the stories of marginalized groups.

 

Film scholars worldwide describe Yearwoods early book, Black Cinema Aesthetics: Issues in Black Independent Filmmaking, as an important work that helped establish the black independent film movement. Yearwoods recent book, Black Film as a Signifying Practice: Cinema, Narration and the African American Aesthetic Tradition, examines the practices and systems of narration in independent cinema.

 

Yearwood holds a bachelors degree from New York Universitys Institute of Film and Television and masters and doctoral degrees from Ohio University.

 

Chattel House is the first of three films to be presented this spring in the New Caribbean Documentary series sponsored by UCFs Latin American, Caribbean, and Latino Studies program. The second film in the series, The Agronomist, directed by Jonathan Demme, is a stunning account of Jean Dominique, the Haitian agronomist who bought a radio station in the early 1960s and became a leading voice of the popular democratic movement in Haiti. Dominique was assassinated in 2000 at the radio station. The films original soundtrack was developed by famous Haitian musician Wyclef Jean.

 

Life and Debt, the final film in the series, is directed by Stephanie Black and focuses on contemporary Jamaica. Life and Debt tells the stories of Jamaicans whose day-to-day lives are shaped by U.S. and other foreign economic agendas. The film includes interviews with World Bank officials and former Prime Minister Michael Manley, along with excerpts from the award-winning nonfiction book A Small Place" by Jamaica Kincaid.

 

For more information, call 407-823-1173 or go to www.cas.ucf.edu/LACLS.
