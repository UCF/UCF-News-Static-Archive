East Asia Expert: Future of China, Taiwan Relations Appears Positive

By Jessica Gagnon Feb. 19, 2010

Photo: Mark Freeman

Minxin Pei

Full Photo

Minxin Pei, a leading expert on East Asia, discussed the ups and downs of relations between China and Taiwan and described why the future appears positive Thursday at the University of Central Florida. The presentation to about 100 people, organized by the UCF Global Perspectives Office, was part of the UCF China-Taiwan Cross-Strait Program.

Pei, director of the Keck Center for International and Strategic Studies at Claremont McKenna College in California, recently wrote his second book, “China’s Trapped Transition: The Limits of Developmental Autocracy.” He also is an adjunct senior associate in the China Program at the Carnegie Endowment for International Peace.

Pei argued that any insight into the future of relations between China and Taiwan must stem from an understanding of the turbulent history of their bilateral relations. Since the Nationalists took over the island in 1949 after losing the mainland to the Communists during China’s civil war, Beijing has claimed Taiwan as its territory, while Taipei has followed its own path of political and economic development, Pei said. The United States has played a key role in this relationship over the last 60 years, he added, often providing military assistance to keep China from forcibly reclaiming Taiwan.

These tensions provoked China and emboldened Taiwan to the point of imminent danger, according to Pei, until a pivotal change in Taiwanese governance in 2008 saw a “ceasefire” of sorts. Under these circumstances, we see and can hope for further positive cooperation between the two, he said. 

Taiwan President Ma Ying-jeou, who has spent the last two years building better relations with China, was democratically elected by a majority of voters to seek peace and growth over independence, Pei said.

Pei asserted that Taiwan’s economic dependence on China for half of its exports, and Beijing’s efforts to forge free trade agreements with other countries in the region, brings about the economic reality that Taiwan will not prosper without closer ties to China. As a result, an accord called the Economic Framework for Cooperation Agreement is being negotiated to ensure the island’s future livelihood.

Drawing from the accord and other gestures, such as China’s agreement to allow Taiwan observer status at the World Trade Organization, Pei concluded that there is a firm foundation for peaceful cooperation between the two and a bilateral consensus to preserve the status quo. 

When asked by a student why the subject of Taiwan is such a sensitive issue for China, Pei answered, “That is something only a Chinese person would truly understand.” He explained that economic prominence and international prestige mean little if Beijing is unable to exert control over such a relatively small island that has been a source of political strife since the founding of the People’s Republic of China. 

With President Ma and China’s President Hu Jintao committed to improving relations, only a political change in Taiwan or an instance of China “getting greedy” – both of which Pei finds unlikely – could derail the positive changes that have occurred and should continue to unfold.

In addition to the Global Perspectives Office, sponsors of the Pei program included the UCF China-Taiwan Cross-Strait Program, C.T. Hsu and Associates, the UCF Political Science Department, the UCF International Services Center, UCF LIFE and the Global Connections Foundation.
