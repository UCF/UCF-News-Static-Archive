UCF's Movie Magic Talent Helps to Improve Reading Among Tweens Nationwide

By Zenaida Gonzalez Kotala Oct. 28, 2008

Photo: Zenaida Gonzalez Kotala

UCF professors Robert Kenny from Digital Media and Glenda Gunter from the College of Education give students at Lakeview Middle School in Winter Garden a lesson on making movie trailers out of their books.

Unmotivated readers are a huge challenge, especially in middle school.

But two University of Central Florida professors have figured out how to use the movie-making power of Hollywood to entice the tween crowd to read and learn more.

Robert Kenny, an assistant professor of Digital Media, and Glenda Gunter, an associate professor in the College of Education, have partnered to create Digital Booktalk. The Web-based multi-media program introduces middle and high school students to using book trailers to select books to read and shows them how to make their own digital book trailers.

The students use computers, editing equipment and their imaginations to turn their required reading into some pretty hip book trailers – some rivaling Hollywood quality. In November students at Lakeview Middle School in Winter Garden got a lesson from Kenny and Gunter on the structure of a book and how to turn it into a trailer.

The idea began as a class project at the university in early 2003 and then with a few K-12 classes in Orange County Public Schools. Since then, it has mushroomed into a national project with its own Web site (www.digitalbooktalk.com) that gets several thousand hits each month. Digital Booktalk recently garnered some education awards, and the two professors frequently discuss the project with national and international audiences.

Students have turned almost 50 books into trailers. The books include classics such as “Crime and Punishment” by Fyodor Dostoevsky and “Dracula” by Bram Stoker and modern tales such as “Twilight” by best-selling author Stephanie Meyer and “My Sister’s Keeper” by Jodi Picoult. With the addition of the UB Director Curriculum Model software on the Web site, students across the country can create their own trailers and submit them to be posted on the site. UCF faculty will review each one and consider it for inclusion on the Web site.

Digital Booktalk merges the technology with a curriculum that encourages kids to read and translates into better reading comprehension.

Several schools in Orange, Pinellas and Seminole counties have participated. Because the trailers are available on the Web site, students across the country also benefit from the project.  A book trailer may be just what they need to become interested in a reading a book.

“The kids get so excited watching the book trailers, and they have a fantastic time creating them,” Kenny said. “It’s a win for the students and it helps teachers because the more students read, the more they learn.”

Christine Roberts, a teacher at Lakeview Middle said the trailers help her children to become better critical readers.

"They are reading at a much deeper level now," Roberts said. "It's a wonderful tool to teach good reading skills that translate into other subject areas."

Gunter said that children who grew up in the digital age naturally process visual information much easier and faster.

“By using a student’s natural desire and technology experience, we are creating readers,” Gunter said.

Their research shows that some students develop reading anxiety.  By helping them develop their visualizing skills, the fear of reading is removed, Gunter added.

Results of the effectiveness of the program in motivating readers has been published in several academic journals, including Contemporary Issues in Technology and Teacher Education earlier this year.
