Nobel Prize Winner Discusses Genetic Breakthrough During Speech at UCF

By Shobana Daniell Dec. 12, 2005

Photo: Karl X. Chai

Kary Mullis spoke to more than 250 UCF students and faculty members on Friday.

Full Photo

Related Contacts and Links:

  • Burnett College of Biomedical Sciences

  • Kary Mullis' Web site

  • Nanotechnology at UCF

Kary Mullis, the 1993 Nobel Prize winner in chemistry, described to a UCF audience Friday his invention of a process that solved a central problem in genetics by quickly replicating DNA.

Mullis developed polymerase chain reactions that can quickly make thousands of copies of specific DNA strands. The process greatly accelerated research in biomedical science and has multiple applications in biotechnology, medical diagnostics, genetics and forensic science.

Mullis began his speech with a recent USA Today news report that recounted a story about a man who was sentenced to life in prison 24 years ago on kidnapping and rape charges. The man was recently released based on DNA research that used Mullis methods. In a similar case, another man released because of new DNA evidence was quoted as saying, I dont know who this Mullis character is, but I would like to buy him a cocktail.  

said he is especially happy when this method rights the wrongs of the judicial system by freeing innocent prisoners with DNA evidence.  Another popular use involves recreating fossil remains of insects that existed millions of years ago. The book Jurassic Park was based on this work. 

Mullis spoke to more than 250 students and faculty members at the Live Oak Room in a presentation sponsored by the UCF Nanoscience Technology Center with the support of the Metro Orlando Economic Development Commission. Mullis recently agreed to serve on the External Advisory Board for the Nanoscience Technology Center.

Conceptualized in 1983, polymerase chain reactions can generate 100 billion similar molecules in a single afternoon beginning with only one molecule of genetic material, Mullis said. The old methods were error-prone, slow and expensive, he said, and it would take seven people in his lab five months to purify the genetic material. 

Mullis said he got the idea for polymerase chain reactions while driving in Northern California on Highway 128. 

At mile marker 46, I stopped the car and walked around thinking and then rummaged around the glove compartment for some paper and a pen to write the idea of replicating a single spec of DNA material, he said before displaying a picture of a weathered road sign given to him by his friends to mark his discovery.  

As a graduate student, Mullis wavered between physics and chemistry but made a practical decision to major in biochemistry because I knew the cold war would end, but the legislators and politicians would always be concerned about their health, and therefore funding would be plentiful in the biomedical sciences. 

He said he is most proud of his first published work, titled Cosmological Significance of Time Reversal, which was published in Nature magazine in 1968. Mullis is the author of several publications in a wide range of subjects, including biochemistry, global warming, astronomy, parapsychology and the HIV virus.

Mullis said he encountered many obstacles from fellow scientists and journals in publishing his work on polymerase chain reactions. He noted that scientists often are not interested in novel work and are slow to appreciate others novel contributions. 

Anyone who walks into a lab at UCFs Burnett College of Biomolecular Sciences, however, will see polymerase chain reaction machines replicating single strands of DNA. Li Mei Chen, who attended the lecture, said she is using the process to clone cancer cells for research. 

Mullis, a native of South Carolina, received his doctoral degree in biochemistry from the University of California at Berkeley in 1972. In 1979, he joined Cetus Corp. in Emeryville, Calif., as a DNA chemist. During his seven years there, he worked on his Nobel Prize winning work on polymerase chain reactions. Later, he worked for other biotechnology companies in Southern California.

Mullis holds several patents and the latest application is for transforming the immune system to defend against invading pathogens and toxins. Based on this work on immunology, he has formed a company called Altermune, LLC. 

Mullis autobiography is entitled Dancing Naked in the Mindfields, and he enjoys surfing on the beaches of California in his spare time.
