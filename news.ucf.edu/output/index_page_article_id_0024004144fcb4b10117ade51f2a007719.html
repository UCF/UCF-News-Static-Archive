Retired Military, Foreign Service Officer Discusses Dissent

By Mark Freeman Jan. 31, 2008

Photo: Courtesy of UCF Global Perspectives

Ann Wright is a retired U.S. Army colonel and former career Foreign Service officer.

Ann Wright, a retired U.S. Army colonel and former career Foreign Service officer, asked a UCF audience of more than 100 Tuesday, “If the government asks you to do criminal things when you know they are wrong, should you do it?”
 
Wright is the author of “Dissent: Voices of Conscience,” which she describes as “a series of profiles of men and women that had concerns about certain government policies and spoke out against them.”

Wright spent 29 years in the military, retiring as a colonel in the U.S. Army, before working an additional 16 years in the U.S. Department of State as a career Foreign Service officer.  Her overseas assignments included serving as U.S. deputy ambassador in Sierra Leone, Micronesia, Afghanistan and Mongolia, as well as other stints in Somalia, Kyrgyzstan, Grenada, Micronesia and Nicaragua.

On March 19, 2003, the eve of the U.S.-led intervention in Iraq, Wright sent a letter of resignation to then-Secretary of State Colin Powell, stating that without the authorization of the United Nations Security Council, the invasion and occupation of a Muslim, Arab, oil-rich country would be a disaster. As Wright told the audience in the Student Union, “We did not have the authorization of the U.N. Security Council, and despite what the administration said about WMDs, they were not there and we had no right to go in.”

Her book, “Dissent,” highlights the stories of many people that have followed the same sort of path while in government service. Wright stated that speaking out is hard for people within governmental positions to do because “whistle-blowing dissent is dangerous…there are people that commit criminal acts and don’t want to be exposed.” 

She also highlighted the contrasting cultures within government between the United States and Britain by stating that four times as many people resigned from positions within the British government because of the Iraq war than within the U.S. government.

Wright ended her presentation by fielding questions from students and members of the Central Florida community.

She responded to one question by saying, “Coming from 29 years in the military, you can probably tell I’m not a pacifist. As a realist, sadly I think we are a nation that will use military force.  I’d like to think that we won’t always use it first, but have it in reserve.”

When asked by a student whether or not she would encourage young people to join the military, Wright responded, “As much as respect the military, I would encourage young men and women to not join.”

In addition to the UCF Global Perspectives Office, sponsors of the forum included the UCF Political Science Department, the UCF Middle Eastern Studies Program, the UCF Diplomacy Program and the Global Connections Foundation.
