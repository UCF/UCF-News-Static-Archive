UCF Survey: Latinos are Purple Voters Who Dont Cast Ballots Along Party Lines

By Zenaida Gonzalez Kotala Oct. 31, 2006

Related Contacts and Links:

  • Annabelle Conroy

    • 407-823-5082

  • Zenaida Gonzalez Kotala

    • 407-823-6120

  • UCF Political Science Department

  • Print this Page

Most Latinos dont vote along political party lines. In fact, party affiliation is the least important factor they consider when casting their ballots, according to a University of Central Florida political scientists survey.

The survey was conducted earlier this year with a sample of Latinos in Osceola County who voted during the 2000 and 2004 elections. A team of undergraduate students led by UCF assistant professor Annabelle Conroy interviewed more than 200 residents, including whites, Hispanics and African-Americans. The survey was a pilot project that lays the groundwork for a larger survey and provides a good indication of basic trends among Latino voters, Conroy said.

Conroy labeled Latino voters purple because they dont vote red or blue, the traditional colors associated with the Republican and Democratic parties respectively. Because of the lack of party loyalty, candidates may want to reach out more directly to them than to other voters, she said.

The main lesson is that Latinos are not beholden to one party, Conroy said. They tend to vote on issues. In this sense, they are purple voters, and candidates need to address the issues that affect their communities if they wish to count on their votes.

Many reports have looked at large Latino voting blocs, including Cuban-Americans and Mexican-Americans. This survey looked at Central Florida Latino voters, who are a complex combination and likely more representative of other parts of the country.

Conroy surveyed voters in Florida House District 49, which straddles Orange and Osceola counties and is made up of 42.4 percent Latinos, including people from Cuba, Mexico, Puerto Rico and other countries. Among the Latino voters, 43.5 percent are registered Democrats, 31.5 percent are Republicans and 21.1 percent belong to minor parties or are not affiliated with any political party.

The report asked two main questions: Are Latinos significantly different than other voters? In what ways are they different. and what are the implications of this for future elections?

According to the survey, the most important issue for Latinos was the war in Iraq (28.8 percent), followed by the economy (24.4 percent) and crime (11 percent). For white non-Hispanics, the most important issue also was the war in Iraq (42.2 percent), followed by the economy (26.5 percent). Neither group mentioned morals or immigration as a significant issue, Conroy said.

Conroy also concluded that Latinos vote less often than other groups because they are not integrated socially into either major political party; there is little direct contact between Latino voters and candidates; and few candidates direct their message to Latino voters using Spanish media, which many Latino voters use to access information.

Some important factors to consider when surveying Latino voters include:

-- 39 percent of adult Latinos in the United States are not citizens and cannot vote.

-- The Latino population is young. Although the population of Latinos in the United States increased by 5.7 million between the 2000 and 2004 elections, 30 percent of Latinos in 2004 were younger than 18 and cannot vote.

Puerto Ricans are a special case. Unlike other Latino voters, they are already citizens with the right to vote. Conroys survey looked at other studies, which indicate that when Puerto Ricans live on the island, they are involved in politics and vote more often. But other studies show that when they arrive on the mainland, the activity virtually stops. Puerto Rican candidates on the island actively seek out votes, making calls to homes and placing ads in Spanish media, research says. That doesnt happen nearly as often on the mainland.
