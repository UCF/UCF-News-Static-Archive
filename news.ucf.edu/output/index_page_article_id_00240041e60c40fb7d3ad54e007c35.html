Student Observers Aid Killer Whale Research at SeaWorld

By Chad Binette April 14,2004

Photo: Chris Gotshall, SeaWorld

Biology students Jan Topoleski, left, and Kelly Flesher make new friends while observing whale behavior.

Lisa Kendig watched SeaWorld trainers work with whales on a second-grade field trip and was hooked.

 

She started to fulfill her wish to become a trainer this semester as one of 85 UCF student volunteers who observed SeaWorlds new killer whale calf and learned what it takes to work a full-time job at the park. The calf, one of nine killer whales at SeaWorld, was born Feb. 9.

 

I got to see the baby born, which was really awesome, said Kendig, a freshman biology major. Ive learned a lot.

 

The largest group of student volunteers in the programs six-year history observed the whale trainers during the spring semester. Nearly 500 UCF students have helped since the programs inception in 1998, and students from Valencia Community College also participate.

 

Students, who are unpaid but can receive credit for their work, are on duty 24 hours every day this semester because of the calf. At other times, students typically work from 6 a.m. to 10 p.m. Students must be available to work two four-hour shifts each week.

 

 

When there is a calf younger than a year old, students mainly watch and record its behavior and primarily how it interacts with its mother, said Otto Fad, SeaWorld Orlandos senior trainer and research coordinator. Students take notes whenever the calf nurses and watch for signs that it is becoming more independent.

 

If there are no young whales, the students observe and note behaviors such as how whales socialize, Fad said.

 

Weve got scores of information and observations which are impossible to duplicate in the ocean, he said. The students contribute greatly to what essentially is the largest database of killer whale calf development anywhere in the world.

 

Some of the students have moved on to careers in animal training after they finish the whale observation program, Fad said. UCF biology professor Linda Walters said many students tell her the program is the best thing theyve ever done in their lives as students.
