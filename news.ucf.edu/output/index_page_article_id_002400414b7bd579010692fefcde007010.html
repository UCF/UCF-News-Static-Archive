New America Series Includes Public Lectures, Student Readings, Art and Films

By Kristi Bartlett Oct. 17, 2005

Full Photo

Related Contacts and Links:

  • Mary Price

    • 407-823-4195

  • Connecting the UCF Community

  • Print this Page

UCF students and professors are examining the forces shaping the social, political and technological landscape of the United States in the 21st century during a two-year initiative that includes class readings, public lectures and student films.

The New America: Mirroring the Present & Mapping the Future, the latest effort in the Connecting the UCF Community series, promotes academic and civic engagement and is designed to create meaningful dialogues among students, faculty and the community. Students will create art and films and write essays in addition to attending lectures and participating in classroom discussions.

Connecting the UCF Community is an effective means to build community at UCF among students, faculty, and administrators, said John Schell, dean of Undergraduate Studies. The initiative encourages the thoughtful discussion of important topics outside of the classroom as well as inside. Also, this common theme program provides common ground for our general education program.

Professors gave freshmen a common reader that contains the works of several speakers taking part in The New America.

Some of the topics addressed in the reader include religious pluralism, ethics and the changing demographics of the nation. The readings will tie into courses in composition, speech and other subjects, and new readings will be given to students each semester to reflect the changing themes.

Scheduled speakers, whose presentations are open to the public, include Neil Howe, an expert on the millennial generation, and John Esposito, an internationally known scholar on Islam who will discuss the challenges that political Islam poses to the United States. Howe and Esposito are scheduled to visit UCF in February.

Social commentator David Callahan, author of The Cheating Culture, and John Elmore, a New York state criminal defense attorney, spoke on campus in mid-October. Elmore addressed Fighting for Your Life: The African-American Criminal Justice Guide. Khaled Hosseini, author of The Kite Runner, discussed religious diversity in America on Oct. 11.

The New America will actively engage students through two creative efforts. Students will be asked to write short essays addressing what it means to be American in the 21st century, how their lives will differ from the experiences of their parents and which social, economic and political forces will most impact their lives. Those three questions, which also are the focus of a separate art contest, embody the theme of the series.

Some of the essays in the MeStories project will be turned into two-minute digital short films or public service announcements. A faculty panel will judge the submissions, and they will be featured in a showcase. Students will be rewarded with scholarships and gifts provided by Apple Computers.

The best artwork in the Visions of the New America contest will be judged by an off-campus panel, and the top works will be exhibited in April.

Duke University and the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill have similar programs, but Duke focuses on freshmen and the freshman experience while North Carolina incorporates both digital film and photography into an event open to all students. Neither is focused on weaving an academic theme into films.

During the last two years, Connecting the UCF Community covered the Brown v. Board of Education decision by the U.S. Supreme Court. Organizers this year decided to expand the effort to include a wider range of students. LEAD Scholars, the Office of Student Involvement and LINK are all involved in The New America. All events are included on the LINK and LEAD Scholars calendars, and students receive credit from those organizations for attending events.

We are one of the largest universities in the nation and we have the responsibility of creating a vibrant academic community for our students, said Mary Price, program coordinator for the Office of Undergraduate Studies.

Future topics for The New America series include the connection between poverty in America, national security and terrorism.

For more information and a full calendar of events, go to http://www.undergraduatestudies.ucf.edu/community/2005_2007/events.html .
