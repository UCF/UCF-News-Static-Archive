UCF to Host Environmentalist Addressing Global Climate Change on Sept. 27

By Mark Freeman Sept. 25, 2007

Related Contacts and Links:

  • Mark Freeman, Global Perspectives

    • 407-823-0935

  • Chad Binette, News and Information

    • 407-823-6312

  • Global Perspectives Office

  • Print this Page

Winona LaDuke, program director of the Honor the Earth Fund, will speak Thursday, Sept. 27, at the University of Central Florida.

LaDuke, who is also the founding director of the White Earth Land Recovery Project, will talk about The Next Energy Economy: Grassroots Strategies for Mitigating Global Climate Change at 3 p.m. in the Pegasus Ballroom of the Student Union. The forum, organized by the UCF Global Perspectives Office, is free and open to the public.

LaDukes presentation is part of UCFs General Education Program Unifying Theme on the environment and global climate change. That theme is being incorporated into courses and other activities throughout the academic year.

In her capacity at the Honor the Earth Fund, LaDuke works on the national level to advocate, raise public support and create funding for frontline native environmental groups. She also works with the White Earth Land Recovery Project, a reservation-based, nonprofit organization focused on land, cultural and environmental issues.

In 1994, LaDuke was nominated by Time magazine as one of America's 50 most promising leaders younger than 40 years of age. In 1996, she received the Thomas Merton Award, which is given to national and international individuals struggling for justice. She also has received the Ann Bancroft Award for Women's Leadership Fellowship and the Reebok Human Rights Award, which helped her begin the White Earth Land Recovery Project.

A graduate of Harvard and Antioch universities, she has written extensively on Native American and environmental issues. She is a former board member of Greenpeace USA, and she serves as co-chair of the Indigenous Women's Network, a North American and Pacific indigenous women's organization. In 1998, Ms. magazine named her Woman of the Year for her work with Honor the Earth. She is the author of Last Standing Woman and All Our Relations, a non-fiction book on native environmental struggles.

In addition to the UCF Global Perspectives Office, sponsors of this forum include UCFs Office of Undergraduate Studies, Focus the Nation, the Political Science Department and the Global Connections Foundation.
