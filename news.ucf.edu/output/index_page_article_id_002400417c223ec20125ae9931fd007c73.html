UCF Economist: (Un)Happy New Year Brings More Job Losses, Slow Growth

By Christine Dellert Dec. 22, 2009

Photo: FreeFoto.com

UCF Economist Sean Snaith says 2010 will start much the same way this year ends -- with job losses and declining population.

The Sunshine State will ring in the New Year in much the same way that 2009 comes to a close, with another wave of job losses and declining population, says a University of Central Florida economist.

That’s the message from Sean Snaith, the director of UCF’s Institute for Economic Competitiveness, in his latest statewide quarterly forecast released this morning. Snaith says Florida’s recovery from the “death throes” of this recession will be confounded by a temporary decrease in population and massive budget shortfalls.

“It will take strong, visionary leadership to ensure that Florida can compete in this new environment, because very difficult choices lay ahead,” Snaith says. “You see a funny thing happened on the way to this recovery -- everything changed.”

The state can’t continue to rely on an influx of new residents to boost revenue in the public and private sectors. Instead, its recovery will depend upon a significant investment in its infrastructure and labor force through education and research opportunities, he explains.

Snaith also predicts that Florida’s unemployment rate will peak near 12 percent next year before payrolls slowly gain momentum and recover to pre-recession levels in 2014.

But, 2010 doesn’t bode all bad news, Snaith adds. Real Gross State Product (GSP), the state-level analogue to GDP, will finally see an annual increase this coming year, and personal income growth will slowly accelerate, as well.  Florida’s demand for health services can only increase, Snaith said.

 “It is still difficult to tell exactly how healthcare reform will play itself out in Washington, but clearly the momentum is toward expanding coverage. Couple that with an aging population in Florida, and it seems clear that demand for health services will remain strong for the foreseeable future,” he says.

Snaith’s forecast offers predictions through 2013 for Florida and its 12 metropolitan regions. Those areas are Naples, Daytona Beach-Deltona, Gainesville, Ocala, Lakeland, Palm Bay-Melbourne, Pensacola, Miami, Jacksonville, Tallahassee, Tampa Bay and Orlando.

His entire forecast is available here.

Other highlights include:

-- Unemployment will still be stubbornly high by the end of 2013, averaging 8.3 percent in the fourth quarter.

-- Florida’s population declined again this year and will stay flat during the first half of 2010 before population growth slowly climbs to 1.5 percent in 2013.

-- Florida’s housing construction sector bottoms out in 2009. Housing starts will climb during the next several years. In 2013, housing starts will recover to 2001 levels, gradually rising to 171,000 annual starts.

-- After two years of contracting, real Gross State Product (GSP) will slowly expand in 2010 and accelerate to 4.2 percent in 2012 before easing to 3.3 percent in 2013. The 2010-13 average growth rate will be 3.1 percent.

-- During 2010-13, retail sales will steadily accelerate after a weak first-half of 2010 and grow at an average pace of 5.6 percent as consumer spending finally returns.

Snaith is a national expert in economics, forecasting, market sizing and economic analysis who authors quarterly reports about the state of the economy. Bloomberg News recently named Snaith as one of the country’s most accurate forecasters for his predictions about the Federal Reserve's benchmark interest rate, the Federal Funds rate.

Snaith is also a member of several national forecasting panels, including the Western Blue Chip Economic Forecast panel, the National Association of Business Economics Quarterly Outlook Survey Panel, the Federal Reserve Bank of Philadelphia's Survey of Professional Forecasters, Bloomberg U.S. Economic Indicator Survey and USA Today Economic Survey Panel.

The UCF Institute for Economic Competitiveness' mission is to expand public understanding of the economy by convening business leaders, scholars, policy makers, civic groups and media to discuss critical issues.
