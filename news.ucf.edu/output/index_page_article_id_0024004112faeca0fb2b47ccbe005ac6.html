Panel: Keep Judges Independent

By Chad Binette February 12, 2004

Photo: Jerry Klein

Good turnout for Thursday's forum on justice and democracy.

Full Photo

Related Contacts and Links:

  • Print this Page

African-Americans probably would have lost important court decisions in their fight for equality if the judges hearing the cases had to worry about getting re-elected, a federal judge said Thursday during a forum at the University of Central Florida. While many county and state judges in the United States are elected, federal judges receive lifetime appointments to their posts, and Congress is barred from lowering the judges salaries during their tenure. Such protections can be especially important in cases that involve the protection of rights for minorities, U.S. District Judge Patricia Fawsett said. Fawsett, Florida Supreme Court Justice Charles Wells and other panelists addressed the benefits and drawbacks of appointing versus electing judges during a forum entitled "Justice and Democracy: The Role of the Judiciary in Promoting a Free and Open Society." The panel also included former Gov. Buddy MacKay; Herbert Kritzer, a political science and law professor at the University of Wisconsin; and moderator Russell Troutman, a Winter Park attorney who is a past president of The Florida Bar and Orange County Bar Association. Panel members discussed the courts involvement with the 2000 presidential election, how the U.S. judicial system compares with courts in other nations and whether appointments to the courts have become more political in recent years. Wells said its important that the public views judges as capable of making independent decisions. He said the high cost of running judicial campaigns in urban areas, such as Orlando and Miami, can prompt questions about whether "someone who has to raise half a million dollars" to win can be truly independent. But Wells also said he believes that, especially at the state level, its good for judges to be held accountable through elections so they remain responsive to people and dont let power go to their heads. "I think the community needs to feel like its got a remedy" for an ineffective judge, he said. Fawsett said its important for judges in cases such as Brown v. Board of Education, the U.S. Supreme Court case in 1954 that eventually led to the integration of schools, to have the courage to make decisions to protect minority rights even if the rulings will be unpopular. "Some cases are too important to play out to the popular vote," said Fawsett, who is the chief judge of the Middle District in Florida. MacKay said he believes judges should be protected from political pressures but said he doesnt support lifetime appointments because they can lead to complacency. He supports a system where judges are retained or not retained based on merit. Asked about the role of partisan politics in the U.S. and Florida supreme court decisions related to the 2000 presidential election, Wells said he and his colleagues "were consciously attempting to make a decision on the basis of the case in front of us." He recalled going to work every morning while the presidential election remained unsettled and seeing many television news trucks parked outside the courthouse. "Werent we so lucky to live in a place where there were TV satellites and not machine guns that were on top of those trucks," Wells said. Kritzer, the political science and law professor, said that while there were some short-term changes in public opinions about the courts after the 2000 elections, the decisions had almost no lasting effects on how the public views the courts. While many countries have courts similar to the United States in some aspects, Kritzer pointed out several differences in how the courts are set up here. He said he knows of only one other country, Switzerland, where citizens elect judges. He said he believes the United States is the only country where citizens elect prosecutors. Also, while any judge in the United States can rule that a law is unconstitutional, only certain judges have that power in most other countries, he said. The forum was one of several spring events coordinated by the UCF Global Perspectives Office, with help from several other university departments, the Orlando Sentinel and Global Connections for Central Florida. For more information on upcoming events, go to www.ucfglobalperspectives.org.
