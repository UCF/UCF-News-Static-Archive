'Latin America and the Caribbean: The Future of Democracy'

By Andrea Perez Feb. 16, 2010

Related Contacts and Links:

  • Mark Freeman, Global Perspectives

    • 407-823-0935

  • Global Perspectives Office

  • Brookings Report on Cuba

  • Print this Page

The evolving nature of democracy in Latin America was the focus of a multimedia forum at UCF on Tuesday, Feb. 9.

About 150 people turned out to see “Latin America and the Caribbean: The Future of Democracy,” which was organized by the UCF Global Perspectives Office. The forum started with a U.S. Department of State videoconference with Secretary of State Hillary Clinton. It also featured a presentation on future prospects for Cuba, along with other Latin American and Caribbean issues, by regional specialist Jorge Pinon.

The State Department videoconference, recently facilitated by the UCF Global Perspectives Office, was conducted only with UCF and two other schools in the nation. When discussing problems faced by Latin America and the Caribbean, Clinton pointed out that “the U.S. cannot solve these problems alone, but many of these problems cannot be solved if the U.S. doesn’t get involved.”

Pinon, the energy fellow at the University of Miami’s Center for Hemispheric Policy, discussed his involvement as a member of the Brookings Institution task force on Cuba. The task force, which included 19 Cuba experts from diverse backgrounds and philosophies, was established to review U.S. policy towards Cuba and provide policy recommendations for the future.

The task force accepted that the political and economic isolation imposed by the United States has not produced the anticipated result of regime change in Cuba, and it pointed to alternative policy strategies. Nine of the panel’s 16 core policy recommendations have been considered by the Obama administration, and many of them are now being implemented. 

Pinon explained that there are at least three scenarios for Cuba in the next five to seven years: continued succession of the current leadership with no real changes, transition or a failed state. Yet, he voiced optimism, saying that for the first time in 50 years he sees a light at the end of the tunnel.

When asked about Venezuela, Pinon was quick to make a comparison with Brazil to illustrate a major shortcoming of Venezuelan President Hugo Chavez. He explained that Venezuela is lacking what Brazil has: stewardship, or the ability of the government to properly manage its financial and economic sectors. In acknowledging the lack of stewardship and political continuity in many Latin American countries, Pinon emphasized the point of view of potential investors who would be cautious to consider countries such as Venezuela, Argentina and even Mexico, despite their wealth of natural resources.

Without a track record of continuity, investors will continue to pass up opportunities in these countries, he said.

Other speakers who had been scheduled to present but could not attend due to inclement weather and other reasons included Ambassador Myles Frechette of the Center for Strategic and International Studies, Finance Minister Maria Elsa Viteri of Ecuador and Ray Walser of The Heritage Foundation.

In addition to the Global Perspectives Office, sponsors included the UCF Latin American, Caribbean and Latino Studies Program; UCF Political Science Department; UCF International Services Center; Lawrence J. Chastang and the Chastang Foundation; UCF LIFE; and the Global Connections Foundation.
