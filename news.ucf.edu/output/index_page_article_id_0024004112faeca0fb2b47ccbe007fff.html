Hitt: Continue Striving for Excellence

By Chad Binette March 1, 2003

Photo: Jerry Klein

President John Hitt recaps UCF's growth and potential at his State of the University talk. Instructional Resources and local media record his remarks.

The University of Central Florida must strive for high standards in education, research and student satisfaction as it climbs the list of the countrys largest universities, President John Hitt said Monday.

In the State of the University address that also marked the 12th anniversary of his first day as president, Hitt outlined how UCF has improved the quality of its students even while growing rapidly in recent years. Average SAT scores and grade-point averages are up for all incoming freshmen at UCF and freshmen in the Burnett Honors College posted better grades and SAT scores than their peers at New College of Florida, the states designated honors college.

As UCFs enrollment on the main campus grows to an anticipated 48,000 in the next six years, faculty, staff and students need to stay focused on excellence, Hitt said.

"A large mediocrity would be an embarrassment," Hitt said. "We have no choice but to continue to work to reach a higher level of excellence in all aspects of our operation. Everything we do as we get bigger, we have to do better."

Hitts goals in the coming years include attracting more graduate students to the university. Hed like graduate students to comprise about 20 percent of UCFs total enrollment, up from about 14 percent this past fall.

Hitt also wants to expand UCFs offerings at joint-use facilities with area community colleges. If more students can be educated on those campuses, UCF may be able to increase its enrollment to more than 50,000 without "overstressing our precious resources," Hitt said.

The president discussed recent trends in student applications, state funding of higher education and funding for research. UCF may receive about 25,000 applications for the 2004-05 school year from students seeking to enter college for the first time. That group of students submitted 21,722 applications for the current school year and 19,909 in 2002-2003.

The increased demand places UCF in a "fortunate position," Hitt said, because the university can increases its enrollment while still being more selective in deciding which students to admit.

The average SAT score for UCF students entering college for the first time was 1176 in 2003, up from 1167 a year ago and 1152 in 2001. The average grade-point average has risen from about 3.5 in 1999 to nearly 3.9 in 2003.

UCF enrolled 33 National Merit Scholars in 2003, up from 25 each of the last two years. The 2003 number ranks second in the state of Florida, behind the University of Florida, and is similar to the number of scholars at schools such as the University of Virginia, Clemson University and Miami University in Ohio, Hitt said.

"Thats not bad company to be in," he said.

Hitt praised Provost Terry Hickey and UCFs deans for finding ways to offer more class seats to students in a year when the state provided no funding to cover enrollment growth. He said hed like to see a slight increase in the number of open seats in general education courses next year to give students more choices for class days and times. This year, 2.7 percent of seats in general education classes were open.

The president lauded Vice President for Research M.J. Soileau and the faculty for bringing in nearly $89 million in 2003, up from $75.4 million in 2002 and $64.9 million three years ago.

After Hitts speech, Dick Nunis, chairman of the UCF Board of Trustees, presented him with framed photographs showing how the campus looked in 1992, when Hitt became president, and how it looks today. Nunis also thanked First Lady Martha Hitt for her service to the university.

To view slides from the State of the University presentation, visit www.ucf.edu/president and click on "State of the University Address."
