Carnegie Foundation Recognizes UCF as Leader in Engaging the Community

By Chad Binette Jan. 26, 2009

Photo: UCF Marketing

Volunteering as part of UCF's nursing courses helped Sarah Dickerman "see how one person really can make a difference."

Full Photo

  • Chad Binette, News and Information

    • 407-823-6312

  • Print this Page

Sarah Dickerman used to dream of becoming a nurse.

But the University of Central Florida senior says her aspirations changed after she volunteered at a senior center and began conducting health screenings for infants, both through service learning courses at UCF. While she still plans to become a nurse, she now is more concerned with helping her community and inspiring others to follow her lead.

“I see how one person really can make a difference,” Dickerman said.

More than 11,000 UCF students enrolled last year in service-learning courses, one of many community engagement efforts that earned the university recognition from the Carnegie Foundation for the Advancement of Teaching. The foundation has classified UCF as an institution that has made substantial commitments in Curricular Engagement and Outreach & Partnership categories through its mission, culture, leadership, resources and practices.

UCF, only the second public university in Florida to hold classifications in both categories, has integrated community engagement and outreach thoroughly in the classroom and students’ extracurricular activities. Several departments – from Student Involvement to Community Relations to the College of Medicine -- include community engagement in their missions, assess the impact of their community outreach and recognize the efforts of faculty, staff and students during several awards ceremonies.

“The Carnegie Foundation’s recognition is a testament to the dedication of our faculty, staff and students to promoting civic responsibility and engaging our community,” said Provost and Executive Vice President Terry Hickey. “A leading example is our partnership with Junior Achievement. The university continues to dedicate more resources to ensure that world’s largest university-Junior Achievement partnership thrives and benefits thousands of youths in our community.”

For the 2007-08 year, UCF students contributed almost 34,500 service-learning hours through JA programs. The Burnett Honors College, the colleges of Education, Business Administration and Arts & Humanities, the Nicholson School of Communication and LEAD Scholars all incorporate JA service learning in their courses.

Several of UCF’s Junior Achievement programs have been lauded as leading national examples of involving youths in the community and inspiring them to improve the quality of their lives.

UCF President John Hitt, other administrators, faculty, staff and students devote their time and talents to helping a variety of community organizations as board members and volunteers. Employees throughout the university also give generously to the Florida State Employees Charitable Campaign ($145,000 was raised last year) and many other charitable causes.

Many faculty pursue research projects that benefit a variety of community agencies while also supporting students’ outreach efforts. 

“By engaging the community throughout our curriculum, we hope to enrich our students’ education and instill in them the value of helping their community throughout their adult lives,” said Vice President and Chief of Staff Rick Schell, a leader in UCF’s service-learning efforts. “We continue to look for ways to establish and expand partnerships with our community.”

Nearly 150 universities applied to the Carnegie Foundation for classifications this year, and 119 were selected.

"We hope that by acknowledging the commitment and accomplishment of these engaged institutions, the Foundation will encourage other colleges and universities to move in this direction. Doing so brings benefits to the community and to the institution," said Carnegie President Anthony S. Bryk.

Service learning has allowed Dickerman to see patients as much more than just clients. She has ridden Lynx buses, helped parents with terminally ill children and aided teen mothers who are working hard to educate themselves and feed their children. Dickerman said those experiences will help her better understand her patients and the daily challenges they face.

“Our service-learning work is very gratifying,” Dickerman said. “After I graduate, I’m definitely going to continue helping my community.”
