Breakfast Opens Annual Celebration of Diversity Week

By Shobana Daniell Oct. 17, 2005

Related Contacts and Links:

  • Valarie King

    • 407-823-6479

  • Office of Diversity Initiatives

  • Print this Page

UCF President John Hitt opened the 13th annual Diversity Week festivities Monday by borrowing a phrase from the Rev. Jesse Jackson and comparing the university community to a quilt.

Many of you know that one of my five goals for UCF is to become more inclusive and diverse, Hitt said during the sold-out Diversity Breakfast in the Pegasus Ballroom. He added that, like each piece of a quilt, each member of the university community needs to be recognized for its own contribution but also woven and held together by a common thread.

Hitt spoke to more than 500 students, staff and faculty members, an audience that reflected the diverse university population.

The theme of this years celebration is Diversity through a Generational Lens, and the keynote speaker at the Diversity Breakfast was Linda Landman-Gonzalez, director of Diversity and Community Affairs at Darden Restaurants Inc. of Orlando.

Landman-Gonzalez focused on changing generational differences. She said that the workplace today includes employees spanning four generations: the traditionalists (born before 1943), the Baby Boomers (born between 1944 and 1960), Generation X (born between 1961 -1980) and the Millenials (born between 1981 and 2000).

Each group has its distinguishing characteristics, said Landman-Gonzalez. Smart companies are designing career paths that take into consideration the driving forces of these generations.

The diverse age groups in the workplace add an element of richness to any workplace or academic environment, speakers said Monday.

Valarie Greene King, director of the Office of Diversity Initiatives at UCF, said generational issues shape employees perspectives because they may have different outlooks and ways of doing things.

Rusty Okoniewski, a program developer at the Office of International Studies who attended the breakfast, said UCF has come a long way in its (journey) toward an inclusive and diverse culture."

The changes are remarkable; the variety of people on campus is considerably more now, added Okoniewski, who started working here in 1977.

The Diversity Breakfast ended with a videotape of 3 to 5 year olds from UCFs Creative School for Children singing many nursery rhymes. The youngsters bring even more age diversity onto a campus with a workforce that spans several decades.

One of our university goals is to become more inclusive Diversity Week is one avenue that is used to accomplish (this), King said. It helps us to realize that we are all in this world together. What impacts one ultimately impacts others. 

Upcoming Diversity Week presentations include:

John Elmore, a New York state criminal defense attorney, will lead a panel discussion on criminal justice issues at 11:30 a.m. Wednesday, Oct. 19, in the Pegasus Ballroom of the Student Union. The Department of Criminal Justice and Legal Studies is sponsoring that presentation.

The Diverse World of Harry Potter, sponsored by Housing and Residence Life, will be at noon Tuesday in Garden Key Room 221 of the Student Union.
