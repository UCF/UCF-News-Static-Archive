UCF's PROSE Lab: Engaging the Humanities Through Technology

By Courtney Gilmartin July 22, 2009

Photo: Courtesy of PROSE Lab

"Veritas University" places players in a virtual academic world and challenges them to approach ethical situations such as plagiarism and living with roommates.

UCF students and faculty are bringing storytelling into the 21st century with computer-animated aliens and a virtual Underground Railroad.

At the Partnership for Research on Storytelling Environments – or PROSE Lab – Digital Media students apply technology to storytelling to create innovative tools for learning, training and entertaining. The PROSE Lab products provide the chance to solve ethical dilemmas and explore other issues in the humanities in a fun, user-friendly way. 

“Our goal is to combine technology with interesting issues in the humanities,” said Rudy McDaniel, a Digital Media professor at the University of Central Florida and the PROSE Lab’s director. “Learning in the humanities is often about exploration, contemplation, decision making using multiple sources of information and understanding one’s own self in relation to one’s environment. We want players to think about these things as they engage with digital technologies.”

The group has created two computer-based games to engage students in history and ethics. The lab is working on a third game, “Knights of Astrus,” due out next summer.

McDaniel said “Knights of Astrus” is the lab’s most sophisticated game yet. The game will explore morals and personal value systems, and it is being funded by a $42,500 grant from the UCF Information Fluency Initiative.

“Knights of Astrus” revolves around a character whose spaceship crashes on an alien planet. Players have to find parts to repair the ship through communicating with different factions of citizens who are distressed because their planet is running out of water. Players must balance their own need to explore the unknown planet with the needs of the various citizens they encounter.
 
While Digital Media students are handling the technical aspects of the game, a Philosophy class is organizing the game’s ethical components.

“Students have been creating the scenarios with ethics content based on their interests,” said Associate Professor of Philosophy Nancy Stanlick. “We have my ethical theory students reading and writing some of the scenarios this summer, and some of them will test the game.”

Stanlick said she hopes the game will allow “students and other users of the game to see the importance of moral reasoning in everyday situations.” 

She is writing an ethics handbook that will accompany the game. The handbook will provide an overview of ethical theories and help students identify their own moral orientations and sharpen their understanding of moral reasoning.

McDaniel began overseeing the PROSE Lab in 2006, the year that the lab created its first game. In that game, the user plays the role of a slave escaping from a plantation and relying on the Underground Railroad to reach freedom. 

In fall 2008, the PROSE Lab created a program for the Web site www.EthicsGame.com, which provides ethical learning tools for people in academic and corporate settings. The lab created “Veritas University,” a 2D-animated teaching game that places players in a virtual academic world and challenges them to approach typical ethical situations that can arise in academic environments, such as plagiarism and living with roommates.

In the Web game, players can interview other characters, consult an ethics guide and ethics coach, and utilize other virtual tools in order to solve their ethical dilemmas. The goal of the game is to prepare students to deal with common scenarios in a safe, game-like environment so that unfavorable outcomes in real-life situations can be avoided.

Next up for the PROSE Lab is a mini-game based on ethical decision making for first-year college students, which is scheduled to be released online for free in December.  PROSE Lab students are also working with David Metcalf from UCF’s Institute for Simulation and Training on a card-game based application that will be used to train U.S. Army combat medics to treat common injuries.

About 45 digital media students have been involved with the PROSE Lab team. In addition to McDaniel, UCF professors Steve Fiore from Philosophy and Clint Bowers from Psychology are also part of the team. Digital Media instructors Jon Friskics and Dan Novatnak have contributed audio engineering and expertise.  Erik Vick, an assistant professor in the Department of Information Technology at the Rochester Institute of Technology in New York, has also contributed to the PROSE Lab.

Students working with the PROSE Lab can earn independent study or internship credit for their work.  Students receiving academic credit must work at least 12 hours a week.

For more information, go to www.dm.ucf.edu/~proselab/.
