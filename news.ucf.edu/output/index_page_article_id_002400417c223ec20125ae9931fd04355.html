Educators Helping More Students Attend and Succeed in College

By Jessica Wycoff Feb. 19, 2010

Photo: Jacque Brund

Dave Harrison is vice provost for UCF's Regional Campuses.

Full Photo

Related Contacts and Links:

  • UCF Regional Campuses

  • Print this Page

Regional academic leaders gathered at the University of Central Florida on Wednesday to discuss how they can work together to improve student retention, increase financial aid and ease students’ transitions from high school to college.

The College Access Summit included more than 150 superintendants, principals, teachers, guidance counselors and community college leaders. Attendees meet regularly to brainstorm ways to make bachelor’s degrees a possibility for every student in the region.

The collaboration among education leaders is important because “we’re all working with the same students,” said Don Upton, president of Fairfield Index, Inc. and the discussion facilitator.

The importance of student retention and the ability of educators to track student success were stressed throughout the morning at the Fairwinds Alumni Center.

Sharing more data among schools was discussed as possible solution to help students. By improving regional communication, educators hope to better identify which groups of students need more assistance. Participants also addressed the importance of student access to UCF and other state universities.

Since the first summit in 2007, UCF and its regional partners have made great strides toward encouraging more students to attend college and earn bachelor's degrees.  A major focus of this regional partnership is helping students and families understand the pathways to a college degree.

Prior summits have helped UCF enhance the DirectConnect to UCF program, which offers graduates of Brevard, Lake-Sumter, and Valencia community colleges and Seminole State College of Florida guaranteed admission to UCF. More than 3,000 of the university's new transfer students last fall were part of the DirectConnect to UCF program.

In addition to encouraging high school students to enroll in rigorous courses, such as Advanced Placement classes, educators aim to create programs to further prepare high school students for college-level work.

Seminole County Public Schools’ Summer Transition Program is one example of this growing effort. High school freshmen who participate are provided with an adult mentor and a voucher for 12 credits at Seminole State if they graduate with a GPA of at least 2.5.

Educators need to continue integrating curricula from kindergarten through college, said Bill Vogel, superintendent of Seminole County Public Schools. He stressed the importance of implementing advanced programs in middle and elementary schools as well as high school.
