Demand High for UCF Medical Laboratory Science Graduates

By Zenaida Gonzalez Kotala Dec. 18, 2006

Related Contacts and Links:

  • Dorilyn Hitchcock

    • 407-823-5220

  • Zenaida Gonzalez Kotala

    • 407-823-6120

  • Medical Laboratory Science program

  • Print this Page

Graduates of the Medical Laboratory Science program at the University of Central Florida have a 100 percent job placement rate and many have two to three job offers before they get their degrees.

Theres a huge demand for this profession, said Dorilyn Hitchcock, the program director of MLS, which is in the Burnett College of Biomedical Sciences. There will be an even greater demand in the future because of the large number of older technologists retiring and the older population demanding more health care.

The bachelors degree program requires two years of prep work that is very similar to the coursework students complete to get ready for medical school admittance, Hitchcock said. The last two years of the program focuses on laboratory work, instead of medicine.

Graduates can get jobs testing samples in hospitals, pharmaceutical companies, government agencies such as the Centers for Disease Control and private testing companies, laboratories, crime labs and forensics centers.

Nearly 44 percent of laboratories in the country reported problems filling positions, according to the American Society of Clinical Pathologys 2005 Wage and Vacancy Survey. Its even worse in the far western and northeastern states, the report says.

Angela Solley, a 2005 UCF graduate, works as a clinical laboratory scientist at Orlando Regional Medical Center. She got a day-shift position right out of school.

Clinical lab scientists are involved in medical diagnosis, surveillance, management, research and education. They use sophisticated equipment such as electronic cell counters, chemical analyzers and computers to figure out exactly whats in the samples they examine, including all kinds of body fluids.

I was very fortunate, Solley said. Day-shift positions are tough to get. But I love my job and the people I work with. Its my dream job.

The fact that many jobs require night work is likely one of the reasons students dont pursue the field. But there is generally a huge pay differential, as much as 30 percent for working nights, Hitchcock said.

Solley, who already had an associates degree, was working on prerequisites for a forensics science major when she heard about the MLS program.

It sounded really interesting and once I got into it, I found it was a good fit for me, she said. I like the hands-on work. I like being a part of solving the mystery thats going to help a patient feel better.

A maximum of 20 students are allowed into UCFs program each semester. The class size is kept small to allow for personal instruction. It is also vital for the hands-on training that goes on in the MLS lab on the East Orlando campus. The lab duplicates a hospital environment on a smaller scale.

An 18-week clinical internship completes the program. UCF has partnerships with several hospitals that facilitate placements for students who may not live in the immediate Orlando area. They include Florida Hospital, Orlando Regional Medical Hospital, Winter Haven Hospital and Holmes Regional Medical Center in Melbourne.

About 400 students have graduated since the program started in 1969. Many graduates are now lab managers, lab supervisors and some have gone on to become physicians.

The small nature of the program also encourages camaraderie among students. Solley and other members of her graduating class still get together regularly to talk about their jobs and lives. Were like a family, she said.
