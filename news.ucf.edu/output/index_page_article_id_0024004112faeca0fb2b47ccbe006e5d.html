Diplomat Calls for Sincere, Humble Leadership

By Tom Evelyn March 3, 2004

Photo: Jerry Klein

Ambassador in Residence Harriet Elam-Thomas.

Full Photo

  • Tom Evelyn

    • 407-823-5988

  • Global Perspectives Office

The best leaders are "sincere and humble" and recognize the vital role their support staffs play in their success, Ambassador Harriet Elam-Thomas told faculty and staff attending UCFs Leadership Institute on Wednesday.

"Real leadership has to do with integrity and performance; neither one can take a holiday," said Elam-Thomas, UCFs diplomat in residence. "They reflect on your character and your soul.  Whether you know it or not, you lead by example."

Elam-Thomas keynote speech kicked off the annual Leadership Institute, a daylong event organized by the Office of Diversity Initiatives and members of the Leadership Enhancement Program to promote leadership skills and diversity among the universitys leaders.

Other speakers included Richard Lapchick, director of UCFs Sports Business Management program, and Claudia Schippert, director of the Religious Studies program. Sessions covered topics such as conflict mediation, effective decision-making and creative problem-solving.

Elam-Thomas said her experience as a senior Foreign Service officer and her admiration of former South Africa President Nelson Mandela have guided her beliefs on leadership. She recalled how she "sat in rapt attention" in the Senate gallery in 1990, when the "quintessential leader" addressed a joint session of Congress after being released from prison.

"Mandela is a master at seeking consensus," and he "believed in the human heart," she said.

Good leaders appreciate the people who work for them, regardless of their role, said Elam-Thomas, whose mother worked as a "domestic" to help pay for her daughters college education. Quality staffers will perform well if theyre respected and complimented for their expertise and given an opportunity to use it, she said.

"Effective leaders are sincere and humble," Elam-Thomas said. "It is not enough to speak to support staff only when its necessary to elicit their services.  Genuine concern about the total employee pays dividends in trust and in confidence."

The most effective leaders, she said, build consensus and teamwork, accept responsibility, are trustworthy and act decisively.

"I challenge all of you to respond to your duties and responsibilities with the same verve and commitment of a Nelson Mandela," Elam-Thomas said, adding that UCF provided "fertile ground" for such leadership. "You have no other option but to succeed."

Elam-Thomas joined the university in August, when the State Department selected UCF as one of 16 universities in the country to host a diplomat in residence for the 2003-04 academic year. She serves as a resource on foreign affairs for the university and the Central Florida community.

Before coming to UCF, Elam-Thomas was ambassador to Senegal and chief of mission to Guinea-Bissau. She has worked assignments at the White House, the U.S. Mission to the United Nations in New York, and in Greece, Turkey, France, Belgium, Mali and the Ivory Coast. She speaks Turkish, Greek and French.

"During her four-decade Foreign Service career, she has demonstrated a lifelong practice of bridging cultures and making connections between seemingly distinct people," Diversity Initiatives Director Valarie Greene King said in introducing Elam-Thomas. "With unique vision and insight, Ambassador Elam-Thomas placed a human face on diplomacy."
