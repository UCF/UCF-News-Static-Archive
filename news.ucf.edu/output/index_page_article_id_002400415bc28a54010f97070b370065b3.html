UCF Students to Help Katrina Victims, Homeless, Environment on Spring Break

By UCF Staff March 8, 2007

Related Contacts and Links:

More than 80 University of Central Florida students will spend their spring break helping others rebuild their lives.

Three teams of students will travel to Mississippi and Louisiana coastal communities March 12 through 16 to help repair homes devastated by Hurricane Katrina in 2005. Other groups are helping the homeless, children with special needs and the environment, while others are headed to France and England to reinforce what they have learned in the classroom.

About 20 students in UCFs DeVos Sport Business Management program will gut for repairs homes in New Orleans that were destroyed by the hurricane. They will also repair youth baseball and softball fields that were damaged by the storm. Richard Lapchick, chair of the program, and his family, will accompany the students.

The trip will be the first under the banner of the Hope for Stanley Foundation, which the students formed two weeks ago as an initiative of UCFs National Consortium for Academics and Sports with assistance from the New Orleans City Council. The students named the foundation in honor of Stanley Stewart, a Lower Ninth Ward resident whose home they gutted during a visit to New Orleans in December.

The foundation aims to create opportunities for people across the United States in sports to participate in the rebuilding and revival of New Orleans. The Stewarts story is just one of many among the thousands of New Orleans residents who would benefit from extra help and support from volunteers. For the DeVos students, Stanley Stewart became the face of New Orleans and the namesake of their Hope for Stanley Foundation.

The two other groups of students are part of UCFs Alternative Spring Break program that matches volunteer students with charity groups that are helping communities in Florida and beyond.

One of those groups is headed for Gulfport, Miss., in partnership with Mississippi State University. Students will build homes for disadvantaged families in the area struggling to rebuild. The other group will focus on downtown New Orleans. That team has partnered with Eastbank Storm Station, a hurricane relief organization working in some of the most vulnerable areas of the city.

Other groups from Alternative Spring Break are working on short-term projects that will benefit the homeless and hungry in Central Florida and Albany, Ga. Other teams are working with Habitat for Humanity, Hands on Orlando, United Cerebral Palsy in Nashville, Tenn., and an environmental group that will be working on conservation and animal rehabilitation projects on Jekyll Island in Georgia.

Many students go on to being leaders in the future and do more volunteer work around our own community, said Faraaz Merchant, director of the Alternative Spring Break program. It is an intense experience meant to educate students on the issues that affect our society and the implications of each.

On the lighter side, a group of about 10 students from the Rosen College of Hospitality Management will travel to France on a Wine and Cultural Tour. The students are enrolled in the International Gastronomy course and will travel to several wineries in the Burgundy region of France as well as visit museums and restaurants in Paris.

They will visit country inns, taste wine and champagne at the winery and walk through the vineyards, said professor Frank Juge, who will be leading the group. For the students, the study abroad experience reinforces concepts taught in class and brings home the food and wine of the region better than anything we could do in class.

Seven students from the Theatre department will travel to London for a week of plays and to soak up Shakespeare.

Its very exciting, said professor Jim Brown, who is leading his first group abroad. Well be looking at the European origins of the American musical theater and this is the first trip for that.

He also is planning trips to Paris, Vienna and towns in Italy that were significant to the development of musical theater.
