UCF to Pursue Stadium Study; Report Appears to Favor New Medical School

By Chad Binette Jan. 18, 2005

Photo: Jerry Klein

UCF President John Hitt, left, and trustees Chairman Dick Nunis discuss a possible on-campus stadium at the trustees' meeting Tuesday.

The University of Central Florida Board of Trustees voted unanimously Tuesday to go forward with a feasibility study for a new football stadium and learned that early results of another study appear to support the need for a medical school.

UCF is looking into the construction of a 45,000- to 50,000-seat stadium on its Orlando campus. The proposed stadium, which would cost about $40 million to build, could be ready in time for the 2006 football season.

President John Hitt and trustees Chairman Dick Nunis toured stadiums at the universities of Louisville and Kentucky last week, and both said they left believing that a similar, steel design could be well-suited for UCF.

The bottom line is this: We really think this merits further study, Hitt said.

A study by MGT of America examining the feasibility of building a medical school at UCF likely will indicate that theres a need for new medical schools in Florida to address a shortage of physicians, even if existing medical schools in the state expand, Provost Terry Hickey said.

It makes a very, very strong case for the need for a medical school  at least one or two more medical schools in the state, Hickey said.

Hitt plans to discuss the possibility of building a medical school at the Jan. 27 meeting of the Florida Board of Governors, which oversees the 11 state universities.

If UCF decides to move forward with a football stadium, it would be built on existing practice fields east of the UCF Arena in the area designated for intercollegiate athletics and recreation. Construction could begin as early as this summer.

The stadium would be funded with donations and revenues from ticket sales, concessions, skybox rentals and advertisements. No state or tuition dollars would be spent on the stadium, which likely would not host any concerts.

Nunis said a stadium can make a campus special while providing a place for alumni to rally together. He also said his support for pursuing a stadium is mainly based on what it would mean for students.

The response of the students has been overwhelming, he said. Theyre all looking forward to it.

Hitt and Nunis said they believe a new UCF stadium and a renovated Florida Citrus Bowl, where UCFs football team now plays, can both thrive. Nunis said not having UCF games at the Citrus Bowl would make it easier for it to accommodate other possible games, such as Florida State-Miami.

Hitt said he will give the results of the stadium study to trustees no later than their next meeting, which is scheduled for March 31. The trustees may hold another meeting before that date if the study is completed sooner.

In other action Tuesday, the trustees approved a minor amendment to the Campus Master Plan that would designate nine acres in the northern part of campus for a softball stadium, softball practice field and a parking lot.

Hitt said a softball field, and hopefully the full stadium, will be ready by the fall. He also said theyre going to come out with a better facility because of the new location.

-- The trustees approved an average increase of 3.6 percent in student housing rental rates. The increased revenues will be used to renovate on-campus housing.

-- The trustees voiced concerns about a state Board of Governors plan that would require UCF to graduate a higher percentage of students in targeted fields that the state believes meet critical needs or deal with emerging technologies.

The list of targeted programs, however, does not include hospitality management, criminal justice and other major programs at UCF that administrators and trustees said are important to the region and society. Administrators are concerned that they would have to cut the number of degrees awarded in those programs to meet the proposed standards if they are approved. That would unfairly limit students access to higher education, Hickey said.

-- Some faculty members quietly protested collective bargaining issues at the end of the trustees committee meetings and at the beginning of the regular meeting. The university and United Faculty of Florida, the union the represents the faculty, are at an impasse in contract negotiations. A special magistrate will be called in to help facilitate the process.
