Community Celebrates Groundbreaking of UCF Med School, Burnham Institute

By Zenaida Gonzalez Kotala and Chad Binette Oct. 3, 2007

Photo: Jacque Brund

A vast open field filled with construction vehicles formed the backdrop Wednesday as more than 500 Central Florida leaders gathered for the first time at the future home of the UCF College of Medicine and Burnham Institute for Medical Research.

University, government and business leaders celebrated during a groundbreaking ceremony at the Lake Nona site, which soon will be home to a "medical city" bustling with scientists trying to find a cure for cancer, future doctors learning about the latest advances in health care and veterans receiving high-quality care at a new hospital. The UCF College of Medicine and a new biomedical sciences building -- already under construction on site -- will form the foundation of the visionary project.

"When this community gets committed to something, they move quickly here," UCF President John Hitt said. "We're going to see the development of something unusual -- if not unique -- here: a medical city."

Joining Hitt on Wednesday were Orlando Mayor Buddy Dyer, Orange County Mayor Rich Crotty, College of Medicine Dean Deborah German and Burnham President and CEO John Reed. Rasesh Thakkar, senior managing director of the Tavistock Group, served as the master of ceremonies.

The leaders celebrated the many partnerships that were vital to the creation of the college and the Burnham Institute's decision to come to Orlando. And they pointed to the dramatic impact the Lake Nona development will have on health care and the economy in the region.

The facility at Lake Nona will combine a medical school, research facilities and working hospitals. Together, they will train the best doctors, provide top care for patients and develop treatments and cures for diseases that affect so many people. The combination of these institutions will also generate $6.4 billion in economic activity and more than 25,000 jobs in the region over the next 10 years.

"We are building the foundation that will enable our children and future generations to change the world," Thakkar said.

The Tavistock Group contributed $12.5 million and 50 acres of land valued at more than $8 million for the medical college.

Overall, UCF has raised $113 million to build the facility at Lake Nona and has embarked on an unprecedented scholarship campaign to fund the entire inaugural class of students. The goal is to enable 40 students to be fully funded throughout medical school. According to the American Association of Medical Colleges, UCF would be the first U.S. school of medicine to make such an offer.

About 20 "founders" of the college donated more than $1 million each, and individuals and businesses have stepped forward with pledges to fund 15 of the 40 scholarships that German wants to offer her first class of students.

Speakers praised former Gov. Jeb Bush's support for the creation of the medical college and the efforts of state legislators, city and county commissioners and UCF trustees. It was only in 2006 that Bush and the Florida Board of Governors approved the UCF medical school as a way to increase opportunities for medical education in Florida, address the growing physician shortage nationwide and provide economic benefits to Central Florida and the state.

A year later, the same leaders with the courage to dream gathered to celebrate the reality that is already taking shape. German reminded everyone that change begins with a small group.

"Never doubt that a small group of thoughtful, committed citizens can change the world. Indeed, it is the only thing that ever has," German said quoting famed anthropologist Margaret Mead.

She also reminded everyone that the groundbreaking is the first of many steps on the journey. She invited the community to stick with UCF as the school evolves.

The mayors lauded the support of all of the partners and stressed the economic powerhouse being built at Lake Nona and how it will help strengthen the region by diversifying the economy.

The highlight of the celebration featured speakers grabbing shovels and donning hard hats to turn a little bit of dirt. German used the same shovel that founding UCF President Charles Millican used for the groundbreaking ceremony of UCF's first building on the main campus in 1966. It seemed appropriate, officials said, given how UCF changed the landscape of East Orlando, much the way the medical city is expected to transform this part of Orlando.

Burnham Institute CEO Reed told the audience that this is just the beginning of more good things to come.

"It's not just about economic impact; it's about saving lives," Reed said.

The College of Medicine expects to open in the fall 2009, pending accreditation from the Liaison Committee on Medical Education. The college expects to enroll an initial class of 40 students and eventually produce about 120 medical graduates each year. The four-year medical program will capitalize on UCF's existing strengths in biomedical sciences, modeling and simulation, and optics and photonics. Other programs in materials science, psychology, chemistry, film and digital media, and nursing will support the program.
 
The UCF Health Sciences Campus at Lake Nona will be comprised of two separate five-story structures, a 195,000 square-foot Burnett Biomedical Sciences Building and a 173,000 square-foot College of Medicine building complete with resource learning center and medical library. UCF will seek certifications of the U.S. Green Building Council's Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design (LEED) standards for both buildings.
