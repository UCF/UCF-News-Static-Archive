UCF-Produced Documentary Focuses on Millennials as 'Next Great Generation'

By Kristi Bartlett June 8, 2007

Photo: Abeer Abdalla

A University of Central Florida documentary exploring the theory that those born in 1982 or later are the "next great generation" will premiere at noon Sunday, June 10, on WMFE-TV.

"Who's Next" explores historians and social researchers William Strauss and Neil Howe's theory about the millennial generation. The show, which will air again at noon Sunday, June 17, was developed and produced by Linda Chapin, director of the Metropolitan Center for Regional Studies at UCF, and Lisa Mills, an assistant professor in the School of Film and Digital Media.

The one-hour documentary was filmed at several Central Florida locations, including UCF's main campus during a student orientation and on the Lake County campaign trail with UCF alumnus and state legislative candidate Robert Thompson. The film features many UCF students and graduates, including a biology graduate student and an Army ROTC cadet.

Chapin learned about Strauss' and Howe's theory about the millennials when she read their book "Millennials Rising." She then approached Mills about producing a documentary on millennials and generational patterns. They posed the question, "Does this generation really have the ability to be the next great generation?"

"We don't answer the question in the film; we want viewers to create a dialogue and walk away wanting to know more about this generation," Mills said.

Howe and Strauss, who published their first book on generations in 1991, discovered that each generation is shaped by its location in history. Millennials are the offspring of Baby Boomers and Generation Xers. Howe and Strauss contend they are poised to make great social, cultural and political changes due to educational reform, better family values and a greater social conscience. These factors mirror the significant changes made by the post-World War II generation.

Chapin and Mills began working together in 2001, when Chapin arrived at UCF. Mills, a former producer for WESH-TV, knew Chapin from her days as Orange County chairwoman.

Chapin and Mills wanted to produce a television series about the relationship between UCF and the community. They developed UCF Metro, which premiered in 2001. Since then, 54 episodes have focused on issues of importance to the university and the Central Florida region. Students have been involved in UCF Metro since its inception, and many of them have gone on to professional roles in film and production.

"Who's Next" is part of Chapin's and Mills' recent foray into documentary filmmaking. About a dozen UCF students gained hands-on experience and college credits for their work on the film. Students researched, filmed and edited segments of the documentary, and two students served as associate producers.
 
Erika Rydell, a documentary major, and Benjamin Jaffe, a Radio/TV major, worked with Mills and Chapin on the film and accompanied Mills to Apopka High School to film millennials in drama class.

Rydell met Mills while taking her documentary workshop class. Students were asked to choose a millennial topic to research, and Rydell picked spirituality. She researched the topic, conducted interviews and edited her work. Rydell also traveled with Mills to the National Archives in Maryland to research and dub film footage from the 1950s. She said working on "Who's Next" was a valuable learning experience.

"I've learned that the best way to learn is to do, and working on this documentary with Dr. Mills has taught me a whole lot about the process of making one, as well as the content itself," Rydell said.

A feature-length film on the generational theories of Howe and Strauss is in the works Both Mills and Chapin will write and produce it, and they plan on working on the film over the summer so they can submit it to festivals in the fall.
