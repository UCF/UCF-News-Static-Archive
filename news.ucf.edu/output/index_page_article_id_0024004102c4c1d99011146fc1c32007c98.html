Seventh-Graders Experience Life as UCF Students During College Shadow Day

By Kristi Bartlett Updated March 23, 2007

Photo: Jacque Brund

Middle school students learned nursing skills on patient dummies with help from UCF student Martin Schiavenato during a College Shadow Day presentation on Friday.

More than 500 seventh-graders learned nursing skills on patient dummies, jammed with a steel-drum band and explored the human bone structure using mammal skulls during the University of Central FloridaÂ’s third-annual College Shadow Day on Friday, March 23.

The Burnett Honors College teamed up with Junior Achievement of Central Florida to help local middle school students experience "a day in the life" of college students. They learned about many of the majors that UCF offers, participated in several interactive sessions with UCF faculty members and heard UCF President John Hitt speak about the importance of a college education.

Memorial, Carver, Stonewall Jackson, Lockhart and Meadowbrook middle schools each sent about 100 students to the event, which was held from 10:30 a.m. to 3 p.m.

One speaker, Frank Logiudice, a faculty member from the Biology Department, combined a 3.5-foot false killer whale skull with advice on preparing for college. Jeff Moore, a percussion faculty member in the Music Department, invited students to join performers on stage to see the action up close and discusses career opportunities for students who complete a music education.

The participating students have spent the last six weeks in a Junior Achievement program called the Economics of Staying in School, which is taught by UCF student volunteers. The Economics of Staying in School emphasizes the importance of education to success in the workplace. For students from underserved schools, seventh grade is considered a pivotal year, and College Shadow Day is a great opportunity for them to learn about the value of going to college.

"UCF sees the value in providing these types of programs to K-12 students because it allows them to see that college is accessible to anyone who wants to attend," said Kelly Astro, director of Research and Civic Engagement for the Burnett Honors College.

Student volunteers from The Burnett Honors College, LEAD Scholars and College of Business Administration Cornerstone classes served as guides for the middle school students and answer their questions.
