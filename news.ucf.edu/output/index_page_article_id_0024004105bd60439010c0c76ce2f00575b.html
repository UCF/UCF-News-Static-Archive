First-Generation College Students to Benefit from New Fund-Raising Effort

By Zenaida Gonzalez Kotala Oct. 5, 2006

Photo: Jacque Brund

Donations to the First Generation Matching Grant Program will benefit students such as Janette Mateo, a sophomore majoring in Molecular Biology and Microbiology. Mateo applied to UCF even though she didn't know how she would pay for college.

The University of Central Florida is working to raise $1.1 million to help students who are the first in their families to attend college.

Coupled with state matching funds, money raised for the First Generation Matching Grant Program will help UCF attract and retain students whose parents may not be able to provide as much guidance as parents who have experienced college life.

The Florida Legislature set aside $6.6 million statewide this year to give to students based on financial need and the fact that they are the first in their families to attend a university. The statewide effort is called the First Generation Matching Grant Program.

UCF can tap into as much as $1.1 million of that pool. The university will receive $1 of state funds for every $1 it raises by Nov. 30. The UCF Foundation is asking donors to make contributions by Nov. 24 to ensure that the state deadline for submitting requests for matching funds is met.

There are a lot of scholarships out there for students, said Janette Mateo a sophomore at UCF majoring in Molecular Biology and Microbiology. But as a first-generation student its harder because you dont have parents who have gone through college and know how it all works.

Mateo, who grew up in Jacksonville, applied to UCF even though she didnt know how she would pay for it. Her parents emigrated from the Dominican Republic and never attended college, but they told Mateo that education was her ticket to success. Once Mateo was admitted, she thought she might have to decline because of the cost. But she qualified for financial aid and works part-time to pay for her studies.

This program sounds like a great idea, she said. If it werent for the help I got, I would probably be at home or maybe going to community college.

To give access to others like Mateo, the UCF Foundation is working to raise the matching money that is required before the university can tap into the state funds.

Lack of money should not be an obstacle to higher education, UCF President John Hitt said. Having a college degree transforms lives. It can change the future for our graduates and their families.

Students dont need to fill out any special paperwork for the grants. As long as they have completed the free national Application for Federal Student Aid, which documents the financial need and provides family education information, students will be eligible for money. Recipients of the first-generation grants must be undergraduates and Florida residents who are not in default of any other school loan programs.

Mateo said financial aid gave her the opportunity to study, and shes not wasting it.

My parents say it will be hard, but that I need to do it so I dont have such a hard time like them, Mateo said. Thats why Im here working really hard. They want something better for me, and so do I.

To make a donation to the First Generation Matching Grant Program, go to www.foundation.ucf.edu.
