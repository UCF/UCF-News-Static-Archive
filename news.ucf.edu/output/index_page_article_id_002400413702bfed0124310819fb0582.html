Scholars Discuss Evolving Relationships Between U.S., China and Taiwan

By UCF Staff Dec. 7, 2009

Related Contacts and Links:

  • Mark Freeman, Global Perspectives

    • 407-823-0935

  • Global Perspectives Office

  • Print this Page

Scholars in Taiwan and at the University of Central Florida discussed the evolving relationship among United States, China and Taiwan during a video conference Tuesday. The conference was the first in a series of such conversations that are part of a new initiative, The China-Taiwan Cross-Strait Program at UCF.

Fu-Kuo Liu, a research fellow and chair of the Research Division of American and European Studies at the Institute of International Relations of National Chengchi University in Taipei, led the discussion from the Taiwanese side. He described what many view as a thawing of relations between Taipei and Beijing since the election of Taiwan’s President Ma Ying-jeou in May 2008.

A proposed free trade agreement, known as the Economic Cooperation Framework Agreement, could be very significant for cross-strait relations, Liu said. Of course, any free trade agreement will encounter political difficulties, he added. For example, Taiwan contends it should be on a more equal footing within the World Trade Organization. Also, Taiwan’s labor market is a fraction of labor markets in mainland China, which puts Taiwan at a disadvantage. However, Liu said he is hopeful that the free trade agreement will be accepted by mid-2010, with full implementation of the economic framework in five to seven years.

Liu also noted other areas of potential cooperation, such as dealing with environmental matters, including global climate change. John Bersia, special assistant to the president for Global Perspectives at UCF and a co-chair of The China-Taiwan Cross-Strait Program, indicated that this issue has particular resonance at UCF, where it serves as the undergraduate unifying theme. In addition, during 2009-2010, the Global Perspectives Office is co-sponsoring a series of speakers, panels and workshops on the topic of climate change and security.

Liu pointed out that while there are some very important political obstacles in the way -- most notably Taiwan’s exclusion from United Nations membership -- he hoped for a positive outcome from the U.N. Climate Change Conference in Copenhagen and for future collaborative efforts between China and Taiwan.

President Barack Obama’s recent trip to China and meeting with President Hu Jintao were also discussed during the video conference.

Roger Handberg, chair of the UCF Political Science Department and a co-director of The China-Taiwan Cross-Strait Program, suggested that the warming of relations between the United States and China will have “no short-term impact for U.S.-Taiwan relations.” The assumption by the United States, he said, is that during the long term Taiwan will work out its issues with China on its own without interference from Washington. Furthermore, “China has internal issues it must deal with over the next 10 to 20 years,” Handberg said.

Quan Li, an assistant professor in Political Science at UCF and an advisory board member for The China-Taiwan Cross-Strait Program, indicated his interest in promoting social, cultural and economic exchange between China and Taiwan. 

“This conference was a great start,” he said. “We were able to expand our knowledge of the Taiwanese side of the issues. I look forward to participating in more of these meetings.”

UCF has established a new fellowship that focuses on China-Taiwan issues. The China-Taiwan Fellowship is an opportunity for a UCF student – a junior, senior or graduate student – to work on a research project related to China-Taiwan cross-strait issues under the direction of staff and scholars from The China-Taiwan Cross-Strait Program and the Global Perspectives Office.

The first China-Taiwan Fellow, for the spring 2010 semester, will be selected soon. To apply, or for more information, click here. 

The China-Taiwan Cross-Strait Program is housed in the Department of Political Science, and supported by the C.T. and Jean Hsu Fund of the Global Connections Foundation and the Global Perspectives Office.
