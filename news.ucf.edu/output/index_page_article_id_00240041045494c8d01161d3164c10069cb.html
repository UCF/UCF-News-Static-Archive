FIEA Grads Deliver Holiday's Hottest Video Games

By Christine Dellert Dec. 10, 2007

Photo: UCF News File

Students learn to develop video games in FIEA's 16-month graduate program. Its third class graduates this week.

Several of the holiday season's must-have games are the work of recent graduates from the Florida Interactive Entertainment Academy at the University of Central Florida.

Already well into this holiday-selling season, tech experts say it may be the best yet for the video-game industry. And former FIEA students are making the year's top picks more visually stunning with advanced player features.

Gamers and makers predict record sales to continue through the new year, as the gaming industry steadily grows its audience.

"That's because we're in the age of interactivity," said 2007 FIEA grad Giovanni Acosta, who helped engineer the newly released "Call of Duty: Modern Warfare" for Nintendo DS at the Orlando-based n-Space gaming company.

"Video games have been around for years, and parents are now playing them as much as their kids," he said.

Acosta joins other former FIEA classmates at big-name video-game makers, such as Electronic Arts and Midway Games, delivering several of the season's most popular products -- "Tiger Woods PGA Tour 08," "NCAA Football 08," "Madden 08," and "Call of Duty: Modern Warfare."

Also contributing to this season's games are students from FIEA's third class set to graduate Dec. 14. The class of 34 is the largest since UCF's 16-month graduate game-development school opened its doors in August 2005.

All of the December graduates will give presentations on their final jobs, gaming internships or projects during two showcases at FIEA in downtown Orlando on Dec. 7 and Dec. 13. Students spent the past months at EA Tiburon, 360Ed, Midway Chicago, Gamelab and Artificial Studios.

FIEA was created in May 2004 as a unique partnership between UCF, the state, the City of Orlando and EA. Since its inception, FIEA has graduated 29 people, with 97 percent landing jobs in their industry of choice and an average starting salary of $50,865.

"FIEA is committed to its students and to support Central Florida's growing gaming and interactive entertainment industry," said Todd Deery, FIEA's communication and admission director. "We're always pleased when graduation rolls around and so many of our students are excelling both locally and across the country."

Acosta, a Miami native, says he's grateful to have worked on n-Space's latest Nintendo DS release, predicted to sell about 300,000 copies. The 27-year-old software engineer helped program multiple aspects of the first-person-shooter game, including multiplayer, gameplay and weapon systems.

It's exciting, he said, to see his work in store windows around Orlando and his hometown.

The same goes for 2006 FIEA graduate Carlos Barbosa, who worked as an assistant producer on the latest "Tiger Woods" game for Xbox 360 and PlayStation 3 at EA's Maitland studio.

Barbosa, who grew up in Fort Lauderdale, worked on the offline game modes, such as Tiger Challenge, which advances players through a golf career and a final match-up with Woods. The 26-year-old also helped to add a new online feature that allows players to save any shot or round of golf online to challenge others virtually.

"It's not like a celebrity status," he said wryly about watching shoppers scoop up the game at Best Buy. "It's more nerve-wracking."
