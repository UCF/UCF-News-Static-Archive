Victim Advocates Help UCF Students, Others Cope With Crimes

By Chad Binette May 27, 2004

Photo: Jerry Klein

Incoming freshmen could pick up brochures about Victim Services during a recent student orientation session.

An abusive boyfriend or girlfriend or a sexual assault can be especially traumatic for students whose parents and best friends live several states away. 

 

But UCF students dont have to go far to find someone whos willing to listen to them talk about what happened, help them deal with the experience and assist with filing a police report or seeking a court injunction if necessary. They can call Victim Services and seek the confidential help of a trained advocate such as Kim Carter-Williams.

 

We dont tell people what to do, said Carter-Williams, who has worked nine years as an advocate for UCF students, faculty and staff. We give them options and empower people to make their own decisions.

 

Carter-Williams is one of four advocates who help members of the UCF community who are victims of crimes  mainly sexual assaults, stalking and domestic and relationship violence.

 

UCF students, faculty and staff can meet with an advocate for free about any crime that happened on or off campus. While advocates mainly help with recent crimes, they also help people cope with crimes that happened several years ago.

 

The advocates work 8 a.m. to 4:30 p.m. weekdays in a modular building next to the Physical Plant Building on the southern edge of the campus. Daytime appointments are available, and walk-ins are welcome. The advocates can be contacted through e-mail at vsanswer@mail.ucf.edu or through several telephone numbers listed on their Web site, www.victimservices.ucf.edu.

 

 

An advocate is always on call at night and on weekends and can be paged through the Police Departments main dispatch number, 407-823-5555. Callers who request that an advocate be paged only need to leave a first name and a phone number where they can be reached.

 

One of the programs main goals is to make more people on campus aware of the services they offer. Since program coordinator Christine Mouton arrived two years ago, the staff has made the offices Web site more thorough and easier to navigate. They have started attending student orientation events to reach out to incoming freshmen. And they speak regularly about sexual assault, stalking and other topics during presentations to classes.

 

Victim advocate LaShawn Rivera said one of the offices main goals is to show students what they can do to prevent abuse and to help them better understand the differences between healthy and unhealthy relationships.

 

During a recent Sociology of Domestic Violence class with Professor Jana Jasinski, Carter-Williams explained the legal definitions of stalking to students and suggested that, if theyre being stalked, they should keep e-mail and phone messages to help build a case against the stalker. The students also discussed scenarios of stalking and explained why they felt particular incidents were or were not severe.

 

On the course evaluations, many students write that they appreciate that a representative from Victim Services came to talk, Jasinski said. That makes a huge difference.

The advocates helped 188 clients in 2003. They've noticed that many people who meet with them learned about the services in class or through a friend who heard a class presentation.

 

 

 

Three of the four advocates, who are also Police Department employees, had ties to UCF before working here, and all of them worked with area social-service agencies before taking their current jobs.

 

Mouton, who received her masters and bachelors degrees in criminal justice from UCF, is a former victim advocate with the State Attorneys Office in Seminole County. She now teaches a night class on victims and the criminal justice system.

 

One of Moutons goals is to move the office closer to the center of campus so its more accessible for students, faculty and staff. The current location on the southern edge of the campus is too remote to attract walk-in clients, she said.

 

Because some potential clients dont view themselves as victims, the office also plans to change its name soon to eliminate victim from the title, Mouton said.

 

Carter-Williams, who has a bachelors degree in psychology and a masters certificate in domestic violence from UCF, worked for a crisis hotline before taking the victim advocate job here nine years ago.

 

Rivera, who came to UCF a year ago, previously interned at the Work-Oriented Rehabilitation Center in Daytona Beach. She also interned at a domestic violence shelter and rape-crisis center in Volusia County. Rivera majored in psychology at Bethune-Cookman College.

 

 

Keri Nola has bachelors degrees in psychology and communications from UCF and is pursuing a masters degree in counseling. She worked for the Reach Peer Education group on campus for 4 ½ years and previously was an intern for Victim Services.

 

Nola said she enjoys having a job that allows her to work with a variety of people from students to faculty to police officers.

 

Its not just a sit-down-at-your-desk job, she said. Were trying to get people to know the services that are available to them.

 

Mouton and Carter-Williams are members of the Florida Crisis Response Team, which sends volunteers to help victims of hurricanes and other tragedies. Shortly after the terrorist attacks on Sept. 11, 2001, Mouton and Carter-Williams spent 10 days volunteering at the New Jersey Family Assistance Center.

 

It reaffirmed my view of the strength of the human spirit, Carter-Williams said. What people go through and still be strong is what keeps me doing this.
