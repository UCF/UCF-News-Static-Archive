UCF Hosts 3rd Annual Hispanic Heritage Festival

By Jessica Saggio Aug. 30, 2007

Related Contacts and Links:

  • Jose Maunez-Cuadra, Latin American, Caribbean and Latino Studies

    • 407-823-1174

  • Hispanic Heritage Festival at UCF

  • Print this Page

With the strum of a Spanish guitar, and the sound of Flamenco dancers, the 3rd annual Latin American Cultural Festival will kick off September 9 at the University of Central Florida.

Beginning with the Rumba Latina concert at 3 p.m. on Sunday, Sept. 9, the university will host an array of events to celebrate National Hispanic Heritage Month. The month, which runs Sept. 15 to Oct. 15 nationally, recognizes the contributions Hispanics have made to mankind from scientific discoveries to art.

The first concert, which is free to the public, is a mix of classical musical instruments and those indigenous to Latin countries. A combination of UCF and community musicians will play Danzas, Danzones, Boleros, Samba, Bomba and Plena. The musicians have envisioned a special performance integrating instruments traditionally used in classical music with percussive instruments, like the conga, timbales, güiro or bongo, usually associated with popular Latin music.

The event not only offers a feast for the ears, but for the eyes as well. Estampas Puertorriqueñas an internationally recognized roving art exhibit on loan from the Centro de Cultura Puertorriqueña de la Florida, Inc. will be on display during the concert. The exhibit is a good example of contemporary Puerto Rican pictorial art.

The university will host eight events on campus throughout late September and October.

In an effort to expand community-wide knowledge about different Spanish-speaking countries, this years festival incorporates exhibits or performances from countries not represented last year, Mexico, Brazil, and Spain, among others.

Because there are so many Latin countries, each year we want to showcase a different set of countries so that during each festival the audience gets a different piece of the pie that makes up the Spanish-speaking population, said José Maunez-Cuadra, Festival Coordinator and director of UCFs Latin American, Caribbean and Latino Studies.

The month-long program includes classical Spanish dance, concerts, and speakers. The Enzian Theater in Maitland will present a special screening of El Violin, an award-winning Mexican film on Oct. 16.

All campus events are free of charge to the public.  The Enzian showing of El Violín, will have a fee of $9.00 general admission and $7.00 admission for students.

With the growing numbers of UCFs Hispanic population (about 13 percent of the entire student-body), the university has made it a goal to recognize the celebration of Spanish heritage and use it as a catalyst to encourage diversity on campus.

We want to give the opportunity to any who wants to visit with us to taste the flavor of Latin American culture, Maunez-Cuadra said. We are contributing not only to diversity but to the mission of the university- to educate. As Hispanic populations grow, the university has a commitment to respond to this and the festival is one of the many tools the university has to contribute to the understanding of the Hispanic community.
