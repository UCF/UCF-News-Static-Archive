UCFs Nine Miss Florida Pageant Contestants Excel in Classroom, Community

By Chad Binette June 22, 2005

Photo: Richard Trainor

UCF's nine Miss Florida contestants. Front row (left to right): Evy "Christina" Tonn, Megan Clementi, Lauren Meyer, Jennifer Hartzler. Back row: Rachel Potter, Sheena Howell, Nichole McPherson, Danielle Marino and Denesha Jenkins

Devoted to helping children learn to read, fighting diabetes, building communities and many other causes, nine University of Central Florida contestants competed for the Miss Florida pageant title June 29 to July 2 in Miami.

 

Denesha Jenkins finished as the third runner-up, while Megan Clementi, Rachel Potter and Danielle Marino also finished in the top ten.

 

Eight UCF students and 2004 graduate Danielle Marino made up about one-fifth of the field that competed for scholarships and the chance to move on to the Miss America pageant. No other university had more than six Miss Florida contestants.

Were all really good friends, and its nice to share this experience and get support from one another, said Sheena Howell, a senior microbiology major who is Miss Ocala/Marion County. All of the girls are intelligent, beautiful and really involved in the community.

Howell, of Leesburg, often speaks to middle and high school students about the importance of abstaining from sex until marriage, and shes promoting the Education Now and Babies Later initiative through her platform. Howell, who wants to become a dentist, is competing in her second Miss Florida pageant.

Miss University of Central Florida Jennifer Hartzler, a senior honors student, wants to help people build communities at the local, state and national levels. Noting that strong communities enhance the overall quality of life while weak communities can produce economic hardship and declining schools, Hartzler encourages residents to volunteer through the USA Freedom Corps. She also plans to work with chambers of commerce to enhance the investments of businesses in their communities.

Ive always believed in service, and Ive always believed in working together to solve a communitys needs, said Hartzler, who chairs UCFs Presidents Leadership Council of student ambassadors.

Hartzler, an economics major who competed in her first pageant two years ago, said she believes the key to performing well is to be confident and comfortable with who you are. She said shes looking forward to having so many other UCF students with her at the competition because many of them are close friends.

The nine of us just had a blast at orientation, said Hartzler, of Oviedo. Its a little sisterhood.

The nine contestants hope to build on UCFs recent success in pageants. Ericka Dunlap, an advertising and public relations major who graduated in the spring, won the Miss America pageant in 2004. UCF alumna Monica Spear, a 2004 graduate who majored in theater, won the Miss Venezuela pageant last year and was the fourth runner-up at the Miss Universe pageant in May.

Nichole McPherson, who won the Miss Apopka title this year, volunteers with the American Diabetes Association and urges children to eat healthy foods. Inspired by her fathers battle with a form of pre-diabetes several years ago, she developed her platform around the theme, Teach Your Children Well: The Importance of Diabetes Education.

Im not going (to Miss Florida) just to win a pretty crown, said McPherson, a senior majoring in organizational communications who was born in New York and has lived most of her life in Orlando. Im going because of what I can do with the crown and for the many children I can reach and talk to not only about diabetes education, but also dedication and percipience.

Denesha Jenkins, who holds the Miss City Beautiful title, is an aspiring teacher who promotes literacy through her platform. Jenkins wants children to get excited about reading. Jenkins, a junior from Belle Glade, is an elementary education major who volunteers in schools in Orange and Seminole counties.

Jenkins, who is competing in her second Miss Florida pageant, is the only one in her family of six to attend college. She said she hopes to win scholarship money that will help her continue with her education and added that pageants have helped her to improve her public speaking skills.

Megan Clementi, a junior from Jupiter and the Miss South Florida Fair titleholder, has volunteered at Give Kids the World Village in Kissimmee since she performed there eight years ago with a youth gymnastics team. The nonprofit resort provides free accommodations, donated meals and tickets to theme parks and other attractions for children with life-threatening illnesses and their families. 

 

Clementi, an electrical engineering major, made helping children with life-threatening diseases her pageant platform because she enjoys doing what she can to ensure that the children can experience fairy tale weeks and forget about their illnesses.

Evy Christina Tonn, a junior political science major and Burnett Honors College student, holds the title of Miss St. Petersburg. Tonn, of Sarasota, is promoting personal responsibility in todays generation through her platform.

Danielle Marino graduated in 2004 after studying theater performance and interior design at UCF. Marino, of Ocala, holds the Miss Seminole County title, and her platform is Character First -- The Power of Self-Respect. Lauren Meyer, a sophomore from Ocala, holds the Miss Winter Park title. A visual language major, Meyers platform is Links for Life: Connecting Our Youth to a Healthier Tomorrow.

Rachel Potter, 20, a junior from Seminole, won the Miss Seminole title. An advertising and public relations major, Potters platform is Developing Outstanding Character in Americas Youth.
