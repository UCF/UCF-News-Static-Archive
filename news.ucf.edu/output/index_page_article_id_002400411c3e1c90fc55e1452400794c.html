Regional Governments Best Way to Rebuild Iraq, Says Official Who Led Effort

By Chad Binette May 26, 2004

Photo: Jerry Klein

Jay Garner talks about his proposal for establishing a workable government in Iraq.

To ensure that Iraqis perceive their new governments as nonthreatening and respectful of cultural, ethnic and religious differences, the United States should help establish separate regional governments for Kurdish, Sunni and Shiite areas of the country, the former head of the U.S. effort to rebuild Iraq said Wednesday at the University of Central Florida.

 

Jay Garner, who was in Iraq in spring 2003, also said the United States should offer Iraqis robust economic incentives, including the forgiveness of the countrys massive debt, and speed up the creation of a security force of Iraqis that can handle everything from routine police duties to border patrols.

 

We need to immediately make the face of security on Iraqi streets an Iraqi face  and not American or British faces, Garner told a crowd of about 225 at the UCF Student Union.

 

Garner recommends creating a federalist type of government similar to what the United States had in its early years with 13 strong colonies and a weak federal government. Forcing a strong national government on Iraq too soon could cause problems with Kurds, Shiites and Sunnis who may fear that one of the other groups would take too much power.

 

Garner said he envisions organizing Iraqs 18 provinces into about five regional governments  one consisting of the Kurdish provinces in the north, a second one made up of Sunni areas northwest and east of Baghdad, two in the Shiite areas south of Baghdad and a fifth in Baghdad itself. Voters in each province would decide which regional government they want to join, and a national assembly would be formed later.

 

Garner stressed that Iraqis should be given the time and authority to set up their new governments and that setting the June 30 deadline for the creation of a new Iraqi government was a mistake.

 

We have to allow them to fail, he said. Were worried about their elections. Hell, our last election was a disaster, and weve been doing it for 225 years. Its OK if they (the Iraqis) dont do it right the first and second times.

 

 

 

Some of the potential problems of creating a strong national government too early include disagreements over womens rights, Sunni and Shiite concerns about Kurdish repression and Kurdish fears of the Middle Easts legacy of Arab nationalism, Garner said.

 

The UCF Office of Global Perspectives coordinated Garners visit, which was also sponsored by the Middle Eastern Studies Program, the Political Science Department and the Global Connections Foundation.

 

A retired lieutenant general and well-decorated Vietnam War veteran, Garner now is president of SYColeman, a company that works with the Department of Defense and private firms on computer network operations, missile test planning and a variety of other tasks. He is an Arcadia native.

 

Secretary of Defense Donald Rumsfeld asked Garner in January 2003 to lead the Office of Reconstruction and Humanitarian Assistance for a post-war Iraq. Garner presided over the rebuilding effort until May 2003, when career diplomat L. Paul Bremer was appointed to take his place. Garner said his appointment was a temporary one and that when he took the post, he wasnt even sure that he would go to Iraq himself.

 

Garner ended up heading to the war-torn nation, where he met with Iraqis in schools, police stations and markets and attempted to begin rebuilding key utilities and services.

 

 

 

Garner praised the skills of Iraqis, saying they are capable of making their country a modern one that is a model for other Middle Eastern nations if theyre given the right tools. Those tools include some kind of economic incentives for residents. Even something as simple as offering families $1,000 each in return for giving up functioning weapons would help significantly at a time when there is so much unemployment, he said.

 

The United States and its coalition partners have made a lot of progress in rebuilding schools, hospitals and other infrastructure in a country that saw almost no technological improvements in the past 30 years, Garner said. About 15,000 schools have been rebuilt, Iraqis have more electrical power than ever and Iraqi children have been given vaccines to protect them from a variety of diseases, he said.

 

Garner defended the United States decision to go to war because of the many human rights violations committed by Saddam Hussein and his allies. He said a nation as powerful as the United States has the moral and ethical responsibility to try to prevent such abuses whenever it can.

 

Many Iraqis thanked Garner and his staff for the coalitions decision to stand up to Husseins regime and added that they only wished Hussein had been removed in the first Iraqi war, Garner said.

 

Garner said he still believes Hussein possessed weapons of mass destruction even though none have been found so far.

 

We gave him 12 years to learn how to hide them, Garner said.
