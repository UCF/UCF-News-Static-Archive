$1.5 Million Grant for UCF Optics Expansion to Boost Research, Jobs in Region

By Chad Binette Oct. 25, 2004

Photo: Jerry Klein

David Sampson, Asst. Secretary of Commerce, at Tuesday's event.

A $1.5 million grant from the U.S. Economic Development Administration will allow the University of Central Florida to build more laboratories at the Florida Photonics Center of Excellence and create jobs by transferring more technology to photonics manufacturers in Central Florida.

UCF will add about 24 new laboratories to the campus building that houses the College of Optics and Photonics and two research centers, including the Florida Photonics Center for Excellence, or FPCE. Start-up companies will work side-by-side with UCF students and faculty, improving the flow of new technology from FPCE labs to companies that can apply it in new or improved products.

U.S. Assistant Secretary of Commerce for Economic Development David Sampson awarded UCF the $1.5 million grant during a ceremony Tuesday morning in the lobby of the College of Optics and Photonics building.

 

Sampson said one of the Economic Development Administration's top priorities is to help universities improve technology and lead economic development efforts.

 

"You're working on research here that's going to make computing much faster," Sampson said. "America can compete no faster than it can compute."

 

State Sen. Lee Constantine, R-Altamonte Springs, and state Reps. Sandy Adams, R-Oviedo; Jim Kallinger, R-Winter Park; and David Mealor, R-Lake Mary, joined Sampson. Other attendees included Pamella Dana, director of the Governor's Office of Tourism, Trade and Economic Development; Randy Berridge, president of the Florida High Tech Corridor Council; and Dick Nunis, chairman of the UCF Board of Trustees.

 

UCF officials projected that the grant will help to create 265 jobs, retain 123 additional jobs and generate up to $47 million in investments from businesses.

This award from the Economic Development Administration will fund another of the successful partnerships between UCF and industry that have become the hallmark of our universitys successful growth, said Eric Van Stryland, dean of the College of Optics and Photonics and director of FPCE and the Center for Research and Education in Optics and Lasers, which is known as CREOL. It will allow us to build a much-needed addition so we can continue to provide our students with the best high-tech research labs and to reach out to an industry that is so critical to the Central Florida economy.

The 12,800-square-foot addition, which also will include 24 research offices, will increase the size of the colleges building to nearly 95,800 square feet. The expansion will benefit some of the approximately 150 optics companies located along the Interstate 4 High Tech Corridor between Tampa and Melbourne.

The Economic Development Administration serves as a venture capital resource for the economic development needs of distressed communities. The Metro Orlando Economic Development Commission helped UCF show that the struggles of the photonics industry since 2001 fit the description of a distressed community.

The grant will be matched with more than $2 million from UCF and the FPCE, a research center at the College of Optics and Photonics that the state created in 2003. UCF received a $10 million grant from the state last year to open the center, which promotes partnerships between UCF, the photonics industry and local economic development agencies.

In a bipartisan letter of support for the Economic Development Administration grant, five Florida members of the U.S. House of Representatives wrote that it is clear that investing in the FPCE as an engine for diversifying our states economy is a top priority for all of us. The April letter was signed by U.S. Reps. Tom Feeney, R-Oviedo; Ric Keller, R-Orlando; John Mica, R-Winter Park; Ginny Brown-Waite, R-Brooksville; and Corrine Brown, D-Jacksonville.

The UCF College of Optics and Photonics has about 160 graduate students and 36 teaching and research professors. In May, the optics program became the first at a major U.S. university to be designated as a free-standing college. CREOL was created in 1985 to help promote growth of the optics industry in Central Florida.
