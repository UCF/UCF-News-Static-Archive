Computer Programming Team Performs Well at World Championships

By Sam Gardner Updated March 15, 2007

Photo: Courtesy of Ali Orooji

A UCF team made up of seniors Walter Mundt and Jobby Johns and first-year graduate student Nadeem Mohsin won the regional contest in Georgia last October, solving 9 of 10 problems correctly.

While many UCF students are spending their spring break at the beach or visiting family back home, three UCF students have been tapping away at a computer and vying for a world championship.

The UCF computer programming team finished in a tie for 44th place at the 31st annual ACM International Collegiate Programming Contest in Tokyo. UCF was tied with schools such as Carnegie Mellon, Cornell and Duke and placed ahead of Harvard, Northwestern and Vanderbilt.

The competition, sponsored by IBM, brought together some of the most talented computer programmers from across the globe for a battle of the brains from March 12 to 16.

This competition is between the top 88 teams, dwindled down from an original pool of more than 6,000 teams from 1,756 universities that took part in the regional competitions in the fall. UCF is one of only 20 U.S. teams in the World Finals.

Ive been fortunate with teams over the years, said Ali Orooji, the teams coach and an associate professor of Computer Science.  Thats why our record is matched by no other school in our region.

Orooji has been the faculty advisor to the computer programming teams for the last 18 years. In his tenure, the team has never placed worst than third at the ACM Southeast Regional Competition.
 
The team is made up of senior Walter Mundt of Orlando, senior Jobby Johns of Jacksonville and first-year graduate student Nadeem Mohsin of India. Those three competed on the UCF team that won the regional contest in Georgia last October, solving 9 of 10 problems correctly. Mundt was a member of the UCF team that received an honorable mention during the 2006 world finals last April in San Antonio, Texas.

To win the worlds smartest trophy, teams of three students use their programming skills to solve intricate, real-world problems. The competitors solve a semesters worth of curriculum  eight or more problems -- under a demanding five-hour deadline. The team that solves the most problems in the least amount of time wins.

Questions can range in nature from the complex arrangement of light switches to configuring a system of bridges to connect all of the buildings in a major downtown setting.

The team practices 25 Saturdays a year for seven hours, including a one-hour lecture on programming strategies followed by a five-hour mock competition.  The competition is followed by a one-hour discussion on the problems practiced.

A team from the United States has not won the world championship since 1997, when Harvey Mudd College, in Claremont, Calif., won the competition.

One aspect of the International Collegiate Programming Contest that distinguishes itself from other programming contests is the fact that each team can only use one computer, despite the fact that each team has three students. The winning team will receive scholarships and prizes from IBM.
