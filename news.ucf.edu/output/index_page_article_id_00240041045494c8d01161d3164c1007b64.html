Former Egyptian First Lady Jehan Sedat Discusses Husband's Drive for Peace (With Video)

By Chad Binette Nov. 15, 2007

Photo: Courtesy of UCF Global Perspectives

Jehan Sadat is a former first lady of Egypt who championed her late husband's drive for peace.

Jehan Sadat, a former first lady of Egypt, described her mission to "continue my husband’s work for peace" and emphasized the importance of education in increasing opportunities for women worldwide during a speech Tuesday at UCF.

Sadat is the widow of President Anwar Sadat, who was assassinated Oct. 6, 1981, after he had reached a historic peace accord with Israeli Prime Minister Menachem Begin. Sadat shared the 1978 Nobel Peace Prize with Begin as a result of the accord.

"He knew he was going to pay a very high price (for peace)," Jehan Sadat told the crowd in the Pegasus Ballroom of the Student Union. "He knew he was going to lose his life. But that didn’t stop him. He wanted new generations to grow up in a peaceful atmosphere."

More than 700 people attended the forum organized by the UCF Global Perspectives Office. During the question-and-answer period, some audience members described Jehan Sadat as their heroine and one even asked if she has any desire to become the first female president of Egypt.

Sadat said she has no such political aspirations. She now splits her time between Egypt and the United States, where she lectures all over the country.

"I do not miss the pomp and circumstance" of being the first lady, she said. "I only miss my husband."

Jehan Sadat married Anwar Sadat just before she turned 16. She had not yet finished high school and ended up completing it at home because, at the time, Egyptian girls did not attend school after they married. She later attended college at Cairo University – and eventually earned a doctoral degree there -- while she was first lady because she wanted other girls to understand the importance of getting an education.

Sadat became well-known for her charitable work during her tenure as first lady. In 1972 she founded the Wafa Wa Amal (Faith & Hope) Rehabilitation Center, which is a fully integrated city for handicapped war veterans and civilians, complete with clinics, rehabilitation centers, vocational training and recreation facilities.

In 1977, Sadat initiated the concept behind S.O.S. Children's Villages in Egypt, which are designed to offer orphans the opportunity to lead a normal family life within a happy and healthy atmosphere. The first of these was established in Cairo, followed by ones in Alexandria and Tanta, and many more are planned around the country.

Sadat has founded or headed many other charitable organizations and associations, including the Egyptian Red Crescent Society, which cares for disaster victims and promotes international humanitarian law and values.

Sadat has taught at Cairo University and has been a visiting professor at American University in Washington, D.C., the University of South Carolina and the University of Maryland.

In her autobiography, "A Woman of Egypt," Sadat candidly shares her story as patriot, wife, mother, feminist and first lady. She is also in the process of building a museum in Cairo in her late husband’s honor.

In addition to the UCF Global Perspectives Office, sponsors of this forum included the UCF Student Government Association, Islamic Society of Central Florida, UCF Office of International Studies, UCF Burnett Honors College, Arab American Community Center of Central Florida, UCF Political Science Department, UCF Middle Eastern Studies Program, UCF Diplomacy Program, UCF International Services Center and the Global Connections Foundation.
