President Hitt Discusses Benefits of Possible Med School

By Tom Evelyn April 28, 2005

Photo: Jerry Klein

UCF has in place a strong foundation for a medical school, including the Burnett College of Biomedical Sciences, which is researching treatments for cancer and cardiovascular, infectious and neurodegenerative diseases.

A medical school at the University of Central Florida could immediately pump $750 million to $1 billion into the Orlando economy and nearly $3 billion a year once its fully operational, UCF President John Hitt told community leaders on Thursday.

The school would also help address Floridas critical shortage of doctors, a problem that will only increase as the states older population grows, said Hitt, who was speaking at the Presidents Focus on Excellence breakfast forum. The state already has nearly 500 doctors fewer than it needs, and that number is expected to grow to 1,600 by 2021.

If Florida expanded its existing medical schools and added residency programs, it would still offset by only 14 percent Floridas need for more physicians, according to a study by MGT of America. The state needs at least two additional medical schools to address the shortage, and UCF is prepared to open one of them, the study determined.

If were going to address this need, we have to have a sense of urgency and get going, Hitt said. UCF is well-positioned to take on the task.

UCFs Board of Trustees voted March 31 to pursue the medical school. The university will seek approval in the next several months from the state Board of Governors, which oversees Floridas 11 public universities. If approved, UCF would seek funding from the state Legislature.

The estimated startup cost would be $5 million to $6 million, with an annual operating budget of about $30 million, Hitt said. The school would eventually enroll about 120 students a year.

Medical schools bring in valuable federal research funding and add high-paying jobs to the community. The average economic impact of each medical school in Florida is $2.7 billion a year. The national average annual salary for medical school employees is $98,000.

Orlando is the largest U.S. metropolitan area without a medical school, even though it is also home to two of the largest medical systems in the country -- Florida Hospital and Orlando Regional Healthcare, both of which have endorsed building a medical school in Orlando.

UCF has in place a strong foundation for a medical school. It is the second-largest producer of bachelors degrees and the fourth-largest producer of medical school preparatory degrees among Florida universities. UCF also had the third-largest number of research contracts and grants between 1994 and 2003 and is the states third-largest producer of patents.

A $10 million donation last year from Al and Nancy Burnett of Winter Park created the Burnett College of Biomedical Sciences, which would be the base for a medical school. The university already has many medically related research centers and programs, including the Center for Research and Education in Optics and Lasers, the Institute for Simulation and Training, the Nanoscience and Technology Center, the Advanced Materials Processing and Analysis Center and micro-electrical mechanical engineering.

Related areas of research at UCF include simulated human patients and virtual endoscopic surgery devices for training medical professionals; nanoscience and drug delivery; mechanized microsurgery; cancer and quality of life for breast cancer survivors; and cardiovascular, infectious and neurodegenerative diseases.

This is a realistic dream that can be realized with the support of this community, said Pappachan Kolattukudy, dean of the Burnett College of Biomedical Sciences.
