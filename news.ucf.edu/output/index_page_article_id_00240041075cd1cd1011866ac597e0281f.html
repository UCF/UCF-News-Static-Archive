Experts Discuss the Future of U.S.-Latin American Relations at UCF Forum

By Briannie Torres Feb. 29, 2008

Related Contacts and Links:

  • Mark Freeman, Global Perspectives

    • 407-823-0935

  • Global Perspectives Office

  • Print this Page

Former American officials and experts on U.S. and Latin American relations discussed free trade and economic development during a public forum at UCF’s Student Union on Wednesday.

The event, sponsored by the UCF Global Perspectives Office, was titled “The U.S. and Latin America: Toward a New Relationship?” 

The forum included a three-part discussion, beginning with Ambassador Myles Frechette, a former president and CEO of the Council of the Americas and a former U.S. ambassador to Colombia.

Speaking to an audience of about 150, Frechette expressed optimism about future hemispheric free-trade prospects. In the last five years, he said, poverty levels have decreased in Brazil, Chile, Mexico and Venezuela, and Brazil and Argentina are now creditor nations. But there are also challenges, including the opposition of Bolivia, Ecuador and Venezuela to globalization and rising inflation rates in Argentina and Venezuela, Frechette said.

Frechette also mentioned that the U.S. invasion of Iraq has deeply affected Latin America. Frechette recommended a new strategy to secure better relations with the region, including a “hands-off approach,” and suggested that when Latin American governments acknowledge the need for change, the United States should offer it.

When asked by an audience member about post-Communist-era strategic goals in Latin America, Frechette responded that the aftermath of the Cold War “brought a change in the U.S. strategic interest in the region.” The United States previously had aimed to contain and defeat communism, but current efforts are concerned with promoting growth and democracy in Latin America, he said.

Following Frechette, a panel discussion moderated by Don Mathews of Daytona Beach College focused on the Dominican Republic. Speakers included Terry McCoy, director of the Latin American Business Environment Program at the University of Florida, and Waltraud Morales, a professor in UCF’s Department of Political Science.

McCoy emphasized the general attractiveness of the Latin American business environment, specifically the Dominican Republic’s. He underscored Latin American strengths such as sustained economic growth with low inflation, and he noted that the Dominican Republic is often ahead of rest of the region in certain respects. Growth is propelled by increasing exports linked with favorable terms of trade, liquid markets and expanding domestic consumption, McCoy said.

McCoy also noted that Latin America has solid macroeconomic policies, rising incomes, falling unemployment and decreased poverty and inequality. However, Latin America – and the Dominican Republic in particular – has several weaknesses, including uncertainty in global financial markets, the possibility of recession in the United States and inflation fueled by rising food and energy prices, he said. The Dominican Republic’s economic boom is driven by exports in coffee, sugar, tin and copper, he said, and foreign direct investments have been significantly high in the past few years. However, McCoy pointed out, imported energy, persistent poverty and inequality are negative issues that the Dominican Republic has to address. 

Morales focused her discussion on the issue of racial strife. She explained that ethnicity has played a tremendous role because of social discrimination based on racial and ethnic identifications. Political corruption is increasing, she said, and contributing to major financial inequalities.   

Ambassador Anthony Quainton, the diplomat in residence at The American University in Washington, D.C., closed the forum with a presentation on the future of U.S.-Latin American relations. Quainton, formerly a U.S. ambassador to Peru and Nicaragua and a former director general of the U.S. Foreign Service, emphasized the need for the United States to be cautious in its assumptions for the future.

In a departure from Frechette’s comments, Quainton suggested that perhaps free trade is not such a good idea for Latin America, in that, in some ways, it fails to deliver the growth in employment that would benefit the reforms free trade agreements typically require.

Furthermore, Quainton said, the U.S. tendency to be overbearing towards Latin America must change. He stressed the need to update the U.S. relationship with the region to one of “mutual dependence and restraint.”

In addition to the Global Perspectives Office, sponsors of the forum included UCF’s Latin American, Caribbean and Latino Studies Program; UCF’s Political Science Department; UCF’s Office of International Studies; UCF’s International Services Center and the Global Connections Foundation.
