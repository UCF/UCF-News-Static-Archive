UCF Counseling Center Can Help Students Cope With Variety of Struggles

By Jessica Saggio June 21, 2007

Related Contacts and Links:

  • Vivian Yamada

    • 407-823-2811

  • UCF Counseling Center

  • Print this Page

When depression, homesickness, relationship problems or crisis strikes, students can turn to the University of Central Florida Counseling Center for a helping hand.

The Counseling Center offers group and individual sessions and employs 17 professionals who can assist students with a variety of issues affecting their academic and personal lives. All of the centers services are free to students.

I think anyone at some time in their life can benefit from counseling, said Vivian Yamada, interim associate director of the Counseling Center. There are issues that everyone struggles with, and even though they may seem minor compared to other issues people go to counseling for, it can be beneficial to seek an outside opinion.

The shooting at Virginia Tech recently focused more attention on the need for psychological services for students. With that in mind, state and UCF task forces have been looking into how to enhance services and reach out to more students.

UCF recently added three new positions in the Counseling Center, including a prevention specialist. Additionally, the center has also started the Question/Persuade/Refer (QPR) suicide prevention program.

QPR is a one-hour session that can be requested by any group, department, or class on campus to educate people on when and how to recognize when others are in distress, Yamada said. People are realizing there is a need to get to the preventative end of things and get rid of the underlying causes of students problems instead of seeing someone after their problems have already emerged.

The goal is for 1,000 students, faculty, and staff to complete the program during the 2006-07 academic year. So far, the program has reached almost 600 people.

The center also offers services to address individual needs.

Typically, individual counseling consists of an average of four to five visits. For more complex cases, students may be referred to a professional outside of the center whose availability and/or expertise may better suit their needs.

To meet with a counselor, students are encouraged to call for a same-day or future appointment. Those who are in a crisis situation are encouraged go to the Counseling Center, which is located just east of the Health Center on the main campus, so a professional can help them as soon as possible. The center is open Monday and Thursday from 8 a.m. to 7 p.m. and on Tuesday, Wednesday and Friday from 8 a.m. to 5 p.m.

Group counseling is also available and typically consists of four to eight students per group. Groups focus on topics such as interpersonal relationships, thesis and dissertation support, family issues, stress management, body image and mood improvement. Students can sign up for groups by contacting the Counseling Center or by meeting with a counselor to determine which group they should attend. Group times and dates can be found on the Counseling Center Web site, www.counseling.sdes.ucf.edu.

While group counseling is ideal for some students, it isnt the best approach for everyone, Yamada said.

Group counseling is recommended for people who are having problems in relationships or who lack support in their lives from friends and family, Yamada said. For situations, like someone having pervasive thoughts of suicide, students should meet one-on-one with a counselor.
