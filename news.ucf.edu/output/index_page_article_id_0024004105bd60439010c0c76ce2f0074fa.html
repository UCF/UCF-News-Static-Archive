Showcase Highlights Undergraduates and Teachers Research in Nanotechnology

By Shobana Daniell July 25, 2006

Photo: Courtesy of Sudipta Seal

Twelve undergraduates and two science teachers participated in a UCF summer research program on nanotechnology. They discussed their projects and displayed posters during a showcase on Friday at the Engineering II building.

The atrium of the Engineering II building was packed Friday with students and visitors who watched demonstrations and read about the projects of 14 students and teachers who completed a ten-week research program in nanoscience.

The 12 students in the Research Experience for Undergraduates program were from universities across the United States, including Puerto Rico, and the University of Warsaw in Poland. Two science teachers from the Orlando area participated in the Research Experience for Teachers program.

Funded by the National Science Foundation, the fifth-annual programs also were sponsored by the UCF Advanced Materials Processing and Analysis Center (AMPAC) and the Office of Research & Commercialization.  The international component of the nanotechnology program is a new feature this year. UCF is hosting the Polish students as part of a cultural and research exchange that also is sending UCF students to Poland.

Professor Sudipta Seal directed the program, and he and other faculty members taught the participants specially designed short courses in Materials Science and Engineering, Nanotechnology, Biosciences and Advanced Nanoscale Characterization techniques.

Seal said winning the National Science Foundation grants, each of which covers three years of research programs, was no easy task.

Its a hard competition, and we have now won this two times, Seal said.

The program focused on the applications of nanotechnology and involved the study of materials at the molecular level. A nanometer is one-billionth of a meter; a sheet of paper is about 100,000 nanometers thick.

The participants designed their own research projects and worked in teams with the supervision of a post-doctoral fellow or graduate student and the assigned UCF faculty from a variety of disciplines, including Engineering, Education, Chemistry, the Nanoscience Technology Center, Physics and Molecular Biology.

The Research Experience for Undergraduates programs are designed to give sophomores a glimpse of high-level research, and goals include involving minorities and community college students. The nurturing program aims to encourage students to continue their interest in nanotechnology research and to help recruit the participants to come back as graduate students at UCF.

Mark Atwater of Pennsylvania College of Technology demonstrated his teams work on algae growth retardation Friday around a colorful aquarium.

I learned a lot about the intricacies of research; there was a lot of hands-on work involved, Atwater said. He also admitted that the experiment didnt work out too well.

Provost Terry Hickey, who attended the presentations, told Atwater, Now you have learned first-hand the challenges of science. Research is about the unknown.

Samuel Subbarao, a sophomore from Columbia University, was pleased with his research on hydrophobic and hydrophilic nanostructures.

It helped me understand that research is very slow and that I cant expect results overnight, he said. I have to keep at it.

Lara Sharp, a chemistry teacher at Olympia High School in the MetroWest area, said her students want to hear about my experiments with alternate forms of energy and (I) will continue work on the nanosized solar cells in my classroom.
