Experts Discuss Political Violence, Terrorism, Iraq and Afghanistan at UCF Forum

By Marina Souto Filha Oct. 18, 2005

Photo: Jerry Klein

Amb. George Ward talks about the role of nonprofit organizations in troubled areas during the kick-off panel at Monday's conference on global trends in violence.

Leading specialists in political violence, terrorism, Iraq and Afghanistan spoke at UCF Monday as part of an all-day national global issues conference that drew speakers and participants from across the United States.

Panels featured a wide range of viewpoints from experts associated with government, news media, academia, business, nonprofit organizations and the U.S. military.

The panel addressing political violence was led by John C. Bersia, special assistant to the president for Global Perspectives at UCF. Panelists agreed that theres still a world of trouble out there, but they offered varying opinions on why it is happening and how much responsibility the United States should take.

George Mason University Professor Monty Marshall, co-author of Peace and Conflict 2005, noted that the locations for political violence have changed. There is less in Central America and Asia, and more in the Muslim countries, he said. Overall, Marshall said there has been a substantial decline in armed conflict since 1992, with fewer people dying.

Retired Maj. Gen. Robert Scales, a military analyst for Fox News and National Public Radio who wrote the U.S. armys history of the first Persian Gulf war, outlined a different picture. He said we are engaged in a death struggle with radical Islam.

Scales had just returned from a visit to Iraq, primarily to evaluate the readiness of the new Iraqi military. He said its not the 101st Airborne Division. But it doesnt have to be" -- as long as it's better than its enemies. Most importantly, he said, the Iraqis have the will to win. As for the insurgents, he said the recruitment in the suicide corps is declining; reenlistments are down and predicted the insurgency would end with a whimper. To prepare the United States better for future struggles, Scales advocated improving Americans ability to understand other cultures and learn foreign languages.

George Ward, who served as coordinator for humanitarian assistance in Iraq in 2003 and is now the senior vice president for International Programs at WorldVision, described humanitarian organizations roles in conflicts as the first lines of battle before the international military forces arrive. And in many places, such as Somalia and the Balkans, the humanitarian organizations remain after the international forces leave. Ward said that, unfortunately, terrorists are now targeting humanitarian agencies in Afghanistan and Iraq.

Ward indicated that non-governmental organizations work best when they are impartial and independent, not part of the U.S. team. He advocated permanent liaisons between government, the military and non-governmental organizations; the early consultation of those organizations in conflicts; and joint civil-military courses.

Waltraud Morales of the UCF Department of Political Science, posed several questions in an attempt to better understand modern political violence. She wondered whether political violence is a sign of rebellion against the global order; whether U.S. international policy has exacerbated global discontent and promoted violence; and whether nation-building and democracy-building can increase violence.

A second panel led by UCF terrorism specialist Stephen Sloan also included Neal Pollard of Hicks & Associates and the Terrorism Research Institute; Austin Bay, a syndicated columnist; and Bernard McCarthy, chairman of the Department of Criminal Justice at UCF.

Retired Lt. Gen. Jay Garner, former post-war administrator in Iraq, returned the discussion to that country during the keynote luncheon speech. Garner expressed optimism, saying there is still a chance to turn the situation around. He also urged young people in the audience to get involved in societys critical areas of need, to go out and do something worthwhile  as an emergency room worker, a firefighter, a humanitarian volunteer in another country  and not simply inhale oxygen and exhale carbon dioxide.

A third panel on Iraq, moderated by Dan Simpson of the Pittsburgh Post-Gazette, continued that discussion. Presenters included Ambassador Robin Raphel, the U.S. Department of States coordinator for Iraqs reconstruction; Najmaldin Karim of the Kurdish Washington Institute; and Robert Miller of the Zor Foundation.

Afghanistan was the subject of the last panel. Moderated by Jonathan Gurwitz of the San Antonio Express-News, it also offered viewpoints by Eugene Huskey of Stetson University and Malcolm MacPherson, author of Roberts Ridge.

The conference was the result of a new partnership between UCF, led by the Global Perspectives Office, and the National Conference of Editorial Writers, a national, nonprofit organization that aims to improve the quality of editorial pages and broadcast editorials and to promote high standards among opinion writers and editors.

The conference was the first in a series of annual working forums on global issues that will be held at UCF and co-sponsored by the National Conference of Editorial Writers. Other UCF sponsors included the Nicholson School of Communication, the Political Science Department and the Middle Eastern Studies Program. Additional sponsors were the Orlando Sentinel and the Global Connections Foundation.
