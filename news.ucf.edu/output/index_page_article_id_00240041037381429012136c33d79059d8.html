EPA Official Describes Climate Change Woes, Strikes Hopeful Tone for Future

By Laura Stockbridge Oct. 2, 2009

Photo: Ariel Dansky

Danny Orlando, Southeast regional program manager for the Energy Star Program at the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency, said that -- with respect to energy, the environment and climate change -- it is easy to get overwhelmed by all of the “doom and gloom.”

However, Orlando accomplished a more difficult feat of developing a sense of hope in an audience of more than 150 students, faculty and staff during a forum Tuesday.

Danny Orlando has worked with the Environmental Protection Agency’s Energy Star Program in Atlanta for more than 16 years.  In addition, he has recently appeared in multiple documentaries discussing the steps everyone can take to create more environmentally friendly lifestyles.  An industrial engineer by trade, Orlando is also a musician and photographer, and he is able to bring multiple perspectives to the issues of energy and climate change.

In his presentation entitled “Storms and Opportunities: A Planetary Energy and Climate Discussion,” Orlando described what he believes humans are doing wrong. The main point, he said, is that we forget we are living on a sphere with nowhere else to go, and we are breaking all of the rules: “Don’t poison the water, don’t pollute the air and don’t touch the thermostat.” 

“Every decision we make can be an opportunity,” Orlando said, and now it looks like we have decided to bankrupt our planetary budget in terms of non-renewable resources, pollute our atmosphere, accelerate population growth and place many species in danger.

Orlando cited data revealing increasing global land and ocean temperatures, rising sea levels, increased glacier melting and very high consumption per capita of energy.  After illustrating all of these points with various graphs and tables, Orlando asked the audience to seriously consider whether or not we can deny that climate change is a real problem that warrants our attention.

The presentation ended on a hopeful note about the future. Though the damage is difficult to reverse, it can be done, Orlando said.  There are new renewable sources of energy and processes such as wind, biomimicry, sunlight, thermal beds, and waves and tides that can sustain society if they are harvested. And people also can make choices that can help, such as sealing air leaks in homes, buying a small car instead of an SUV and installing compact fluorescent light bulbs.

When asked by a student what more UCF can do to be a leader in terms of being a green campus, Orlando responded that he was very impressed with what has already been done at UCF, particularly the university-wide unifying theme, “The Environment and Global Climate Change.”

This forum was sponsored by the UCF Department of Civil, Environmental and Construction Engineering; the UCF Global Perspectives Office; the UCF Office of Undergraduate Studies; the UCF Political Science Department; UCF LIFE; and the Global Connections Foundation.
