UCF Goes Green: College of Medicine Seeking LEED Silver Certification

By Zenaida Gonzalez Kotala July 16, 2008

Photo: Jacque Brund

The College of Medicine is being built in an environmentally friendly manner, with a goal of earning LEED silver certification.

Construction of the new UCF College of Medicine at Lake Nona is proceeding at a brisk pace and when complete is expected to obtain the Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design (LEED) silver certification.

Construction at the University of Central Florida is part of a national trend to go green. Despite millions of dollars in budget cuts and a slow down of the economy, the university remains committed to being environmentally responsible.

That’s why UCF President John Hitt announced last year that construction of all new university buildings will meet at least the silver rating of LEED.

The United States Green Building Council developed the LEED standards. It is a point system that awards certified silver, gold and platinum rating system based on a checklist of prerequisites and credits that are environmentally sensitive. LEED standards have become the nationally accepted benchmarks for achieving sustainable buildings.

The College of Medicine classroom building and the Burnett Biomedical Sciences building at Lake Nona will have less of an impact on the environment, will be healthier for those who work and study there and will be less costly to run than their conventional counterparts. The extra cost to make the buildings more environmentally sensitive is about 3 percent, but the cost will be recovered from the savings the buildings will achieve by being more energy and water efficient, said Maher Chatila, construction manager for the UCF project at Lake Nona. Chatila is one of three LEED Accredited Professionals at UCF who are ensuring that the buildings meet LEED standards.

Many local governments and private entities across the nation are starting to require that all new construction meet LEED standards. Some municipalities are even requiring major renovations to meet LEED standards. Los Angeles this year became the largest city in the nation to require that all new construction meet LEED standards. Others are encouraging developers to meet LEED standards by offering economic incentives.

In Florida, 11 government and commercial buildings, including Gulf Trace Elementary School in Pasco County, have obtained LEED’s silver designation. Eight buildings, including the North Sarasota Library, have obtained the gold designation. There are no platinum designations in the state, although UCF announced in June that its Sustainability Center on the main campus will seek the platinum standard.

The UCF College of Medicine was established in 2006 to increase opportunities for medical education in Florida, address the growing physician shortage nationwide and provide economic benefits to Central Florida and the state through research and technology.

The four-year medical program will capitalize on UCF’s existing strengths in biomedical sciences, modeling and simulation, and optics and photonics. Other programs in materials science, psychology, chemistry, film and digital media, hospitality and nursing will support the College of Medicine. The college will eventually produce about 120 medical graduates each year. It is set to open in fall 2009.

The college will be located in the Lake Nona community just east of the Orlando International Airport. The site is south of State Road 417 at a new interchange between Boggy Creek and Narcoosee roads.
