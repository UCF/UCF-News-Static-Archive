Nontraditional UCF Cocoa Student Honored for Sharing Life Lessons

By Christine Dellert Oct. 11, 2007

Photo: Courtesy of Michele Ross

Michele Ross, a UCF Cocoa student honored for her ability to overcome life's obstacles, worked with students at McNair Middle School in Rockledge last spring.

A nontraditional University of Central Florida student received a $1,000 scholarship last week toward earning her masters degree in Reading Education at UCFs Cocoa campus. At 52, Michele Ross has a lot more to teach her students than Shakespeares Othello and 10th-grade vocabulary. Ross was chosen for her ability to overcome lifes obstacles and lead others through equally tough times. She will spend the next few semesters in classrooms at Melbourne High School and UCF, preparing for her next challenge: teaching high-school language arts.

Five years ago, Michele Ross was pushing papers at a beverage company just outside New York City. She was an office manager, a loving wife and a mom of two scholar-athletes.

She rowed, some days for hours, on the Long Island Sound, drifting between a lighthouse and surrounding harbors. Those steady strokes became her only solace when her husband left her for a 24-year-old woman. When her college-aged son was given a triple dose of drugs that left him permanently ill. When her daughters friend was killed in a car accident. And when her 14-year-old diabetic cat, Sugar Cane, died.

The spiral of events threw Ross into a rocky depression. And eventually, with the coaxing of her parents, she moved to Florida and into their beachside home in Indialantic.

It was a rough road getting here, says Ross, who spent three weeks of her move to Florida in a Johns Hopkins University dorm room while her 21-year-old son, Marshall, was hospitalized nearby. Doctors diagnosed him with a permanent psychosis from the drugs.

Marshall moved to New Mexico to live with his father. And Michele Ross went back to school at Brevard Community College, finishing up her associates degree with academic credits shed earned at 17.

Ross then enrolled in the English Language Arts Education program at UCFs Cocoa campus. She also continued volunteering for Cocoa High Schools mentor program and the Melbourne High School crew team.

No matter what weve been through, if it helps people in any way, its worth it to share our stories, she says.

Ross worked part-time at the front desk of UCFs regional education office and became Cocoas first Student Government Association Regional Campus senator in three years. She has served as the sergeant-at-arms and service-learning liaison for the Florida Future Educators of America. And now she is a member of the Kappa Delta Pi education honor society.

Co-workers such as Sheryl Schagen call her inspirational.

Ross was nominated by Schagen, the education coordinator for UCFs Cocoa campus, and other faculty for the $1,000 scholarship recently donated by Russ and Genie Morcom of Brevard County. In her application, Ross listed more than 700 hours of community service since moving to Florida in 2004.

She plans to use the money toward a masters degree in Reading Education at UCF Cocoa.

Its a Thursday afternoon at Melbourne High. And Ross is reading Arthur Millers The Crucible with her 11th grade class as a senior-year intern teacher. In the hallways, she sometimes passes her 79-year-old father, who still works as a substitute teacher.

A lot of them have come through tough times, she says of the students and starting teachers at the high school  many of whom are less than half her age.

I have that extra boost, that life experience behind me. But when you open up, you find you have a lot more in common with people than you think.
