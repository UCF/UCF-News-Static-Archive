UCF College of Medicine Launches Luminary Speaker Series

By Zenaida Gonzalez Kotala Feb. 11, 2009

Related Contacts and Links:

  • Zenaida Gonzalez Kotala, UCF News and Information

    • 407-823-6120

  • UCF College of Medicine

  • Print this Page

Scientist Henry Daniell launches the University of Central Florida's Luminary lecture series this week with a talk about his latest research in the areas of diabetes, cancer and malaria. 

The Burnett School of Biomedical Sciences and the College of Medicine organized the series of free lectures to raise awareness about the cutting-edge health care research at UCF. Sponsors are Dean Mead and Fifth Third Bank.

Daniell kicks of the series with “Green Technology for Health Care.”  He is well known locally and internationally for his pioneering work in chloroplast genetic engineering – a new way to produce vaccines and biopharmaceutical in plants.

Daniell has developed vaccines that hold promise for curing diabetes and preventing malaria and cholera as well as similar vaccines for warding off bioterrorist agents such as anthrax and the black plague. He is investigating the plant-made interferon for effectiveness against lung cancer.

Studies show mice given the vaccine for diabetes had no signs of the condition after treatment. And those mice exposed to the black plague and cholera survived after getting Daniell’s vaccines.

If the results can be duplicated in humans, it could mean that vaccines could be produced for some of the deadliest diseases and chronic conditions on the planet.

His research also may revolutionize the way certain pharmaceuticals are produced, as his method uses lettuce to grow vaccines instead of processing them with expensive equipment. Research shows that the green vaccines may be even more effective than those commercially available now. The green oral vaccines also are less costly.

Daniell was born in India and has dedicated his life to finding treatments and cures to diseases that ravage poor countries. He is conducting research into the top 10 diseases as ranked by the World Health Organization and Centers for Disease control.  He became only the 14th American in the last 222 years to be elected to the Italian National Academy of Sciences and in 2007 he was named a Fellow of the American Association for the Advancement of Science.

Daniell also has received UCF’s Pegasus Professor Award, the most prestigious honor the university gives to a faculty member. The Pegasus Professor Award recognizes excellence in teaching, research and service.

Daniell’s laboratory research has been featured on the Discovery Channel, in Scientific American and many other media around the world.

This month's program is already full, but at least three more lectures are planned by the end of the year.
