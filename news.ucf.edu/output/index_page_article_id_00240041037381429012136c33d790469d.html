NFL Receives Highest Grades Ever in UCF Institute's Report Card

By UCF Staff Sept. 24, 2009

Related Contacts and Links:

  • Jessica Bartter

    • 407-823-4884

  • Full study

  • Print this Page

An African-American coach or general manager has led five of the last six teams to play in the Super Bowl. Three African-American head coaches were hired during the off-season --there were only two in the entire league just eight years ago.

The National Football League has made exceptional strides in advancing diversity during the last 10 years. The league received an overall score of a B, its highest grade ever, on the 2009 NFL Racial and Gender Report Card, released Thursday by the nation's foremost expert in diversity in sport -- the University of Central Florida's Richard Lapchick.

The NFL received an A-minus grade for its racial hiring practices and a C for gender hiring practices.

"For the third consecutive year, African-Americans played a significant role in the Super Bowl when Pittsburgh's Coach Mike Tomlin helped lead his team to the Super Bowl championship,” Lapchick said. “In 2007, two African-American head coaches (Indianapolis’ Tony Dungy and Chicago’s Lovie Smith) faced each other in the Super Bowl for the first time and an African-American general manager (the New York Giants' Jerry Reese) helped lead his team to a win in the 2008 Super Bowl.”

Lapchick also praised the league for showing sustained progress in the number of minorities who are head coaches and general managers. There have been six African-American head coaches and five African-American general managers for each of the past three seasons.

A pioneer for racial equality known as the "social conscience for sport," Lapchick produces annual reports evaluating how well professional and college sports advance racial and gender diversity. As the director of UCF’s DeVos Sport Business Management program, Lapchick launched the Institute for Diversity and Ethics in Sport, which publishes annual Racial and Gender Report Card for professional and college sports.

Lapchick co-authored the NFL report with DeVos program students Chris Kamke and Derek McMechan.
