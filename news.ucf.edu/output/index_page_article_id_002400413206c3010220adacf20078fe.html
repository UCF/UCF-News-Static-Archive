Pioneering Civil Rights Family to Speak at City Hall, UCF

By Bernadette Davis March 21, 2005

When Patricia Stephens Due was arrested for sitting in at a lunch counter in 1960, she and other Florida A&M University students made history by serving 49 days in jail rather than paying a fine for their protest.  They were part of the first jail in during the U.S. civil rights movement, and it marked the beginning of a life seeking justice for Patricia Stephens Due.

 

She and her husband, civil rights attorney John Due, have fought for human rights since their days on the campus of Florida A&M University.  John Due is a 1963 graduate of the FAMU College of Law.

 

The Dues, along with their daughters, will speak about their experiences in the civil rights movement and the legal fight for human rights, at 6:30 p.m. Wednesday, March 30, at Orlando City Hall, 400 S. Orange Ave. The presentation is free and open to the public.

Patricia Stephens Due, who co-authored "Freedom in the Family: A Mother-Daughter Memoir of the Fight for Civil Rights" with her daughter, award-winning novelist Tananarive Due, will tell her story as well as those of other grassroots activists. John Due, an activist and civil rights attorney, will address the legal aspects of civil rights. Johnita P. Due, senior counsel for CNN and Lydia Due Greisz, also an attorney, will join their sister and parents to talk about the familys ongoing activism.

 

The event is sponsored by the University of Central Florida School of Film and Digital Media, the UCF Office of Undergraduate Studies, the Florida A&M University College of Law, The Academy Foundation and City of Orlando Commissioner Daisy W. Lynum.

 

The presentation will be followed by a book signing with Patricia Stephens Due and Tananarive Due.  Tananarive Dues other works include "The Between" (1995),  "My Soul to Keep" (1997), "The Living Blood" (2001), "The Black Rose" (2000) and "The Good House" (2003).

 

On Thursday, March 31, Tananarive Due will speak to two groups of UCF students and faculty. At 4:30 p.m., she will discuss turning My Soul to Keep into a feature film with actor Blair Underwood with a School of Film and Digital Media audience. Her presentation, in Room 119 of the Health and Public Affairs building, is open to the public.

 

Tananarive and Patricia Stephens Due also will speak at 1:30 p.m. Thursday, March 31 to faculty and students from the departments of Womens Studies and English. Their presentation, in Room 119 of the Business Administration building, is also open to the public. The Dues also will attend an invitation-only luncheon on  campus sponsored by the UCF Women's Resource Center.
