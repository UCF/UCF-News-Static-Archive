Educators Outline Doctor Shortage During Board of Governors Meeting

By Tom Evelyn March 16, 2004

Related Contacts and Links:

  • Print this Page

Florida has a shortage of doctors and opportunities to train them, medical educators andÂ  professionals told a committee of the Florida Board of Governors Wednesday at UCF.

Board members heard presentations about how there is a need for more physicians in Florida and how that need could be met by expanding or adding medical schools. UCF and Florida International University are interested in opening medical schools.

Following current trends, there will be a nationwide physician shortage of about 20 percent, or 200,000 doctors, within the next 20 years, said Dr. Richard Cooper, director of the Health Policy Institute at the Medical College of Wisconsin.

Indications of current shortages show up in longer waiting times for patients and the refusal by doctors to accept Medicare patients, he said. With nearly 49,000 doctors, Florida ranks 17th nationally in the number of doctors per 100,000 people, according to the American Medical Association.

But the bigger problem may be the shrinking number of medical residency opportunities in Florida, said Dr. Mathis Becker of the state Department of Health's Graduate Medical Education Committee. Florida ranks 45th among states in the number of medical residents per 100,000 people, Becker said. Florida would have to add about 2,000 residency positions funded by the state over the next five years to meet the national average, Becker said.

Several experts said during the board's workshop that doctors are more likely to practice in the state in which they serve their residencies. Central Florida's medical community has outlined a need for a medical school in Orlando, UCF President John Hitt told the board, adding that many experts have pointed out that Orlando is the largest metropolitan area without such a school.

Officials at Florida Hospital and Winter Park Memorial Hospital have publicly supported the idea. "We already have a number of shortages" in some specialties in Central Florida, Hitt said. "It's only going to get worse."

UCF's Board of Trustees in November approved conducting a feasibility study for building a medical school. The university is in the process of selecting a firm to conduct that study. UCF now participates in an accelerated medical school program with the University of South Florida's College of Medicine that allows students to earn a UCF bachelor's degree and a USF medical degree in seven years.

USF, Florida State University and the University of Florida are the public universities in the state that have medical schools.

The Board of Governors will continue committee meetings at 8 a.m. today, March 18, in the Student Union. The full board will begin its regular meeting at 11 a.m.

The board is expected to vote on whether to proceed with a study of whether it's best to meet the demand for more doctors by expanding medical schools or building new ones. At the regular meeting, board members plan to discuss the 2004 legislative agenda.

Chris Sullivan, chairman of the Florida Council of 100 and chairman and chief executive officer of Outback Steakhouse, is scheduled to give a presentation entitled, "We Must Do Better: Moving Florida's State University System to the Next Level in Quality and Accessibility."

The Board of Governors was created by Constitutional Amendment 11, which was approved by voters in 2002. The board governs the university system separate from public school districts and community colleges, which remain under the Florida Board of Education. The next Board of Governors is scheduled for April 22. For more information and agendas, go to www.fldoe.org/bog/meetings/default.asp.
