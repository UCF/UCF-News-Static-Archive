Trustees Hear Comments About Proposed Stadium, Approve Pursuing Med School

By Tom Evelyn and Chad Binette March 31, 2005

Photo: Jacque Brund

The University of Central Florida Board of Trustees on Thursday received a feasibility study and heard comments from residents on a proposed on-campus football stadium.

The board also voted at its regular meeting to pursue building a medical school at UCF after receiving a feasibility report that outlined a dramatic need for more physicians in Florida and why Central Florida is an ideal site for a new medical school.

UCF now will ask the Florida Board of Governors, which oversees all of the state universities, for permission to create a medical school. The medical school also would require funding from the Legislature.

The medical school study, which was conducted by MGT of America, points out that the shortage of doctors nationwide will worsen without expanded medical education opportunities. It also demonstrates that UCF, which has expanded its biomedical research in recent years, is well-positioned to support a medical school.

We are right now about 2 1/2 medical schools shy of where we need to be to meet the shortage (of physicians in Florida), Provost Terry Hickey said.

The trustees also heard an update Thursday on plans to expand the UCF Library and ratified contracts with three unions representing university faculty and staff.

UCF is considering building a 45,000-seat stadium in the athletics complex in the north end of campus. The study released Thursday shows that a $43 million stadium could support itself through ticket and concessions sales, sponsorships and advertising. No tax dollars or tuition would be used to build or maintain a stadium.

If the stadium is approved, construction could start this summer and be completed by fall 2006, the study says. The stadium would be about 60 feet tall, about the same height as the new fieldhouse, and would include 44 rows of seats, including about 1,000 club seats.

The report by seven consultants also includes recommendations on how traffic and parking issues could be handled successfully, and it includes financial projections based on an average attendance of 23,000 in 2006-07 and 25,900 in 2010-11.

We have a lot of individuals who are excited about making this happen, Athletic Director Steve Orsini said.

During a public hearing at the meeting, several speakers urged UCF to build the stadium to boost school spirit, help the local economy and strengthen the universitys athletics program and overall reputation. Other speakers voiced concerns about traffic and parking issues and asked UCF to consider other locations on campus.

The trustees are scheduled to meet again on April 14, when they may decide if they want to build a stadium on campus.

In the meantime, the university will host a community forum from 6 to 9 p.m. April 11 at the Student Union to discuss plans for the proposed stadium and allow for more comments from the public. UCF has also planned a series of meetings with homeowners groups through April and two informal, community information sessions from 6 to 8 p.m. April 13 at Lawton Chiles Middle School and April 21 at Carillon Elementary School.

Also on Thursday, the trustees approved the collective bargaining agreements that the university has negotiated with the Central Florida Police Benevolent Association, the union that represents University Police; United Faculty of Florida, the union representing UCF faculty members; and the American Federation of State, County and Municipal Employees, the union that represents staff members.

Were very pleased with the contract, Hickey said of the United Faculty of Florida agreement. We think its a good contract for the faculty and the university.

The UFF contract nearly doubles the amount of sabbatical and professional development leave time available for union members, Hickey said. He also said the university is working hard to get raises to members before the end of the spring semester.

The trustees also heard an update on plans to expand the UCF Library above the existing John T. Washington Center bookstore and retail area. The expansion would connect the library and Washington Center buildings and add a new entrance in that area.

The expansion would add 164,600 square feet and nearly double the number of seats in the library from 1,713 to 3,272. It would help the library increase its collection from about 1.1 million volumes in 2003 to a projected 2.3 million volumes in 2019.

Construction could begin in summer 2006 and is expected to cost about $82.8 million. The library would remain open during construction, as the new portion would be built before renovations begin at the existing library.

In other action Thursday, the trustees:

-- Approved naming the Morgridge National Reading Center at the College of Education after Orlando philanthropists John and Carrie Morgridge who will donate $2 million to build the center.

-- Approved two new degree programs: a master of fine arts in creative writing, which would begin this summer, and a master of arts in anthropology, which would start in fall 2006.

-- Approved an honorary doctorate for Ambassador Harriet Elam-Thomas. Elam-Thomas has served at UCF as a diplomat in residence from the U.S. Department of State since 2003.

-- Endorsed a Joint Resolution on Class Size Amendment offered by Gov. Jeb Bush to provide flexibility to the constitutional requirements for class size and increase beginning teacher pay.

Also, new trustees Al Weiss, president of the Walt Disney World Resort, and Tico Perez, an Orlando attorney with Baker & Hostetler, attended their first board meeting Thursday. Weiss and Perez, both of whom are UCF alumni, replaced outgoing trustees Gerri Ferris and Rick Lee.
