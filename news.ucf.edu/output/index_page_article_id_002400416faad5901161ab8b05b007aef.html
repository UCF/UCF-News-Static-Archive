Experts Discuss the Future of India and China at UCF Conference

By Gabriela Othon and Abeer Abdalla Nov. 7, 2007

Related Contacts and Links:

  • Mark Freeman, Global Perspectives

    • 407-823-0935

  • UCF Global Perspectives

  • Print this Page

Specialists from a wide variety of disciplines and backgrounds gathered at UCF’s Fairwinds Alumni Center on Monday to offer informed, diverse perspectives on China and India, two increasingly important players on the global stage.

The event, titled “Emerging Powers: China and India,” was sponsored by the UCF Global Perspectives office, in a partnership with the National Conference of Editorial Writers and others.  

In his opening remarks, Shubhro Sen – an accomplished entrepreneur and global consultant – emphasized the importance of India and China as economic powerhouses, pointing out that by 2050, 36 percent of the world’s population will be Indian or Chinese. Both countries’ rapidly growing populations and industries have already made them “market makers” in many global sectors, Sen said. For example, the price and availability of cement has been affected by the two economies’ ever-increasing industrial needs. Sen suggested that a wealth of opportunities exists for Florida to engage with these two countries, giving the state’s similarly rapid growth. 

Following Sen, a panel on India focused on issues ranging from outsourcing and trade to security. Associate Professor M.B. Sarkar of UCF’s Management Department discussed India’s economic rise, pointing to a rapidly transforming, flattening global economy and the Indian entrepreneurial spirit.

India’s importance in the global security environment was also emphasized. John Wolfsthal, a senior fellow at the Center for Strategic and International Studies in Washington, D.C., outlined India’s nuclear history and its pending cooperative deal with the United States. He stressed the possible implications of the deal in terms of U.S. nuclear negotiations with other states, particularly Iran and North Korea. Gregory Kalbaugh, a director and counsel to the U.S.-India Business Council, was also on that panel. 

A second panel focusing on China addressed trade disputes with the United States, security concerns and China’s strategic goals. James Bacchus, a former U.S. congressman and chief judge of the World Trade Organization’s dispute-settlement body,
identified intellectual property rights, product safety and climate change as three major issues in the relationship between the United States and China.

Bacchus, now the head of the global trade practice at Greenberg Traurig in Washington, emphasized that climate change presents the best opportunity for the two countries to partner for positive change. Arms-control specialist Jack Mendelsohn referred to U.S.-China ties as one of the most critical bilateral relationships of this time. The panel also featured Gerrit Gong of Brigham Young University and the Center for Strategic and International Studies, as well as Robert Moore, an anthropology professor and director of international affairs at the Hamilton Holt School of Rollins College.

Amy Chua, author of the recently published “Day of Empire: How Hyperpowers Rise to Global Dominance – and Why They Fall,” explored the future of the two emerging nations by comparing them to hyperpowers of the past.

In her keynote presentation, she explained that throughout history, the great hyperpowers, such as the Roman and British empires and the United States today, had certain features in common. These included economic and military dominance, sheer size and tolerance relative to their time. In every case, she said, the “most indispensable feature was tolerance.”

Ironically, Chua said, the same tolerance that helped hyperpowers rise to their positions also contributed to their decline when they reached a tipping point that opened the door to conflict. Her belief is that neither China nor India necessarily aspires to hyperpower status. China, specifically, may not desire the global burden that accompanies such a position, she said. However, Chua’s overall view of the two nations mirrored those of the entire group of conference speakers. Essentially, she said that China and India deserve close attention and will continue to rise in power and importance.

The conference was part of the Office of Global Perspectives’ 2007-2008 university-wide, primary theme of China and India as emerging powers. Other UCF sponsors were the College of Business Administration, Student Government Association, LIFE, the Political Science Department, the Diplomacy Studies Program, the International Services Center and the Nicholson School of Communication. External partners, in addition to the National Conference of Editorial Writers, were the Global Connections Foundation, C.T. Hsu and Associates, P.A., OCI Associates, Inc. and Engineering & Environmental Design, Inc.
