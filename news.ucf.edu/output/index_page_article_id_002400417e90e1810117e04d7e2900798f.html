President Hitt Commends 'The Little Car That Could'

By Suhtling Wong and Chad Binette Feb. 13, 2008

Photo: Jacque Brund

UCF President John Hitt congratulates Remo Pillat, a Team UCF member and a graduate student at the School of Electrical Engineering and Computer Science, during a ceremony on Wednesday.

Full Photo

President John Hitt on Wednesday congratulated the UCF students whose self-driving vehicle -- which he dubbed “the little car that could” -- made it to the national finals of the DARPA Urban Challenge last year.

Team UCF, one of 11 squads that competed in the finals, outpaced more expensive, high-tech vehicles from competing universities such as MIT, Carnegie Mellon and Stanford. The self-driving vehicles had to navigate through an urban setting at the former George Air Force Base in Victorville, Calif.

With its specially built sensors, GPS system and laser scanners, Knight Rider -- a 1997 Subaru Outback -- had to maneuver through traffic, avoid obstacles and obey traffic rules and stop signs.

During a ceremony Wednesday at the College of Engineering and Computer Science, Hitt congratulated the team of UCF staff and students and industry partner Ben Patz of Coleman Technologies for its dedication and hard work. In contrast to the newer, million-dollar vehicles that competed, UCF’s Knight Rider was the oldest and least expensive model.

Hitt said it was the human mind, spirit and capital that led to the UCF team’s “magnificent achievement.”

Carnegie Mellon University won the competition’s top prize of $2 million. The UCF vehicle lasted more than two hours, navigating the course at a top speed of 30 mph, before its GPS system failed.

Patz, who is a UCF alumnus, and lead engineer Don Harper, of the College of Engineering and Computer Science, combined their expertise to help equip Knight Rider. Harper donated the vehicle in 2004 for the project.

In addition to Patz and Harper, Team UCF members who traveled to California were School of Electrical Engineering and Computer Science graduate students Remo Pillat and Gary Stein, undergraduate Andrew Miller, faculty member Hassan Foroosh and former visiting faculty member Yiannis Papelis.

Other contributing faculty and team members included students David Lyle, Sean Elms and Jian Yang, faculty members Zhihua Qu, Samuel Richie, Niels da Vitoria Lobo and Mark Heinrich, former visiting faculty members Hans Fernlund and Yan-Fei Liu and David Norvell of the UCF Physical Plant.

For more information, go to http://www.darpa.mil/grandchallenge. Team UCF's Web page can be viewed at http://knightrider.eecs.ucf.edu.
