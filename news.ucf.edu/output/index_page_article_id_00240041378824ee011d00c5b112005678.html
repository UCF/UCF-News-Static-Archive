Sustainability Week to Help Students, Campuses Create 'Green Culture'

By Chad Binette Oct. 15, 2008

The University of Central Florida will encourage students, faculty members, businesses and other universities to adopt a “green culture” during its first Sustainability Week.

Featured events will include the three-day Campus and Community Sustainability Conference beginning Sunday, Oct. 19, a presentation by the National Wildlife Federation’s immediate past chairman on Wednesday, Oct. 22, and a Green Fair hosted by the Student Sustainability Alliance, a component of the Student Government Association, on Thursday, Oct. 23.

“Sustainability Week will showcase our commitment to educating our students, faculty, staff and the community about how they can be good stewards for our environment,” said Alaina Bernard, assistant director of the UCF Environmental Initiative.

“My main goal is to have students recognize how easy it is to make simple changes in their lives that can reduce their impact on the environment,” added Michelle Murphy, the environmental and sustainability coordinator for the Student Government Association. “They don’t have to change their lives completely.”

The Campus and Community Sustainability Conference, which UCF is hosting for the first time, will focus on helping schools, businesses and communities become more environmentally friendly. Speakers and exhibits will cover education and research, how to protect ecological systems and how to make economic improvements in a sustainable way.

Scheduled speakers include Orange County Mayor Rich Crotty and John Masiello, director of Demand Side Management and Alternative Energy for Progress Energy Florida, the conference’s lead sponsor.

Jennifer L. Languell, the founder and president of Trifecta Construction Solutions, also will speak. Languell educates the development and construction industries and homeowners about the benefits of green building and sustainable development.

Seventy-five UCF students have agreed to volunteer for five hours in return for attending the conference for free. For more information on the program or registration, go to http://www.sustainablefloridaconference.org.

Conservationist Jerome Ringo, the immediate past chairman of the National Wildlife Federation, will speak at 7 p.m. Wednesday, Oct. 22, about “The Apollo Project for Our Time: Creating Energy Independence and a Clean Energy Future.”

Ringo is now president of the Apollo Alliance, a coalition of diverse leaders working to free America from foreign energy dependence. Ringo will speak in room 101 of the College of Sciences Building on the main campus. His presentation, coordinated by the UCF Global Perspectives Office, is free and open to the public.

At the Green Fair, UCF agencies and departments, along with area governments, utilities and businesses, will set up displays in front of the Student Union from 10 a.m. to 2 p.m. The fair is designed for students and is also free and open to the public. Giveaways will include fluorescent light bulbs and reusable grocery bags.

Exhibits will feature the UCF green roof, an Orlando Utilities Commission hybrid car and organic T-shirts made by Green Logo. The UCF Recreation and Wellness Center will promote outdoor activities as a “carbon free day” option for students.

The UCF Bookstore will hold a green book sale. The Sustainability & Energy Management department will showcase everyday methods of energy conservation for students. UCF’s Environmental Initiative will display information for students seeking green recreational opportunities on campus.

Sustainability Week is the latest of many environmental initiatives on campus. The Environment and Global Climate Change is the undergraduate unifying theme that is being woven throughout the curriculum for a second year.

UCF became the first university in the state to install a green roof in 2005. On top of the Student Union expansion, the 1,600-square-foot roof includes Florida-native plants that help to conserve energy and reduce storm-water runoff.

The Sustainability & Energy Management department is tracking which sources account for the most campus carbon-dioxide emissions. By 2010, UCF wants to reduce its energy consumption up to 20 percent in all education and general buildings on campus.

UCF also became the first university in the state last fall to convert one of its fleet vehicles to a plug-in hybrid for limited all-electric driving. The 2007 Toyota Prius plug-in teaches the community about energy-saving transportation and produces about 30 percent less emissions per mile compared to its gas-guzzling counterparts.  For UCF’s other fleet vehicles, biodiesel fuel has replaced diesel fuel.
