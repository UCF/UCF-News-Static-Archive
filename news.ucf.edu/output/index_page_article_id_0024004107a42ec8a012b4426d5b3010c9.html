Analyst's GQ Spin on Economy: High Style, But Lackluster

By Zenaida Gonzalez Kotala Dec. 3, 2010

Full Photo

The economy has become a popular subject in GQ magazine, so much so that University of Central Florida economist Sean Snaith has created a spinoff publication -- "GdpQ."

This limited-edition publication wraps tongue-in-cheek commentary around Snaith's fourth-quarter economic forecast in a series of GQ-style magazine articles. 

Today's report from the U.S. Labor Department that only 39,000 jobs were added in November and that the unemployment rate rose to 9.8 percent supports Snaith's take on the economy, which he describes with literary style. 

"This labor market report evokes Oliver Twist's appeal -- 'Please, Sir, I want some more,'" said Snaith, the director of UCF's Institute for Economic Competitiveness. "The labor market is the ugly scar left over from the economic and financial trauma we have endured. This scar will take a long time to fade. 

That's why readers won't be able to put down "Black Friday, Just Economic Spanx," where he explains the temporary glow of the economy. And "Ben Bernanke Has 600 Billion Ways to Pump Up Your Price Level," which explores why the Federal Reserve is trying to boost inflation, can't be missed either.

The "Q&A With the Relationship Doctor" column is sure to be a hit, especially with single economists. The column tackles the question of why the unemployment rate didn't drop when the economy added more than 150,000 jobs. On a lighter note, Snaith also offers advice to help an economist impress a woman on a blind date.  

"Black Friday, Just Economic Spanx" explains how a good Black Friday is only momentarily hiding the underbelly of our economy. It works just like "Spanx," the modern-day version of a full-body girdle that covers up the human body's imperfections.

"The overflowing shopping carts, preliminary sales totals and the smiles of shoppers who have scored big discounts momentarily shift our attention away from unemployment, slow job creation and underwater mortgages," Snaith said.

"The reports surrounding Black Friday give the false impression that the consumer is back and is spending freely once again. But, as the images of this past weekend fade away and the economic 'Spanx' are peeled off, the reality of the consumer's plight will spill back into plain sight."

Our future still consists of slow job creation, a lagging housing market and a painfully slow recovery, Snaith says. Don't expect the labor market to recoup all of the jobs lost during the recession until 2014 at the earliest.

Snaith also dedicates an article to politicians, the 2013 forecast for an $18 trillion nation debt and the difficult choices that need to be made to get the country's finances back in order.

He states that a national bipartisan commission created to deal with the fiscal mess simply "provides cover for politicians who do not have the guts to make these choices on their own." 

To view Snaith's entire forecast, go to http://www.iec.ucf.edu.

Snaith is a national expert in economics, forecasting, market sizing and economic analysis who authors quarterly reports about the state of the economy. Bloomberg News recently named Snaith as one of the country's most accurate forecasters for his predictions about the Federal Reserve's benchmark interest rate, the Federal Funds rate.

Snaith is also a member of several national forecasting panels, including the Wall Street Journal Economic Forecasting Survey, the Associated Press Survey of Top Economists, the National Association of Business Economics Quarterly Outlook Survey Panel, the Federal Reserve Bank of Philadelphia's Survey of Professional Forecasters, the Livingston Survey, Bloomberg U.S. Economic Indicator Survey and USA Today Economic Survey Panel. Western Blue Chip named him the most accurate forecaster for California in 2007.

The UCF Institute for Economic Competitiveness' mission is to expand public understanding of the economy by convening business leaders, scholars, policy makers, civic groups and media to discuss critical issues.
