World-Known Flute Player Performs at UCF as Part of Latin American Cultural Festival

By Zenaida Gonzalez Kotala Sept. 22, 2006

Photo: Jerry Klein

UCF Associate Professor Nora Lee Garcia's office is adorned with wooden flutes from the countries she’s visited on tour.

University of Central Florida Associate Professor Nora Lee Garcia knew she was in love with the flute the moment she laid her eyes on one in her second-grade classroom in San Juan, Puerto Rico.

She was only 8, but she took to the instrument instantly. She made her recital debut at age 20 in Merkin Concert Hall in New York. She has performed throughout the world and teaches master classes in Europe and South America each summer.

On Sept. 23, Garcia performed a free concert at UCF as part of the second-annual Latin American Cultural Festival of Orlando. Her 8 p.m. performance drew more than 300 people to the Visual Arts Building Auditorium at UCF.

Ive been all over the world, but this is one of my favorite concerts, Garcia said. Its a venue where I can play what I want. And having been to many of the places where the music was created makes it very special.

During the Sept. 23 performance, Garcia played tangos from Argentina as well as music from Peru, Venezuela and Brazil.

We all speak Spanish, she said of Hispanics. But we all speak differently, with our own flavor, our own expression. Still, we all have the same happy attitude. Thats why I chose this program. Its like us.

The second-annual festival is part of UCFs ongoing commitment to diversity and recognizing the contributions Hispanics have made to American society.

Garcia is a good example. Aside from teaching the next generation of flute players, she provides private tutoring lessons to children and has produced two CDs of her own music. She is an artist for Powell Flutes, and she recorded a musical K-12 program for Warner Brothers. Garcia recently began recording music for movies, including the independent film The End of the Spear.

When shes not in Europe or Latin America in the summer, she runs UCFs Summer Flute Institute. During the one-week program, students from around the world descend on UCF to learn about the flute and its beauty.

Garcia also is director of the UCF Flute Choir, which this year was invited to perform at the National Flute Association Convention in August. The choir auditioned along with 399 choirs. Only 14 were invited.

Next month Garcia will return to Mexico for another concert with the Monterrey Symphony Orchestra. She plays with the flute quartet Latin Flute Fusion and plays principal flute with the Florida Chamber Orchestra. In the past, she has played with the Pegasus Wind Quintet, Millennium Trio, the Garcia/Scharron flute and guitar duo and the Brevard Symphony Orchestra.

Her office is adorned with wooden flutes from the countries shes visited on tour.

When at home, she coordinates performances that will benefit UCF students and the community. For example, she persuaded Yale University Flute Professor Ransom Wilson to spend a day with her students in February 2007.

Wilson is the founder and conductor of Solisti New York Orchestra as well as the artistic director of Oklahomas OK MOZART International Festival.  He has recorded more than 30 albums and is a three-time Grammy nominee. He is scheduled to be at UCF for Flute Day on Feb. 27, a day that will include a concert, master classes and other activities.

Theres so much out there, and so many talented people that we can learn from, Garcia said. Thats why I like for my students to travel and participate in international music programs, and thats why I spend time getting top people to come here.
