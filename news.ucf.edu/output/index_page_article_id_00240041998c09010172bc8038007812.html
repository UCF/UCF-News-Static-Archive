Political Columnist Discusses Social Security, Foreign Policy

By Joshua Smith Feb. 10, 2005

Photo: Ed Heiland

George Will's columns appear in more than 500 newspapers in the United States and Europe.

George Will, a nationally syndicated political columnist, explained President George W. Bushs motives behind reforming Social Security and the dynamic nature of foreign and domestic dangers Thursday at the University of Central Florida.

Inside a packed Pegasus Ballroom, Will expressed his understanding of President Bushs Social Security reform and the reasoning behind the changes. He said Americans have informed the president that they want to live in a welfare state, but to make this happen, the conservatives of the American political system must make sure that these programs are earned and not given out as yet another entitlement.

A man who asks himself WWMD (What would Madison do)?, Will also told audience members that even if the Social Security system were solvent, the president would still be attempting to reform it and would be morally obligated to do so. He said Americans have become too used to living in a country where entitlements are commonplace and that citizens expect the government to give them too much without doing any of the work themselves. Referring to this mindset, Will said, no one washes a rental car.

Will's columns, which have been syndicated since 1974, appear in nearly 500 newspapers in the United States and Europe. He won a Pulitzer Prize in 1977. He became a founding panelist on ABC television's "This Week" in 1981.

 

On the topic of foreign policy, Will said a tiny vial of smallpox can create more devastation than another nations armed forces. A country as large as the United States cannot have tight border security, and that makes the use of preemptive force imperative, Will said.

Will referred to our continued involvement in Iraq as a you break it, you buy it situation. Discussing how long we may be in Iraq, he concurred with President Bush and said that is going to require the work of generations.

Even though Will said he is a pessimist, he said America should be celebrating a great time of cheerfulness due to the fact that we are now much safer than we were prior to attacking Iraq.

 

Wills speech was sponsored by the UCF Global Perspectives Office, the Lou Frey Institute of Politics and Government at UCF, the Student Government Association, the Orlando Regional Chamber of Commerce, the Orlando Sentinel, the UCF Department of Political Science and the Global Connections Foundation.
