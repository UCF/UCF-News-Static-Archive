Founding President Millican Honored

By Chad Binette December 1, 2003

Photo: Jerry Klein

Charles and Frances Millican and friend before Monday's event.

Exactly 38 years after he reported to work as the first president of a university with no buildings, no students and no staff, Charles Millican was honored Monday for "laying the foundation for the University of Central Floridas rise from promise to prominence."

Millican, 87, was president of Florida Technological University from 1965 to 1978, when the schools name was changed to the University of Central Florida.

Key decisions during his tenure included setting up the campus as a series of concentric circles, making it walkable for students and faculty, and creating the first bachelors degree in the state in computer science. He also decided that the schools logo would be the Pegasus and that the motto would be "Reach for the Stars

"This university has grown in quantity, quality and stature and is now a dynamic, innovative university," Millican said during the ceremony at the Reflecting Pond, in front of the administration building named in his honor.

Millican received a key to the City of Orlando from Mayor Buddy Dyer. State Sen. Lee Constantine, an Altamonte Springs Republican and 1974 graduate of UCF, presented Millican with a proclamation from the Florida Senate.

Orange County Commission Chairman Rich Crotty, also a UCF alumnus, gave Millican a crystal mousepad with the county logo. UCF President John Hitt presented Millican with framed photographs of how the campus looked during the early years and now.

"Thank you, Charles Millican, for laying the foundation for the University of Central Floridas rise from promise to prominence," Hitt said.

Millican remains active at UCF. He works in public relations for the UCF Foundation, helping the school to raise private money to continue achieving many of the goals he set as president. He will speak at a commencement ceremony on Dec. 15.

While he appreciated the ceremony, Millican said much of the credit for UCFs success goes to other faculty and staff members who worked during the early years. He also praised the efforts of Hitt and former President Trevor Colbourn.

On Dec. 1, 1965, Millican arrived at a school that had 1,227 acres of "scrub oak, scrub pine, palmettos and weeds" but little else. He first worked out of a temporary office at Church Street and Orange Avenue.

"I had no faculty, no buildings, no equipment and a budget for the next seven months of $75,000," Millican recalled.

FTU opened in the fall of 1968 with nearly 1,500 students, 90 faculty members and six buildings.

Millican said he strived to make all students "know they were important." In a practice that has continued under future presidents, Millican held multiple commencement ceremonies each year so that all students could have their names read aloud, walk across the stage and shake hands with the president.

"Its an effort to say, in just a small way, that we do have an interest in each student, each person who comes to this campus," Millican said. "Im deeply proud of the way that they (other presidents) have worked to continue that basic idea."

Millican stressed the importance of research and of the universitys involvement with nearby communities and said he always believed that "if youre going to do anything, do it right."

Millicans wife, Frances, and two of his nephews attended the ceremony. The crowd also included many current and former UCF faculty and staff members and state Reps. Jim Kallinger, R-Winter Park, and David Mealor, R-Lake Mary.

The event was one of many UCF is holding throughout the year to commemorate the 40th anniversary of its establishment by the Florida Legislature.
